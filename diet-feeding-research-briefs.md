## Reptiles

### Leopard Gecko

**Feeding schedule**
Sources agree on the general shape (younger geckos eat more often, in smaller portions, than adults) but give somewhat different specific numbers, so ranges are given rather than one settled figure.

Babies/hatchlings (roughly 0-4 months, and general "young" geckos per vet source VCA): feed daily. Reptile-specific vet source VCA Hospitals states young geckos should be fed daily. Pet Meal Guide's age breakdown gives hatchlings/babies up to 4 months about 4-8 crickets or 1-3 worms per feeding, insects sized around 1/4 inch. Zen Habitats corroborates "juveniles fed daily." One source (Acuario Pets, via search) says young geckos may eat two to three times a day.

Juveniles (roughly 4-12 months): Pet Meal Guide/Krawlo-style breakdown suggests 5-6 days a week with 6-10 medium crickets per feeding. VCA Hospitals and Zen Habitats describe this stage more broadly as "every 1-2 days" or "every other day/every 3 days."

Adults (roughly 12-18+ months, generally once tail is as thick as the neck): most sources converge on feeding every 2-4 days, though the exact figure varies: VCA Hospitals says 2-3 times per week; PetMD-style summary says "every other day"; MedVet says every 2-4 days; Zen Habitats says every 5 days once the tail is fully thickened; a general search summary said "2-3 times a week, 6-10 larger crickets per feeding." Portion-size guidance is fairly consistent: insects should not be larger than the space between the gecko's eyes (PetMD source) or no bigger than half the width of the gecko's head (other sources), and only 1-2 insects offered at a time, or "as many appropriately-sized bugs as it can eat in about 15 minutes" (Zen Habitats).

Feeding time: several sources note leopard geckos are crepuscular/nocturnal hunters, so feeding in the evening mimics natural hunting activity.

**Diet composition**
Leopard geckos are obligate insectivores (unlike some other pet geckos, they do not eat fruit purees). Staple feeder insects cited consistently across sources: commercially-raised (never wild-caught) crickets as the primary staple, plus dubia roaches, discoid/red runner roaches, mealworms, superworms, black soldier fly larvae ("calciworms"/"Phoenix worms"), silkworms, and hornworms (captive-bred hornworms are blue/aqua colored; wild hornworms, which are bright green, are toxic and must not be fed). Waxworms and superworms are repeatedly flagged as high-fat/occasional-treat items rather than staples. Some adult females may also be offered an occasional pinky mouse (Safarivet: no more than one per week; MedVet notes adult females may accept 1-2 day-old pinkies once acclimated), though this is a minority recommendation, not universal.

Preparation/supplementation:
- Gut-loading: feeder insects should be fed a nutritious diet (fresh vegetables like carrots, sweet potato, squash, dark leafy greens, turnip/collard greens, or a commercial gut-load product) for roughly 24-72 hours (sources vary: VCA says at least 24 hours, Reptifiles/other sources say 2-3 days) before being offered to the gecko.
- Calcium dusting: insects should be lightly dusted with calcium powder before every feeding (or most feedings). A shallow dish of plain calcium (no D3) should also be left in the enclosure at all times as free-choice supplementation, refreshed every 1-2 days.
- Vitamin D3: because most leopard geckos are kept without significant UVB exposure historically, calcium with D3 is typically dusted roughly twice weekly (multiple sources converge here: MedVet, Exotic Reptile Care), alternated with D3-free calcium on other feedings.
- Multivitamin: dusted roughly once weekly to once every other week (sources vary between "once or twice a week" and "every other week").
- A veterinary source (Safarivet) adds that many reptile multivitamin formulas rely on beta-carotene, which leopard geckos cannot efficiently convert to usable vitamin A, so preformed vitamin A sources/appropriate supplement choice matters; vitamin A deficiency can itself cause decreased appetite, poor shedding, and mouth rot.
- Insects should be appropriately sized (no bigger than the width between the gecko's eyes, or half the head width) to prevent choking or impaction.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe/occasional treats: waxworms (high fat, ~22% fat per one source, limit to about 2-3 per week), butterworms, and superworms — but superworms are for adults only, since their size and hard chitin exoskeleton make them unsuitable/risky for juveniles.

Unsafe/dangerous: Fireflies/lightning bugs are highly toxic to leopard geckos and reptiles generally and must never be fed (confirmed independently by a veterinary clinic source, Safarivet, and general search results). Wild-caught insects generally are discouraged by most veterinarians due to pesticide/insecticide exposure and parasite risk. Fruits and vegetables are not appropriate direct food for leopard geckos — one veterinary-adjacent source (VCA Hospitals) states plainly that their bodies aren't designed to digest produce, since they are strict insectivores (unlike omnivorous reptile species). Substrate hazards that cause impaction if accidentally ingested during feeding are also repeatedly flagged: loose sand, corncob bedding, and crushed walnut shell should be avoided as enclosure substrate for this reason.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation (winter dormancy)* (normal): A natural hibernation-like slowdown triggered by cooler temperatures/shorter days, causing lethargy, inactivity, and reduced or absent eating and defecating for extended periods, with brief waking to drink. One source notes ambient winter temperatures dropping toward 50F can trigger it.
- *Shedding (ecdysis)* (normal): Geckos commonly go off food, become dull-eyed/cloudy, and hide more in the day or two before and sometimes after a shed; appetite typically returns once the shed is complete.
- *Breeding season / reproductive behavior* (normal): Both males and females may reduce or stop eating during courtship, mating, or (for females) gravidity/egg-laying periods.
- *Low enclosure temperature* (depends): Leopard geckos are ectotherms with temperature-dependent digestion; multiple sources identify insufficiently warm basking/warm-side temps (targets cited around 88-95F warm side, 75-85F cool side) as one of the single most common causes of appetite loss, since a gecko that is too cool cannot digest food properly and will refuse it.
- *Stress (new environment, handling, tankmates, noise/light)* (normal): Newly acquired geckos, recent rehoming, overhandling, or housing with an incompatible/aggressive cage mate can suppress appetite; reducing handling and disturbance typically resolves it.
- *Prey boredom / pickiness* (normal): Some geckos become disinterested in a single repeated feeder insect type and will resume eating once offered a different feeder species or live vs. non-moving prey.
- *Illness or underlying disease (parasites, mouth rot, metabolic bone disease, impaction, infection, vitamin A deficiency)* (concerning): Appetite loss accompanied by other symptoms such as bloating, straining to defecate, loose stools, skin/tail discoloration, poor shed quality, shrunken eyes, shriveled skin, or visible weight loss points to a medical cause rather than a normal cycle.
- *Impaction* (concerning): Ingestion of loose substrate or oversized prey can physically block the digestive tract, causing the gecko to stop eating along with straining or bloating.

**When to actually worry**
Sources give a range rather than one agreed number (see caveats). General consensus across care-sheet and forum/vet-Q&A sources: brief food refusal of several days up to about two weeks is commonly described as within normal range, especially around a shed, a temperature dip, or a new environment. Some sources put the outer bound of "still probably fine, keep monitoring" at two to three weeks for a healthy adult with a plump, fat tail, since tail fat reserves let adults go extended periods without harm. Others are more conservative, saying to seek vet care if refusal passes roughly two weeks combined with any other symptom, while one source frames a full month of refusal as the clearer trigger point for a vet visit regardless of other signs.

Regardless of the exact day count, the recurring, concrete red flags that sources agree warrant a reptile-experienced vet visit rather than continued at-home monitoring are: visible weight loss or a visibly thinning/shrinking tail (the tail is the fat reserve, so a tail that gets noticeably thinner over a couple of weeks of not eating is a specific, trackable warning sign), lethargy or unusual inactivity beyond typical brumation behavior, bloating, straining to defecate or loose stools, discoloration of skin or tail, poor or incomplete shedding, sunken/shrunken eyes, or shriveled/wrinkled skin (a dehydration sign). One source specifically recommends weighing the gecko regularly (twice weekly during a suspected brumation period) since geckos should not be losing significant body weight even while brumating, and continued weight loss despite normal brumation behavior is a reason to get it checked. If a gecko fails to respond to basic habitat corrections (raising warm-side temperature, reducing stress/handling, offering a different feeder insect) within a reasonable window, that is also treated as a signal to escalate to a vet rather than keep waiting.

**FunFact candidate**
A leopard gecko's tail isn't just for balance, it's a dedicated fat-storage organ (mostly triglycerides) that the animal metabolizes to survive weeks or, with a well-fed plump tail, reportedly even months without food. Even more striking: a peer-reviewed study in the Journal of Experimental Zoology (via PubMed/ScienceDirect) found that when a juvenile leopard gecko drops and regrows its tail, the regenerated tail actually stores more fat, muscle, and skeletal mass than the original tail did, even in geckos on a growth-limiting diet, suggesting the animal biologically prioritizes rebuilding its emergency energy reserve over general body growth after tail loss.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two real numeric disagreements exist across sources, so treat any single figure with caution:

1) Adult feeding frequency: sources range from "every other day" (PetMD-derived summary) to "2-3 times per week" (VCA Hospitals, a veterinary source) to "every 2-4 days" (MedVet, a veterinary source) to "every 5 days" once the tail is fully thickened (Zen Habitats). These aren't wildly inconsistent (they overlap around 2-4 day intervals) but they are not identical, and no single number should be presented as the definitive standard; a range is more honest than picking one.

2) "How long without eating is normal before worrying": search-aggregated advice varied from as short as roughly 7-14 days, to two to three weeks for a healthy adult, up to a full month before treating it as a hard vet trigger. This is a meaningful spread and was explicitly called out because a prior article on this same site was corrected after stating a disputed figure as settled fact — recommend presenting this as a range tied to accompanying symptoms (tail condition, weight, lethargy) rather than a single day count.

Minor secondary inconsistency: supplementation cadence for multivitamins was described as "once or twice weekly" by MedVet-style sources versus "once every other week" by another (Exotic Reptile Care / search-aggregated summary) and "twice monthly" by Safarivet — general direction (multivitamin used far less often than plain calcium) is consistent, but exact frequency differs by source.

### Corn Snake

**Feeding schedule**
Sources broadly agree on the trajectory (less frequent as the snake ages) but disagree on exact intervals, especially for babies. Specialist reptile-care guides (ReptiFiles, Zen Habitats, Krawlo) give the most granular breakdown, usually keyed to age AND weight:
- Hatchlings (roughly 0-3 months old, under ~30g): every 5-7 days, starting on pinky mice.
- Young juveniles (~3-9 months, 30-100g): every 7 days.
- Juveniles (~9-18 months, 100-300g): every 7-10 days.
- Sub-adults (~18-36 months, 300-600g): every 10-14 days.
- Adults (3+ years, 600g+): every 14-21 days.
PetMD (a vet-content site) gives a simpler, more frequent version: babies "every other day," juveniles "once a week," adults "every one to two weeks." This is a real discrepancy for the baby stage in particular (every-other-day vs. every 5-7 days) — see caveats.
Prey sizing (fairly consistent across sources): roughly 1 to 1.5x the width of the snake's body at its widest point, or about 10-15% of body weight per meal; if in between sizes, two smaller prey items can substitute for one larger one. Multiple sources stress that body condition/weight trend (monthly weigh-ins) should override a rigid calendar — and at least one source (Krawlo) warns that feeding adults on a too-frequent schedule can produce obesity within 12-18 months.

**Diet composition**
Staple diet is whole, frozen-thawed rodents — pinky mice for hatchlings, moving up through fuzzies/hoppers to adult mice or small rats as the snake grows. Frozen-thawed is strongly preferred over live prey across every source consulted: live rodents can bite, scratch, or claw the snake, and PetMD explicitly warns this can cause "life-threatening infections." Prey should come from commercial/captive-bred sources, not wild-caught, to avoid parasites, pesticides, and disease. A few guides note dietary variety is possible with other whole prey — young rats, quail, quail eggs, day-old chicks, and small lizards like green anoles or house geckos — though mice remain the default staple in the pet trade.
Thawing: refrigerator overnight, then warmed in warm (not boiling) water to roughly 98-102°F to mimic living prey's body heat and scent; never microwaved, never re-frozen once thawed and refused.
Supplementation: most sources agree whole prey (bones, organs included) provides complete nutrition and that routine supplementation is NOT necessary for corn snakes eating a normal whole-prey diet, unlike insect-eating reptiles. Where dusting is recommended at all, it's specifically for hatchlings on pinky mice (light dusting with a calcium/multivitamin mix every 3-4 feedings, per Krawlo and a Furry Critter Network summary), tapering off once the snake is on fuzzies or larger. Vitamin D3 supplementation is generally considered unnecessary since corn snakes don't require UVB exposure the way many lizards do, and multiple sources warn over-supplementing calcium can itself cause problems (mineral imbalance, kidney stress, soft-tissue calcification).

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Corn snakes don't have much of a "treat" culture the way omnivorous pets do — appropriately-sized whole rodent prey is both the staple and effectively the only food. A few guides mention occasional dietary variety (day-old chicks, quail eggs) as acceptable rather than a true "treat."
Unsafe/to avoid: live prey (injury/infection risk to the snake, not the other way around); wild-caught prey of any kind (disease, parasites, pesticide residue); meat scraps, pieces of meat, or cooked/processed food; prey that was thawed, refused, and then re-frozen (bacterial risk); insects — corn snakes reportedly have an aversion to insects and they are not a natural or recommended food source for this species; and feeding again within about 24 hours of the last meal, since that's not enough time to digest.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Shedding (ecdysis)* (normal): Sources consistently describe reduced appetite roughly 5-7 days before a shed (recognizable by the eyes clouding over/going 'milky' or blue-tinged) and sometimes for a short window after. Corn snakes are more vulnerable and uncomfortable during this process.
- *Brumation (winter dormancy)* (normal): Either a deliberate breeder practice or a seasonal instinct even in some pet setups. Properly done, keepers gradually drop enclosure temps over 1-2 weeks to roughly 50-55°F on the cool side / 60-65°F on the warm side, and the snake then goes without food for a commonly cited 8-12 weeks (sources range 2-4 months). Critically, multiple breeder sources stress a mandatory 10-14 day pre-brumation fast at normal temperature first, because undigested food sitting in a cooled gut can rot and cause a fatal internal infection.
- *Breeding season food refusal (mainly males)* (normal): Males in particular commonly stop eating for weeks to months post-brumation while focused on searching for a mate, showing increased restlessness and tongue-flicking instead. Multiple sources say this is normal as long as the snake stays active and doesn't lose excessive body condition, though it requires closer weight monitoring than usual.
- *Environmental stress (new enclosure, handling, disturbance)* (depends): Recent rehoming, cage changes, excessive handling, or an insecure enclosure (inadequate hides) can suppress feeding response; several guides suggest waiting about 48 hours after a disturbance before offering food again.
- *Wrong husbandry conditions (temperature)* (concerning): Corn snakes need a warm side around 82-85°F to digest food properly; if the enclosure runs too cold, the snake may refuse food because it physically can't digest a meal safely. This is a fixable husbandry problem, not a benign phase.
- *Prey presentation issues (temperature, scent, live-to-frozen transition)* (normal): Cold or improperly-thawed prey, or a snake that's become a 'creature of habit' and balks at a switch from live to frozen-thawed feeders, can cause refusal that isn't health-related. Scenting the frozen prey (rubbing it against live-prey scent, soiled bedding, or a small amount of broth/tuna juice) is a commonly cited fix.
- *Illness, parasites, or respiratory infection* (concerning): When food refusal is paired with other signs (see whenToWorry), an underlying medical issue becomes the likely explanation and needs veterinary diagnosis.

**When to actually worry**
Sources disagree meaningfully on the threshold for concern (see caveats), so the honest range is: a healthy adult corn snake can physically survive roughly 2-3 months without food thanks to a slow reptile metabolism, and several reptile-specialist sources state it's "not normal" for a captive corn snake to refuse food for more than about 1-2 months outside of a planned brumation or a female's pre-egg-laying fast — implying refusals shorter than that are often not an emergency by themselves. PetMD, by contrast, gives much more conservative vet-oriented guidance: consult a vet if the snake misses more than a couple of feeding sessions, especially if it isn't currently shedding.
Babies/hatchlings have far less reserve than adults — one source specifically warns that a baby corn snake going without food for over about a week can already become a serious health issue and may need veterinary force-feeding.
Regardless of duration, the accompanying red flags that always warrant a vet visit are: visible weight loss, lethargy or loss of coordination, respiratory signs (wheezing, abnormal discharge, open-mouth breathing), vomiting or regurgitation, diarrhea, abdominal swelling, and sores in the mouth or on the skin. Practical guidance repeated across sources: track body condition with monthly weigh-ins rather than fixating on a day count, and give more benefit of the doubt to a snake that's active, alert, and holding weight (e.g., a breeding-season male or a pre-shed snake) than to one that's lethargic or visibly thinning.

**FunFact candidate**
Digesting a meal is metabolically dramatic for a corn snake. A peer-reviewed study on corn snakes (Pantherophis guttatus) specifically, "Determinants and repeatability of the specific dynamic response of the corn snake" (published research indexed on PubMed), measured this "specific dynamic action" (SDA) directly: as meal size increased from 5% up to 45% of the snake's body mass, oxygen consumption after eating rose incrementally higher and stayed elevated longer — and the broader snake-physiology literature on SDA notes oxygen consumption can spike roughly four- to six-fold above resting levels after a big meal. The same corn snake study found that how much the snake ate — not its body temperature or its size — was the main thing driving how big and how long that post-meal metabolic surge lasted.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Baby/hatchling feeding frequency: PetMD (vet-content site) says feed babies "every other day," while every specialist reptile-care source consulted (ReptiFiles, Zen Habitats, Krawlo) independently converges on every 5-7 days for hatchlings under ~3 months. That's roughly a 2-3x difference in frequency for the same life stage — worth stating as a real disagreement rather than averaging. The specialist consensus (5-7 days) is more consistent across independent sources and is what I'd weight more heavily, but PetMD's figure should not be silently dropped.
2) Adult feeding interval: PetMD says "every one to two weeks" (7-14 days); specialist sites converge on 14-21 days for a fully mature adult. There's overlap at 14 days but PetMD's lower bound is more frequent than any specialist source recommends for a full adult, and one specialist source (Krawlo) explicitly warns that feeding grown adults too often risks obesity within 12-18 months.
3) How long without food is okay before worrying: this is the biggest disagreement found. PetMD's threshold for calling a vet is very conservative — "more than a couple of feeding sessions." Multiple reptile-specialist/breeder sources (TerrariumQuest, aggregated forum/care-sheet consensus) are far more lenient, stating healthy adults can safely go 2-3 months without eating and that refusal isn't considered abnormal until it passes roughly 1-2 months (outside of planned brumation or pre-lay fasting). I presented both rather than picking one, since the gap is large and both source types are legitimate but talking past each other (vet caution vs. keeper/breeder field experience with a species known for long voluntary fasts).

### Red-Eared Slider

**Feeding schedule**
Sources agree on the basic shape but differ on exact frequency wording. Juveniles/young turtles: feed daily (VCA Animal Hospitals: "will eat heartily every day"; Mesa Veterinary Hospital: "fed daily"; Hepper/vet-reviewed by Dr. Amanda Charles: "once or twice a day is recommended"). Adults: feed every 2-3 days, not daily (VCA: "offered a good-sized portion of food every two or three days"; Mesa Vet: "fed every other day"; Hepper: "every 2 to 3 days is a good rule of thumb"). Portion size: the widely repeated rule of thumb (Hepper vet-reviewed guide, and echoed by Petco-sourced material) is to feed as much as the turtle can finish in about 15-20 minutes, then remove uneaten food. One additional juvenile-specific portion cue that appears in retailer material: an amount roughly the size of the turtle's own head, or as much as it will eat in 10-15 minutes.

**Diet composition**
Red-eared sliders are omnivores whose protein-to-plant ratio shifts with age: young/growing turtles need proportionally more animal protein, and adults shift toward being mostly herbivorous. VCA Animal Hospitals states the carnivorous portion of the diet "should compose no more than two thirds of the diet of juveniles and about half of the diet of adults." Mesa Veterinary Hospital says younger turtles need "up to 40% of their food from protein sources," with a general adult breakdown of roughly 25% commercial pellet food, 25% animal protein, and about 50% plant matter. (See caveatsOrDisputes — other non-veterinary sites give more specific but conflicting percentage splits, so treat exact percentages as approximate guidance rather than settled fact.) Specific foods actually fed: Commercial pellets — named vet-cited brands include Fluker's, Mazuri, and Zoo Med (larger floating pellets for big turtles, smaller sinking pellets for juveniles/hatchlings). Animal protein — live feeder fish (goldfish, guppies, minnows; note VCA flags feeder fish as a possible parasite/bacteria carrier so use with some caution), earthworms, snails, crickets, mealworms, waxworms, and occasionally small amounts of cooked (not raw) chicken or beef. Plant matter — dark leafy greens such as romaine, collard, mustard, and kale, plus dandelion greens, shredded carrots/carrot tops, squash, and green beans; fresh is preferred but frozen vegetables are acceptable occasionally; some fruit (apples, melons, berries) can be offered in small amounts. Supplementation: VCA and Mesa Vet both note vets commonly recommend a balanced commercial reptile multivitamin about once a week, plus an added calcium source (calcium block or cuttlebone) about twice a week. Earthworms and other feeder insects can be "gut-loaded" (fed a nutrient-dense formula, e.g. Fluker's gut-load products) before being offered to the turtle. Turtles must eat while at least partially submerged in water — this isn't a preparation step so much as a biological requirement (see interestingFact).

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats/foods: leafy dark greens (kale, collard, mustard, dandelion greens), carrots/carrot tops, squash, green beans, berries, melon, apple (in moderation), earthworms, crickets, mealworms/waxworms, and gut-loaded feeder insects. Unsafe or problematic foods, per Voyo Pets and corroborating sources: avocado (contains persin, potentially toxic, can affect heart/respiratory function), onion, and rhubarb are flagged as potentially toxic; iceberg lettuce is not toxic but is nutritionally near-empty and should be avoided in favor of nutrient-dense greens; dairy products should be avoided because turtles cannot digest lactose; processed human foods like bread or pasta offer poor nutrition and can cause digestive/obesity issues; raw grocery-store meat and wild-caught/outdoor-caught organisms are flagged by VCA specifically as things not to feed (parasite and contamination risk); spinach is not strictly forbidden but contains oxalates that can bind calcium if fed too often, so it should be occasional rather than a staple.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation (seasonal dormancy)* (normal): A natural cold-season slowdown, roughly October through March per one source (SPAH), during which appetite drops or stops even though the turtle stays otherwise responsive. Multiple sources caution that indoor pet sliders kept at stable warm temperatures often do not fully brumate, so sudden appetite loss in a consistently warm tank is more likely something else (stress, illness, temperature problem) than true brumation.
- *Water/basking temperature too low* (concerning): Described as one of the most common and correctable causes. One source gives concrete target ranges: water 75-80°F (24-27°C) and a basking spot of 85-90°F (29-32°C); water below about 72°F was described as slowing metabolism enough that digestion essentially stops. Other sources (e.g. All Turtles' vet-cited material) similarly point to cold tanks or insufficient basking/lighting hours as a top cause of appetite loss.
- *Stress from a new or changed environment* (normal): A recent tank change, rearrangement, move, or new handling routine can suppress appetite for roughly one to two weeks as the turtle adjusts.
- *Poor water quality* (concerning): High ammonia/nitrite from an undersized filter or infrequent water changes causes chronic stress that can suppress appetite.
- *Shedding of scutes / diet-switching pickiness* (normal): Sliders may eat less while shedding scutes, and can be neophobic, refusing a newly offered food type when switched from a familiar one.
- *Gravidity (egg-bearing females)* (depends): A gravid female commonly loses her appetite, even for favorite treats, because developing eggs take up significant internal space; this is typically a normal, temporary phase. However, if she shows gravid signs, then goes a week or more without laying, becomes weak, or strains without producing eggs, that indicates egg binding (dystocia) -- a veterinary emergency, not a normal appetite dip.
- *Illness (respiratory infection, vitamin A deficiency, parasites)* (concerning): Not eating combined with other signs -- wheezing/clicking breathing, mucus or bubbling from the nose/mouth, open-mouth breathing, swollen or partially closed eyelids (linked to vitamin A deficiency), abnormal/uneven floating, or shell abnormalities -- points to underlying illness rather than a normal appetite lull. VCA and Mesa Veterinary Hospital both list "not eating" among general common signs of illness in aquatic turtles.

**When to actually worry**
Guidance converges around a roughly 2-3 week threshold for total food refusal outside of brumation. One source (SPAH) gives a specific number: call a vet if the slider "hasn't eaten for more than three weeks outside the October to March brumation window," or sooner if warning signs appear alongside the appetite loss. A separate search-aggregated source similarly states "more than 2-3 weeks of complete food refusal -- especially paired with lethargy or other symptoms -- warrants a vet visit," and notes that appetite loss plus lethargy can indicate illness rather than brumation, especially if tank temperatures are stable and warm (i.e., there's no seasonal/cold-related excuse). Accompanying signs that should prompt a vet visit regardless of how long the turtle has refused food: respiratory symptoms (wheezing, clicking, open-mouth breathing, nasal/mouth discharge), swollen or closed eyes, floating unevenly or abnormally in the water, a soft or discolored shell, and significant lethargy even when the tank is at proper temperature. For a gravid female, straining without producing eggs, or going a week or more past expected laying while weak, is described as an emergency requiring immediate reptile-vet care ("do not wait it out").

**FunFact candidate**
Red-eared sliders lack salivary glands, so they physically cannot swallow food on land -- they can only eat while at least partially submerged, using the surrounding water the way saliva works for land animals to help move food down. This is why a slider that has stopped eating should always be offered food in its water, not on a basking dock, and why owners sometimes mistake "won't eat on land" for a health problem when it's just normal anatomy.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
There is real, unresolved disagreement across sources on the exact protein-to-plant percentage split by age, and none of the conflicting numeric breakdowns comes from a clearly vet-authored primary source -- treat any specific percentage as a rough guideline, not a settled figure. Examples of the spread found: (1) VCA Animal Hospitals (a genuine veterinary source, but doesn't give an age-banded percentage table) says carnivorous food should be "no more than two thirds of the diet of juveniles and about half of the diet of adults." (2) Mesa Veterinary Hospital cites juveniles needing "up to 40% protein" and gives an adult-oriented split of roughly 25% commercial pellets / 25% animal protein / 50% plant matter. (3) Aggregated retailer/care-sheet content (Petco/Hepper-sourced search summaries) describes a starker split: under-1-year sliders at roughly 50% protein/50% vegetables with protein fed daily, versus over-1-year sliders at roughly 25% protein/75% vegetables with protein only 1-2x/week. (4) Other non-veterinary care sites (tortoiseturtle.com, turtletimes.com) gave yet another split -- 60% protein/40% plant for juveniles, 30% protein/70% plant for adults fed three times weekly -- which does not match any of the above. Given this spread, the safest framing for the article is qualitative (young turtles eat proportionally more protein than adults, and the ratio should shift toward vegetables/greens with age) rather than presenting any single percentage as the definitive number. Feeding frequency for adults also has minor variation ("every other day" vs. "every 2-3 days" vs. "every third day" in different sources), though this is a narrower, less consequential disagreement than the percentage split. ### Crested Gecko

**Feeding schedule**
Sources consistently break feeding down by life stage:
- Hatchlings/juveniles (roughly 0-12 months): commercial crested gecko diet (CGD) offered daily, with live insects added 1-2 times per week, mainly for enrichment/extra protein (dubiaroaches.com care sheet; moonvalleyreptiles.com care guide).
- Adults (12+ months): CGD every 2-3 days (some sources say "every other day" or "3 times per week," which is functionally the same cadence), with live insects offered about once a week (dubiaroaches.com; moonvalleyreptiles.com; Geckos Unlimited breeder forum threads).
Portion size cited: roughly 1/2 teaspoon of mixed CGD for a juvenile and about 1 teaspoon for an adult per feeding, adjusted based on how much is actually eaten (Geckos Unlimited community threads, corroborated by multiple care-sheet sites). Uneaten prepared food should be removed within about 24-48 hours to prevent mold/spoilage; some sources specify offering only as much as the gecko will finish within about 4 hours. Insects should be sized no larger than the width of the gecko's head, and any not eaten within 15-20 minutes should be removed so they don't stress or nip the gecko overnight.

**Diet composition**
In captivity, the staple is a complete powdered "crested gecko diet" (CGD) - a meal-replacement powder mixed with water to a ketchup/pudding-like consistency, designed to supply protein, fruit, calcium, and vitamin D3 in one product. Trusted commercial brands repeatedly named across sources: Pangea and Repashy (the two most established), plus Leapin' Leachie, Lugarti, Black Panther Zoological, Gecko Pro, and Arcadia (dubiaroaches.com care sheet). Keepers are advised to rotate at least 2-3 flavors/brands, both for enrichment and because crested geckos can be notably picky and may refuse a flavor they dislike (MorphMarket and Geckos Unlimited keeper community threads; one source specifically calls out Pangea's watermelon flavor as having unusually high acceptance among picky eaters).

CGD is meant to be the nutritional foundation; live insects (dubia roaches, discoid roaches, red runner roaches, crickets, hornworms, occasionally superworms) are fed as a supplemental/enrichment protein source, not the base diet. Feeder insects should be gut-loaded 24-48 hours before feeding using a commercial gut-load plus a hydration source, then lightly dusted with a reptile calcium supplement (Repashy Supercal NoD or Arcadia EarthPro CalciumPro Mg were the specific products named) before each feeding; occasionally skipping a dusting is described as fine. Care sheets caution against introducing insects before a gecko is reliably eating CGD, since geckos can learn to "hold out" for insects and refuse the complete diet, risking malnutrition.

Fresh pureed fruit (mashed/baby-food style, not chunks) can be offered as an occasional treat: raspberries, grapes, melon, banana, peaches, mango, apricot, and papaya were named across a veterinary-hospital-style feeding overview and breeder/keeper sites. Frequency suggested by one source was roughly every 2 days for fruit puree as a treat alongside CGD, though most care sheets frame fruit as occasional rather than on a fixed schedule, warning that fruit fed too often can cause a gecko to reject its nutritionally complete CGD.

In the wild, crested geckos are omnivorous frugivore/nectarivore/insectivores native to New Caledonia. A fecal-analysis diet study (Seipp & Henkel, "Rhacodactylus: Biology, Natural History and Husbardry," 2000) found the wild diet was roughly half insects (about 49.6%, dominated by Orthoptera, beetles, roaches, caterpillars/moths, and flies) plus berries, pollen aggregates, soft seeds, and fruit juices, with the remainder made up of small vertebrate prey.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat items named across sources: pureed/mashed banana, mango, papaya, apricot, peach, melon, and berries offered occasionally alongside (not instead of) a staple CGD diet; gut-loaded, calcium-dusted dubia roaches, discoid roaches, red runner roaches, crickets, and hornworms as occasional live-food supplements.

Unsafe/toxic items named: avocado (contains persin, toxic to many animals including reptiles); citrus fruits (oranges, grapefruit, lemon, lime - high acidity/oxalates that interfere with calcium absorption and can upset digestion); rhubarb and other very high-oxalate produce; fireflies/lightning bugs (described as highly toxic to reptiles and should never be fed, a point echoed independently of the crested-gecko-specific searches for reptiles generally); wild-caught insects, which risk pesticide exposure or parasites and are commonly advised against; and household/plant hazards such as onion, garlic, and eggplant, plus common houseplants like English ivy, oleander-type ornamentals, and cleaning-product residue (bleach, phenols from pine/cedar substrate) in the enclosure.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Shedding (ecdysis)* (normal): Shedding is described as energy-intensive and uncomfortable; appetite commonly dips for a few days before and/or after a shed, with one source citing roughly 2-3 days on either side, another describing the whole reduced-appetite window as lasting up to about a week.
- *Breeding season / gravidity in females* (normal): Female crested geckos have a natural breeding cycle cited as spanning roughly 8-10 months of the year with clutches roughly every 4-6 weeks; hormonal shifts and physical abdominal pressure from developing eggs can suppress appetite for a week or more. Gravid females often look visibly plump/distended and become less active.
- *New environment / handling / general stress* (normal): A new enclosure, excessive handling, loud noise, inadequate hides/enrichment, or other pets in the household are commonly cited stressors that can make a crested gecko go off food while it adjusts.
- *Seasonal slowdown (winter, non-true brumation)* (normal): Crested geckos are tropical and, unlike some temperate reptiles, are not supposed to be put through a deliberate cooling/brumation cycle by pet owners. Even so, keepers report captive cresties sometimes eating less and being less active in winter months due to internal seasonal rhythms as well as ambient light/temperature shifts, even when husbandry conditions stay stable.
- *Picky eating / flavor or texture rejection* (normal): Crested geckos are frequently described by breeders as pickier than most reptile keepers expect - a gecko may refuse one CGD flavor/brand or texture (thick vs. runny) while readily eating another; this is treated as an individual-preference issue solved by rotating brands/flavors, not a health issue.
- *Wrong husbandry (temperature, humidity, enclosure size)* (concerning): Heat stress (temperatures above about 85°F are cited as risking being fatal), incorrect humidity (60-80% cited as the target range), poor ventilation, or an undersized enclosure can suppress appetite and cause lethargy.
- *Illness/parasites/infection* (concerning): Named medical causes of appetite loss include mouth rot (infectious stomatitis), parasites (pinworms, coccidia), respiratory infection, gastrointestinal impaction (sometimes from ingesting loose substrate), metabolic bone disease from inadequate calcium/D3, and organ (liver/kidney) compromise from toxin exposure or infection.
- *Egg binding (dystocia) in females* (concerning): Distinct from normal pre-lay appetite dips, dystocia (eggs that cannot be laid) is described as a dangerous condition requiring immediate veterinary attention, not something to wait out.
- *Dehydration* (concerning): Insufficient humidity/misting or water access can cause dehydration, which shows as lethargy and a wrinkled/loose-skinned appearance and can suppress feeding interest.

**When to actually worry**
Multiple sources converge on the same rough window: a healthy, good-weight adult crested gecko can go about 2-3 weeks without eating and be physically fine, but if refusal passes about 2 weeks (some phrase it as "after the second week"), a vet consult is recommended, especially if weight loss is also visible. Juveniles have much less reserve than adults, so a young gecko refusing food for more than just a few days is considered more urgent than an adult doing the same and should not be given the same 2-3 week grace period. Concrete red flags cited for seeking veterinary care regardless of how long it's been: visible weight loss, a "bony" spine/hip/tail base, sunken eyes, lethargy or loss of grip strength, a soft or rubbery lower jaw (a metabolic bone disease sign), and any rapid (not gradual) weight loss. Separately, crested geckos are described as able to go only about 3 days without water, which is a shorter and more urgent clock than the food-refusal timeline. General practice recommended by breeder/care sources is to weigh the gecko roughly monthly (or every 3-4 days if intentionally monitoring through a seasonal slowdown) to catch a real declining trend early rather than reacting to a single skipped meal.

**FunFact candidate**
A fecal-analysis study of wild crested geckos (Seipp & Henkel, "Rhacodactylus: Biology, Natural History and Husbandry," 2000) found their natural diet is roughly half insects by volume, plus fruit, berries, and pollen - but it also documented remains of smaller lizards, including young of their own species, making occasional cannibalism/small-lizard predation a real, if minor, part of the wild diet rather than the popular "fruit-loving gecko" image alone.

1. Raspberries: one veterinary-hospital-style feeding overview lists raspberries among safe pureed fruit treats, while a separate source (petengineers.com) lists raspberries under "high-oxalic" fruits to avoid alongside walnuts and rhubarb. ### Bearded Dragon

**Feeding schedule**
Sources agree feeding frequency drops as the dragon ages, but the exact numbers vary by source type (teaching-hospital/vet sources are more conservative than enthusiast care sites). Using the two clinical sources as the backbone:

- Hatchlings (under ~1 month): NC State's veterinary teaching hospital says feed 2-3 times daily, primarily flightless fruit flies and gut-loaded pinhead crickets. Enthusiast sites (reptile.guide, Dragon's Diet, Ovipost) commonly cite 3-5 feedings/day with as many appropriately-sized insects as the dragon will eat in a 5-10 minute window - more granular than the vet source but not vet-sourced itself.
- 1-4 months: NC State says twice daily, insects plus vegetables introduced, with occasional super/mealworms.
- Juveniles (~4 months to adulthood, roughly 12-18 months): NC State says once daily (crickets, super/mealworms, occasional pinky mice, salad every other day). VCA Animal Hospitals more generally says "young dragons" once or twice daily. Enthusiast sites often specify 1-2 insect feedings/day of 5-6 appropriately-sized insects.
- Adults (18+ months / sexually mature): VCA and NC State agree - once daily or every other day (VCA phrases it as every 24-72 hours), since growth has stopped and metabolism slows. Insects are offered a few times a week rather than daily; salad/greens are offered daily.

Bearded dragons are ectotherms, so "how much" also depends on whether basking/ambient temperatures let them digest - feed only when bask temps are in the correct range, per NC State and PetMD.

**Diet composition**
Diet is a mix of live feeder insects and plant matter, with the ratio flipping with age (juveniles insect-heavy, adults plant-heavy) - this mirrors a documented wild dietary shift in the genus (see interestingFact).

Feeder insects (all vet sources): calcium-dusted crickets, Dubia roaches, black soldier fly larvae, silkworms, and hornworms as regular staples. Mealworms and superworms are acceptable but should not be a daily staple because they're fatty and, per PetMD/dental sources, their hard exoskeletons can contribute to tooth wear; waxworms are explicitly called out by NC State/VCA as a fatty "treat only" item to avoid feeding daily to prevent obesity. Insects should be no larger than the space between the dragon's eyes/roughly half the width of its head (multiple vet-adjacent sources) to avoid choking, impaction, and tooth damage. Never feed wild-caught insects (pesticide/parasite risk) or fireflies (see safeUnsafeFoods).

Gut-loading and dusting (VCA, NC State agree): feeder insects should be gut-loaded on a calcium-rich diet 24-72 hours before being fed to the dragon, and used within about 12 hours of gut-loading. Insects are also lightly dusted with a phosphorus-free calcium powder (calcium carbonate/gluconate/lactate) - VCA/NC State say daily on food, with calcium+D3 powder used 2-3 times a week; PetMD frames it as calcium dusting plus a reptile multivitamin powder 1-2 times weekly. Dusting is explicitly not a substitute for gut-loading. UVB lighting is called essential (NC State) for the dragon to synthesize/use vitamin D3 for calcium absorption - without it, dusting alone is insufficient.

Vegetables/greens (VCA, NC State): should make up the large majority of plant matter (NC State: 80-90% of plant material as leafy greens). Good staples: collard greens, mustard greens, turnip greens, dandelion greens, watercress, escarole, bell peppers, green beans, squash, carrots. Feed sparingly, not never: spinach, Swiss chard, and beet greens (oxalates that bind calcium); cabbage, kale, and mustard greens in caution due to goitrogens if overfed. Avoid iceberg lettuce and celery - not toxic, just nutritionally empty (mostly water/fiber).

Fruit is a minor component/treat, capped around 5-10% of the diet depending on source (NC State: max 5% of adult diet; PetMD similar).

Larger adult dragons may occasionally get a pinky/fuzzy mouse (NC State specifically flags this as appropriate mainly for reproducing/gravid females, at most about once weekly) - this is not a routine food item.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (fed sparingly, not as staples): papaya, melon/watermelon, banana, figs, dates, apricots, strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, mango, peaches - consistently listed across VCA, NC State, and PetMD as occasional fruit treats (capped around 5-10% of total diet).

Unsafe/toxic - do not feed:
- Avocado (contains persin, an antifungal toxin that's genuinely dangerous to bearded dragons)
- Rhubarb, especially the leaves (anthraquinones plus oxalates - serious digestive distress)
- Onion, garlic, leeks, chives (linked to red blood cell damage and GI upset)
- Fireflies/lightning bugs and other bioluminescent insects (contain lucibufagins - described as potentially lethal even in small amounts; never feed wild-caught fireflies or any wild-caught insects)
- Wild-caught insects generally (pesticide/parasite exposure risk)
- Dog food, cat food, raw meat, rabbit food, and eggs (NC State/PetMD-adjacent sources list these as inappropriate, nutritionally unbalanced items some owners mistakenly offer)
- Citrus fruits (digestive irritation)
- Iceberg lettuce and celery - not toxic, but nutritionally near-empty and shouldn't replace real greens
- High-oxalate greens (spinach, Swiss chard, beet greens) and goitrogenic greens (cabbage, kale, mustard greens) should be limited/rotated rather than fed as a daily staple, per VCA and NC State
- Insects noticeably wider than the space between the dragon's eyes (choking/impaction/tooth-damage risk, not a toxicity issue but a real safety rule repeated across sources)

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation (seasonal dormancy)* (normal): A metabolic slowdown similar to hibernation that most captive bearded dragons can enter, typically starting between roughly 1 and 3 years old. Duration is commonly cited as 1-3 months (one vet-reviewed source) up to 1-4 months, occasionally longer. Appetite often first increases, then drops off until the dragon largely stops eating and rests in its hide, sometimes only emerging to drink.
- *Shedding* (normal): The skin-shedding process makes dragons itchy/uncomfortable and is commonly associated with a temporary drop in appetite and increased lethargy in the days surrounding a shed.
- *Gravidity (egg-carrying females, even without a male present)* (normal): Female bearded dragons can produce infertile egg clutches without mating. Many gravid females eat voraciously during early follicle development, then go off food substantially in the 1-2 weeks before laying, often becoming more lethargic and digging/searching for a lay site.
- *Stress* (depends): New environment/rehoming, an enclosure that's too small, cohabitation with another dragon, or overhandling can trigger stress hormones that suppress appetite and cause lethargy.
- *Incorrect basking/ambient temperature* (concerning): As an ectotherm, a bearded dragon needs correct basking temperatures to produce stomach acid and digestive enzymes. If the enclosure runs too cold, appetite wanes, digestion slows or stalls, and food can sit undigested, which can also contribute to impaction.
- *Impaction / GI blockage* (concerning): A blockage in the digestive tract (from oversized feeder insects, loose substrate ingestion, or slowed digestion from cold temps) that can present with reduced appetite alongside reduced or absent stool, straining, or a firm/distended abdomen.
- *Illness/parasites/metabolic bone disease* (concerning): Infections, internal parasites, mouth/dental problems, or metabolic bone disease (from inadequate UVB/calcium) are all cited as genuine medical causes of appetite loss, usually accompanied by other symptoms like lethargy, abnormal stool, or visible deformity/weakness.
- *Picky/neophobic phases or diet boredom* (normal): Multiple care sources note dragons can simply go off a food item they're bored with or reject new foods; this is described as a normal, non-medical possibility to rule out before assuming illness.

**When to actually worry**
Guidance differs by source and by what exactly is being measured (baseline resilience vs. a trigger to call the vet), which is worth being explicit about:

- Babies/juveniles are the most time-sensitive: multiple sources agree you should not let a baby or juvenile go more than about 2-3 days without food given their rapid growth and higher metabolic needs, and NC State's teaching hospital lists "refuses food for 1-2+ days" (in adults) as a reason to seek care - implying an even shorter tolerance is appropriate for growing juveniles.
- Adults: NC State frames "refuses food for 1-2+ days" as a concern trigger specifically for adults, while a vet-reviewed (DVM) Hepper article states healthy adults CAN physiologically go 1-2 weeks without food since they're not growing and digest more slowly. These aren't strictly contradictory - one is a "call if concerned" threshold, the other is a statement of physical resilience - but they will read very differently to an owner, so present both rather than picking one.
- During confirmed brumation, not eating for the 1-4 month window is expected; the same Hepper brumation article (DVM-reviewed) says to consult a vet if brumation-like dormancy extends past roughly 3.5 months, or if a dragon loses more than about 10% of its starting body weight during that time - cited elsewhere as a reason to actively stop/interrupt suspected brumation and get an exam.
- Regardless of the specific day count, the accompanying signs matter more than a fixed number: seek vet care for lethargy, stopped or abnormal (watery, discolored, foul-smelling) stool/no defecation, a swollen or distended abdomen, visible straining, sunken or closed eyes, unexplained/progressive weight loss, hind-limb weakness, or a "black beard" stress display that doesn't resolve.
- After brumation ends, appetite return is expected within about 2-3 weeks; if it hasn't returned by then, or stress marks persist, a reptile vet visit is recommended.

**FunFact candidate**
A peer-reviewed dietary study of wild eastern bearded dragons (Pogona barbata, Australian Journal of Zoology/CSIRO Publishing) found a genuine ontogenetic diet shift: juveniles are active insectivorous predators (invertebrate prey reported making up 70-80% of juvenile diet), while mature dragons - especially large adult males - become predominantly herbivorous, living mostly on vegetation. The researchers noted this degree of sex- and age-based dietary divergence "has rarely been reported" in reptiles, and hypothesized it's tied to males relying on visual "sham" territorial displays rather than costly protein-fueled aggression once mature. This wild pattern is exactly why every captive care guide has you feed hatchlings mostly insects and gradually flip a pet dragon's plate toward mostly greens as it reaches adulthood - the ratio isn't an arbitrary husbandry rule, it echoes real feeding biology.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1. Exact feeding frequency numbers conflict between clinical/academic sources and enthusiast care-sheet sites. Veterinary teaching-hospital sources (NC State CVM, VCA) give broader ranges (e.g., "young dragons once or twice daily," "adults every 24-72 hours"), while commercial/enthusiast sites (reptile.guide, Dragon's Diet, Ovipost, beardiehusbandry) give much more specific numbers (e.g., "babies fed 5x/day," "adults get insects 2x/week") that are widely repeated online but are not sourced to a veterinarian or study. Treat the vet-affiliated ranges as the more authoritative floor/ceiling and the specific enthusiast numbers as commonly-practiced defaults within that range, not clinically established facts.

2. Diet-composition percentages differ meaningfully by source: NC State's teaching hospital gives an adult formula of 50% dark leafy greens / 20% chopped vegetables / 25% animal protein / up to 5% fruit. PetMD instead frames it by life stage as ~70% insect/20-30% veg for juveniles flipping to ~20-30% insect/70-80% veg for adults. VCA states a simpler general "50% plant material, 50% animal material" guideline without breaking out juvenile vs. adult separately. All sources agree on the direction (more insect when young, more plant when adult) but the specific numbers are not consistent enough to present as one settled figure - I've kept ranges/attributions distinct above rather than averaging them.

3. "How long can a bearded dragon safely go without eating" has a real tension between two vet-linked sources: NC State's hospital lists "refuses food for 1-2+ days" as an adult vet-visit trigger, while Hepper's DVM-reviewed article says healthy adults can physiologically fast 1-2 weeks. Both can be true (a short refusal can be a benign trigger to just monitor, while true fasting tolerance is longer) but a reader skimming for "the number" will get different answers depending which source they land on - flagged explicitly in whenToWorry rather than resolved into a single figure.

4. Brumation duration also varies across sources: "1-3 months" (DVM-reviewed Hepper), "1-4 months, occasionally up to 9 months" (other aggregated sources). I used the more conservative, vet-reviewed range as primary and noted the wider range exists.

### Pacman Frog

**Feeding schedule**
Life-stage split is real and consistently reported: juveniles/froglets are fed much more often than adults because they're growing fast and have higher metabolic demand.

- Juveniles: fed daily, per PetMD, Chewy, and Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center. Josh's Frogs (a specialty amphibian retailer) narrows juvenile prey items to "small prey items every 1-2 days" rather than strictly daily.
- Adults: sources disagree on exact frequency (see caveatsOrDisputes) — PetMD and Chewy say 1-2 times/week; Josh's Frogs also says 1-2 times/week; Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center (an exotics vet clinic) says 2-3 times/week; one secondary source (Bernard's Pest, lower-authority) claims a sex split of adult females 2-3x/week vs. adult males 1-2x/week.
- Amount per feeding: no source gives a fixed count of prey items — instead the consistent rule is to size prey no wider than the frog's head/the gap between its eyes, and to judge portion by body condition (frog should look "round" but not visibly bloated), because Pacman frogs are described by multiple sources (Josh's Frogs, Tree of Life vet clinic, Merck Veterinary Manual) as highly food-motivated animals that will keep eating past the point of being full.

**Diet composition**
Staple diet: gut-loaded (recently fed, nutrient-dense) crickets, Dubia roaches, and earthworms/nightcrawlers — cited consistently across PetMD, Chewy, Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center, and Josh's Frogs as the core rotation.

Occasional/supplemental items: mealworms and waxworms (flagged specifically as fatty, "occasional treat only" items, not staples), silkworms and hornworms (soft-bodied, considered lower impaction risk). For adults only, thawed (never live, never microwaved) frozen rodents — pinky mice up to adult mice, offered roughly every other week — appear in Chewy, PetMD, and Tree of Life's guidance, though the vet source specifically cautions to use pinky mice "sparingly due to obesity and liver complications." Juveniles should not be given rodents.

Gut-loading and dusting: feeder insects should be gut-loaded (fed a nutrient-dense commercial gut-load diet) for roughly 24-72 hours before being offered (Chewy). Calcium (with vitamin D3) dusting is recommended daily for juveniles and a few times per week for adults per Chewy; Tree of Life's vet guidance says dust with calcium at every other feeding; a general reptile multivitamin/mineral powder is added about once a week across multiple sources. The Merck Veterinary Manual (a core veterinary reference) flags cricket-only diets as a specific metabolic bone disease risk because most feeder insects, aside from earthworms, are naturally low in calcium relative to phosphorus — reinforcing why dusting and dietary variety matter, not just quantity.

Feeding method: always offer with feeding tongs, not fingers or bare hands — cited by PetMD, Chewy, and Josh's Frogs — both because Pacman frogs have a genuinely strong bite (they have both vomerine and maxillary teeth, per Hepper's vet-reviewed facts page) and to avoid the frog associating fingers with food.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe staples/treats: gut-loaded crickets, Dubia roaches, earthworms/nightcrawlers (staples); mealworms, waxworms, silkworms, and hornworms as occasional treats (mealworms/waxworms specifically flagged as fatty and not for regular feeding); for adults only, thawed frozen pinky/adult mice roughly every other week, fed sparingly.

Unsafe/dangerous: live rodents (risk of the rodent biting and wounding the frog, potentially causing life-threatening infection — PetMD/Chewy); microwaved thawed rodents (creates internal "hot spots" that can burn the frog's mouth); refrozen previously-thawed food; wild-caught insects in general (parasite and pesticide-residue risk — multiple sources); and specifically fireflies (Photinus genus), which contain lucibufagin toxins that are documented as lethally toxic to insectivorous reptiles and amphibians that eat them — this is corroborated by Cornell University entomologists' reporting on firefly toxicosis in captive animals, not just hobbyist sites. Loose/particulate substrates (sand, gravel, bark, calcium sand) aren't a "food" but are repeatedly flagged as an ingestion/impaction hazard because frogs strike blindly at prey and can swallow substrate along with it.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Temperature or humidity out of range* (depends): Cited across multiple sources as one of the most common causes of appetite loss. Target ranges given: daytime ~75-85°F (24-29°C), nighttime ~65-75°F (18-24°C), humidity roughly 50-80%.
- *Stress from a new home, handling, or enclosure change* (normal): Newly acquired frogs or frogs in a freshly changed enclosure commonly refuse food for a period while they adjust; over-handling is specifically named as a stressor.
- *Shedding* (normal): Pacman frogs, like other amphibians, periodically shed their skin (and typically eat the shed) and may show reduced interest in food during that cycle.
- *Aestivation / dormancy (a heat/drought response, not true winter hibernation)* (depends): In the wild, Pacman frogs burrow down and secrete a hardened mucus cocoon around themselves during hot/dry periods, surviving for months without food or water. In captivity this can be triggered any time of year by environmental drift, not just seasonally. Sources specifically warn that frogs under about 12 months old should not be allowed to aestivate, since young frogs can lose dangerous amounts of body mass and even die during it — keepers are advised to prevent it in juveniles by keeping temperature/light consistent.
- *Breeding-cycle changes* (normal): Breeding in this genus is tied to a deliberate cool, dry hibernation-like period (commonly cited as around 60 days) followed by warm, wet conditions to trigger spawning; breeding pairs are reported to eat and drink very little during that pre-breeding dormancy period. This is a deliberate husbandry practice for breeders, not something that happens to an ordinary pet on its own.
- *Picky/neophobic eating* (normal): Multiple sources describe Pacman frogs as capable of being fussy about unfamiliar food items and may refuse a new prey type they aren't used to.
- *Illness (parasites, bacterial/fungal infection, including red-leg syndrome)* (concerning): The Merck Veterinary Manual lists anorexia (loss of appetite) as a clinical sign alongside lethargy and weight loss in infectious amphibian disease, including Aeromonas-driven red-leg syndrome, which also presents with reddening of the belly/legs.
- *Gastrointestinal impaction* (concerning): Pacman frogs lunge blindly at prey and can accidentally ingest loose substrate (sand, gravel, bark, calcium sand) along with food, causing a physical blockage that stops normal digestion and can itself cause appetite loss and reduced defecation. Multiple sources recommend avoiding loose/particulate substrates for this reason.

**When to actually worry**
Sources agree Pacman frogs are unusually fasting-tolerant thanks to large fat reserves in their pear-shaped bodies, but the exact "safe" duration is not consistently pinned down and comes mostly from lower-authority hobbyist sites rather than veterinary sources, so treat the specific week/day counts as rough hobbyist consensus, not clinical fact (see caveatsOrDisputes). Commonly repeated figures: healthy adults can reportedly fast 2-4 weeks without concern, with some individuals tolerating up to about 6 weeks; juveniles are said to tolerate roughly up to about a week safely before it becomes worth investigating (multiple hobbyist sources, e.g. Berry Patch Farms, PAC-MANFROG.COM, thepetenthusiast.com).

What actually matters more than the calendar, per these same sources plus general vet guidance: body condition and behavior. Because Pacman frogs are sedentary, rapid or visible weight loss is flagged as a genuine warning sign rather than something to wait out — one source suggests weighing weekly and treating a roughly 10% weight loss over a couple of weeks as a signal to intervene/reassess. A frog that stays active, alert, hydrated, with normal-looking feces and stable weight is generally considered fine even while skipping meals; a frog that is lethargic, visibly losing weight/body mass, sunken-looking, not producing normal feces, or refusing food for a month or more (or showing any illness signs like red/inflamed skin, unusual discharge, or labored breathing) warrants a vet visit with a reptile/amphibian-experienced exotics vet. Young frogs (under about a year) that stop eating and appear to be going dormant/aestivating should not be left to "sleep it out," since sources specifically warn this can be fatal at that age.

**FunFact candidate**
Pacman frogs are true macrophages: a peer-reviewed field study of the related species Ceratophrys stolzmanni (published in PeerJ, archived on PMC) documented adults regularly eating prey nearly as large as themselves, with an average predator-to-prey size ratio around 1.24, including full-grown frogs and even snakes, and juveniles cannibalizing similarly sized or smaller froglets (22 documented instances, ratio ~1.26) — always caught head-first. The same study recorded a case of necrophagy: an adult male attempting to eat an already-dead, flattened frog carcass found on a road. It's the biological basis for the well-known captive-care rule that food should never be wider than the frog's own head — in the wild these frogs will genuinely try to eat things nearly their own size.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Adult feeding frequency is not settled across sources: PetMD, Chewy, and Josh's Frogs (a specialty amphibian retailer) all give 1-2 times per week for adults; Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center (an exotics veterinary clinic) gives 2-3 times per week; a lower-authority hobbyist/SEO source (Bernard's Pest) claims a sex-based split — adult females 2-3x/week, adult males 1-2x/week — that I could not corroborate elsewhere and would treat with caution. Present a range (roughly 1-3x/week for adults) rather than a single number, and note that portion/body-condition monitoring matters more than hitting an exact frequency, since multiple sources (including the Merck Veterinary Manual) stress these frogs will overeat if allowed and are prone to obesity/fatty liver disease.

"How long is safe to fast" numbers (2-4 weeks typical, up to 6 weeks for adults, ~1 week for juveniles) come from hobbyist/enthusiast sites (PAC-MANFROG.COM, Berry Patch Farms, thepetenthusiast.com) rather than veterinary or academic sources — they're consistent with each other and with the general "large fat reserves" biology, but I could not verify them against a veterinary reference, so they should be framed as commonly cited hobbyist guidance, not clinically established figures, and paired with the body-condition/behavior guidance which is better corroborated.

Calcium-dusting frequency also varies slightly by source: Chewy says daily for juveniles/a few times a week for adults, while Tree of Life's vet guidance says dust at every other feeding — these aren't strictly contradictory (both land in a similar "most feedings" range) but the exact cadence differs by source.

### Blue Tongue Skink

**Feeding schedule**
Sources broadly agree on the shape of the schedule but disagree meaningfully on adult frequency (see caveats). Babies (roughly hatching to 3 to 5 months): feed daily, some sources specify 2 to 3 times per day with as much as the baby will eat in one sitting, plus one designated fasting day per week (Dubia Roaches, Zen Habitats, The Bio Dude, Reptile.Guide). Juveniles (roughly 3 to 10 months, exact window varies by source): 3 times weekly (Dubia Roaches, Zen Habitats, The Bio Dude) to every other day (Reptile.Guide) i.e. 3 to 4 times per week, similar portion sizes to baby feedings. Subadults/Adults (roughly 8 to 12+ months): this is where sources genuinely split. LafeberVet (veterinary-authored) says feed adults every 1 to 2 days; an exotics veterinary clinic (Bird & Exotics Vet, Long Island) says every 2 to 3 days; but a cluster of pet-industry care sheets (Dubia Roaches, Zen Habitats, The Bio Dude, and ReptiFiles per its own site's search-indexed text) say adults only need feeding once to twice per WEEK, with Reptile.Guide splitting the difference at about 3 times per week. Portion guidance: one meal roughly the size of the skink's own skull (Dubia Roaches/Zen Habitats), or about 1 to 2 tablespoons per feeding for adults (Zen Habitats).

**Diet composition**
Blue tongue skinks are true omnivores. Diet composition shifts with age across essentially every source, though the exact cutoff age and percentages vary: young skinks (under 12 months per most sources, under 24 months per The Bio Dude) should get a protein-heavy diet, roughly 70-80% animal matter to 20-30% plant matter; mature adults flip toward plant-heavy, roughly 40-60% animal protein and 45-60% leafy greens/vegetables, with about 5-10% fruit as treats (Zen Habitats, Bird & Exotics Vet gives ~40% protein/50% greens/10% fruit for adults). Protein sources named across sources: gut-loaded crickets, dubia roaches, black soldier fly larvae, grasshoppers/locusts, silkworms, hornworms, butterworms, superworms, mealworms, earthworms, captive-bred snails, occasional pinky/fuzzy mice or hatchling chicks, cooked chicken/turkey or chicken organ meats (hearts, gizzards), hard-boiled or cooked eggs, and high-quality canned/raw dog or cat food (no fish-based formulas). Feeder insects should be gut-loaded 24-48 hours before feeding and sized no larger than the space between the skink's eyes. Vegetables/greens named: collard greens, dandelion greens, mustard greens, kale, escarole, endive, arugula, turnip greens, plus peas, green beans, corn, squash, carrots/carrot greens, sweet potato, cucumber, zucchini, bell peppers, parsley, hibiscus flowers/blossoms. Fruit treats (limited): mango, papaya, berries, melon, banana, pear, peach/nectarine, cherries (pitted). Supplementation: calcium powder (calcium carbonate or gluconate, low/no phosphorus, minimum Ca:P ratio of 2:1) dusted on food; frequency varies by source from every feeding (if using a D3-inclusive product) to 2-3 times weekly to once weekly for non-breeding adults (LafeberVet says once weekly for adults); a separate reptile multivitamin is used more sparingly, once to twice weekly on non-calcium days per Zen Habitats, or as infrequently as once to twice a month per Bird & Exotics Vet and The Bio Dude.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat items named across sources: strawberries and other berries, mango, papaya, melon (cantaloupe/honeydew), banana, pear, peach/nectarine, pitted cherries, hibiscus flowers/blossoms, cooked egg, snails, and small amounts of high-quality canned cat or dog food. Unsafe/toxic items named specifically for this species: avocado (contains persin, cited as toxic to reptiles), onion and garlic (can damage red blood cells), rhubarb (very high oxalic acid), fireflies/lightning bugs (a well-documented lucibufagin toxin risk for lizards generally, called out specifically for blue tongues by Zen Habitats), monarch butterflies (toxic from milkweed-derived cardenolides), citrus fruits and citrus seeds, eggplant and tomato leaves/greens (nightshade foliage), wild-caught insects (pesticide residue and parasite risk), centipedes and spiders, chocolate/caffeine, and excessively fatty or heavily processed/high-sodium human foods. A softer caution rather than an outright ban: spinach, beet greens, and Swiss chard are high in oxalates that bind calcium and are generally recommended to be limited or avoided as a regular staple rather than used as a everyday green (one source lists them under "never feed," most others frame it as "feed sparingly").

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation (winter dormancy)* (normal): A natural seasonal slowdown, typically occurring roughly November through March (varies with climate/setup), lasting about 3 to 4 months in most descriptions (one source cites up to 3 to 6 months as an outer bound). Brumating skinks hide, sleep more, and largely stop eating and drinking; if temperatures are kept appropriately low, they generally lose little to no weight during this period.
- *Shedding* (normal): Like many reptiles, blue tongue skinks commonly go off food or become picky eaters shortly before, during, and briefly after a shed. Appetite typically returns once the shed is complete.
- *New environment / acclimation stress* (normal): Newly acquired skinks, especially wild-caught ones, may hide and refuse food while settling in. Captive-bred skinks are generally reported to acclimate within about 2 weeks; wild-caught individuals can take substantially longer (one source says up to 2 months).
- *Breeding/mating season* (normal): Several pet-care sites (not the veterinary sources consulted) describe reduced appetite during breeding season, roughly April through September depending on climate, tied to sexual maturity (cited as ~12-16 months in males, ~24-30 months in females). This claim appears consistently across general care-blog sources but was not independently corroborated by the veterinary sources in this research, so treat it as plausible but less rigorously verified.
- *Dietary boredom / pickiness* (depends): A repetitive diet (same 2-3 foods on rotation) is frequently cited as a cause of selective food refusal; varying protein sources, greens, and offering mixed-in favorites (e.g. blending vegetables into canned cat food) is the commonly recommended fix.
- *Inadequate basking temperature or UVB* (concerning): Blue tongue skinks need a warm basking spot to digest food; if the enclosure runs too cool or UVB lighting is insufficient, appetite and digestion both suffer. This is a husbandry problem to correct, not something to wait out.
- *Illness, parasites, or infection (respiratory infection, internal parasites, mouth rot, metabolic bone disease)* (concerning): Loss of appetite is described as an early, common first symptom of most illnesses in this species. Mouth rot presents with visible redness/crusting/discharge around the mouth and makes eating painful; parasitic load is typically confirmed via a vet fecal exam.
- *Impaction* (concerning): Ingested loose substrate or oversized feeder items can cause a blocked gut, showing as bloating, constipation, and poor appetite together; described as sometimes requiring imaging and even surgery.
- *Dehydration* (concerning): Signs cited include wrinkled/loose-looking skin and thick or stringy saliva; mild cases are addressed with fresh water and water-rich vegetables, severe cases need veterinary fluid support.

**When to actually worry**
There is a real gradient here rather than one universal number. Short-term appetite dips (days to roughly 1-3 weeks) in an otherwise active, normal-weight adult are widely described as unremarkable, especially around a shed, a diet change, or a new enclosure. During genuine seasonal brumation, going without food for up to about 3 months is described as expected and not itself a red flag, provided temperatures are managed correctly and the skink isn't losing significant weight. Outside of brumation season, multiple sources converge on the same general trigger: if a skink refuses food for more than a few weeks with no clear seasonal/shedding explanation, or shows accompanying weight loss, lethargy, or other symptoms, that's the point to involve a vet rather than keep waiting. On weight specifically, sources converge in the 7-10% body-weight-loss range as the threshold that warrants urgent veterinary attention (one source states this as "more than 10%," others as "7-10%"), and the standard advice is to weigh the skink weekly on a gram-scale accurate to 0.1g and keep a log so a slow decline isn't missed. Baby and juvenile skinks are repeatedly flagged as a different, higher-risk case: because they carry much less body fat than adults, they cannot safely fast anywhere near as long, and prolonged refusal in a baby should be taken seriously much sooner than in an adult.

**FunFact candidate**
In the wild, larger-bodied blue tongue skinks (genus Tiliqua) don't just eat snails, insects, carrion, and plant matter, they also dig up and eat underground truffle-like (hypogeous) fungi, apparently locating them by smell. Because these fungi form symbiotic relationships with the roots of surrounding trees and shrubs, the skinks that eat them and pass viable spores in their droppings are functioning as fungal spore dispersers, an ecological role usually associated with mammals, not lizards. This mycophagy is documented in peer-reviewed research (Australian Zoologist journal; a global review in the journal Mycosphere) as a genuine, if under-appreciated, part of the species' natural feeding ecology.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
The most significant disagreement found: how often to feed ADULT blue tongue skinks. Veterinary sources (LafeberVet's Northern Blue-Tongued Skink info sheet, and Bird & Exotics Veterinary Clinic of Long Island) recommend feeding adults every 1-2 to every 2-3 days, essentially near-daily to every-other-day. Multiple widely-used pet-industry care sheets (Dubia Roaches, Zen Habitats, The Bio Dude, and ReptiFiles' own published figures) instead say adult skinks (8+ months) only need to be fed once to twice per WEEK, with Reptile.Guide landing in between at about 3 times per week. That is roughly a 3-to-7x difference in feeding frequency depending which source you follow, not a minor rounding gap, so this article should present it as a real range/disagreement rather than pick one number as settled. Secondary, smaller disagreements: (1) the age at which to start reducing the protein share of the diet is given as 12 months by most sources but as 24 months by The Bio Dude; (2) the safe vet-concern weight-loss threshold is given as "7-10%" by some sources and "10%" by others, a narrow range rather than a real conflict; (3) the claim that breeding season itself suppresses appetite appears only in general pet-care blogs, not in the veterinary sources consulted, so it's included as plausible but not vet-verified; (4) exact brumation duration ranges from "3-4 months" to looser "up to 3-6 months" language depending on source. Note also that ReptiFiles' own pages (reptifiles.com) actively blocked direct fetching in this research (403 errors) and a specialist hobbyist forum, bluetongueskinks.org, had an expired TLS certificate blocking direct fetch; both were still incorporated via their content as it appeared in search-engine result snippets, which is a slightly weaker form of sourcing than a direct page read and is flagged here for transparency.

### Uromastyx

**Feeding schedule**
Life-stage split is real and consistently reported, though the exact adult frequency varies by source. Babies/juveniles (roughly hatchling through 1-2 years, sources differ on the cutoff) should be fed fresh vegetables/greens daily, offering as much as they'll clean up in one sitting. Adults are fed less often, but sources disagree on exactly how much less: Dubia.com's care sheet and a ReptiFiles-sourced summary say 4-5x/week; a lizards101 roundup says adults 3+ years can drop to 5-7 days/week; lizards101's own detailed diet guide says "2-4 times weekly is typically sufficient" for healthy adults and explicitly warns overfeeding is a real risk ("there is a common misconception that you cannot overfeed Uromastyx"); Reptile Craze's synthesis puts healthy adults at roughly every-other-day. Multiple sources agree on timing regardless of frequency: put food down about 1-2 hours after the basking light/heat comes on (so the animal is warm enough to actually digest what it eats) and leave it available for daytime grazing; one source recommends pulling the dish 2-3 hours before lights-out.

**Diet composition**
Uromastyx are treated as strict-to-near-strict herbivores in captivity. The bulk of the diet is dark leafy greens and edible flowers: collard greens, dandelion greens and flowers, mustard and turnip greens, endive, escarole, chicory, arugula, watercress, bok choy (moderate — contains goitrogens), clover, alfalfa (plant, not sprouts), hibiscus leaves/flowers, and spring mix, chopped or shredded into bite-sized pieces (whole leaves are a choking/impaction risk, especially for young animals). Sources disagree on the exact proportion: one care sheet gives roughly 60% dark leafy greens / 30% other vegetables / 10% seeds, while another gives roughly 90% greens-and-flowers / 5% hard vegetables / 5% dry foods — a real, unresolved gap in how much seed/dry food should make up the diet. "Other vegetables" fed a few times weekly include cactus pad/prickly pear (notably high calcium), butternut and spaghetti squash, zucchini and other summer squash, bell peppers, shredded carrots, green beans, peas, and okra. Seeds and legumes (soaked/sprouted lentils, white millet, safflower, small amounts of sesame) are a recognized protein/fat source but sources disagree sharply on frequency — from "no more than 1x/week to avoid obesity" to "seeds 4-5x weekly in small amounts" — so treat seeds as a limited, not daily, component. Fruit (papaya, fig, apple, prickly pear, berries, cherries, apricot) is a rare treat; frequency guidance ranges from "one piece a month" to "weekly, ~5-10% of diet" — another real disagreement, so err toward less. Calcium powder (ideally without added phosphorus) is dusted onto greens, with most sources recommending it more often for hatchlings/juveniles and tapering with age, aiming for at least a 2:1 calcium-to-phosphorus ratio; multivitamin powder is used more sparingly (roughly weekly to every 1-2 weeks). Several sources note that with a genuinely varied, calcium-rich green/flower diet plus real UVB, dusting can become minimal — and that dietary vitamin D2 is poorly used by reptiles, so UVB lighting (not oral D3) is the preferred vitamin D source. Uromastyx get most of their water from food and often don't drink from a bowl.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Reasonable safe treats (fed sparingly): papaya, fig, apple, prickly pear/cactus pad, berries, cherries, apricot, sprouted/soaked lentils, dandelion flowers, hibiscus flowers, and bee pollen (used by some keepers as an appetite stimulant, limited to about once a week). Foods to avoid or exclude entirely, per multiple sources: spinach (high oxalates, specifically linked to bladder stones in this species); avocado (toxic); iceberg lettuce and celery (essentially empty nutrition, "pale greens"); citrus fruit and tomatoes (too acidic, can cause diarrhea); corn, potatoes, and bananas (high phosphorus, work against the calcium:phosphorus ratio the animal needs); sunflower seeds (too high fat); frozen greens fed long-term (linked to thiamine/B1 deficiency causing tremors that can mimic metabolic bone disease); grapes/raisins per one source; and mushrooms, leeks, chives, and rhubarb per another. High-oxalate items (beets, kiwi, parsley, pomegranate, star fruit, chard) and goitrogenic items (broccoli, cabbage, kale, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, bok choy, rutabaga, soybeans) are described as fine only in small/occasional amounts, not as staples. Any wild-foraged plant should come from an area confirmed pesticide-free for at least two years and be positively identified — several "false dandelion" lookalikes exist.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation (seasonal winter dormancy)* (normal): Triggered by cooler temperatures and shorter daylight; metabolism and activity slow and appetite drops or stops. Most sources cite roughly 1-2 months of reduced/no eating as typical (one source frames this as a fall/winter, e.g. Nov-Feb, pattern); one source claims some individuals/species can go up to six months without food or water, which is an outlier figure not corroborated elsewhere in this research and should be treated with caution.
- *Shedding* (normal): Young, fast-growing Uromastyx can shed every month or two; appetite commonly dips around a shed and this is considered a normal, temporary reaction.
- *Breeding-cycle changes* (depends): After mating, females often bask, move, and eat less for a period; males can become territorial/aggressive toward other males during breeding season, which affects feeding behavior. Considered a normal seasonal pattern but worth monitoring since it can look like something is wrong.
- *New-environment stress / acclimation* (normal): Newly acquired Uromastyx commonly refuse food for roughly one to two weeks while settling in; considered a normal adjustment period rather than illness.
- *General husbandry stress* (depends): Inadequate hides, handling too soon or too often, loud noise, or an unsuitable enclosure setup can suppress appetite. Usually resolves once the stressor is corrected, but persistent stress-driven refusal is worth addressing promptly.
- *Insufficient basking/enclosure temperature* (concerning): Uromastyx digest via hindgut fermentation that only works well at very high body temperatures (sources cite basking surface targets from roughly 120-130°F up to 131-140°F depending on the care sheet). Too-cool temps blunt appetite and, even when the animal does eat, can leave food poorly digested, causing weight loss despite eating.
- *Illness or pathology* (concerning): Includes mouth rot (infectious stomatitis), respiratory infection, internal parasites (more common in wild-caught animals — annual fecal checks are recommended), metabolic bone disease, gout, and liver/kidney disease. These are genuine medical causes of appetite loss.
- *Gastrointestinal impaction* (concerning): Usually from ingesting loose substrate or unchewed fibrous material; signs include not defecating, straining, or passing thin/stringy stool alongside reduced appetite.
- *Picky/monotonous-diet phases* (normal): Repeatedly offering the same one or two vegetables can lead to boredom-driven refusal; rotating greens and vegetables often resolves it.

**When to actually worry**
Short refusals are usually not an emergency: acclimation-related fasting of about one to two weeks in a newly acquired animal, brief dips during shedding, or a seasonal slowdown of roughly one to two months during brumation are all commonly described as normal — provided the animal still looks alert, hydrated, and isn't losing significant weight. The recurring concrete threshold across sources is: if appetite loss persists beyond about a week outside of an obvious brumation/shedding/acclimation context, it's time for a reptile-experienced vet visit. For brumating animals specifically, the distinguishing signal is weight, not just fasting: losing more than roughly 5-10% of body weight while not eating is described as inconsistent with ordinary brumation and warrants veterinary evaluation, which is why keepers are advised to weigh a brumating or fasting Uromastyx weekly. Regardless of how long it's been, certain accompanying signs should prompt a prompt vet visit rather than a wait-and-see approach: lethargy, sunken eyes or tented/wrinkled skin (dehydration), inability to defecate or passing thin/stringy stool (possible impaction), visible swelling, mouth discharge or lesions (possible mouth rot), labored breathing, or any sign of injury. Annual fecal parasite screening is also recommended, especially for wild-caught or newly acquired animals, since parasites are a real driver of chronic appetite loss.

**FunFact candidate**
Uromastyx don't just need heat to feel comfortable — they need it to physically digest their food. Their gut relies on hindgut fermentation (the same basic strategy horses use) to break down tough, fibrous desert plants, but that microbial fermentation only functions properly at very high body temperatures, which is why care sheets call for basking surface temperatures roughly in the 120-140°F range. If the enclosure runs too cool, a Uromastyx can keep eating normally and still lose weight, because the food is sitting in the gut without being properly broken down — appetite and digestion are two separate things for this species.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several genuine, unresolved disagreements turned up in this research and should not be flattened into a single number: (1) Adult feeding frequency ranges from "4-5x/week" to "5-7 days/week" to "2-4x/week is typically sufficient" to "every other day" across otherwise reputable care sources — there is no consensus figure. (2) Diet-percentage breakdowns differ substantially between sources: 60% dark leafy greens/30% other vegetables/10% seeds versus roughly 90% greens-and-flowers/5% hard vegetables/5% dry foods — a real gap in how much seed/dry food is considered appropriate. (3) Insects are the biggest point of dispute: some commercial/traditional care sheets (including a feeder-insect vendor's own care sheet) recommend small amounts of mealworms or superworms weekly for juveniles and roughly biweekly to monthly for adults as a protein source. In contrast, the experienced-keeper community (e.g., UroWiki) argues Uromastyx should be treated as strict herbivores and that insects should be minimized or eliminated, citing wild-diet field studies (droppings analyses of Uromastyx ornata and U. aegyptia microlepis showing insects at roughly 1-6% of the diet and likely consumed by accident, e.g., ants riding on flowers) and concern that a Uromastyx's digestive system is not well suited to sustained high protein intake. This is presented here as an open disagreement, not a settled recommendation either way. (4) Fruit frequency guidance ranges from "one piece a month" to "weekly, ~5-10% of diet." (5) Basking/digestion temperature targets differ by source: roughly 120-130°F in some care sheets and 131-140°F in another. (6) One source's claim that brumation can extend "up to six months without food or water" for some individuals/species stands well outside the 1-2 month figure that recurs across most other sources and could not be independently corroborated here — treat it as an outlier rather than typical. (7) The "5-10% body-weight-loss" threshold for distinguishing illness from normal brumation is commonly repeated in aggregated keeper/vet Q&A material, but this research pass could not trace it to a single named primary veterinary study specific to Uromastyx, so it should be treated as widely-repeated general guidance rather than a hard clinical figure.

### Gargoyle Gecko

**Feeding schedule**
Sources broadly split feeding into a powdered "Crested Gecko Diet" (CGD) component and a live-insect component, offered on different schedules — and they don't fully agree on the numbers.

CGD/prepared diet: The Bio Dude and Dubia.com's care sheets (near-identical wording, likely a shared original source) both give: hatchlings/juveniles (0-12 months) get fresh CGD daily; adults (over 12 months) get it every 2-3 days. Reptile Supply's guide instead breaks life stages out as hatchling (0-12 weeks, fed 2-3x daily), juvenile (12-18 months) and subadult (12-24 months) (both once daily), and adult (24+ months, every other day) — a meaningfully different set of age cutoffs and frequencies, not just a rewording.

Live insects: The Bio Dude/Dubia say insects 1-2x/week for juveniles, 1x/week for adults. Reptile Supply says 2-3x weekly as "enrichment." Gecko Time's older overview says food generally "at least 3 times per week" for adults, daily for juveniles. ReptiFiles' guide is the outlier: it states animal prey "should not be offered more than twice per month," specifically citing obesity risk in gargoyles (a much lower frequency than every other source found — flagged below rather than averaged).

**Diet composition**
Staple is a commercial powdered "Crested Gecko Diet" (CGD)/meal-replacement mix — brands repeatedly named: Pangea, Repashy (Crested Gecko Diet Classic and the higher-protein Repashy Superfoods Grubs N Fruit), Zoo Med Complete Gecko Diet, Clark's/Black Panther Zoological, Gecko Pro, Lugarti, Arcadia, Exo-Terra Day Gecko Food. Mixed with water to a "ketchup/smoothie" consistency, commonly cited as roughly 2-3 parts water to 1 part powder, offered in a small cup or on a feeding ledge (gargoyles are arboreal climbers). Rotating 3+ brands/flavors is recommended for variety and to avoid pickiness.

Because gargoyle geckos are unusually carnivorous for New Caledonian geckos (see fact below), live insects are described as a required supplement, not optional enrichment: gutloaded dubia roaches, discoid roaches, red runner roaches, crickets, black soldier fly larvae (BSFL), silkworms, and appropriately-sized superworms as staples; hornworms, waxworms, and butterworms as occasional treats (fatty/high-hydration); mealworms are flagged by one source as harder to digest due to chitin content. Insects should be gutloaded 24-48 hours before feeding and dusted with a calcium supplement (D3-free products like Repashy Supercal NoD or Arcadia CalciumPro Mg for most feedings; a D3-inclusive supplement periodically). Occasional pinky mice (roughly once or twice a month per Reptile Supply/Gecko Time) are mentioned as an enrichment treat some keepers offer, with explicit warnings that overuse causes obesity.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe occasional treats: calcium-rich, low-phosphorus fruit purees in moderation (as part of or alongside CGD); waxworms, butterworms, hornworms, and (sparingly) pinky mice as enrichment/protein treats.

Foods to avoid, per multiple care-sheet sources: avocado (contains persin, described across sources as toxic/poisonous to geckos); citrus fruits (too acidic, high vitamin C linked to diarrhea/dehydration); starfruit and rhubarb (high oxalate content — rhubarb specifically called potentially lethal); garlic, onion, and eggplant; dairy (geckos are lactose intolerant); wild-caught insects (pesticide/parasite contamination risk); baby food and processed/sugary human food (preservatives, wrong nutrient profile).

**Why they stop eating**
- *Shedding* (normal): Many geckos skip a meal or two in the day(s) leading up to a shed; appetite normally returns once the shed is complete.
- *Breeding season* (normal): Males commonly go off food at the start of breeding season; females may stop eating while gravid/ovulating or before egg-laying due to physical discomfort and hormonal changes, sometimes for a week or more.
- *Stress from environment or handling* (depends): New enclosure setup, rehoming/shipping, substrate changes, excessive handling, or being bullied by a tankmate (juveniles housed together can be cannibalistic toward each other's tails) can suppress appetite; geckos may "hold out" for weeks while adjusting.
- *Enclosure too cool* (concerning): Gargoyle geckos need adequately warm temperatures to digest food properly; if the warm side of the enclosure runs too cool, digestion slows and appetite drops as a husbandry-driven symptom.
- *Mild winter slowdown* (normal): Unlike species that undergo true brumation with a deep temperature drop, gargoyle geckos (like their crested gecko relatives) show a milder seasonal winter slowdown — reduced appetite, less climbing/exploring, more hiding, and reduced breeding interest — typically lasting a few weeks and not requiring deliberate cooling.
- *Illness (impaction, parasites, infection, metabolic bone disease)* (concerning): Reduced eating accompanied by other symptoms — visible lesions, a swollen belly, rubbery/soft limbs, respiratory issues, mouth sores, or eye film/swelling — points to an underlying health problem rather than a normal appetite dip.

**When to actually worry**
Figures found (mostly discussing crested/gargoyle geckos together as close relatives with similar physiology): a healthy, well-hydrated adult can reportedly go up to 2-3 weeks without food; juveniles are more vulnerable and shouldn't go past about 4-5 days without eating given their growth needs; sick or already-underweight geckos should not go more than a few days without food. Water is described as more time-critical than food — sources caution against letting a gecko go more than 2-3 days without water access regardless of feeding status.

Vet consultation is recommended if the gecko hasn't eaten in about two weeks and is also losing weight, or if appetite loss persists beyond roughly a week alongside other red flags: rapid or visible weight loss (most easily seen as thinning at the tail, which gargoyle/crested geckos use as a fat-storage organ), lethargy or weakness, visible lesions, mouth sores, eye film or swelling, labored breathing, or rubbery/soft limbs (a metabolic bone disease sign). Regular weighing is recommended as the most objective way to catch a real problem early, since a small amount of weight fluctuation (a few grams, depending on the gecko's size) is not itself alarming.

**FunFact candidate**
Gargoyle geckos belong to the same New Caledonian gecko group as the largely fruit-and-nectar-eating crested gecko, but they're notably more carnivorous: multiple care guides and Gecko Time's species overview note that wild gargoyle geckos prey on other lizards — including, reportedly, young crested geckos — and even young birds, alongside insects, sap, and nectar. That's the biological reason regular insect feeding isn't optional for pet gargoyles the way it can be for cresties. A related quirk: juvenile gargoyle geckos housed together can nip and eat each other's tails, which is one reason breeders keep juvenile group sizes small.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Insect/prey feeding frequency is genuinely disputed across sources, not just worded differently. The Bio Dude and Dubia.com recommend live insects 1-2x/week (juveniles) and 1x/week (adults); Reptile Supply says 2-3x weekly; Gecko Time's overview says food generally at least 3x/week for adults. ReptiFiles, by contrast, states animal prey should be offered no more than twice a month, explicitly warning about obesity — a substantially lower frequency than every other source found here. I have not silently averaged these; treat the weekly-ish figures as the majority view and the twice-monthly figure as a notable minority/more conservative position.

Life-stage age brackets and CGD frequency also differ: The Bio Dude/Dubia use a single 12-month juvenile/adult cutoff (daily CGD under 12 months, every 2-3 days after). Reptile Supply instead uses four stages (hatchling 0-12 weeks fed 2-3x/day; juvenile 12-18 months and subadult 12-24 months each fed once daily; adult 24+ months fed every other day) — this is a materially different schedule, not just different phrasing of the same one.

Avocado toxicity is repeated verbatim-ish across several consumer pet-care blogs (including a claim that a high enough dose "can lead to death within 24 hours"), but I could not trace this specific figure to a veterinary toxicology source — it appears to be pet-blog folklore that gets copied between sites rather than a cited clinical finding. Treat the general "avoid avocado" guidance as solid consensus, but don't present the 24-hour death claim as a hard verified fact.

### Mourning Gecko

**Feeding schedule**
Sources broadly split feeding into a powdered "crested gecko diet" (CGD/MRP) component and a live-insect component, and they disagree on exact frequency for each, so treat the following as a range rather than one fixed number.

Hatchlings (under about 2 months): the most detailed life-stage breakdown, from New England Herpetoculture (NEHERP), calls for a dime-sized dab of powdered gecko diet offered every other day, plus Drosophila melanogaster fruit flies dusted with calcium about once a week, with misting 1-2 times daily for hydration and shed support.

Juveniles (2 months to sexual maturity, roughly 9-10 months): NEHERP scales portions up (a nickel-sized dab, or a 1.5oz deli cup about 1/4 full) and increases feeder-fly quantity as the gecko grows, moving to larger D. hydei flies around 2 inches in length; misting increases to twice daily. Multiple general care sites likewise say juveniles and gravid females need feeding stepped up versus adults, with calcium-dusted insects offered more often (near-daily) and powdered diet offered daily.

Adults: here the sources genuinely diverge. Dubia.com, Reptile Supply, and Pangea Reptile's own retail care page describe keeping powdered CGD essentially always available, replaced every 24-48 hours, with calcium-dusted insects added just 1-2x/week. The Bio Dude and ReptileDirect instead recommend offering both CGD and insects 2-3x/week (ReptileDirect specifies CGD 3-4x/week). Pangea Reptile's own blog care sheet gives a different discrete rotation: Pangea Fruit Mix 2 days/week, dusted gut-loaded insects 3 days/week, and 2 fasting days with no food at all. CozyLizard simply says "every other day." NEHERP's approach is closer to constant staple access (MRP kept refrigerated and refreshed roughly weekly) with D. hydei flies as the regular live feeder.

Gravid/breeding females: several sources (Reptile Cymru, general breeder guidance) note mourning geckos lay eggs continuously (roughly every 4-6 weeks, all year, even without a mate, since the species is parthenogenetic), so feeding — especially calcium-dusted insects and powdered diet — should be offered more frequently/consistently during active egg production than for a resting adult.

**Diet composition**
Staple: a powdered, commercially formulated "crested gecko diet" or "meal replacement powder" (MRP/CGD) mixed with water into a ketchup/smoothie-like paste and offered in a small cup — sources recommend mounting the cup on a wall or vertical surface rather than the enclosure floor, since mourning geckos are arboreal. Named brands recurring across care sheets: Repashy (Gecko Diet, Grubs & Fruit, Superfoods), Pangea (Fruit Mix, including flavors like Banana & Apricot), Zoo Med, Lugarti, Black Panther Zoological, Leapin' Leachie, and Clark's. Mix ratio cited by one source is roughly 2-3 parts water to 1 part powder; NEHERP notes the prepared mix can be refrigerated and used for about a week, refreshing the offered portion regularly, while other sources say food left in the cage should be discarded/replaced every 24-48 hours to avoid mold and bacterial growth.

Live insects (for enrichment, protein, and hunting behavior, not just nutrition): pinhead crickets scaling up to roughly 1/8-3/8 inch crickets sized to the gecko; Drosophila melanogaster fruit flies for hatchlings/small geckos and D. hydei for larger juveniles/adults; newborn dubia and discoid roach nymphs; red runner roach nymphs; small mealworms and extra-small superworms in moderation; bean beetles and rice flour beetles; occasionally small "Phoenix worms" (black soldier fly larvae), which NEHERP notes should NOT be dusted with additional supplement (unlike other feeders); hatchling snails are mentioned by a couple of sources as an occasional feeder too. General rule from several sources: prey should be no larger than the width of the gecko's head, to avoid impaction.

Supplementation: insects should be gut-loaded before offering, then lightly dusted with a calcium (with or without vitamin D3, depending on whether UVB lighting is provided) at most feedings — Repashy Supercal NoD and Arcadia CalciumPro Mg/MinerAll Outdoor were named specifically. One source recommends rotating in a multivitamin powder for roughly one weekly insect feeding instead of straight calcium. Because mourning geckos (all female, reproducing by parthenogenesis) lay eggs on an ongoing basis, calcium supply is described as a continuous rather than seasonal need.

Wild/natural diet (context, not a captive feeding recommendation): small arthropods (moths, flies, ants, beetles, spiders), isopods/pill bugs, nectar, pollen, ripe fruit and plant sap. A study of an invasive urban population in Belém, Brazil documented these geckos opportunistically eating human food scraps — ripe papaya and banana, fruit juice, sugar, beans, eggshells, even beer — illustrating extreme dietary flexibility in the wild, which is part of why the species has colonized so many regions, but this is not advice for what to feed a pet.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe/occasional treats mentioned across sources: small amounts of soft ripe fruit such as mango, melon, and strawberry; waxworms and small superworms as occasional fatty treats — used sparingly, since (by analogy to the closely related leopard/crested gecko literature that keepers explicitly extend to mourning geckos) overuse of fatty feeders like waxworms can lead to picky/selective feeding where the gecko refuses other foods.

Foods to avoid, specifically flagged for mourning geckos by ReptileDirect's care sheet: wild-caught insects (risk of pesticide exposure and parasites) and citrus fruit. General portion-size caution repeated across multiple care sheets: don't offer prey larger than the width of the gecko's head, to avoid impaction. NEHERP separately warns against baby food and unsupplemented "feeder-only" diets as inadequate long-term nutrition. One lower-confidence, not mourning-gecko-specific source cautions against honey as a stool-loosener/diarrhea risk — flagged here as general gecko-keeper advice rather than something confirmed in a mourning-gecko-specific source.

**Why they stop eating**
- *New-enclosure/acquisition stress* (normal): The single most commonly cited reason a mourning gecko stops eating is being freshly brought home; multiple keeper sources describe giving a newly acquired gecko several days to about a week of quiet, undisturbed time before it resumes normal feeding.
- *Shedding* (normal): Geckos commonly show a duller/grayer color, reduced activity, and reduced appetite in the day or so leading up to a shed. As long as enclosure humidity is adequate (roughly 60-80% cited for the species), the shed and the appetite dip should resolve on their own.
- *Gravidity / continuous egg production* (normal): Because mourning geckos are all-female and parthenogenetic, an individual pet female lays eggs on her own on an ongoing cycle (roughly every 4-6 weeks per some sources) without needing a mate. Egg development pulls significant energy and can cause a partial appetite dip, along with a visibly swollen abdomen and some lethargy.
- *Egg-binding (dystocia)* (concerning): If a gravid female's appetite loss is total (not just reduced) and paired with a swollen abdomen, lethargy, and straining to lay, this points to egg-binding, often linked to calcium deficiency. This is described as needing prompt veterinary attention rather than home management.
- *Overcrowding / social competition* (concerning): In a colony setup, more dominant geckos can out-compete or bully subordinate/smaller tankmates away from food, suppressing their appetite and, in juveniles, contributing to malnutrition or metabolic bone disease if it continues unaddressed.
- *Prey mismatched to gecko size* (depends): Forum/keeper reports note that pinhead-sized insects can stop triggering a hunting response once a gecko is around a year old and larger — the fix is simply upsizing to 2nd-instar crickets or similar, not a health issue.
- *Low temperature / cold housing conditions* (concerning): Insufficient warmth slows digestion and suppresses appetite. Mourning geckos are a tropical species and, unlike temperate species such as leopard geckos, are not typically deliberately cooled/brumated in home care — so an appetite drop tied to a cold room or seasonal draft is best treated as a husbandry problem to correct (check the enclosure's temperature range) rather than a natural dormancy phase to simply wait out.
- *Long-term inadequate diet history* (concerning): NEHERP's care sheet specifically warns that geckos previously raised on inappropriate food (baby food, or unsupplemented insects as the sole diet) can be harder to wean onto a proper regimen and may show poor feeding response as a result.
- *Illness or parasites* (concerning): General appetite loss combined with weight loss is flagged across sources as a sign of possible parasitic or other underlying disease requiring diagnosis rather than a wait-and-see approach.

**When to actually worry**
A short dip in eating — a few days after coming home, or a day or two around a shed — is considered normal and not something to intervene on beyond checking husbandry basics (humidity, hides, quiet). The recurring threshold across several keeper-oriented care sources is that appetite loss stretching beyond about one week (some phrase it as 7-10 days) should prompt first a husbandry check (temperature, humidity, prey size, possible tankmate competition) and then, if unresolved, a visit to a reptile-experienced veterinarian.

Concrete red flags that warrant concern regardless of how many days it's been: visible weight loss; a thin, stick-like tail (mourning geckos store fat reserves in their tail the way many gecko species do, so a shrinking tail signals the animal is burning through reserves); lethargy or reduced activity beyond a normal shed cycle; sunken or cloudy eyes outside of the pre-shed period; a swollen abdomen paired with straining (possible egg-binding, treated as an urgent vet issue); loose stools; or skin/shed-quality problems. Any of these should move up the timeline to a vet visit rather than waiting out the full week.

As an emergency stopgap only (not a substitute for diagnosing the underlying cause), a couple of sources mention that a dangerously inappetent gecko can be syringe-fed a thinned slurry of the commercial powdered gecko diet, fed slowly to avoid choking.

**FunFact candidate**
Every mourning gecko is female — the species reproduces entirely by parthenogenesis, with offspring genetically identical clones of the mother — which means a single pet gecko with no companion at all will still lay a pair of eggs roughly every 4-6 weeks, year-round, with no breeding season to pause it. Because that egg production never really stops, her calcium and nutritional demands from diet are effectively constant rather than seasonal, which is part of why care sheets stress consistent calcium dusting even for a lone, "unbred" pet.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Feeding frequency for the powdered CGD/MRP staple is genuinely inconsistent across otherwise credible sources: Dubia.com, Reptile Supply, and Pangea Reptile's own retail care page describe near-constant availability (replaced every 24-48 hours); ReptileDirect says 3-4x/week; The Bio Dude says 2-3x/week; CozyLizard says every other day; and Pangea Reptile's own separate blog care sheet gives a stricter discrete rotation of just 2 days/week of fruit mix plus 2 fasting days with no food at all. Dusted-insect frequency shows the same spread: 1-2x/week (Dubia.com, Reptile Supply) versus 2-3x/week (The Bio Dude, ReptileDirect) versus a flat 3 days/week (Pangea Reptile blog). I have presented these as a range rather than picking one as definitive.

Whether powdered CGD/MRP alone is nutritionally adequate long-term is also disputed: most retail/commercial sources present it as a workable staple, but Reptile Cymru's independent care sheet explicitly states "it's difficult to say long-term if this is nutritionally adequate" and cautions against relying on it as the sole food source, especially for growing juveniles.

I was unable to directly fetch ReptiFiles' dedicated feeding and shedding pages (reptifiles.com returned HTTP 403 to direct fetches); the ReptiFiles-derived claims here came from search-engine summarization of that content rather than a full direct read, so I'd treat those specific points as somewhat lower-confidence than the sources I fetched directly. Similarly, the "~1 week without eating = see a vet" guidance is a consensus synthesized across several keeper-facing care sites rather than a single cited peer-reviewed or veterinary-primary figure specific to this species.

### African Fat-Tailed Gecko

**Feeding schedule**
Sources agree on the broad shape but disagree on exact adult/juvenile frequency (see caveats). Consensus points:

BABIES/HATCHLINGS (under ~4 months): fed daily, about 5 crickets per day, sized about ¼-inch (progress to ½-inch crickets around 10g body weight, full-size crickets around 18g). Reported consistently by Reptiles Magazine, DubiaRoaches.com, and Reptile.Guide.

JUVENILES: ReptileSchool.com and the DubiaRoaches-derived synthesis say juveniles should be fed daily (larger daily quantities as they grow, removing uneaten prey after 1-2 hours). However, DubiaRoaches.com and Reptile.Guide instead lump "juveniles and adults" together at about 9 crickets/mealworms 3 times per week — a real discrepancy in how "juvenile" is being defined/treated across sources.

ADULTS: reported frequency varies by source: "3 times per week" (~9 crickets/mealworms per session — DubiaRoaches, Reptile.Guide); "3-4 times per week" (ReptileSchool.com, general synthesis); or age/condition-tiered — young adults fed every other day or every 3 days, mature adults whose tail is visibly fatter than their neck fed only every 5 days (a scheme sourced from cbreptile.com and echoed in cross-source search synthesis). Portion rule of thumb given by multiple sources: about 2 appropriately-sized bugs per 1 inch of the gecko's body length, or simply as much as it will eat in about 15 minutes / one sitting.

Prey size guidance is consistent: offer nothing larger than the width of the gecko's head / the space between its eyes.

**Diet composition**
Obligate insectivore. Staple feeders reported across sources: crickets, dubia roaches, discoid roaches, red runner roaches, mealworms, superworms, and black soldier fly larvae. Occasional/supplemental items (higher fat, not staples): silkworms, hornworms, waxworms, and pinkie mice — an exotic-vet source (Cadillac Vet) also lists grasshoppers/locusts and darkling beetles as acceptable variety items. All feeder insects should be captive-bred/farmed rather than wild-caught, to avoid pesticide exposure and parasites (Cadillac Vet).

Preparation/supplementation: gut-load feeder insects 24-48 hours before offering them, using a commercial gut-load product or a mix of baby cereal, fish flakes or high-quality dry dog/cat food plus leafy greens (endive, dandelion greens, romaine) or sweet potato. Calcium dusting: most care-sheet sources (Reptile.Guide, DubiaRoaches.com, ReptileSchool.com, general synthesis) say dust prey with plain calcium (no D3) at every feeding, with a calcium+D3/multivitamin combo used about once a week. A veterinary clinic source (Cadillac Vet) frames it slightly differently: calcium powder at least 3 times a week (not necessarily every single feeding), with the calcium/D3 combo used 1-2 times a week, and explicitly warns against calcium supplements that also contain phosphorus, which they say can damage kidneys and cause gout. Fresh water in a shallow dish should be available at all times, changed daily.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe/occasional treats (feed sparingly, not as staples, due to high fat content): waxworms, silkworms, hornworms (commercially raised feeder hornworms — not wild ones), and pinkie mice. Unsafe: never use sand as a substrate, since ingested sand can cause digestive impaction (multiple care sheets). An exotic-vet source (Cadillac Vet) lists specific insects that should never be fed, generally because they're wild-caught/toxic species rather than farmed feeders: ladybugs, fireflies, monarch butterflies, lubber grasshoppers, tomato-plant (wild) hornworms, box elder bugs, assassin bugs, centipedes, hairy/spiny caterpillars, milkweed bugs, bees, wasps (unless de-stung), certain wild spiders, butterflies/moths, and isopods. The same source also warns against calcium supplements that contain phosphorus, citing kidney damage and gout risk. General rule reiterated across sources: only feed captive-bred/farmed insects, never wild-caught ones, due to pesticide and toxin exposure risk.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Brumation / seasonal appetite drop* (normal): As daylight hours shorten in fall/winter, adult fat-tails commonly reduce food intake or stop eating for a period; juveniles typically keep eating normally through this. Reported by Reptiles Magazine-derived synthesis and corroborated by Gecko Time.
- *Breeding season / ovulation (females)* (normal): Female geckos may go off food for a few days before laying eggs, and breeders report appetite dropping around ovulation or when a male is present/courting. Cadillac Vet (exotic vet clinic) notes this ties to an annual breeding season spanning roughly 4-5 months.
- *Shedding* (depends): Shedding is energy-intensive and is commonly accompanied by a temporary drop in appetite as the gecko focuses on growing/releasing new skin. This alone is normal, but if humidity is too low the shed can get stuck (retained shed, including retained eye caps), which can restrict movement and cause lethargy, weight loss, and reduced eating that does require intervention (soaking 15-30 minutes in warm water, raising humidity).
- *Stress / new-environment or handling acclimation* (normal): Newly acquired geckos, or geckos recently handled or moved, may refuse food temporarily due to stress; multiple care sheets recommend minimal handling until the animal is reliably eating and defecating.
- *Insufficient enclosure temperature* (concerning): If the enclosure isn't warm enough, the gecko's body may respond as though winter/brumation is starting, suppressing appetite even outside the normal seasonal window. This is an environmental/husbandry issue to correct, not a health emergency by itself.
- *Dehydration* (concerning): Inadequate humidity or water access can suppress appetite; one source (ReptileSchool.com) specifically warns that if shedding-related dehydration continues beyond about one week, it can become fatal, so it should be corrected promptly.
- *Illness (parasites, bacterial infection, rarely tumors)* (concerning): Internal parasites, bacterial infections, or (rarely) tumors can suppress appetite; these are typically accompanied by weight loss and lethargy and require a reptile vet.
- *Old age / dental loss* (concerning): Elderly geckos can lose teeth and struggle to catch or chew hard-bodied insects like crickets, which can progress to starvation or dehydration if the diet isn't adapted (e.g., softer prey).
- *Extended 'picky eater' phases with no other symptoms* (depends): Gecko Time documents individual fat-tailed/leopard-type geckos refusing food for extended stretches — one owner reported roughly 6 months, another about 7 months — while the animal showed no illness signs: stable weight (only modest tail tapering, not real weight loss), normal activity and behavior. The tail's large fat reserve (can be close to half the gecko's body mass) is credited with buffering these long gaps.

**When to actually worry**
No source gives one single universal "X days" cutoff specific to this species; guidance instead centers on body condition and accompanying symptoms rather than duration alone, because the tail fat reserve genuinely lets healthy adults fast far longer than most reptiles without harm. Concrete red flags reported across sources: real weight loss (distinguish this from just a slightly slimmer/tapering tail — a genuinely thin tail, especially one thinner than the neck, is the concerning sign, not a tail that's merely less plump than before); a bloated belly; lethargy or weakness beyond the normal seasonal/brumation slowdown; retained/stuck shed that isn't resolving with humidity and soaking; and dehydration signs persisting alongside not eating (one source flags this as potentially fatal if unresolved beyond about a week). A veterinary clinic source (Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center) advises scheduling an exam if the gecko "stops eating, loses weight, has retained shed, or shows signs of weakness," and recommends annual wellness visits plus routine weight monitoring to catch problems early. Cadillac Vet similarly flags rapid weight gain or loss as a reason to seek care. In practice, hobbyist/breeder sources (Gecko Time) report that otherwise-healthy adults can go weeks to even several months without eating and be fine, provided weight and behavior stay normal — but any owner uncertain whether what they're seeing is "normal tail tapering" versus real weight loss should have a reptile vet check body condition directly rather than guessing from duration alone.

**FunFact candidate**
An African fat-tailed gecko's tail can hold close to half of its total body mass in stored fat — a survival adaptation that lets it coast through genuinely long stretches without food. Hobbyist records compiled by Gecko Time include individual geckos that voluntarily skipped food for roughly six to seven months at a stretch while staying at a stable weight and behaving completely normally, only for their appetite to return on its own — a reminder that for this species, "not eating" and "starving" are not the same thing, and tail thickness (not the calendar) is the real thing to watch.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two areas of genuine, unresolved disagreement across the sources found:

1. JUVENILE vs. ADULT FEEDING FREQUENCY: Some care sheets (ReptileSchool.com; the general cross-source synthesis pulled from Reptiles Magazine's care sheet) say juveniles should be fed daily, distinct from adults. Others (DubiaRoaches.com, Reptile.Guide) instead group "juveniles and adults" into one bracket fed about 9 crickets/mealworms 3 times a week, with no separate juvenile schedule at all. These cannot both be describing the same husbandry practice — a reader following one versus the other would feed a juvenile gecko roughly twice as often. I did not find a single authoritative source (e.g., a vet clinic or academic source) that resolves this; it appears to be genuine variation in hobbyist/retailer convention.

2. ADULT FEEDING FREQUENCY SPECIFICALLY: reported as "3 times per week" (DubiaRoaches.com, Reptile.Guide), "3-4 times per week" (ReptileSchool.com), or a condition-based tiered schedule of every-2-3-days for young adults down to every 5 days once the tail is visibly fatter than the neck (cbreptile.com, echoed in initial search synthesis). These are presented by their respective sources as settled facts, but they don't agree with each other, so I've presented the range rather than picking one.

3. CALCIUM DUSTING FREQUENCY: most hobbyist care sheets say dust with plain calcium at every single feeding; the one veterinary-clinic source found (Cadillac Vet) instead says "at least 3 times per week," which is a milder/less frequent recommendation than "every feeding." Not a large gap, but worth flagging since it comes from a clinical source versus retailer/hobbyist sources.

4. "How long is safe without eating" has no single sourced number — I deliberately did not manufacture one. The range mentioned by hobbyist sources (weeks to several months, in otherwise-healthy geckos) is real but comes from anecdotal breeder reports (Gecko Time), not a clinical study, so I've presented it as such rather than as a guaranteed safe window.

## Invertebrates

### Tarantula

**Feeding schedule**
Life-stage breakdown (sources vary somewhat, numbers below are the ranges actually reported, not an average):

SLINGS/SPIDERLINGS (roughly under 2"): Feed every 2–3 days. An exotic-pet veterinary clinic (Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center) says "three times weekly"; VetVerified says every 2–3 days; breeder resource Tom's Big Spiders says "every two or three days... is perfect," or once weekly with a larger prey item. Use pinhead crickets, fruit flies, or other tiny prey — no larger than the sling's abdomen.

JUVENILES (roughly 2–4"): This is where sources disagree most. VetVerified says every 4–7 days; Tree of Life (vet clinic) says every 7–14 days for "mature and developing specimens"; Tom's Big Spiders says a larger prey item once or twice a week, noting growth slows at this stage; other keeper guides (Arachnoboards/Tarantula Heaven) describe roughly weekly as a common default. Treat all of these as a starting point to adjust based on abdomen size and appetite, not a fixed rule.

SUBADULTS/ADULTS: Commonly cited baseline is every 1–2 weeks (VetVerified; Chicago-area exotic vet source cited "once or twice a week depending on hunger level"). But actual need is strongly species-dependent: Tom's Big Spiders notes an adult Grammostola porteri/rosea may need only about four or five crickets a month, while fast-growing tropical species like Theraphosa or Pamphobeteus can eat multiple prey items a week.

MATURE ADULT MALES: A distinct pattern — after a male's final ("ultimate") molt into sexual maturity, many keepers report he eats markedly less or stops eating almost entirely, redirecting energy toward wandering and mating; some individuals never take another meal after maturing (Arachnoboards keeper consensus; corroborated by Scientific American's reporting on male tarantulas' post-mating life cycle). This is treated as a natural, not concerning, end-of-life pattern.

General across stages: prey should be no larger than the tarantula's abdomen, and uneaten prey should be removed within 24 hours (crickets left in the enclosure can injure or kill a freshly molted, vulnerable tarantula).

**Diet composition**
Captive tarantulas eat live insect prey, not a formulated diet. Staples: crickets (the default feeder — high protein, low fat, easy to digest and widely available) and dubia roaches or B. lateralis (discoid) roaches, especially for larger spiders (roaches are described as nutrient-dense and slower-digesting than crickets). Supplemental/occasional items: mealworms and superworms (superworms and larger roaches can bite — hobbyist guidance is to crush the head before offering them to a tarantula) and waxworms, offered only occasionally as a treat because of their high fat content. The largest species (e.g. Theraphosa blondi, the goliath birdeater, and Pamphobeteus) can occasionally take a pinky mouse, though multiple sources note this is not necessary for a healthy diet.

Gut-loading: feeder insects should be fed a nutritious diet (greens/grains or a commercial gutload) roughly 24–48 hours before being offered, so that nutrition transfers to the tarantula when it's eaten.

Calcium/vitamin dusting — do NOT use it the way you would for reptiles: tarantulas are invertebrates with no bones, and multiple hobbyist/care sources describe calcium as essentially a foreign compound their bodies aren't equipped to process, with anecdotal reports linking excess calcium to molting problems (e.g. bad/wet molts). One source is careful to note there is "no strong scientific evidence" calcium actually causes harm, so this is best framed as a precautionary hobby-wide practice rather than a proven toxicology finding — but the consistent guidance across care sites is: don't dust feeders for a tarantula.

Feeding mechanics/prep: prey is offered live and whole (not chopped) since tarantulas digest externally — they inject digestive enzymes through their fangs to liquefy the prey's internal tissue, then use a muscular "sucking stomach" to pump the liquid meal into the gut, leaving the indigestible exoskeleton behind as a compact leftover pellet.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
SAFE / STAPLE: crickets (default feeder insect), dubia roaches and B. lateralis (discoid) roaches (especially for larger species), mealworms, superworms (crush the head first — they can bite), and pinky mice occasionally for the largest species only (e.g. Theraphosa blondi, Pamphobeteus) though not required.

SAFE BUT TREAT-ONLY: waxworms — fine occasionally, but high in fat, so not a staple.

UNSAFE: wild-caught insects of any kind (real risk of pesticide/fertilizer contamination and parasites — buy from a breeder/pet store instead); fireflies specifically (contain lucibufagin toxins that are documented as lethal to a range of predators — this is repeated as a hard 'never feed' rule across tarantula-keeping sources, though the strongest published toxicity data is for reptiles like bearded dragons rather than tarantulas specifically, so treat it as a strong precaution rather than tarantula-specific lab-proven fact); and any processed human food (meat, dairy, sugary snacks) — not part of a natural diet and not appropriate. Also avoid reptile-style calcium/vitamin powder dusting on feeders (see diet composition notes).

**Why they stop eating**
- *Premolt fasting* (normal): The single most common reason a tarantula stops eating. Appetite loss, a darkening/plumping abdomen, and lethargy typically precede a molt. Duration is highly variable: slings may fast a couple of weeks to a month, while juveniles/adults can fast for several months; outlier cases (e.g. a keeper-reported Grammostola rosea) have gone over a year before molting. Tom's Big Spiders: 'There is no set time for how long a tarantula can be in premolt.'
- *Post-molt recovery window* (normal): Right after molting, a tarantula's new exoskeleton and fangs are soft and it should not be fed. Keeper/vet guidance suggests waiting about 3–7 days for slings/juveniles and 7–14 days for adults before offering food again.
- *Mature male post-ultimate-molt / mating drive* (normal): Once a male reaches his final molt into adulthood, many stop eating heavily or altogether as they focus on wandering and searching for a mate — some never eat again after maturing. This is a natural late-life-stage pattern, not illness.
- *New enclosure or environmental stress* (normal): Rehoming, handling, or husbandry that's slightly off (temperature, humidity, substrate, hide availability) can make a tarantula 'uncomfortable, anxious, and not want to eat,' with adjustment periods lasting several days to weeks after a move or big habitat change.
- *Species-specific fasting tendency* (normal): Some species — Chilean rose hairs (Grammostola rosea/porteri) are the most frequently cited example — have a reputation among keepers for long, seemingly unprompted fasts 'regardless of any other external factor.'
- *Prey pickiness/refusal* (normal): A tarantula may reject one prey type (e.g. crickets) while accepting another, or refuse prey that's too large or moves too erratically. Not a health issue by itself.
- *Illness, injury, or dehydration* (concerning): Loss of appetite paired with other signs — not drinking, visible injury, uncoordinated or dragging legs, or the 'death curl' (legs curled tightly beneath an upright body) — points to a genuine problem rather than routine fasting.

**When to actually worry**
Not eating by itself is usually not an emergency for a tarantula — this is the species where 'skipping meals' is genuinely normal biology, not something to generalize from other pets. A plump, active tarantula that refuses food for weeks is typically fine and often just in premolt.

The closest thing to a concrete vet-sourced threshold: an exotic-pet veterinary clinic (Tree of Life Exotic Pet Medical Center) advises that for juveniles specifically, refusing food for more than 6–8 weeks accompanied by weight loss or lethargy warrants a veterinary consult. Adults can safely fast far longer during premolt (weeks to several months is well within normal, per breeder/keeper consensus), so that 6–8-week figure should be read as a juvenile-specific concern trigger, not a hard universal cutoff — sources don't converge on one number for adults.

The bigger red flag isn't hunger, it's dehydration. Per giantspiders.com (Stanley Schultz's long-standing tarantula-care resource), the real danger sign is a shrunken, shriveled, 'raisin-like' abdomen along with uncoordinated legs or inability to lift the body off the ground — this can be fatal within days if not corrected, and is treated by providing immediate access to a shallow water dish. That same source notes lab observations of tarantulas surviving without any food for up to roughly two years — an extreme outlier, not a keeping benchmark, but it underscores that food refusal alone is rarely the emergency; lack of water is.

Bottom line for keepers: check water access and enclosure humidity/ventilation first when a tarantula stops eating; watch the abdomen (shriveled = urgent, plump/dark = likely premolt); and see an exotics vet if food refusal is paired with lethargy, injury, not drinking, leg curling, or (for juveniles) goes past roughly 6–8 weeks with visible weight loss.

**FunFact candidate**
Tarantulas can't chew solid food at all. They pierce prey with their fangs and pump digestive enzymes into it, liquefying the internal tissue into a broth outside their own body (external digestion) — then a muscular 'sucking stomach' in the cephalothorax works like a pump to draw that liquid meal into the gut, while the empty, indigestible exoskeleton is discarded afterward as a small, compact leftover pellet.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several places where sources genuinely diverge rather than just phrasing things differently:

1. Juvenile feeding frequency has no single agreed number: an exotic vet clinic source says every 7–14 days, a general pet-health site (VetVerified) says every 4–7 days, and a breeder resource (Tom's Big Spiders) implies roughly weekly to twice-weekly. This spread is plausibly explained by species/size differences, but no source reconciles it explicitly — present as a range, not a fixed rule.

2. Premolt fasting duration: mainstream guidance is 'days to a few months,' but breeder/keeper anecdotes (Tom's Big Spiders, Arachnoboards) describe an outlier Grammostola rosea fasting over a year before molting. That's a documented extreme, not typical, and should not be presented as an expected timeframe.

3. Calcium dusting: hobbyist care sites are fairly unanimous in telling keepers not to dust feeder insects for tarantulas (citing anecdotal links to bad molts), but one of those same sources explicitly admits 'no strong scientific evidence' establishes actual harm. This is a precautionary consensus, not a settled toxicological fact — worth stating carefully rather than as proven danger, given this site's history of overstating a disputed figure.

4. Starvation survival time: giantspiders.com cites lab observations of tarantulas surviving without food for 'up to two years' in some documented cases. This is real but an extreme outlier under controlled/lab conditions, and could easily be misread by a reader as 'don't worry about your tarantula for two years' — it should be presented alongside the more conservative, vet-sourced 6–8-week concern threshold for juveniles, not in place of it.

5. Firefly toxicity specifically to tarantulas: widely repeated in tarantula-care content as an absolute no, but the toxicology research actually found documents lucibufagin toxicity in vertebrate predators (e.g. bearded dragons), not tarantulas directly. The hobby-wide precaution is strong and consistent, but I could not verify tarantula-specific lethality data — flagging this gap rather than stating it as directly proven for tarantulas.

### Jumping Spider

**Feeding schedule**
Sources converge on the same shape (frequent small meals for spiderlings, tapering as they mature) but disagree noticeably on exact day-counts for adults, so present this as a range rather than a single number.

By life stage (Phidippus-genus consensus):
- Spiderlings (1st-3rd instar, ~2-6mm): fed most often, generally every 1-2 days on 1-2 flightless fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster). One UK breeder source (arachnamoria.co.uk) recommends even more frequent small daily feedings (2 small fruit flies/day) at this stage — a real difference in intensity from the "every 1-2 days" sources, not just phrasing.
- Small/mid juveniles (4th-5th instar): every 2-3 days, stepping up prey to D. hydei fruit flies, then pinhead crickets or small houseflies.
- Sub-adults (6th instar to penultimate): every 2-4 days with small crickets or bottle flies; several sources specifically warn against overfeeding right before the final molt since a distended abdomen raises the risk of rupture during that molt.
- Adults: this is where sources diverge most. Reported intervals range from every 2-3 days, to every 3-5 days, to (for males specifically, per one source) as infrequently as every 10 days. Gravid/egg-guarding females are reported to eat more (some sources say daily or every 3 days) while actively guarding an egg sac, or conversely to refuse food almost entirely during guarding — both patterns are mentioned across sources, so treat individual variation as normal.

General practice recommended everywhere: offer food rather than force a schedule, and let the spider's abdomen size guide you — a shrunken/wrinkled abdomen means it's hungry and can be fed early; a plump, rounded abdomen noticeably wider than the cephalothorax means skip the next feeding. Feed in the morning (jumping spiders are visual daytime hunters) and pull any uneaten live prey out within a few hours to overnight so it doesn't harass or injure the spider, especially near a molt.

**Diet composition**
Obligate carnivore/insectivore — no plant matter, no commercial "spider food."

Staple feeders by size:
- Flightless fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster for the smallest spiderlings, graduating to the larger D. hydei) for slings and small juveniles.
- Pinhead-to-small crickets, houseflies, and bluebottle/greenbottle flies for juveniles through adults — flies are frequently singled out as a preferred, low-injury-risk staple because they can't bite back or burrow.
- Small roaches (e.g., dubia nymphs) as another low-risk staple option mentioned by multiple keepers.
- Mealworms and waxworms as occasional additions/treats rather than staples — waxworms in particular are called out repeatedly as high-fat and best fed sparingly (one source specifically suggests using them post-molt when the spider needs quick calories).

Prey sizing: keep prey noticeably smaller than the spider — guidance ranges from about half the spider's body length up to roughly 3/4-1.5x the spider's size depending on the source, with the consistent underlying rule being "smaller than the spider's abdomen, matched to the chelicerae." Oversized prey is the most repeated safety warning in the diet section — a cricket that's too big can injure or kill a jumping spider, especially during/near a molt.

Gut-loading: the standard supplementation method (rather than calcium dusting, which is standard for reptiles but only mentioned by one lower-confidence source here and is not corroborated as typical spider-keeping practice). Feeder insects are fed a nutritious diet — pureed vegetables/fruit like carrot, apple, banana, celery for crickets, or a sugar/yeast mix for fruit fly cultures — for roughly 24-48 hours before being offered to the spider, so the spider gets those nutrients secondhand. This matters most for breeding/gravid females.

Hydration: not "diet" in the food sense but universally covered alongside feeding — a shallow water dish and/or light daily misting of the enclosure walls (not the spider directly) for drinking droplets. One source specifically warns against wetting the spider's abdomen because water can seep between the book-lung "pages" and cause suffocation.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe: flightless fruit flies (D. melanogaster/D. hydei), pinhead-to-small crickets, houseflies, bluebottle/greenbottle flies, small dubia roach nymphs, mealworms (gut-loaded), waxworms (occasional treat only, due to fat content), small moths.

Unsafe / to avoid, named consistently across sources:
- Ants — repeatedly flagged as the top thing never to feed; they can bite and/or spray formic acid, and jumping spiders appear to have an instinctive fear response to them (ants are a natural predator/threat of jumping spiders in the wild).
- Hard-shelled beetles and pill bugs/isopods — exoskeleton too tough for the spider to bite through effectively.
- Fireflies — explicitly listed as unsafe by one source (consistent with fireflies' known toxicity, lucibufagins, to other small predators such as reptiles and amphibians, though the specific spider-toxicity claim comes from a single source here).
- Other spiders — risk of cannibalism or mutual injury; not a nutrition issue so much as a physical-injury one.
- Wild-caught/unknown insects, especially anything from a yard or area treated with pesticides/herbicides — flagged across multiple sources as a poisoning risk; captive-bred, store-bought, or home-cultured feeders are the recommended alternative.
- Any prey larger than roughly the spider's own body/abdomen size, regardless of species — an injury risk rather than a toxicity one.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Pre-molt fasting* (normal): Jumping spiders commonly stop eating in the days to weeks before shedding their exoskeleton. Reported windows vary by source and life stage — roughly a few days for spiderlings up to one to several weeks for larger juveniles/sub-adults approaching a molt (one source cites as long as 1-4 weeks pre-molt in some individuals). Keepers are consistently told not to force-feed or leave live prey in with a spider showing this pattern.
- *Post-molt recovery* (normal): Immediately after shedding, a jumping spider's new fangs/chelicerae are soft and it cannot safely bite or digest prey. Sources generally agree on waiting about 2-3 days after a molt before offering food again.
- *Breeding and egg-guarding* (normal): Courting/mating adults, and especially females guarding an egg sac, are reported to eat differently than usual — some sources describe reduced or refused feeding for the duration of guarding, others describe increased appetite in gravid females to support egg development. Both patterns show up across sources, so either direction of appetite change around breeding is considered normal.
- *Environmental stress or unsuitable conditions* (depends): Temperatures that are too cool, low enclosure humidity, a recent rehousing, or excessive handling/disturbance are cited as triggers for appetite refusal outside of a molting window.
- *Old age / senescence* (normal): Older adult spiders — particularly past their final molt — are described as naturally slowing their feeding pace, with some sources describing fasts of a week or more, and in a couple of accounts up to about a month, as tolerable in mature/aging individuals without indicating illness.
- *Individual pickiness / prey-type preference* (normal): Multiple keeper sources note that individual jumping spiders can be selective about prey type (preferring flies over crickets, or vice versa) and may ignore a feeder they simply don't want, even while otherwise healthy.
- *Dehydration or illness* (concerning): Refusal to eat combined with a shrunken/wrinkled ("raisin-like") abdomen, lethargy, poor jumping/climbing ability, or curled-inward legs points to dehydration or a health problem rather than a normal fasting phase.

**When to actually worry**
There is no single universally-cited number of days for jumping spiders, and the more precise-sounding figures below come from lower-confidence care-sheet-farm sites rather than an authoritative/veterinary source, so treat them as directional ranges, not fixed rules.

Rough consensus across sources: a healthy adult jumping spider can safely go roughly 2-3 weeks without eating, and older/adult individuals are anecdotally reported tolerating fasts of a month or more, particularly around a molt or during egg-guarding — none of that alone is cause for alarm. Spiderlings and small juveniles have much smaller energy reserves and shouldn't be left that long; keepers are advised to keep offering appropriately-sized food every couple of days at that stage even if it's sometimes refused.

Reasons to actually get concerned, per the sources reviewed:
- Food refusal that runs well past two weeks with no molt ever occurring (i.e., it's not resolving into a shed).
- No interest in food for roughly a week or more after a molt has already completed and fangs should have hardened.
- A visibly shrunken, wrinkled, or "raisin-like" abdomen — the most repeated dehydration indicator — especially paired with lethargy or reluctance to hunt visible prey.
- Legs curling inward, an inability to right itself if flipped, visible injury, or fluid leakage — these are described as urgent/emergency-level signs warranting an exotic-animal vet rather than a wait-and-see approach.
- Failed or "stuck" molting (dysecdysis) — a spider partially out of its old exoskeleton with no progress for several hours, or limbs left behind — is flagged by more than one source as among the more serious/dangerous events in captive jumping spider keeping, alongside dehydration.

Given how thin and inconsistent the numeric guidance is across the available hobbyist sources, this is a good area to phrase as "general ranges keepers report" rather than presenting any single day-count as a settled medical threshold.

**FunFact candidate**
Jumping spiders judge the exact pouncing distance to prey using a hunting trick found almost nowhere else in the animal kingdom: their large principal (front-center) eyes have four light-sensitive layers stacked behind one lens, arranged so a projected image is sharply focused on one layer while automatically blurred on another layer just behind it. The spider's brain compares that blur against the sharp image to compute depth — a mechanism called "depth from image defocus." Researchers confirmed this in a 2012 Science study by bathing jumping spiders in red vs. green light: because their photoreceptors are tuned to green wavelengths, spiders under green light judged jump distances accurately, while spiders under red light consistently undershot their target. In other words, this spider's whole hunting-and-feeding strategy depends on a defocus trick most animals don't use at all.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several real disagreements surfaced and should not be silently averaged into one number:

1. Adult feeding frequency spans a wide range depending on the source: "every 2-3 days" (jumpingspidercare.com), "every 3-5 days" (multiple sources), and males specifically at "every 10 days or so" with females at "every 5 days" (spiderswebhq.com). These aren't just rounding differences — they reflect real variation keepers report, likely driven by temperature, prey size, and individual spider condition, so the feeding guide should present a range with "let abdomen condition guide you" rather than one authoritative number.

2. Spiderling feeding intensity differs: most US-hobbyist sources say every 1-2 days for the youngest instars, but one UK breeder source (arachnamoria.co.uk) recommends up to 2 small fruit flies per day at that stage — meaningfully more frequent, not just a different phrasing of the same schedule.

3. Whether calcium dusting is used for jumping spiders: only one (lower-confidence) source mentioned calcium-dusting prey; it is not corroborated by the other care sheets and does not appear to be standard practice for spiders the way it is for reptiles, so I did not present it as routine.

4. Numeric thresholds for "how many days without food is too long," exact pre-molt fasting windows, and specific temperature cutoffs varied most on pages that read like programmatically-generated/SEO-farm content (exopetguides.com, spectrumcare.pet) — these sites gave suspiciously precise-sounding figures (exact °C cutoffs, "second only to dehydration" mortality rankings, exact vet-cost ranges) without citing underlying data. I treated those specific numbers as low-confidence and leaned on the more consistent, plainer-language ranges from established hobbyist/breeder sources (jumpingspidercare.com, mypetjumpingspider.com, bugsincyberspace.com, spiderswebhq.com, exoticssource.com, arachnamoria.co.uk) wherever they diverged. I was not able to find a true veterinary or academic source giving concrete "days without food" thresholds for pet jumping spiders specifically — that guidance is entirely hobbyist-sourced and should be labeled as such on the site, not presented as vet-verified.

5. Prey-size guidance also varies (roughly "half the spider's body size" vs. "3/4 to 1.5x the spider's size" vs. "no bigger than the abdomen") — the underlying safety principle (don't go oversized) is consistent, but the exact ratio is not.

### Madagascar Hissing Cockroach

**Feeding schedule**
Sources do not agree on a single frequency. Hobbyist/pet-industry care sheets (Reptile Cymru, ReadyReptileFeeders) recommend keeping a dry protein source — high-quality dry dog food, cat food, or a commercial roach/insect chow — available essentially all the time, topped up with fresh fruit and vegetables about 2-3 times per week, in a quantity the colony can finish within about 24 hours (ReadyReptileFeeders) to 24-48 hours (Fluker Farms/Dubia.com) so leftovers don't mold. By contrast, the academic Oklahoma State University Extension fact sheet describes a noticeably sparser regimen for a small pet colony: dry dog food or mouse chow offered about once a week, with fruit like apple or banana added only every couple of weeks. That is a real disagreement in degree (near-constant access vs. weekly rationing), not just phrasing, so both are presented rather than averaged.

Life-stage breakdown: none of the sources consulted give a distinct feeding frequency for nymphs (babies/juveniles) versus adults. Newly born nymphs are independent immediately after the mother releases them and feed themselves from birth (Dubia Roach Depot). The one documented stage-based difference is that breeding females need reliable access to protein-rich food (dog kibble, grains, fish flakes) to produce healthy oothecae/broods (Dubia.com), not a different meal schedule tied to age.

**Diet composition**
Base diet: a high-quality dry dog food is the most consistently recommended protein/staple item (multiple sources say it has the best overall nutritional makeup for hissers versus cat or bird food, though those work as substitutes); a commercial roach/insect chow (e.g., a dubia-style roach diet) or "reptile munchies" fruit-and-veg mix is an accepted alternative or supplement. Grain items mentioned include oats, wheat bran, oatmeal and pearl barley.

Fresh produce, offered a few times a week: romaine lettuce and other dark leafy/spring greens, dandelion greens, carrots, squash, sweet potato, apples, bananas, oranges, peaches, plums, grapes, tomatoes, celery, peas and potato. Iceberg lettuce is specifically called out as a poor choice (low nutrition) versus romaine. For context, their wild/ancestral diet is decaying plant matter and fallen, overripe fruit, plus occasional fungi or animal detritus — captive diets are built to approximate that.

Preparation: rinse produce and use pesticide-free/organic when possible; chop or slice fruit/veg into manageable pieces; remove any uneaten fresh food within about a day or two, since mold and fruit flies are the main colony-health risk from feeding. Dedicated gut-loading protocols (keeping dietary protein under ~20% and using calcium/vitamin-heavy gut-load foods like kale, spinach, alfalfa) are documented specifically for hissers being raised as feeder insects for reptiles, not as a requirement for hissers kept purely as pets — worth distinguishing since this is a pet-care context.

Water: most sources favor a water source that can't drown a roach — a cotton ball or sponge saturated with water in a shallow cap or dish, or commercial insect "water crystal gel" — changed/refreshed regularly (daily to weekly depending on the source), because plain open water dishes pose a drowning risk especially to small nymphs. One source (Reptile Cymru) simply recommends a shallow water dish, so there is some disagreement on this point. Moisture from fresh fruit and vegetables also contributes meaningfully to hydration.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Good safe treat items, repeated across sources: apple slices, banana, orange, romaine lettuce, dandelion greens, carrots, grapes, sweet potato/squash.

Avoid or use cautiously: iceberg lettuce (explicitly flagged as nutritionally poor compared with romaine); any produce treated with pesticides (use organic/pesticide-free, or wash thoroughly); spoiled or moldy leftover food (remove within 24-48 hours rather than letting it sit); and diets that are unnecessarily protein-heavy. Multiple keeper/breeder sources describe hissers as evolutionarily "protein-sparing" — they don't need much protein and can have problems if overfed a meat- or pellet-heavy diet, so dry dog food and produce should form the bulk of the diet rather than straight high-protein feed.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Approaching a molt (ecdysis)* (normal): Nymphs go through about six molts over roughly five months to reach adulthood; adults stop molting. In the run-up to a molt, roaches commonly go quiet/inactive and may eat less, and they need elevated humidity (roughly 80-90% per one keeper-health source) during this window to shed successfully. Freshly molted individuals are pale/white and soft for a few hours and are especially vulnerable, but this is a normal developmental stage.
- *Stress from a new environment or recent acquisition* (normal): Newly acquired hissers are commonly reported to need an adjustment period before settling into normal feeding; environmental change and handling stress can suppress appetite short-term.
- *Being housed alone / isolation* (normal): Keeper forum discussion (moderate-authority, practical/anecdotal source) suggests solitary hissers can become insecure and hide rather than feed visibly, since they're normally kept in groups/colonies.
- *Nocturnal, light-averse feeding habits* (normal): Hissers are negatively phototactic and most active after dark, so keepers frequently just don't witness them eating even though feeding is happening overnight — this can look like appetite loss when it isn't.
- *Enclosure too cool* (depends): Multiple care sheets note that temperatures dropping much below about 70°F (roughly low-70s and under) cause lethargy and slow activity, including feeding and breeding. This is a husbandry issue that resolves once the correct 75-85°F range (the range most sources cluster around) is restored.
- *Illness* (concerning): A keeper-authored health-issues source (not veterinary-authored, so treat as moderate confidence) lists genuine sickness signs as: lying in one place without hissing when touched, lethargy beyond normal sluggishness, a dull exoskeleton, weight loss, discharge from the mouth or eyes, visible sores, and abnormal feces. The same source states visibly sick roaches often survive only about 2-3 days once symptoms are obvious, meaning the window to act is short.
- *Seasonal dormancy / brumation* (normal): No source consulted documents a hibernation- or brumation-like dormancy period for this species in captivity — unlike many temperate reptiles, Madagascar hissing cockroaches are a tropical species and the care sheets reviewed describe year-round activity at stable warm temperatures, not a seasonal fasting period. Flagged explicitly so this generic exotic-pet cause isn't assumed to apply here without evidence.

**When to actually worry**
The only concrete duration figure found — that Madagascar hissing cockroaches "can survive a week without food or water" — comes from a single zoo-attraction website (BlueZoo) rather than a veterinary or academic source, and no other source consulted corroborated or contradicted that specific number, so it should be treated as a rough, thinly sourced benchmark rather than settled fact. No stronger vet-sourced numeric guidance on exactly how long is "fine" to go without eating was found; a veterinary-practice blog (South Wilton Vet) covers general hisser husbandry but does not address appetite loss or illness signs at all, which is itself worth noting as a gap in the available sourcing.

What should prompt real concern, per a keeper/breeder health write-up: inactivity (lying in one place), failure to hiss when touched or handled, lethargy beyond the normal sluggishness sometimes seen before a molt, a dull-looking exoskeleton, visible weight/body-condition loss, discharge from the mouth or eyes, visible sores or abrasions, and abnormal feces. That source also states that once a hisser is visibly sick, it commonly survives only about 2-3 days, which argues for acting quickly (isolating the animal, checking temperature/humidity, and consulting an exotics-experienced vet) rather than waiting once multiple symptoms stack up alongside food refusal.

**FunFact candidate**
Despite being one of the largest cockroach species, hissers are described by breeder/keeper sources as evolutionarily "protein-sparing" — their ancestral diet of fallen, overripe fruit and decaying plant matter is naturally low in protein, so captive diets are built around modest dry dog food and produce rather than meat-heavy feed. One practical consequence: keepers are advised to feed the colony regularly and adequately in part because underfed hissers are more prone to cannibalizing weaker or freshly molted (soft, pale, defenseless) colony-mates — a feeding-biology quirk that ties appetite directly to colony safety, not just individual nutrition.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Feeding frequency: hobbyist/pet-industry sources (Reptile Cymru, ReadyReptileFeeders) recommend near-constant dry food access plus fresh produce 2-3x/week; the academic OSU Extension sheet describes weekly dry food and fruit only every couple of weeks — a meaningfully sparser regimen, not just a phrasing difference.
2) Humidity: most care sheets cluster around 60-90% (Zilla: 60-80%; Reptile Cymru/Dubia Roach Depot: 70% minimum, 80%+ optimal; a keeper-health source cites 80-90% specifically around molting), but ReadyReptileFeeders (a feeder-breeder-focused site) recommends a notably lower 40-50% for optimal molting/breeding — a direct numeric contradiction worth flagging rather than averaging.
3) Temperature: most sources cluster around 75-85°F days / mid-to-high-70s nights, but ReadyReptileFeeders (optimizing for breeding output) recommends a warmer 82-88°F. Not wildly contradictory but notably higher than the pet-care consensus.
4) Water delivery: most sources favor cotton-ball/sponge or gel water sources to prevent drowning (especially of nymphs); Reptile Cymru instead simply recommends a shallow water dish. This is a genuine methodological disagreement about drowning risk, not just preference.
5) Lifespan: most sources agree on roughly 2-5 years in captivity (several state "up to 5 years"), though Dubia Roach Depot narrows the average to 2-4 years while noting some individuals live longer — a minor variance, included for completeness.
6) The "can go about a week without food" figure rests on a single lower-authority source (a zoo attraction site) with no independent corroboration found; treat as approximate, not a validated veterinary benchmark.

## Fish

### Betta Fish

**Feeding schedule**
Sources do not fully agree on frequency for adults. Aquarium Co-Op's care guide recommends feeding once a day, "as much as they can eat in one minute." PetMD's care sheet and Bettafish.org both say once or twice per day is acceptable. A vet-branded source (PangoVet/The Vet Desk, which publish near-identical mirrored text) goes further, recommending "at least twice a day," meals spaced 6-8 hours apart, and says feeding can even be split into three smaller daily meals. Portion size is consistently described relative to the fish's own eye: multiple independent sources (Bettafish.org, Hygger, PangoVet) state a betta's stomach is roughly the size of its eyeball, and recommend feeding about 2-4 pellets per meal (as few as 2-3 or up to 6-7 depending on pellet size/brand) or whatever amount is cleared within about 60 seconds. One source gives a numeric daily total of about 1.8 grams of food, but this figure appears only in the PangoVet/VetDesk mirrored content and is not independently corroborated elsewhere, so treat it as lower-confidence. Most sources that address digestive health (Bettafish.org, multiple constipation-focused vet/care pages) recommend one fasting day per week (no food for 24 hours) to aid digestion and prevent bloat/constipation. Life-stage breakdown: Fry (newly hatched, first 3-4 days) are fed infusoria several times a day; as they grow (Hepper's vet-reviewed fry guide and a breeder feeding-schedule site) feeding frequency is 4-5 small meals/day for newly free-swimming fry, dropping to 3-4 times/day by weeks 3-4, then 2-3 times/day as juveniles. Once juveniles are jarred/separated, they're fed like adults — small amounts twice daily, with one source capping juveniles at 3-4 pellets per day total.

**Diet composition**
Bettas are obligate carnivores. In the wild (per academic/species-profile sources), they are ambush surface feeders with an upturned mouth adapted to snatch insects, insect larvae (especially mosquito larvae), small crustaceans, and zooplankton from or near the water's surface, using rapid suction feeding. In captivity, the recommended staple is a betta-specific high-protein pellet (not tropical flakes or goldfish pellets, which lack the right nutrient profile and contain more filler). Sources differ slightly on the protein floor to look for: Bettafish.org says a minimum of 30% protein, while Aquarium Co-Op's guide implies looking for 40%+ protein formulas; both agree good pellets list dried meats like brine shrimp, krill, or fish meal near the top of the ingredient list, and named quality brands include NorthFin Betta Bits, New Life Spectrum, and Ocean Nutrition. Pellets that expand (some formulas do) should be pre-soaked in tank water before feeding to reduce bloat/constipation risk. Diet should be varied, not bloodworms/pellets alone: supplement the staple pellet with frozen, freeze-dried, or live protein treats 1-3 times a week — bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, tubifex worms, mysis shrimp, mosquito larvae, and wingless fruit flies are all named across sources. Freeze-dried foods should be soaked/rehydrated first to prevent constipation. Bloodworms specifically are called out as too fatty for daily feeding — limit to 1-2 times per week, only 1-2 worms per meal, since bettas readily gorge on them.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (in moderation, 1-3x/week as supplements to pellets, not the staple): frozen or freeze-dried bloodworms (limit 1-2x/week, 1-2 worms per meal — too fatty for daily use), brine shrimp, daphnia, tubifex worms, mysis shrimp, mosquito larvae, wingless fruit flies, and blanched/shelled/peeled peas (used specifically as an occasional natural laxative for constipation, not a regular food). Unsafe/toxic foods named across sources: bread (swells with water/yeast, causes bloating and constipation, minimal nutrition), onions and garlic (contain sulfur compounds toxic to fish), salt (can cause severe dehydration, potentially fatal), sugary foods (risk of obesity and other health problems), processed/cured meats like hot dogs, lunch meat, bacon, and sausage (excess sodium, preservatives, and saturated fat that stress liver/kidneys), citrus fruits (highly acidic — can crash tank pH and burn gill tissue, potentially fatal even in small amounts), and dairy products (bettas are effectively lactose intolerant; dairy risks severe bloating/GI distress). General tropical fish flakes and goldfish pellets are also flagged as inappropriate as a primary diet due to wrong nutrient/filler profile for an obligate carnivore.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Environmental stress (new tank, rehoming, handling, tankmate aggression, sudden change)* (normal): A vet-reviewed source (DialAVet) and multiple care sites state that changes in environment or sudden water-temperature fluctuations stress bettas and suppress appetite.
- *Poor water quality (ammonia/nitrite/nitrate spikes)* (concerning): DialAVet and other sources note high ammonia or nitrate levels make bettas unwell and unwilling to eat; this is frequently cited as the most common underlying cause of appetite loss and general illness.
- *Water too cold or unstable* (concerning): Bettas are tropical fish needing roughly 76-82F water (exact range varies slightly by source: PetMD says 76-81F, DialAVet says 76-82F, PangoVet says 78-82F). Cold water slows their metabolism and reduces appetite; U.S. home ambient temps (mid-to-high 60s F) are too cold without a heater.
- *Picky eating / rejecting unfamiliar or low-quality food* (normal): Bettas are widely described as picky eaters that may refuse a new brand, flakes, or food they simply dislike; care guides suggest trying a different food type or brand rather than assuming illness.
- *Overfeeding / constipation / swim bladder disease* (concerning): Overeating or gulping food too fast can swell the stomach against the swim bladder, or cause constipation; symptoms include a swollen belly, stringy feces, spitting out food, lethargy, and buoyancy problems (floating/sinking, S-shaped spine). This is one of the most commonly cited causes of both appetite loss and betta death overall.
- *Illness (bacterial infection, parasites, disease)* (concerning): Diseases such as bacterial infections or parasitic infestations are named directly as causes of appetite loss in the vet-reviewed source, often alongside other symptoms like white spots (ich), clamped fins, or difficulty swimming.

**When to actually worry**
Multiple sources (DialAVet's vet-reviewed guide, plus several care/forum sources) state a healthy adult betta can physically survive up to about 14 days without food, but this is described as an emergency-only ceiling, never a target. The vet-reviewed threshold for actively investigating is 5-7 days of refused food despite intervention (trying different foods, checking/fixing water quality and temperature) — at that point, or sooner if other symptoms appear, DialAVet recommends veterinary consultation. Watch for these accompanying red flags rather than relying on days-without-eating alone: a swollen or bloated belly, stringy or absent feces, lethargy or hiding, clamped fins, white spots on the body (possible ich), an S-shaped/curved spine or visible buoyancy problems (floating at the surface or sinking to the bottom — signs of swim bladder disease), and labored or difficult swimming. One lower-authority source (a betta breeder/seller site) claims young or already-sick bettas may only safely go 3-5 days without food rather than the full 14, implying the safe window shrinks with age/health status, but this specific modifier isn't independently corroborated by the higher-authority sources consulted, so treat it as plausible rather than firmly established. For routine short absences (a few days, e.g., a weekend), an automatic feeder or a fasting day is considered normal and low-risk; the concern threshold is the week-plus mark combined with any of the physical symptoms above.

**FunFact candidate**
A betta's stomach is roughly the size of its own eyeball — a fact repeated across multiple independent sources — which is the actual basis for the "2-4 pellets per meal" portion guideline and why overfeeding is considered one of the single most common causes of death in pet bettas (via bloating, constipation, swim bladder disease, and the water-quality crashes that come from uneaten food rotting in the tank). It ties directly to their wild feeding biology: bettas evolved as surface-ambush predators with an upturned mouth built for snatching insects and larvae off the water's surface using rapid suction feeding, which is also why floating pellets suit them better than sinking food. A peer-reviewed morphology study (published via Wiley/Journal of Morphology) adds a nuance many care sites get wrong: Betta splendens is technically a facultative air-breather (it can rely on gills alone if it must), unlike its cousin the blue gourami, which is a true obligate air-breather — though in practice pet bettas still surface-gulp air regularly via their labyrinth organ.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several real disagreements surfaced and were deliberately not averaged: (1) Adult feeding frequency — Aquarium Co-Op (a well-regarded fish-specific retailer/educator) recommends once daily; PetMD and Bettafish.org say once OR twice daily; PangoVet/The Vet Desk (mirrored content across two domains, so effectively one source counted twice) recommends at least twice daily, up to three smaller meals, 6-8 hours apart. There is no single consensus number — present the range, not one figure. (2) Minimum pellet protein content — Bettafish.org states 30% minimum; Aquarium Co-Op's guide implies 40%+; both are presented as guidance, not a single verified threshold. (3) Ideal water temperature — cited as 76-81F (PetMD), 76-82F (DialAVet), and 78-82F (PangoVet); close but not identical, so use "roughly 76-82F" rather than a single exact number. (4) Days-without-food ceiling — "up to 14 days" for a healthy adult is well-repeated, but one lower-authority breeder/seller source claims young or sick bettas can only manage 3-5 days; this modifier is plausible but not corroborated by the higher-authority sources (DialAVet, PetMD, Bettafish.org) consulted, so it's flagged as less certain rather than stated as fact. (5) The 1.8-gram daily food-weight figure appears only in the PangoVet/VetDesk mirrored content and isn't corroborated by any other source checked — treat it as a soft, low-confidence data point if used at all, not a firm number.

### Koi

**Feeding schedule**
Koi feeding is driven almost entirely by water temperature (not air temperature), and multiple independent sources (Hikari, a major koi food maker; Stodels; Hydrosphere/PondExperts; Pond Informer) converge on roughly the same bands, though exact cutoff numbers vary slightly by source:

- Below ~48-50°F (9-10°C): STOP feeding entirely. Koi go into torpor (a hibernation-like dormant state) and their digestion effectively shuts down; they live off stored body fat until spring.
- ~50-59°F (10-15°C): Feed sparingly, at most 1-2x/day, wheat-germ-based "cold water" food only, ideally in the warmest part of the day (roughly 10am-2pm). One source narrows this further: 2-3x per WEEK (not per day) in the 41-50°F range, dropping to zero below 41°F.
- ~59-68°F (15-20°C): 1-2 feedings/day; digestive capacity is declining, so watch activity and cut back if fish seem sluggish.
- ~68-86°F (20-30°C), the optimal/growth range: 2-4 feedings/day (one manufacturer source says up to 4-6x/day at the top of this range on very active systems).
- Above 86-90°F (30-32°C): cut back to 1-2 smaller, easily-digested feedings/day; above ~90°F stop, as heat stress impairs digestion too.

Amount: the traditional, widely-repeated rule of thumb is the "5-minute rule" — offer only what fish visibly clean up within about 3-5 minutes (Stodels, PondExperts/Hydrosphere). A body-weight-based alternative commonly cited is roughly 1-4% of body weight per day depending on temperature and size. One koi-specific care site is explicitly critical of the 5-minute rule, arguing keepers who rely on it "consistently overfeed by 50-100%" and recommending measured body-weight rationing plus body-condition checks instead — I could not corroborate that specific overfeeding percentage elsewhere, so treat it as one source's opinion rather than an established figure.

Life stage: Baby/juvenile koi tolerate warm water and frequent feeding better than adults — multiple sources note baby koi can be fed up to 4x/day even at the highest summer temperatures where adults are capped at 1-2x/day. One care site gives a more granular size-based table (roughly 5-7% body weight/day for 2-4in koi, tapering down to 1.5-3%/day for koi over 22in), but this specific breakdown comes from only one source and should be treated as indicative rather than a universally-agreed standard.

**Diet composition**
Staple diet is commercial floating koi pellets, switched seasonally by protein content:

- Warm-season "growth/color" formula (used once water is consistently above ~60-64°F): 32-40%+ crude protein, primarily fish meal and krill meal, often with spirulina and carotenoid/astaxanthin additives (color-enhancing formulas cited in the 300-500+ mg/kg range) for growth and color.
- Cool-season "wheat germ" formula (spring/fall, roughly 50-64°F, and the only thing offered near the bottom of the feeding range): lower protein (roughly 25-32%), wheat germ as the first ingredient, ~3% fat from residual wheat germ oil, easier to digest at low metabolic rates; some formulas add vitamin C (cited around 400-500 mg/kg) and probiotics for spring recovery.

Supplemental treats (not a daily staple — cited as 2-3x/week): fruit (watermelon, orange segments/wedges, banana, berries, grapes, melon — seeds, pits, and rind removed, fed sparingly), vegetables (lettuce and spinach can be fed raw; broccoli should be cooked; peas are disputed — see caveats), and protein/meaty treats (brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, mealworms, silkworms, crickets, earthworms, shrimp, crayfish; small amounts of unseasoned cooked chicken). Vegetables/fruit are generally recommended blanched or chopped into bite-size pieces rather than fed whole. No gut-loading or calcium-dusting practice applies to koi (that's reptile-specific husbandry, not relevant here).

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat foods (2-3x/week, not as a diet replacement): watermelon, orange segments, banana, berries, grapes, melon (seeds/pits/rind removed); lettuce and spinach (can be fed raw); broccoli (cooked); brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, mealworms, silkworms, crickets, earthworms, shrimp, crayfish; small amounts of unseasoned cooked chicken.

Unsafe or problematic foods: bread and other processed human snack foods (crackers, chips) — low in nutrients, high in refined carbs, pollute pond water, and can cause digestive issues; cat or dog food — koi aren't equipped to digest animal fats/dairy well; corn, especially hard or dried — difficult to digest and a blockage risk. Fruit seeds/pits and citrus rind should always be removed before feeding.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Cold water temperature (torpor/dormancy)* (normal): Below roughly 48-50°F, koi enter a hibernation-like dormant state and their digestive system essentially shuts down; they survive on stored body fat. This is the single most common, entirely expected reason a koi stops eating each year.
- *Poor water quality* (concerning): Ammonia/nitrite spikes, pH or KH swings, or overcrowding stress koi and suppress appetite; if multiple fish stop eating at once (not just one), sources specifically flag this as a signal to test water immediately rather than assume a benign cause.
- *Stress from environment or handling* (depends): New pond introduction, predator visits (herons, raccoons), loud noise/vibration near the pond, netting, or transport can suppress feeding, generally short-term.
- *Spawning (typically late spring/early summer, ~65-75°F)* (normal): Around and after the spawning chase, appetite can dip, especially in females, who reportedly take longer than males to recover; feeding is expected to resume at normal frequency afterward.
- *Parasites or bacterial infection* (concerning): Stress-weakened immune systems open the door to parasites (e.g., gill flukes) and bacterial infections, which commonly present as reduced appetite alongside other symptoms.
- *Koi herpesvirus (KHV)* (concerning): A serious, notifiable viral disease. Symptoms include lethargy, appetite loss, isolating from the group, gasping/gathering near aerators, pale or reddened/rough skin, sunken eyes, fin erosion, and sometimes disorientation. Outbreaks and mortality are worst in a specific water-temperature window (roughly 61-77°F / 16-25°C), with mortality commonly cited at 70-100% and no available treatment.
- *Diet monotony or low-quality food* (normal): Some sources describe koi becoming disinterested in stale or poor-quality feed ("food boredom"); varying treats and food brands is the suggested fix.
- *Individual/single-fish variation* (normal): One fish skipping food for a single day, with no other symptoms, is described as not inherently concerning — normal appetite variation exists between individual koi.

**When to actually worry**
Context matters a lot here. In winter/cold water, going weeks to months without eating is by design (torpor) and not a red flag on its own. In warm water, one source notes koi can go roughly two weeks without dedicated feeding in summer because they forage naturally on algae and insects in the pond, so a few days of reduced eating in warm water isn't automatically an emergency.

Genuine warning signs, per the sources reviewed: multiple fish (not just one) refusing food simultaneously, especially with no water-temperature explanation, should prompt immediate water testing (ammonia/nitrite/pH/KH). Appetite loss that persists beyond "a few days" in warm water, or that's accompanied by sluggish movement, color loss, visible sores/ulcers, hiding behavior, sunken eyes, rough or discolored skin, gasping at the surface, or a suddenly thin/emaciated body, is described as a genuine concern warranting closer inspection and likely veterinary involvement. One source gave a concrete rule of thumb of contacting a koi veterinarian if loss of appetite persists beyond 48 hours, though this specific number was not corroborated across multiple sources, so treat it as a reasonable but not universally-agreed threshold rather than an established medical standard. Weight loss combined with lethargy is consistently flagged as a signal to involve a vet.

**FunFact candidate**
Koi have no true stomach at all. Food passes straight from a short esophagus (where throat-mounted "pharyngeal teeth" grind it up) into an elongated intestine that does double duty as digestion and absorption organ. That's precisely why cold water is dangerous for a fed koi: if a koi goes into cold-water torpor with food still in its gut, the undigested material can let harmful bacteria cross the intestinal wall into the bloodstream — which is the real biological reason keepers are told to stop feeding once water drops below the mid-40s to 50°F, not just a rule of thumb.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1. Exact cold-cutoff temperature varies by source: some say stop feeding entirely below 50°F, others put the hard stop at 48°F (with a reduced 2-3x/week wheat-germ-only period between 41-50°F). All sources agree on the general principle (stop well before ice-up, use only easily-digestible wheat-germ food as it gets cold) even though the precise degree cited differs by a few degrees.

2. Peas are genuinely disputed across sources. Several general "koi treats" roundups call peas "one of the healthiest treats" you can give. A more detailed care site (Pond Informer) instead says peas are acceptable only in very small quantities when thoroughly cooked, and separately lists hard/dried peas alongside corn as a choking/blockage risk. The disagreement seems to hinge on preparation (soft-cooked and shelled vs. hard/dried) rather than being a flat contradiction, but the site should not present peas as an uncomplicated "safe treat" without that caveat.

3. I found one low-quality-looking source (an AI-generated-style blog, oreateai.com) claiming koi can survive "up to two years" without food. This could not be corroborated by any of the koi-specific or veterinary-adjacent sources I checked and reads as an outlier/likely exaggeration, so I deliberately excluded it from the schedule/worry fields above. If this site ever wants to cite an extreme survival duration, it should not use that figure without better sourcing.

4. Protein-percentage claims for warm-season koi food varied somewhat by source/manufacturer (38% cited by one, "32-36%" by another, "40% minimum" by a third) — these aren't necessarily contradictory since they describe different specific products/brands, but I've presented it as a range (32-40%+) rather than a single hard number.

5. The specific claim that 5-minute-rule feeders "overfeed by 50-100%" and the granular size-based body-weight percentage table (5-7% down to 1.5-3% by koi length) both trace to a single source (expert.koi-life.com) whose editorial/authority background I could not independently verify, phrased in a way that reads somewhat AI-generated. ### Goldfish

**Feeding schedule**
Life-stage breakdown (sources broadly agree on this structure, though exact frequencies vary):

FRY (newly hatched): Do not feed for the first ~2 days after hatching — they're still absorbing their yolk sac. Once free-swimming, feed infusoria or baby brine shrimp (Artemia) nauplii. Frequency during the first month is cited as 3-6 times a day by different care sites (Hepper's fry guide and caringpets.org both land in this range), tapering to about 3 times a day through roughly the first 4 months, giving only what's consumed in about 2 minutes to protect water quality.

JUVENILES: 2-3 small feedings a day is the consistent recommendation (Fish Vet/cafishvet.com, PetMealGuide), reflecting higher caloric needs for growth.

ADULTS: This is where sources genuinely diverge. RSPCA's goldfish-specific guidance says feed once a day, only what's eaten in under 2 minutes. But RSPCA's own general fish-diet page recommends 2-3 times daily on a routine schedule, and PetMD, TankZen, and PetMealGuide all describe 1-2 times daily as normal, with many aquarists preferring to split into two feedings since goldfish have no stomach to store a large single meal in. There is no single settled number — treat "1-2 small meals/day" as the safe consensus range rather than a fixed rule.

PORTION SIZE: Also inconsistently specified — cited rules include "only what's eaten in 30-60 seconds" (the eye-size rule: total dry food roughly the volume of one fish eye), "under 2 minutes" (RSPCA goldfish page), and "2-5 minutes" (RSPCA general fish page, PetMD, Hepper). All sources agree on the underlying principle (feed less than you think, remove uneaten food) even though the exact minute count differs.

POND/OUTDOOR GOLDFISH: Feeding should scale down with water temperature. Above ~50°F (10°C), a normal diet is fine; between roughly 39-50°F (4-10°C), switch to a reduced, more digestible wheat-germ-based or "spring/fall" formula fed less often; below ~39-50°F (sources vary on the exact cutoff), stop feeding entirely, since fish enter a low-metabolism dormancy and uneaten food just fouls the water.

**Diet composition**
Goldfish are omnivores. A quality sinking pellet or gel food formulated specifically for goldfish (not tropical fish flake, which the RSPCA specifically warns against since nutrient profiles differ) should be the base diet. Flakes are usable, especially for smaller/younger fish, but several sources note flakes lose up to ~90% of certain vitamins within about 30 seconds of hitting water, making pellets the more nutritionally stable staple. Sinking pellets are also preferred over floating ones for fancy (round-bodied) goldfish varieties, since gulping floating food at the surface makes them prone to swallowing air, which is linked to swim bladder trouble.

Reported protein/fat targets vary by source (see caveats) but cluster around roughly 30-35% protein with modest fat, plus fiber to prevent constipation.

Supplement 2-3 times a week with:
- Blanched/softened vegetables: shelled peas (deshelled, mashed or whole), spinach, kale, lettuce, zucchini, and cucumber. Softening/blanching makes them digestible and easier to eat.
- High-protein treats in moderation: bloodworms (fresh, frozen, or freeze-dried), brine shrimp, and daphnia — RSPCA specifically cautions to feed these only in small amounts due to their high protein content relative to a goldfish's needs.
- Algae wafers.

Gut-loading and calcium dusting (common reptile/invertebrate-feeder practices) are not applicable to goldfish — nutritional balance instead comes from diet variety (pellet base + vegetables + occasional protein treats) rather than pre-loading live prey.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
SAFE treats: shelled/deshelled peas (mashed or whole, often used to relieve constipation), spinach, kale, lettuce, zucchini, cucumber (all blanched/softened before feeding), bloodworms (fresh/frozen/freeze-dried), brine shrimp, daphnia, algae wafers, and occasional small bits of orange, watermelon, or squash. Peas in particular are repeatedly recommended as a mild-fiber intervention for constipation/swim bladder issues, typically after a short fast, though one vet-authored source (Dr. Emma Chandley, BVetMed MRCVS, via Hepper) cautions peas shouldn't become a long-term food source and to check with a vet before relying on them repeatedly.

UNSAFE/to avoid: Bread (swells in the digestive tract, can cause blockage or constipation, minimal nutrition), avocado (contains persin, described as potentially deadly to fish), chocolate (theobromine, described as toxic even in tiny amounts), citrus fruits (acidity can irritate gills and disrupt tank pH), raw meat, cheese, onions, and any high-fat/high-sugar/high-salt human food. Tropical fish flake is also called out specifically (RSPCA) as inappropriate — not toxic, but nutritionally mismatched to a goldfish's needs. If contamination/toxic ingestion is suspected, a large partial water change is the commonly cited first response.

**Why they stop eating**
- *New tank syndrome / water quality problems* (concerning): Ammonia, nitrite, or nitrate spikes (a full nitrogen cycle can take up to ~3 months to establish in a new aquarium) are described across multiple sources as the single most common reason a goldfish stops eating — reported ammonia toxicity threshold cited is above roughly 0.1 ppm.
- *Overfeeding / constipation / swim bladder disorder* (concerning): Excess or low-fiber food can cause gas buildup and constipation, which presses on the swim bladder and causes appetite loss along with abnormal floating or sinking. This is largely preventable and self-inflicted through husbandry rather than illness.
- *Stress from environment or tank mates* (concerning): Moving to a new tank, incompatible or aggressive tank mates, bullying, or an undersized enclosure (adult goldfish reach roughly 8-12 inches and are commonly cited as needing at least a 20-gallon tank) are repeatedly named as stress triggers, and appetite loss is described as often the first visible sign.
- *Disease, parasites, or infection* (concerning): Ich (Ichthyophthirius multifiliis, visible as white spots), internal or external parasites, and fungal infections (cotton-like growths) are cited as causes where refusal to eat is often the earliest symptom, preceding more visible signs.
- *Low water temperature / seasonal pond dormancy* (normal): Below roughly 50°F (about 10°C), outdoor pond goldfish slow into a low-metabolism dormancy (sometimes described as a form of torpor) and naturally eat little to nothing until water warms again in spring. This is a normal seasonal pattern for outdoor/pond fish, not illness.
- *Breeding/spawning season* (normal): In late spring/early summer as temperatures rise (often cited around 68-70°F as an ideal spawning trigger), goldfish preparing to spawn — especially males engaged in chasing behavior — may temporarily go off food. This is described as a normal, temporary behavioral shift tied to reproductive hormones, not a health problem.
- *Diet monotony / picky eating* (depends): An unbalanced or overly repetitive diet lacking protein, vitamins, or plant matter (spirulina, spinach, etc.) can reduce interest in food; also, feeding too much live/treat food can make a goldfish less interested in its regular pellets.

**When to actually worry**
Sources distinguish between "safe to skip feeding for" and "can survive without food," and conflating the two is a common mistake (see caveats). For a healthy adult goldfish in an established, well-maintained tank:

- Short gaps (up to about 3-4 days) are described by Hepper's vet-adjacent feeding guide as fine without any special arrangement.
- Beyond about 5 days, sources note effects on health may start to show, and beyond roughly a week to 10 days, arranging a feeder or automatic feeder is recommended.
- Multiple sources put outright survivability (not the same as "healthy") at roughly 1-2 weeks (commonly cited as 8-14 or 10-14 days) for a healthy adult in a mature, cold-water tank, thanks to a slow cold-water metabolism — this is an upper survival bound, not a target.

Watch for these accompanying red flags rather than fixating on day-count alone — sources consistently say these, especially in combination with prolonged appetite loss, warrant an aquatic vet:
- Pronounced lethargy or apathy
- Pineconing (scales standing out, a classic dropsy warning sign)
- Clamped fins
- Floating sideways or sinking abnormally (swim bladder involvement)
- Gasping at the surface or rapid gill movement (respiratory distress, often a water-quality signal)
- Visible wounds, ulcers, white spots (ich), or cotton-like fungal growths
- A gaunt or visibly thinning body, indicating muscle/body wasting rather than just a skipped meal

If a goldfish hasn't eaten for more than roughly 24-48 hours and shows any of the above, that combination — not the fasting alone — is the actual trigger to seek veterinary/aquatic-specialist attention.

**FunFact candidate**
Goldfish have no true stomach at all — their esophagus leads directly into the intestine, so there's no internal pouch to store a large meal (documented in multiple aquatic-biology writeups, e.g. Solid Gold Aquatics' breakdown of goldfish digestive anatomy). That's the actual mechanical reason vets and care guides push small, frequent feedings over one big daily meal, and why overfeeding is so disproportionately dangerous for this species — there's nowhere for the excess to go except straight through, fast, fouling the water and stressing the swim bladder along the way. On the flip side of that slow-but-steady metabolism: two goldfish named Shaggy and Daphne famously survived 134 days completely without food or a working filter, sealed in their tank inside a Christchurch, New Zealand office building after the 2011 earthquake — a case documented by NPR and multiple other outlets, and one that aquarium curators attributed to their unusually large 26-gallon tank, tank algae, and bacterial activity rather than something typical goldfish can rely on.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several real numeric disagreements surfaced and should not be flattened into one "true" figure:

1) Adult feeding frequency: RSPCA's goldfish-specific page says feed once a day; RSPCA's own general fish-diet page instead recommends 2-3 times daily on a fixed routine; PetMD, TankZen, and PetMealGuide describe 1-2 times daily as standard. These are genuinely inconsistent even within the same organization's own materials — I presented "1-2 small meals/day" as the safe range rather than picking one.

2) "Eat within X" portion rule: cited variously as 30-60 seconds (eye-size rule), under 2 minutes (RSPCA goldfish page), and 2-5 minutes (RSPCA general fish page, PetMD, Hepper). No single number is consistently used across sources.

3) Protein/fat targets: cafishvet.com gives a goldfish-specific ~32-35% protein / 7-8% fat; a generic PetMD fish-nutrition table cited 35-45% protein for "herbivores" (an oddly high figure for an herbivore category and not goldfish-specific); other aquarist sources describe ~30% protein. I reported this as a rough 30-35% range rather than a settled figure, since the sources don't agree and some numbers appear to come from generic fish-nutrition tables rather than goldfish-specific formulations.

4) How long a goldfish can go without food: this is the most tangled figure. "Safe to leave without arranging feeding" is cited as low as 3-4 days (Hepper, non-vet-credited article) or up to ~7-10 days (other care sites) before action is needed; separately, outright survivability under good conditions is cited as 8-14 days or "1-2 weeks" by other sources. These are answering different questions (what's healthy/recommended vs. the outer survival limit) but get run together across the source ecosystem — I kept them distinct in the schedule/worry fields rather than merging them into one number. The 134-day earthquake-survival case is a well-documented, NPR-covered extreme outlier, explicitly not represented as a normal or safe benchmark by any source, including the one that mentions it (Hepper).

5) Source quality note: much of the pet-fish content ecosystem is SEO care-site material rather than primary veterinary or academic sourcing; the strongest sourcing I found was RSPCA (UK animal welfare charity, official care guidance) and one Hepper article explicitly authored/reviewed by a credentialed veterinarian (Dr. Emma Chandley, BVetMed PGCertSAS MRCVS). Where a claim came only from an uncredentialed care-blog, I noted that in-line above rather than presenting it with the same weight as the RSPCA/vet-credentialed material.

### Angelfish

**Feeding schedule**
Multiple independent care guides converge on: **adults** fed once or twice daily (Fishkeeping World frames it as "at least two feedings per day"; aqua-fish.net says "once or twice daily"), giving only what the fish clear in a short window — Fishkeeping World specifies "a pinch of food at a time, ensuring the fish eat for two whole minutes" before removing uneaten food, while aqua-fish.net gives a slightly longer "4 to 5 minutes" window. **Juveniles** get fed more often — 2 to 3 times daily per aqua-fish.net — to support faster growth. **Fry** are fed most intensively: sources agree fry don't eat for the first several days post-hatch (living off the yolk sac — TFH Magazine, Aquarium Co-op), then once free-swimming are started on live baby brine shrimp (Artemia salina). Exact frequency claims vary: aqua-fish.net cites "freshly hatched baby brine shrimp four times daily" starting day 7; another aggregated source found 2–3 feedings/day once free-swimming; general hobbyist consensus (Aquarium Co-op, Fishkeeping World) is simply "feed live baby brine shrimp several times a day" without a fixed count. Fry are gradually weaned onto crushed flake — aqua-fish.net puts this at weeks 3–4, while Fishkeeping World puts the full transition to flake/dry food at week 6+. Breeding adults being conditioned for spawning get intensified feeding — aqua-fish.net cites up to 4 times/day with high-protein foods like tubifex or bloodworms.

**Diet composition**
Angelfish are omnivores with a protein-leaning diet. Staple: high-quality flake or pellet food (Fishkeeping World notes flake is generally preferred over pellets/granules for this species). Regular protein supplementation (recommended at least twice a week by multiple sources) via live or frozen: bloodworms, brine shrimp (Artemia), daphnia ("water fleas"), mysis shrimp, and tubifex worms — though several sources specifically warn tubifex/blackworms should come from reputable cultured/commercial sources, never wild-collected, due to parasite and disease risk. Mosquito larvae are also cited (TFH Magazine) as a captive food. Plant matter in smaller amounts: blanched (briefly boiled, then cooled) spinach, zucchini, and shelled peas, used both as dietary fiber and as a remedy for bloating/constipation. Fry-specific diet: newly hatched (freshly harvested) baby brine shrimp nauplii is the universally cited first food once fry are free-swimming, prized because the nutrient-dense yolk sac of the newly hatched nauplii benefits the fry; some sources also mention powdered starter foods (e.g., "First Bites"-type products) as an alternative/supplement. No gut-loading or dusting practices apply (that's a reptile/amphibian husbandry concept, not used for fish) — the closest equivalent is timing brine shrimp harvest to feed them freshly hatched rather than after their yolk reserves deplete.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe/beneficial treats, corroborated across multiple guides: live or frozen bloodworms and brine shrimp (top-tier protein treats), mysis shrimp (larger, protein- and fiber-rich, good for adults), daphnia (also functions as a mild natural laxative — useful if a fish looks bloated), and blanched (briefly boiled, cooled) shelled peas, zucchini, or spinach offered roughly weekly for fiber and to prevent/relieve constipation and bloating. Foods to avoid or use cautiously: tubifex worms or any worms collected from unknown outdoor/wild sources, since they can carry parasites and disease — commercially cultured/frozen tubifex is treated as acceptable by the same sources that warn against wild-collected. Overfeeding generally (regardless of food type) is repeatedly named as the single most common feeding mistake, linked to poor water quality, bloating, and swim-bladder problems. Small fish/fry under roughly 2 inches (e.g., neon tetras) are natural prey for angelfish given their ambush-predator biology — not a toxicity issue, but a real husbandry pitfall when people expect angelfish to coexist peacefully with small tankmates.

**Why they stop eating**
- *New-tank/acclimation stress* (normal): Multiple hobbyist sources agree new angelfish commonly refuse food for the first 2-4 days in a new tank due to stress and unfamiliar surroundings; pH shock during acclimation is specifically flagged as a common contributor. This is considered normal within roughly the first 3-4 days.
- *Poor water quality* (concerning): Loss of appetite combined with hiding is repeatedly cited as a top indicator of degraded water conditions (ammonia/nitrite buildup, etc.) rather than illness per se.
- *Territorial/social stress from tankmates or tank rearrangement* (depends): Angelfish are territorial; aggressive tankmates or disturbing the tank layout can suppress appetite.
- *Internal parasites (Cryptobia iubilans, Spironucleus/Hexamita)* (concerning): Per the Merck Veterinary Manual, Cryptobia iubilans specifically targets cichlids (angelfish's family) and invades the stomach; affected fish can even remain 'voracious' feeders while showing extreme weight loss and a sunken belly. Spironucleus/Hexamita in cichlids cause weight loss, anorexia, and white stringy feces, with elevated mortality in fry/juveniles.
- *Picky/selective feeding on an unfamiliar new food* (depends): Hobbyist sources note newly acquired angelfish are sometimes reported as picky and may reject an unfamiliar diet before accepting it once acclimated.

**When to actually worry**
Sourcing here is mixed and should be presented with the disagreement flagged (see caveats). Aggregated blog sources put a general 'fine to fast' window at roughly 7-10 days for a healthy adult angelfish, with some hobbyist forum posts citing similar figures; a separate hobbyist care guide (aqua-fish.net) states angelfish 'can survive up to two weeks without food, but one week maximum is recommended' before it becomes a welfare concern — i.e., survival capability and a responsible recommended maximum are two different numbers and sources blur them. The concrete physical warning sign cited across sources is a sunken/pinched belly: per aggregated guidance, a sunken belly signals the fish has exhausted glycogen/fat reserves and started catabolizing muscle tissue, indicating advanced starvation that may not reverse even with refeeding. The Merck Veterinary Manual specifically documents Cryptobia iubilans, a stomach parasite common in cichlids (the family angelfish belong to), producing 'extreme weight loss, fish may be voracious, but retain a sunken belly' — i.e., a fish can keep eating and still be dying, so appetite alone isn't a reliable health check. Merck also documents Spironucleus/Hexamita in cichlids causing weight loss/anorexia plus white, stringy feces, with elevated mortality in fry and juveniles specifically. Practical guidance distilled from sources: a single day or two of refusal with otherwise normal behavior is not urgent (especially in a new/rearranged tank); refusal beyond about 4-5 days, or any refusal accompanied by a visibly sunken belly, clamped fins, lethargy, color loss, labored breathing, or stringy/white feces, warrants water-quality testing, quarantine of the affected fish, and if unresolved, consulting an aquatic veterinarian or experienced aquarist.

**FunFact candidate**
Despite their serene, ornamental reputation, angelfish are true ambush predators in the wild: TFH Magazine describes them stalking small fish and invertebrates from cover, and their laterally compressed, diamond-shaped body is a hunting adaptation that lets them slip between plant stems and root tangles to close in on prey, while their dark vertical stripes are thought to function like a zebra's stripes, camouflaging them against vegetation while they wait to strike. That predatory drive doesn't switch off in the aquarium — TFH notes captive angelfish "are gluttons and will gorge themselves on food," which is exactly why a disciplined feeding schedule (small portions, cleared within a couple of minutes) matters more for this species than simply offering the right foods.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two real disagreements worth flagging rather than averaging: (1) How long a healthy adult angelfish can safely go without food — one hobbyist guide (aqua-fish.net) states survival "up to two weeks" with "one week maximum recommended," while other aggregated sources cite roughly 7-10 days as the generally-fine window; these aren't the same claim (survival ceiling vs. responsible practice), and none of these sources are peer-reviewed veterinary literature, so treat any specific day-count as a rough hobbyist consensus, not an established clinical threshold. (2) The timeline for weaning angelfish fry off live baby brine shrimp onto flake/dry food varies by source: aqua-fish.net says fry accept crushed flake by weeks 3-4 (with the fry still not tank-ready until week 9), while Fishkeeping World puts the full transition to flake/dry food at week 6+, coinciding with when it says fry are large enough for a community tank. Additionally, exact fry feeding frequency (4x/day vs 2-3x/day) differs between sources depending on the fry's exact age/stage, and no source in this research was a peer-reviewed study specific to captive angelfish feeding (a relevant peer-reviewed paper on first-feeding rate/frequency in angelfish fry exists on ScienceDirect but was paywalled and could not be verified for specific figures, so its numbers were not used here). Most of the "not eating" and feeding-schedule guidance comes from hobbyist care-guide sites and forums rather than veterinary literature; the Merck Veterinary Manual entries used here are the one clinically authoritative source and were cited only for disease-specific appetite-loss mechanisms, not for routine feeding-frequency numbers.

### Guppy

**Feeding schedule**
Sources broadly agree on the shape of a guppy feeding schedule but give somewhat different frequency numbers, so ranges are presented rather than one figure.

FRY (newborn to roughly 2-4 weeks): Highest feeding frequency of any life stage because of tiny stomach capacity and rapid growth. Aquarium Co-Op (a well-regarded LFS/care-guide source) recommends increasing fry feedings to "three to five times a day" with smaller portions each time to avoid fouling the water. Other hobbyist sources push this higher, citing feeding every 2-3 hours, or 4-6 times daily, especially in the first few days when fry can only take infusoria-sized (very fine, liquid or powdered) foods. FishLab similarly describes feeding fry "every 2-3 hours."

JUVENILE (roughly 1 to 3-4 months, until they reach adult size/sexual maturity): Aggregated hobbyist guidance places this at 3-4 meals a day, more frequent and higher-protein than adult feeding, tapering down as fish approach adult size around 3-4 months old. This life-stage breakout was less consistently sourced than fry/adult figures, so treat the exact number as approximate.

ADULT: Sources give a range here rather than one number. Aquarium Co-Op says "once or twice a day." PetMD says "one to three times daily, depending on their size." FishLab suggests "2-3 times daily." All sources agree on the same portion rule regardless of frequency: feed only what the fish can fully consume within about 1-2 minutes (PetMD/Aquarium Co-Op say 1-2 minutes; FishLab says 2-3 minutes; a couple of hobbyist sites say as little as 30-60 seconds). Overfeeding, not underfeeding, is described repeatedly as the more common real-world mistake with guppies because they beg for food regardless of hunger.

**Diet composition**
Guppies are omnivores. As pets, the base diet should be a high-quality tropical fish flake or small pellet formulated for community/tropical fish (not goldfish food, which has a different nutrient profile). On top of that base, sources consistently recommend rotating in protein-rich live, frozen, or freeze-dried foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, mosquito larvae, tubifex worms, and microworms (the latter especially for fry). Vegetable matter should also be offered, typically blanched and cut small: spinach, zucchini, cucumber, peas, lettuce, cabbage, broccoli, plus algae wafers or dried seaweed; guppies will also naturally graze on algae growing in the tank. Small amounts of cooked egg yolk, boiled beef heart, or plain cooked chicken (fat removed) are mentioned by some sources as occasional protein treats.

Frozen foods should be thawed before feeding (never microwaved) and any leftover thawed food should not be refrozen, per PetMD, since that encourages bacterial growth. Fry require smaller, higher-protein foods than adults: infusoria or liquid fry food in the first few days (their mouths are too small for flake), moving on to newly hatched baby brine shrimp and microworms as they grow past roughly one to two weeks old. Because guppies have small mouths with teeth set toward the back, flake or finely crushed pellet generally works better for them than large pellets.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat/supplement items named specifically across sources: live, frozen, or freeze-dried brine shrimp and daphnia; bloodworms; mosquito larvae; tubifex worms; microworms (fry); blanched peas, zucchini, cucumber, and spinach; algae wafers; small amounts of boiled egg yolk.

Foods to avoid, named specifically: goldfish food (formulated for a different, lower-protein diet and considered nutritionally mismatched for guppies); bread, cereal, and other processed human foods (contain additives with no nutritional value for fish and foul the water); vegetables with jelly-like/seedy cores like tomato or cucumber centers, which some sources say should have the center scooped out before feeding; spoiled or expired fish food, which guppies will typically nibble then reject; and, generally, any food item too large for a guppy's small mouth. The single most-repeated "unsafe" behavior across every source, more than any specific food, is overfeeding itself — described as the most common real husbandry mistake with guppies, causing constipation, swim bladder problems, and water-quality crashes from uneaten food rotting in the tank.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Impending birth / late pregnancy* (normal): Female guppies (livebearers) often eat less or refuse food entirely in the day(s) immediately before giving birth. Sources attribute this to reduced stomach capacity from a belly full of developing fry and the female's focus shifting to finding a secluded spot to deliver. Multiple hobbyist sources note appetite typically returns quickly after birth, and that skipping a single meal in this context is not itself a red flag as long as the female otherwise looks and behaves normally.
- *Stress from environment or social dynamics* (depends): New-tank relocation, tank cleaning/maintenance, aggressive or incompatible tankmates, and an imbalanced male-to-female ratio (sources recommend roughly 1 male per 2-3 females to reduce mating-chase stress) are all cited as common stress triggers that suppress appetite. This is treated as normal in origin but worth fixing at the root cause rather than ignoring indefinitely.
- *Poor water quality / parameter swings* (concerning): Ammonia or nitrite spikes, incorrect pH, and low oxygenation are repeatedly cited causes of appetite loss and lethargy. This is flagged as a genuine concern requiring water testing and partial water changes, not something to wait out.
- *Water temperature outside the ideal range (cold stress in particular)* (concerning): Guppies are tropical fish; sources give an ideal range of roughly 72-82°F, with a vet-reviewed source (Hepper) narrowing the 'best' steady range to about 73-79°F (23-26°C). As ectotherms, their metabolism slows in cold water, which multiple sources link directly to lethargy and loss of appetite, plus weakened immune function and higher disease susceptibility. One source states temperatures sustained well below the ~72°F baseline for more than about two days put guppies at serious risk.
- *Illness or disease* (concerning): Conditions named specifically include Ich (white-spot disease), Velvet disease, bacterial infections, and dropsy (fluid buildup/swollen 'pinecone' appearance linked to liver or kidney dysfunction). These are consistently flagged as concerning and are usually accompanied by other visible signs, not appetite loss alone.
- *Picky eating / rejecting unfamiliar or wrong-textured food* (normal): Guppies may reject food that's an unfamiliar type, texture, or too large for their small, rear-set teeth and mouth. Sources describe this as a normal, easily corrected issue: switch food types or crush pellets smaller.
- *Low-quality or expired food* (depends): Stale, spoiled, or nutritionally poor/monotonous food may be nibbled and then refused. Framed as a diet-management issue rather than a health emergency.

**When to actually worry**
There is no single, universally agreed number across sources for exactly how long a guppy can safely go without food, and this is worth flagging rather than presenting as settled (see caveats). The clearest concrete threshold found comes from PetMD, a vet-affiliated source, which advises contacting a veterinarian if a guppy shows decreased appetite lasting more than one day. Hobbyist and care-site sources are generally looser: several explicitly say that a guppy skipping a meal, or even going a day without eating, is not itself cause for alarm if the fish otherwise looks and behaves normally (active swimming, normal color, no other symptoms), especially around a known trigger like a female's pregnancy or a recent tank disturbance.

What multiple sources agree should prompt real concern regardless of exact duration: appetite loss combined with lethargy, hiding, or unnatural/erratic swimming; gasping at the water surface; clamped, swollen, or discolored fins; cloudy or swollen eyes; visible injuries; white spots (possible Ich) or a swollen, pinecone-like body (possible dropsy); or any of these spreading to other fish in the tank, since bacterial outbreaks are described as capable of killing an entire tank population within days if untreated. If a guppy is refusing food and showing any of these accompanying signs, sources agree that's a signal to test water parameters immediately and consider veterinary or fish-health-specialist consultation rather than waiting.

**FunFact candidate**
A peer-reviewed study of wild Trinidadian guppies (Poecilia reticulata) found that gut length physically adapts to diet: guppies eating more plant matter/detritus developed proportionally longer intestines than guppies eating more invertebrate prey, and this relationship held consistently across different wild populations. Even more strikingly, guppy populations from high-predation versus low-predation stream environments maintained different relative gut lengths even when eating the same seasonal diet, suggesting their digestive anatomy reflects evolutionary history as much as day-to-day food choice. It's a reminder that "just a feeder fish" guppies are actually a classic model species in evolutionary biology, and that the same flexibility that let them evolve differently across Trinidad's streams is part of why they tolerate a varied captcaptive diet so well.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two real disagreements surfaced across sources, both worth stating as ranges rather than single settled figures:

(1) Adult feeding frequency: Aquarium Co-Op says once or twice daily; PetMD says one to three times daily; FishLab says two to three times daily. These aren't contradictory so much as a spread with no single consensus number — but a site presenting "feed twice a day" as the definitive rule would be overstating the certainty here. All sources DO agree on the portion rule (feed only what's cleared in roughly 1-2 minutes).

(2) "How long without food is okay": PetMD's vet-affiliated guidance (contact a vet if decreased appetite lasts more than one day) is notably stricter than hobbyist/care-site consensus, which repeatedly frames a guppy skipping a single meal or even a full day (particularly a late-pregnancy female) as not inherently concerning if no other symptoms are present. This is a real gap between a clinical/vet-oriented source and hobbyist experience, not a case where one side is simply wrong, and both framings should probably be represented rather than picking one as "the" answer, given this site was previously corrected for presenting a disputed figure as settled fact.

Life-stage feeding numbers for "juvenile" guppies specifically (as opposed to fry or full adults) were the least consistently sourced across searches, drawing mostly from aggregated hobbyist care sites rather than a vet or academic source, so that figure (3-4 meals/day) should be treated as a softer, lower-confidence data point than the fry and adult figures.

## Small Mammals

### Rabbit

**Feeding schedule**
Life stages and sources differ slightly on exact cutoffs, but the general arc, drawn mainly from the House Rabbit Society (houserabbit.org/diet) and PetMD:

- Birth to ~7 weeks (nursing kits): Mother's milk only at first. Notably, doe rabbits nurse far less often than most mammals — typically once, rarely twice, a day for only about 2-10 minutes per session, usually at dawn or dusk (rabbit milk is unusually rich in fat/protein, so kits can go roughly a day between feedings). Some sources note newborns in the first few days may nurse more often. Kits are usually weaned by around 8 weeks.
- 3-7 weeks (transition/"teenage"): Alfalfa hay and alfalfa-based pellets are introduced with unrestricted (free-choice) access alongside nursing. New greens are typically introduced starting around 12 weeks in small amounts (roughly 1/2 oz to start).
- 7 weeks-7 months (juvenile): Unlimited alfalfa hay and alfalfa-based pellets (higher calorie/protein/calcium supports growth). Vegetables are added gradually from about 3 months old, one new item at a time to watch for soft stool.
- ~4-6 months (transition to adult diet): Alfalfa is phased out in favor of grass hays (timothy, orchard, oat, brome), and pellet portions are reduced over several weeks (one source describes cutting pellets ~10%/week over 4-6 weeks) as unlimited grass hay takes over as the diet base.
- Adult (7 months/6 months+ depending on source, through ~5 years): Unlimited grass hay (should be ~80-85% of total diet, sources vary slightly: Petco says "at least 80%," rabbit.org/HRS say 85%). Measured pellets — commonly cited as roughly 1/8 to 1/4 cup of high-fiber (18%+ fiber) timothy-based pellets per 5-6 lbs of body weight per day; exact ratios vary by source (Petco: 1/4 cup per 6 lbs; PetMD: 1/4 cup per 5 lbs; furric.com: ~25-30g/1/8 cup for an adult). Vegetables: sources also disagree on quantity — House Rabbit Society/Petco say a minimum 2 cups chopped vegetables per 6 lbs body weight/day (at least 3 varieties of leafy greens); rabbit.org gives 1-2 cups per 4 lbs; PetMD gives 2-4 cups per 6 lbs. Fruit/treats are capped low across all sources — roughly 1-2 tbsp (or ~1 tsp per 2 lbs body weight) once or twice a week, not daily.
- Senior (6+ years): Unlimited grass hay continues; alfalfa hay/pellets may be reintroduced if the rabbit is underweight or has trouble maintaining condition; House Rabbit Society lists a higher treat allowance (~2 oz) for seniors, reflecting more individualized, vet-guided feeding at this stage.

Fresh water must be available at all times (bottle or bowl) in every stage.

**Diet composition**
Hay is the foundation of a pet rabbit's diet at every adult life stage — unlimited grass hay (timothy, orchard, oat, meadow, or brome) should be available 24/7, per House Rabbit Society (houserabbit.org/diet) and rabbit.org (rabbit.org/care/food-diet). Alfalfa hay is higher in protein and calcium and is reserved for babies, juveniles, pregnant/nursing does, and underweight seniors — not healthy adults, because excess calcium and calories can contribute to obesity, bladder sludge/stones, and other problems.

Pellets should be a fresh, high-fiber (minimum ~18% fiber per rabbit.org, some sources cite 22%+ as ideal), plain timothy-based pellet — not the colorful mixes with seeds, nuts, corn, or dried fruit, which are high in carbohydrates/fat and associated with obesity, dental disease, and GI upset. Pellets are a measured supplement for adults, not free-fed (unlike hay).

Vegetables should be fresh leafy greens as the bulk of the "fresh" portion — romaine, cilantro, parsley, basil, arugula, bok choy, spring mix, carrot tops — with at least 3 different types offered daily and new items introduced one at a time to watch for soft stool. Non-leafy vegetables (bell pepper, cucumber, zucchini) and carrots are fed in smaller, limited amounts due to higher sugar/starch content.

Fruit functions purely as a treat (sugar content), fed in small amounts a couple times a week at most, not daily.

No gut-loading or calcium-dusting applies here (that's a reptile/insect-feeder practice) — the closest equivalent husbandry step is gradual diet transitions (new pellet brands, new hay types, new vegetables) to avoid disrupting the cecal gut flora and triggering soft stool or GI stasis.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (small amounts, not daily staples): fresh herbs like parsley, cilantro, basil, mint; small pieces of apple (no seeds), banana, strawberry, or blueberry; bell pepper, cucumber, zucchini; carrots in strictly limited amounts (high in sugar relative to their reputation as a rabbit food). Fruit and carrots should be treated as occasional treats, not daily vegetables, per rabbit.org and House Rabbit Society.

Unsafe/toxic foods, per rabbit.org, Oxbow Animal Health (oxbowanimalhealth.com/blog/foods-rabbits-should-never-eat), and rabbitholehay.com: avocado (contains persin, which can cause respiratory distress and fatal heart failure in rabbits), chocolate (theobromine/caffeine toxicity), onions and other allium-family vegetables like garlic and chives (can cause hemolytic anemia), rhubarb (high oxalic acid, linked to kidney damage), and iceberg lettuce (low nutritional value and contains lactucarium, which can cause digestive upset in quantity). Also avoid processed human foods that are sometimes mistakenly given as "treats" — bread, crackers, cookies, cereal, pasta, and notably yogurt drops/yogurt-based treats, which are explicitly called out by rabbit.org despite being sold commercially as rabbit treats; they're high in sugar/fat and inappropriate for a rabbit's digestive system. Nuts and seeds should also be avoided for the same high-fat/high-carbohydrate reasons that make seed-mix pellets a problem.

**Why they stop eating**
- *GI (gastrointestinal) stasis* (concerning): A slowdown or stoppage of normal gut motility — described by rabbit.org (rabbit.org/care/gi-stasis) and PetMD as one of the most common life-threatening emergencies in pet rabbits. It's often the downstream result of low-fiber diet, dehydration, pain, or stress rather than a standalone 'cause,' but once it starts it becomes self-perpetuating and dangerous on its own.
- *Dental disease (overgrown teeth/molar spurs)* (concerning): Rabbit teeth grow continuously; malocclusion or spurs can cut the mouth or make chewing painful, causing a rabbit to reduce or stop eating — cited by PDSA and rabbitholehay.com as one of the most frequent underlying causes of appetite loss, and one that can develop silently before appetite loss is even noticed.
- *Stress from environmental change* (depends): New housing, a new pet in the household, loud noises, or handling changes are repeatedly cited (PDSA, rabbitholehay.com) as real triggers for reduced appetite in rabbits — described as a 'major cause' even when the trigger seems minor to the owner. It can resolve on its own once the stressor is removed, but if unaddressed it can progress into full anorexia and GI stasis.
- *Abrupt diet change* (depends): Switching hay, pellet brand, or introducing new vegetables too quickly can disrupt the cecal bacteria balance and cause a rabbit to go off food or develop soft stool/diarrhea — rabbit.org and several vet sources recommend all diet transitions be gradual for this reason.
- *Pain from an unrelated illness* (concerning): Urinary tract disease, gas, respiratory infection, or reproductive disease (e.g., uterine issues in unspayed does) can all cause secondary appetite loss, per PDSA and PetMD's GI stasis overview.
- *Fur ingestion during a heavy molt* (depends): Rabbits groom themselves and swallow fur; during a heavy shed this can combine with dehydrated gut contents to form a hard-to-pass mass, which several sources link to GI slowdown. Regular brushing during molts is the standard preventive advice.
- *Certain medications (e.g., opioids, some antibiotics)* (concerning): PetMD notes some drugs used to treat rabbits for other conditions can themselves slow GI motility and reduce appetite as a side effect.

**When to actually worry**
All sources agree on the core point even where they disagree on the exact clock: because rabbits are obligate continuous grazers whose gut relies on constant food throughput, any noticeable drop in eating or in fecal output should be treated as urgent, not "wait and see." PetMD states it's abnormal for a rabbit to go more than about 8 hours without eating or producing droppings, and that fasting beyond roughly 24 hours risks hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver). House Rabbit Society and the Minnesota Companion Rabbit Society describe 12+ hours with no food or feces as an outright emergency. Some vet-facing sources (cited via rabbitholehay.com and a UK exotics source) set the alarm even earlier, at 3-6 hours. PDSA (a UK veterinary charity) doesn't give a specific hour count but says to contact a vet immediately at the first sign of reduced eating, framing any appetite drop — not just total refusal — as a reason to call.

Practical takeaway for owners, per these sources: don't wait through a full day hoping it resolves — same-day (same few hours, ideally) vet contact is the safe default the moment a rabbit skips a meal or stops producing normal fecal pellets. Warning signs that make it more urgent, regardless of the exact hour count: no fecal pellets at all, or pellets that are small, misshapen, or strung together with mucus; a hunched/tense posture; audible tooth grinding (bruxism, a pain sign); a bloated or tense abdomen; lethargy; drooling or a wet chin; and reduced water intake. PetMD notes roughly 70% of rabbits recover with prompt veterinary treatment, but severe or delayed cases can require days to weeks of hospitalization, and rabbit.org cites at least one case where normal stool didn't return for 14 days even with treatment — reinforcing that early intervention matters.

**FunFact candidate**
Rabbits practice cecotrophy (a form of coprophagy): they produce two distinct types of droppings — the familiar dry, round fecal pellets, and separate soft, glistening cecotropes formed in the cecum, usually produced and eaten directly from the anus during early morning/nighttime hours. Per GuineaDad's rabbit-specific breakdown (guineadad.com/blogs/bunnydad-blogs/rabbit-poop-cecotropes), cecotropes carry roughly twice the protein and half the fiber of regular pellets, plus B vitamins and vitamin K that weren't fully absorbed on the food's first pass through the gut. This isn't optional or gross-out behavior — it's a genuine second-pass digestion step, and experiments that physically prevented rabbits from eating their cecotropes resulted in malnutrition, confirming the behavior is nutritionally essential rather than incidental.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several concrete numbers genuinely disagree across otherwise credible sources, and none should be presented as the single settled figure:

1. Emergency timeframe for not eating: sources range from 3-4 hours ("red flag," per a UK exotics vet source cited via rabbitholehay.com) to 8 hours (PetMD) to 12 hours (House Rabbit Society/rabbit.org and Minnesota Companion Rabbit Society). All agree the safe move is to treat any missed meal as urgent rather than waiting, but the specific hour count to publish should be framed as a range/"vets differ," not a single number.

2. Daily pellet amount for adults: cited variously as ~1/4 cup per 6 lbs body weight (Petco), ~1/4 cup per 5 lbs (PetMD), and ~25-30g (~1/8 cup) flat for an adult (furric.com) — roughly consistent in the same ballpark but not identical, and dependent on the rabbit's actual weight, which no source substitutes for.

3. Daily vegetable amount for adults: ranges from 1-2 cups per 4 lbs body weight (rabbit.org) to a minimum 2 cups per 6 lbs (House Rabbit Society/Petco) to 2-4 cups per 6 lbs (PetMD) — again broadly consistent but not identical; publishing a single "X cups" figure without the per-body-weight caveat would overstate precision.

4. Hay as percentage of diet: "at least 80%" (Petco) vs. "85%" (rabbit.org, House Rabbit Society) — a minor but real discrepancy.

Given this site was previously corrected for presenting a disputed figure as settled fact, I'd recommend presenting feeding amounts as ranges tied to body weight with an explicit "check with your vet/rabbit-savvy vet for your rabbit's exact needs" rather than picking one source's number.

### Hamster

**Feeding schedule**
Adults: Sources genuinely disagree on delivery method. VCA Animal Hospitals and Vets4Pets both describe pelleted food as something that "should remain continuously available" / left out for free-choice feeding, since hamsters are natural hoarders and rarely overeat pellets. Other feeding-chart sources (TheVetDesk/PangoVet) instead describe a twice-daily measured feeding of a set ration. Daily amount estimates also vary by source: roughly 2 tablespoons/day of pelleted mix for a standard-size hamster and about 1 tablespoon/day for dwarf hamsters per one chart, versus a broader ⅛–⅓ cup/day range given elsewhere — treated here as an approximate range rather than a precise figure. PDSA gives a firmer number for fresh extras: about 1 teaspoon of fresh vegetables per day, plus roughly 1 teaspoon of fruit about 3 times a week (AnimalLama similarly says "a teaspoon or two a day" of fresh produce). VCA caps total fresh food (veg + fruit) at under 10% of the diet, and treats (seeds, nuts, pasta, unsalted popcorn, whole-grain crackers) at 1-2 times a week and under 5-10% of total diet.

Babies/juveniles: Weaning timing varies by source/species — Chewy states hamster pups nurse for up to about 3 weeks with independent solid-food eating beginning around week 3; another source distinguishes dwarf hamsters as typically weaned around 3 weeks versus Syrians nursing to roughly 26-28 days, with all species fully weaned and self-sufficient by 4-5 weeks. Pups begin using their teeth on small seeds around day 5, and solid food (soaked, easily accessible) is introduced as the mother starts discouraging nursing around 2-3 weeks. Orphaned/hand-reared pups (per Chewy) are fed a warmed evaporated-milk/water or puppy-formula mixture by syringe as often as every 30-60 minutes under 2 weeks old, tapering as weaning approaches — this is rescue/emergency guidance, not typical for owners with a nursing mother present. Pregnant/nursing females were not covered with specific ration increases in the sources found.

**Diet composition**
Staple: a nutritionally complete species-appropriate hamster pellet or "lab block" — RSPCA, PDSA, and VCA all specify pellets (or a properly formulated seed mixture per RSPCA) over loose "muesli-style" mixes, because hamsters selectively eat the sugary/fatty bits from mixes and leave the fiber-rich pieces, which PDSA links to dental problems and obesity. VCA explicitly warns against a strict seed-only diet as too high in fat and nutritionally poor.

Fresh additions: small daily amounts of fresh vegetables (broccoli, cucumber, bell peppers, kale, spinach, peas, cauliflower, sweetcorn, courgette/zucchini, pumpkin, squash, celery, asparagus, carrots per various sources) plus occasional fruit (apple with seeds removed, banana, melon, pear, peach, tomato). RSPCA also endorses cleaned root vegetables and apple in moderation. Hay (e.g., timothy) is recommended by several vet sources, including VCA, to encourage chewing that wears down continuously growing teeth, though it's a supplement rather than the diet base (unlike rabbits/guinea pigs).

Protein/enrichment treats: PDSA notes hamsters are omnivores that eat insects in the wild, and recommends protein-rich treats like mealworms; other sources add boiled/unseasoned egg, small amounts of nuts and seeds, unsalted popcorn, plain pasta, and whole-grain crackers as occasional (1-2x/week) treats. No gut-loading or calcium-dusting protocol applies here — that's reptile/insectivore-feeder terminology, not relevant to a hamster's diet.

Feeding style: Multiple sources (RSPCA, AnimalLama) recommend scattering or hiding food around the cage rather than only using a bowl, to encourage natural foraging/hoarding behavior. Fresh water must be constantly available via a valveless sipper-tube bottle, checked daily for leaks/blockages (RSPCA, VCA).

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (in moderation): mealworms and boiled/unseasoned egg (protein, reflecting hamsters' omnivorous wild diet per PDSA); small amounts of nuts and seeds, plain pasta, unsalted popcorn, whole-grain crackers (VCA, 1-2x/week, under 5-10% of diet); fresh vegetables such as broccoli, cucumber, bell peppers, kale, spinach, peas, cauliflower, sweetcorn, zucchini, pumpkin, squash, celery, carrots, asparagus; fresh fruit such as apple (seeds removed), banana, melon, pear, peach, tomato; herbs like parsley and sage; timothy hay for chewing/dental wear.

Unsafe/toxic foods, named specifically: grapes and rhubarb (RSPCA explicitly flags both as poisonous to rodents); raw onion and raw garlic (contain thiosulphate, linked to hemolytic anemia and digestive upset — though several sources note cooked, small amounts are considered lower-risk, which is a nuance worth being cautious about rather than fully endorsing); chocolate, especially dark chocolate (theobromine toxicity); bitter almonds (contain trace cyanide compounds — distinct from common sweet almonds, a frequent source of confusion); raw kidney beans (toxic raw); raw potato and eggplant (contain solanine); avocado (very high fat and acidic); pork and other fatty/salty meats; sugary processed treats (dental disease, obesity, diabetes risk); unwashed produce (pesticide residue); spices/seasoning (GI upset).

Disputed/nuanced: citrus fruit. RSPCA-aligned and Vets4Pets-style guidance lists citrus among foods to avoid due to acidity; a specialist hamster-food resource (AnimalLama) instead argues there's no solid evidence citrus is unsafe and that occasional citrus is fine for Syrian hamsters, while still recommending caution for diabetes-prone dwarf species because of sugar content. Treat citrus as a genuinely disputed "avoid to be safe" item rather than settled fact.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Cold-induced torpor* (depends): Below roughly 65°F (18°C), hamsters can enter torpor, a hibernation-like state with lowered body temperature, minimal activity, and reduced or absent eating/drinking; a restricted food supply reportedly makes torpor bouts deeper. It can look like the hamster has died. Warming the animal gradually (moving the cage somewhere warm, or body heat) is the first response; sources note this is distinct from true hibernation, which pet hamsters do not undergo.
- *Dental problems (overgrown or broken teeth)* (concerning): Cited by SmallPetExpert as the single most common cause of appetite refusal in hamsters — since their incisors grow continuously, overgrowth or injury makes chewing painful, so the hamster avoids food even when hungry.
- *Wet tail (bacterial intestinal infection)* (concerning): A serious diarrheal illness, especially dangerous in young or recently weaned hamsters, that can be fatal within about 48 hours if untreated; appetite loss is an early sign alongside a soiled, wet hindquarters.
- *Stress from a new environment, handling, or cage changes* (normal): Temporary appetite reduction tied to environmental stress or unfamiliar surroundings is described as a lower-urgency, normal cause in the SmallPetExpert breakdown, as opposed to illness-driven refusal.
- *Respiratory infection or other illness* (concerning): General sickness, pain, or infection is repeatedly cited as a driver of appetite loss and typically comes with other symptoms like labored breathing, discharge, or lethargy.
- *Diabetes in diabetes-prone dwarf species* (concerning): Campbell's (and Campbell's hybrids) and Chinese dwarf hamsters have a documented high susceptibility to diabetes; sugary fruit/treats don't cause it outright but can exacerbate risk in predisposed individuals, and it's listed as a specific cause of appetite/energy changes in dwarf hamsters.
- *Old age* (depends): Reduced appetite is noted as more common in older hamsters (roughly 1.5+ years, given hamsters' short ~2-3 year lifespan), though it still warrants a vet check to rule out treatable underlying causes rather than being dismissed as "just aging."
- *Picky eating / stale or unpalatable food / sudden diet change* (normal): RSPCA specifically warns against sudden dietary changes and stale food as digestive upsetters; a hamster may also simply be sorting through a bowl for preferred items in a muesli-style mix rather than truly refusing to eat.
- *Cheek pouch impaction* (concerning): Uncommon, but soft/moist foods can clump and lodge in a cheek pouch so it can't empty properly; signs include one-sided pouching, persistent swelling, face-pawing, drooling, and sudden disinterest in collecting food. Requires professional (vet) treatment.

**When to actually worry**
Sources give slightly different hour-thresholds but converge on urgency. One vet-content source frames it in tiers: 6-12 hours without eating while otherwise active is worth watching; 12-24 hours without eating, or any refusal combined with lethargy, a hunched posture, or diarrhea, warrants contacting a vet; 24+ hours without any food intake is described as a critical situation; and beyond roughly 48 hours the situation is considered life-threatening, since hamsters have fast metabolisms and the body starts metabolizing muscle tissue as organ function declines. A separate source states more simply that a healthy adult hamster "should not go more than 24 hours without food" as the baseline rule of thumb.

Important nuance: because hamsters hoard food in cheek pouches and cache it around their enclosure, an owner may not directly witness eating for a day or more even though the hamster is fine and quietly eating from its stash — some sources note hamsters can go 3-4 days relying on a pre-provisioned hoard (e.g., if an owner leaves extra food before a short trip). That's different from a hamster that is visibly refusing available fresh food while showing other symptoms — the latter is the true emergency.

Concrete red flags that mean "see a vet now" regardless of exact hours elapsed: labored breathing or clicking/wheezing sounds, wet/soiled hindquarters (wet tail — can be fatal within about 48 hours untreated), discharge from eyes or nose, drooling, a swollen abdomen, seizures, noticeable weight loss, or a hunched/lethargic posture. If a hamster is unresponsive to gentle warming and stimulation (ruling out cold-induced torpor), that also requires immediate veterinary care.

**FunFact candidate**
A hamster's cheek pouches can expand to hold roughly 20% of its own body weight in food, stretching out past the sides of its head when full — which is how hamsters haul seeds, veg, and other foraged food back to underground/burrow storage caches rather than eating everything on the spot.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Feeding delivery method (free-choice vs. scheduled): VCA Animal Hospitals and Vets4Pets describe pelleted food as something that should be continuously/free-choice available, since hamsters rarely overeat and naturally hoard surplus; other feeding-chart style sources (TheVetDesk/PangoVet content) instead describe twice-daily measured feedings with per-day amount targets (~2 tbsp for standard hamsters, ~1 tbsp for dwarfs, or alternatively ⅛-⅓ cup/day). These are not fully reconcilable "correct" numbers — daily amount and exact delivery method vary meaningfully by source, hamster size/species, and individual metabolism, so I've presented both approaches rather than picking one as definitive.

2) Weaning age: sources give somewhat different timelines — one describes Syrian hamsters nursing until about 26-28 days versus dwarf hamsters weaned around 3 weeks, while another (Chewy) more generally describes nursing "up to 3 weeks" with independent eating starting around week 3 for hamsters broadly, and all species considered fully weaned by 4-5 weeks. Treat the exact day-count as approximate/species-variable rather than a single fixed number.

3) Citrus fruit safety: genuinely disputed, see safeUnsafeFoods field above — some vet-adjacent orgs list it as unsafe/avoid, a specialist hamster-diet resource argues it's fine occasionally for Syrians with no solid evidence of harm, while flagging extra caution for diabetes-prone dwarf breeds due to sugar rather than citrus itself.

4) Onion/garlic: most sources call raw onion/garlic toxic (thiosulphate, hemolytic anemia risk), but at least one source claimed hamsters "can eat cooked garlic and onion" with even "some health benefits." Given the toxicity risk is well-documented and the cooked-safety claim is comparatively thin, I'd treat raw onion/garlic as unsafe with high confidence and the cooked exception as unverified/lower-confidence — worth a cautious, not confident, mention if included at all.

5) "How long can a hamster go without food" carries two different numbers depending on context: ~24 hours as a general "don't let this happen" threshold for a hamster with no food access at all, versus hamsters surviving 3-4 days when relying on a pre-existing hoarded food cache while an owner is briefly away. These describe different situations (true food deprivation vs. unobserved eating from a stash) and should not be conflated into one number.

### Guinea Pig

**Feeding schedule**
Guinea pigs are near-constant grazers, so continuous access matters more than a fixed number of "meals." Fresh grass hay (timothy, orchard, oat/meadow) should be unlimited and always available for every life stage — sources differ slightly on exact share of diet (Oxbow says "at least 70%," a PetMD/Chewy care sheet says "80%") but agree hay should never run out. A measured daily portion of guinea-pig-specific fortified pellets is given once a day, typically in the morning, for the animal to graze on (PetMD/Chewy); Oxbow instead recommends feeding an age-specific formula (Young, Adult, or Senior) daily without giving a universal amount. Fresh vegetables/greens are offered as a "daily small handful" (AEMV), any time of day, with uneaten portions pulled after roughly 30 minutes (AEMV) to a couple of hours (PetMD/Chewy) to avoid spoilage. Treats, including fruit, should stay under about 10% of the daily diet (Pet Advocacy Network). A vitamin C source must be supplied every single day since guinea pigs can't store or synthesize it.

By life stage: Babies are precocial — born fully furred with eyes open and teeth erupted — and start nibbling solid hay, pellets, and vegetables within roughly 6–12 hours of birth even while still nursing (Furry Critter Network); they're typically fully weaned off nursing between about 3 and 5 weeks old (Hutch Happiness; Furry Critter Network says 3–4 weeks). Juveniles (roughly under 6 months) do best with alfalfa hay added alongside or instead of grass hay because its extra protein/calcium supports growth (AEMV; Oxbow; Pet Advocacy Network). Adults should eat grass hay only as their hay staple — alfalfa is not meant for regular adult feeding since the extra calcium/protein can contribute to obesity and other problems; PetMD/Chewy says to avoid it outright for adults, while Oxbow is softer, allowing it "only as an occasional treat." Pregnant, nursing, senior, or ill guinea pigs benefit from alfalfa hay and need meaningfully more vitamin C — about 2 to 3 times a healthy adult's requirement, per the AEMV veterinary fact sheet.

**Diet composition**
The staple is grass hay (timothy, orchard, oat/meadow), fed unlimited. It isn't just filler — it supplies the long-strand fiber that keeps digestion moving and the abrasive chewing that wears down teeth that never stop growing (VCA; AEMV; Oxbow). Alfalfa hay is a separate, richer category (more protein/calcium) reserved for babies, juveniles, pregnant/nursing sows, and sick or senior animals rather than fed regularly to healthy adults.

On top of hay: a daily measured portion of commercial pellets made specifically for guinea pigs — not rabbit or other rodent pellets, which contain no added vitamin C because rabbits and most other rodents can make their own (AEMV Hypovitaminosis C fact sheet) — fortified with stabilized vitamin C (ideally listed as L-ascorbyl-2-monophosphate). That vitamin C degrades with light, air, and time, so bags should be used within about 90 days of manufacture (VCA) or discarded roughly 3 months after opening (AEMV).

Fresh vegetables/leafy greens are given daily in a small handful, rotated among varieties so no single nutrient (especially calcium, since many vitamin-C-rich greens are also calcium-rich) is overloaded. Options named repeatedly across sources: bell pepper, tomato (ripe only), romaine/leaf lettuce, broccoli, cauliflower, endive, carrot (root and tops), beet greens, spinach, kale, mustard greens, Brussels sprouts, watercress, dandelion greens, parsley, cilantro, basil, dill, asparagus, celery, cucumber, and squash. Because guinea pigs can't synthesize vitamin C themselves (like humans, they lack the necessary enzyme), a daily source is mandatory — fortified pellets, vitamin-C-rich vegetables, and/or a direct oral tablet or liquid supplement. Vitamin C should never go in the water bottle: it oxidizes rapidly in water, and guinea pigs are picky enough about taste that medicating the water can reduce how much they drink (AEMV).

Fruit is an occasional treat, not a staple, due to sugar — small amounts of orange, kiwi, apple, or pear are commonly cited. Total treats (fruit included) should stay under roughly 10% of the daily diet (Pet Advocacy Network). Fresh water must be available at all times via bowl and/or sipper bottle, checked daily for leaks or blockages.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (moderation): bell peppers (also a top vitamin C source), ripe tomato, romaine/leaf lettuce, cucumber, carrot, celery, broccoli, cauliflower (and its leaves), dandelion greens, parsley, cilantro, basil, dill, watercress, spinach, kale, mustard greens, Brussels sprouts, asparagus, squash, and small bits of orange, kiwi, apple, or pear. Iceberg/head lettuce isn't toxic but is discouraged — nutritionally near-empty and can contribute to soft stool.

Unsafe/toxic foods named specifically and repeatedly: avocado (persin throughout fruit/skin/leaves, linked to heart muscle damage); chocolate (theobromine and caffeine — cardiac/nervous system effects); onions, garlic, chives, and leeks (allium compounds that cause hemolysis; symptoms can take 3–5 days to appear); rhubarb (very high oxalic acid — interferes with calcium absorption and is linked to bladder stones); raw potato and potato/tomato plant leaves, stems, and unripe tomatoes (solanine/tomatine, both toxic glycoalkaloids); mushrooms; fruit pits/seeds (trace cyanide plus choking hazard); and ornamental/houseplant toxins like lilies, daffodils, and buttercups. Also avoid high-starch or high-fat items — nuts, seeds, sunflower seeds, peanut butter, corn, beans/peas, cakes, cookies, bread, pasta, chips, dry cereal — since guinea pigs will selectively pick these over healthy pellets/hay, causing nutrient gaps and obesity (Oxbow). As strict herbivores, meat, eggs, and dairy are also off-limits — they lack the enzymes to digest them.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Dental disease (overgrown or misaligned molars)* (concerning): Named as the single most commonly cited cause of reduced appetite (Chewy, Vetster). Guinea pig teeth grow continuously, and uneven wear creates sharp points or malocclusion that make chewing painful. Because the problem teeth are at the back of the mouth, owners often can't spot it themselves — it requires a vet exam.
- *GI (gastrointestinal) stasis* (concerning): Gut motility slows or stops, usually triggered by inadequate fiber, dehydration, pain, or stress. It's both a cause and effect of not eating (a feedback loop) and is a genuine emergency — PetMD's veterinary content states it 'can become life-threatening within 24-48 hours if untreated.'
- *Illness or pain elsewhere in the body (respiratory infection, urinary tract issues/bladder stones, arthritis, injury)* (concerning): Pain or discomfort from an unrelated condition commonly suppresses appetite; multiple sources flag URIs and UTIs/bladder stones specifically as frequent culprits.
- *Stress from environmental change (new home, cage move, new cage mates, loud noise, handling)* (depends): Guinea pigs are prey animals, described as 'nervous animals by nature' (Pet Advocacy Network); a cage move or sudden diet change alone can make one temporarily refuse food and water. A bonded companion guinea pig helps reduce this stress. Should resolve quickly on its own; prolonged refusal still needs a vet.
- *Dietary neophobia / early food imprinting* (normal): AEMV notes guinea pigs 'establish their food preferences very early in life and do not adapt well to changes in their diet.' A sudden new hay type, pellet brand, or vegetable can be turned down simply because it's unfamiliar, not because of illness — new foods should be introduced gradually.
- *Pregnancy toxemia (pregnancy ketosis) in late-gestation sows* (concerning): Listed among AEMV's 'common disorders' of guinea pigs under reproductive conditions. Overweight or late-term pregnant sows are especially at risk; it presents with appetite loss and can be fatal without prompt treatment.
- *Vitamin C deficiency (hypovitaminosis C / scurvy)* (concerning): Decreased appetite is one of the earliest signs, appearing within about 2-3 weeks of a vitamin-C-deficient diet, alongside pododermatitis (foot inflammation), weight loss, limping, and a rough coat (AEMV fact sheet). It's both a cause and consequence of reduced eating, and is preventable/treatable with correct diet and supplementation.

**When to actually worry**
For guinea pigs, skipping meals is basically never "normal" the way it can be for some other pets — their digestion depends on constant food throughput, so the safe window is short. Two independent sources (Chewy, Vetster) put the outer limit at 8–12 hours without eating or producing droppings before real risk of GI stasis sets in; Vetster specifically states a guinea pig that hasn't eaten for 6–12 hours should be seen by a vet as soon as possible, and describes 12 hours as the maximum safe gap. That 8–12 hour figure is the trigger to act — not the point of actual danger: once GI stasis has actually set in, PetMD's veterinary content states it "can become life-threatening within 24-48 hours if untreated." With prompt veterinary treatment (fluids, pain control, assisted feeding, prokinetic medication), many guinea pigs begin improving within 24-72 hours (PetMD).

Signs that warrant a same-day call, not a wait-and-see approach: reduced or absent droppings (or droppings turning small, dry, or infrequent), lethargy or reluctance to move, a hunched or tense posture, audible teeth grinding, noticeable weight loss, a bloated or firm abdomen, reduced water intake, drooling, and labored or rapid breathing. Multiple veterinary-sourced materials (PetMD/Chewy, Oxbow, Pet Advocacy Network) frame any of these — or really any noticeable drop in eating at all — as reason to contact an exotics-experienced vet promptly rather than waiting to see if appetite returns on its own.

**FunFact candidate**
Guinea pigs practice cecotrophy: their hindgut ferments fibrous food and produces a distinct type of soft, nutrient-dense dropping (a cecotrope, different from their normal firm fecal pellets) that the guinea pig re-ingests directly, usually overnight when owners rarely witness it. This "second pass" through the gut is how they absorb B vitamins (including B12, thiamine, riboflavin, and pantothenic acid), vitamin K, protein, and gut-healthy short-chain fatty acids that their bodies can't fully extract — or synthesize — on the food's first trip through. Eating their own droppings isn't a behavioral quirk to discourage; it's a required part of normal, healthy digestion for this species.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several numbers vary by source rather than being settled figures:

1) Vitamin C dosing is framed differently across sources. VCA states a flat 10-50 mg/day (varying with age, health, stress, reproductive state). The AEMV veterinary fact sheet on hypovitaminosis C instead gives a weight-based dose — 10-25 mg/kg/day for a healthy adult, with growing, pregnant, lactating, or ill animals needing 2-3 times more. An older AEMV client-education trifold simplifies this to a flat "at least 25mg daily" for all guinea pigs. These aren't truly contradictory once you account for body weight (a typical ~1kg pig lands in a similar range), but a reader shouldn't be given one flat number as unanimous fact — better to present it as a range and note some sources dose by body weight rather than a flat daily amount.

2) Hay's share of the diet is quoted differently: Oxbow says "at least 70%," while a PetMD/Chewy care sheet says "80%." Both agree hay should dominate and be unlimited; only the precise percentage differs.

3) "How long can they go without eating" has two related but distinct numbers that could get conflated: Chewy/Vetster put the outer safe limit at 8-12 hours (the point to call a vet), while PetMD's GI stasis article separately states that once stasis actually sets in, it can turn life-threatening within 24-48 hours. These describe different stages of the same problem, not disagreeing figures, but I've kept them distinct rather than merging into one number.

4) Guidance on alfalfa hay for healthy adults differs in strictness: PetMD/Chewy says to avoid it outright for adults, while Oxbow and Pet Advocacy Network allow it as an occasional treat. All sources agree alfalfa is appropriate for babies, pregnant/nursing, senior, or ill guinea pigs.

### Chinchilla

**Feeding schedule**
Adults: Free choice grass hay (timothy, orchard grass, meadow, or oat hay) available 24/7, refreshed daily since uneaten hay should be discarded to avoid mold. On top of that, 1 to 2 tablespoons per day of a plain, chinchilla specific pellet, either given once daily or split morning and evening. This "1 to 2 tablespoons of pellets daily" figure is essentially the consensus number, independently stated by VCA Animal Hospitals, PDSA, RSPCA, and Purdue University's veterinary hospital. Fresh water must be available at all times, ideally from a sipper bottle rather than a bowl (lower contamination/tipping risk), changed and checked for clogs daily.

Babies (kits): Nurse from the mother and may start nibbling hay and pellets while still nursing, though this isn't nutritionally necessary yet. Weaning happens at roughly 8 weeks of age, provided the kit has also reached a minimum weight of about 200 grams (per breeder resource Cuddlebug Chinchillas and echoed by other breeder sites); some breeders wait until 12 weeks, especially to separate same-sex littermates before sexual maturity. If a kit isn't gaining about 2 grams a day or loses weight for 3 days, hand feeding (goat's milk formula via dropper, every 2 hours around the clock, gradually spaced out over 4 to 6 weeks) becomes necessary.

Juveniles/portion by age: this is where sources disagree and are weaker (breeder/enthusiast sites, not major vet orgs) - see caveats field. Roughly, expect smaller pellet portions than an adult until 6 months old, then adult portions after.

Treats: most sources say keep treats to a small fraction of the diet, offered a few times a week, not daily, with quantified figures ranging from under 5% to under 10% of total intake depending on source. One breeder source recommends withholding treats entirely until a kit is about 4 months old.

**Diet composition**
Staple: unlimited grass hay, timothy is the most commonly recommended type, with orchard grass, meadow hay, and oat hay as acceptable variants. Hay must be dust extracted/mold free; it's the single most important item for both digestion and continuous tooth wear.

Concentrate: a small daily ration (1 to 2 tablespoons) of a commercial pelleted chinchilla feed, ideally "nothing but hay" pressed into pellet form, though many commercial pellets add fillers like soybean meal or wheat. One nutritional breakdown cited crude protein around 16 to 20%, crude fat 2 to 5%, and crude fiber in the high teens to 30%. Chinchilla specific pellets are preferred over rabbit or guinea pig pellets. Loose seed/grain "muesli style" mixes are explicitly discouraged by RSPCA and PDSA because chinchillas selectively eat the tasty high fat pieces and leave the rest, unbalancing the diet.

Alfalfa hay/pellets: generally treated as an occasional or life stage specific item rather than an everyday staple for adults, since its higher calcium and protein levels are linked to bladder stone risk (Merck Veterinary Manual). PDSA notes an exception: alfalfa is appropriate for chinchillas that are pregnant, nursing, or still growing, when the extra protein and calcium are actually needed.

Fresh produce: kept as a minor, occasional addition, not a dietary pillar, since chinchillas evolved on a very sparse, high fiber, low moisture diet. Where sources do recommend vegetables, examples given include dark leafy greens such as romaine or green leaf lettuce, bell pepper, carrot tops, and celery (Merck Veterinary Manual), though (see caveats) other sources dispute whether lettuce belongs on the safe list at all.

Digestive physiology relevant to feeding: chinchillas are hindgut fermenters and practice cecotrophy, they produce a distinct type of soft, shiny, nutrient and bacteria laden dropping called a cecotrope (different from their normal dry fecal pellets), usually at night, and eat these directly to run food through the gut a second time. This is normal and necessary, not a sign of illness, since the small intestine only captures a portion of the nutrients on the first pass through their very fibrous diet, and re ingestion also helps them reclaim water, an adaptation to their native arid Andean habitat.

Introduce any new food, whether a new hay batch, a new pellet brand, or a vegetable, gradually over several days; multiple sources (Merck, RSPCA, VCA, Purdue) independently warn that sudden diet changes are a real trigger for digestive illness in this species.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Reasonably well corroborated safe treats (small amounts, a few times weekly, not daily): plain rolled oats, wheat biscuit or shredded wheat pieces (small, roughly coin sized), grass based treat pellets, dried dandelion leaves/root, dried nettles, dried chamomile flowers, rosehips (occasional), a small piece of apple or pear (roughly a quarter apple, once weekly per one source), a small carrot piece once weekly, a couple of blueberries, and raisins limited to one or two per week (not recommended for young chinchillas). Safe chew items for dental wear: applewood, pearwood, elm, ash, maple, or birch branches, cholla wood, hazel sticks, and pine cones.

Well corroborated foods to avoid entirely: nuts and seeds (high fat and phosphorus, linked to liver strain/ulcers and generally discouraged across every source consulted), dried fruit fed in any quantity, mixed seed/grain "muesli" style feed, chocolate, onion, garlic, avocado, dairy products (chinchillas are lactose intolerant), asparagus, and rhubarb including its leaves (described as poisonous). VCA specifically warns against dehydrated fruits and vegetables as treats, saying they cause severe digestive disturbance, distinct from fresh produce. Cedar, plum, redwood, and cherry wood should not be offered as chew items, they're toxic.

Genuinely disputed items, see caveats: apple, carrot, banana, grapes, and lettuce appear on both the "safe occasional treat" list and the "avoid" list depending on the source.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Dental disease (overgrown or misaligned teeth, spurs cutting the cheek/tongue)* (concerning): Chinchilla teeth grow continuously throughout life. A South Pasadena Animal Hospital vet blog identifies this as the single most common reason a pet chinchilla stops eating, since chewing becomes painful; signs include dropping food from the mouth and a wet, drooling chin.
- *GI stasis (the gut slows down or stops moving)* (concerning): Usually secondary to another problem (insufficient hay/fiber, dehydration, dental pain, stress, or a sudden diet change) but becomes a self-perpetuating, life-threatening emergency on its own once it starts. Described by exotic-pet care sources as able to progress from mild to life-threatening within hours to about a day.
- *Heat stress / heat stroke* (concerning): Chinchillas evolved in the cool Andes and are exceptionally heat-intolerant: their fur grows roughly 60 to 80 hairs per follicle (versus one for humans) for insulation, but they lack sweat glands and cannot pant effectively, so they have almost no way to dump heat. Multiple sources put the ideal ambient range at 60 to 70F, calling anything above about 75F risky and 78 to 80F+ actively dangerous. Reduced appetite is described as an early warning sign, before more severe symptoms like open-mouth panting, red ears, or seizures.
- *Respiratory infection or other illness* (concerning): General sickness reduces appetite both directly (reduced sense of smell, malaise) and because the body is diverting energy to fighting infection; watch for wheezing, clicking, or crackling breathing sounds alongside reduced eating.
- *Environmental/handling stress (new home, cage rearrangement, loud noise, travel, loss of a cage companion)* (depends): A new or disrupted environment can suppress appetite temporarily. One source describes stress-related appetite loss as typically resolving within about 3 to 5 days once the animal feels settled again, but this should be watched closely rather than assumed benign if it doesn't improve.
- *Sudden diet or hay/pellet brand change* (concerning): Chinchillas have sensitive digestive systems and can react to abrupt food changes with reduced eating or digestive upset. Vet and care sources consistently recommend transitioning any new food gradually over roughly 5 to 7 days rather than swapping overnight.
- *Pain from other sources (bladder stones, urinary tract infection, injury)* (concerning): Painful conditions elsewhere in the body can suppress appetite the same way dental pain does; often accompanied by other signs like straining to urinate, blood in urine, or reluctance to move.
- *Dehydration / inadequate water intake* (concerning): Reduces gut motility and is cited as a contributing cause of GI stasis, so a clogged water bottle or empty water source can indirectly show up as reduced eating.

**When to actually worry**
Multiple veterinary-adjacent sources converge on roughly the same window: a chinchilla that isn't eating for more than 12 to 24 hours should be seen the same day, not just monitored at home. This specific figure is stated explicitly by a veterinary clinic's own blog (South Pasadena Animal Hospital, spah.la) and echoed by a specialist small-pet care site (smallpetexpert.com); the larger general veterinary/welfare organizations (PDSA, RSPCA, Merck Veterinary Manual, VCA) all say the same thing qualitatively, i.e. treat any reduction in eating or droppings as a same-day vet matter, without themselves stating an exact hour count, so treat the 12 to 24 hour figure as coming from exotic-animal-focused clinical/enthusiast sources rather than a headline number from Merck or VCA.

Droppings are just as important a signal as food intake. PDSA and RSPCA both instruct owners to check daily that the chinchilla is "eating every day" and "passing plenty of dry droppings," treating a drop in either as a reason to contact a vet. One specialist source puts a number on this: complete cessation of fecal output for more than about 12 hours is described as requiring immediate veterinary attention.

Accompanying red flags that make any appetite change more urgent: a bloated or tense abdomen, audible teeth grinding (bruxism, a pain indicator), drooling or a wet chin, labored or noisy breathing, hunched posture or reluctance to move, noticeable lethargy, and weight loss. If GI stasis is underway, sources describe it as able to go from "concerning" to "life-threatening" in under 24 hours, but also note that chinchillas that get prompt veterinary treatment (fluids, motility/pain medication, syringe feeding of a critical care formula) generally have good recovery odds, so speed matters more than waiting to see if it resolves on its own.

**FunFact candidate**
Chinchillas are hindgut fermenters that practice cecotrophy: alongside their normal dry fecal pellets, they produce a second, distinct type of dropping called a cecotrope, soft, shiny, and produced mainly overnight in a pouch called the cecum where gut bacteria ferment their fibrous diet. Rather than being waste, cecotropes are nutrient dense, and chinchillas eat them straight away to send their food through the digestive tract a second time, since their small intestine only absorbs a portion of the available nutrients on the first pass. It's also thought to help them reclaim water, a useful trait for an animal adapted to the arid high Andes. A chinchilla that's prevented from doing this can actually become nutrient deficient, so what looks like an odd or gross habit is a required part of normal digestion, not a behavioral problem.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Several concrete disagreements turned up, rather than a single settled picture:

1) Apple and carrot as treats: PDSA (UK veterinary charity) explicitly advises avoiding apples, carrots, grapes, and bananas as chinchilla treats, citing digestive upset from high sugar/moisture content. In contrast, Merck Veterinary Manual, Purdue University's veterinary hospital, and several other care sites list small weekly amounts of apple or carrot as acceptable, standard treats. This is a direct source-level conflict, not just a wording difference, so I've flagged both items as disputed rather than picking a side.

2) Lettuce: Merck Veterinary Manual lists romaine or green leaf lettuce as an acceptable small daily vegetable addition, while at least one enthusiast care site (planetchinchilla.com) lists lettuce generally as unsafe, citing bloating risk. Merck is the more authoritative source here, but I'm flagging the conflict rather than silently deferring to it, since the enthusiast site isn't obviously wrong either (lettuce advice varies a lot across small-herbivore species).

3) Treat quantity ceiling: sources vary on the maximum share of the diet treats should occupy, one gives "under 5%," another (Purdue-adjacent source) gives "up to 10% or about 1 tablespoon per day." Neither is an official veterinary-body number; I did not average them, both are reported.

4) Baby/juvenile pellet portions: vet organizations (VCA, PDSA, RSPCA, Merck) all give one flat adult figure, 1 to 2 tablespoons of pellets per day, and do not break this out by life stage in the pages I could access. Breeder and enthusiast sources fill this gap but disagree with each other: one breeder resource (Cuddlebug Chinchillas) caps kits under 6 months at 1 tablespoon/day and steps juveniles over 6 months up to 2 tablespoons/day, while another care site gives babies under 6 months up to 2 tablespoons/day from the start. I've presented the breeder number as the more detailed but lower-confidence figure, and noted that major vet orgs don't independently confirm either specific breakdown.

5) Alfalfa: broadly, sources agree adult chinchillas shouldn't get alfalfa as a staple hay (calcium/bladder stone concern per Merck), but PDSA is the one source that explicitly carves out an exception for pregnant, nursing, or growing chinchillas, where I did not find independent confirmation from the other vet sources one way or the other.

6) The "105 to 106F causes irreversible organ damage" figure in a heat stroke source was ambiguous about whether it referred to ambient room temperature or the chinchilla's own core body temperature; given normal chinchilla body temperature runs around 100 to 102F, this is much more likely a core body temperature threshold, but the source itself didn't disambiguate, so treat that specific number with some caution even though the broader "60-70F ideal, 75F+ risky" guidance was corroborated across sources.

I did not find a major veterinary organization (Merck/VCA/PDSA/RSPCA) stating an exact "X hours without eating = emergency" number; the 12-24 hour figure used in the whenToWorry field comes from an actual veterinary clinic's blog and a specialist small-pet care site, and should be understood as clinically-informed guidance rather than a figure sourced from a peer-reviewed veterinary manual.

### Ferret

**Feeding schedule**
Ferrets have an extremely fast gut transit time — food passes through in roughly 3 to 4 hours (Merck Veterinary Manual; corroborated by Chewy and Ferret World) — so they need frequent access to food rather than one or two meals a day.

Adults: The standard approach recommended by VCA Animal Hospitals is free-feeding — "leaving food out 24 hours a day, allowing your ferret to graze as desired" — with high-quality dry kibble always available. Oxbow Animal Health's official care guide says fortified kibble should make up about 80% of the diet and be offered "free choice" (always available). Chewy notes ferrets naturally want to eat roughly every 4-6 hours. VCA adds that ferrets prone to obesity should have portions controlled rather than free-fed.

Kits (babies): Winter Park Veterinary Hospital's client handout states deciduous teeth erupt around 2 weeks of age, which is when weaning to solid food begins, with commercial kitten chow recommended starting at 4 weeks; kits reach adult body weight around 4 months. A ferret rescue/adoption source (not a veterinary source) instead put weaning at 6-8 weeks — see caveats.

Ferrets with insulinoma (a common condition in the breed): A ferret health rescue organization (EWASH) recommends 4-6 small meals spread through the day rather than 1-2 large meals, to avoid blood-sugar swings — this is a medical-management schedule, not general advice for healthy ferrets.

**Diet composition**
Ferrets are obligate carnivores with a short, simple digestive tract (no cecum) that cannot properly digest plant matter, so the diet is built around animal protein and fat, not vegetables or grains.

Base diet: A high-quality, ferret-specific dry kibble (named examples: Mazuri Ferret Food, Oxbow Essentials Ferret Food) fed as the staple. Multiple sources give slightly different target ranges: VCA lists 32-40% protein / 10-15% fat / max 4% fiber; Merck Veterinary Manual lists 35-40% protein / <2.5% fiber; Chewy lists 35-45% protein / "at least 30%" fat (see caveats — the fat figures disagree substantially). Merck notes high-quality kitten food is an acceptable substitute if ferret-specific food isn't available; Winter Park Veterinary Hospital's handout agrees adult cat food is unsuitable long-term (too high in fiber, too low in protein/fat) and warns that soft/semi-moist food can cause gum disease and tooth loss, so kibble should be dry.

Whole-prey/raw feeding: Merck Veterinary Manual notes some owners feed whole rodents, rabbits, or pigeons, or "bone and raw flesh" (BARF) diets, which has become more popular in some regions — but stresses any raw meat must be human-grade and free of pathogens like Salmonella or Campylobacter. Oxbow's care guide similarly allows raw/dehydrated diets, raw meat, and whole prey as supplemental (not primary) enrichment, alongside homemade options like boiled/cooked chicken, cooked eggs, and dehydrated organ meats.

Life-stage formulas: Merck notes specialized commercial diets exist for kits, pregnant/nursing jills, and senior ferrets, though it doesn't give specific nutrient numbers for each.

Water: Always available; a sipper/water bottle is generally preferred over an open bowl because ferrets play in and spill bowls (Merck; Winter Park Vet Hospital), though Winter Park notes either bottles or heavy ceramic crocks work if changed and cleaned regularly.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (in small/occasional amounts): freeze-dried single-ingredient meats (rabbit, chicken, duck), whole cooked eggs, cooked muscle meat like chicken, meat-flavored baby food (occasional only, per VCA), and named commercial supplement/treat products such as Ferretone (a fatty-acid supplement, also used by Winter Park Veterinary Hospital to distract ferrets during nail trims/exams) and Oxbow's Critical Care Carnivore (best used under veterinary guidance, especially for sick or recovering ferrets).

Unsafe/dangerous: Chocolate is called out by VCA as fatal to ferrets. Sugar/sweets and high-sugar treats (raisins, sugary cereals, honey used outside of an emergency) are flagged across sources as risky, especially because they can trigger blood-sugar spikes and crashes tied to insulinoma. Dairy products (ferrets are lactose intolerant), fruits and vegetables, grains/carbohydrates (bread, pasta, rice, cereals), peas/legumes (linked to urinary problems per Chewy), loose bones or bone fragments, and long-term feeding of cat or dog food (wrong protein/fat/fiber ratios) are all listed as foods to avoid. Oxbow's official guide is emphatic that fruits, vegetables, cereals, and grains should be avoided entirely because ferrets "are unable to properly digest these foods." Non-food hazards worth flagging alongside diet: rubber items and sponges, which ferrets will chew and swallow, causing intestinal blockages requiring surgery (Winter Park Veterinary Hospital).

**Why they stop eating**
- *Fast-paced normal hunger cycle / missed meal* (normal): Because a ferret's gut empties in about 3-4 hours, brief pickiness or a skipped feeding shows up faster and looks more alarming than in a cat or dog, even when nothing is wrong.
- *Stress or a new environment* (normal): Ferret World and other care sources list stress from rehoming, cage changes, or handling as a legitimate but usually short-lived cause of appetite dips.
- *Dislike of a new food or brand (picky/neophobic phase)* (normal): The American Ferret Association publishes a dedicated 'Switching Kibbles' guide, reflecting that ferrets can be resistant to diet changes; slow, gradual transitions are recommended.
- *Dental pain or mouth disease* (concerning): Ferret World describes 'pseudo-anorexia' where the ferret wants to eat but can't comfortably chew, from dental disease or mouth pain.
- *Gastrointestinal foreign-body obstruction* (concerning): Ferrets are notorious for swallowing foam, sponges, and rubber items; Winter Park Veterinary Hospital specifically warns rubber chew toys and chewed sponges can cause intestinal impaction requiring surgery, which presents with appetite loss.
- *Insulinoma (pancreatic tumor causing low blood sugar)* (concerning): A very common disease in middle-aged/older pet ferrets; hypoglycemia from insulin overproduction causes weakness, drooling, and appetite/behavior changes (EWASH ferret health resource).
- *Stomach ulcers (Helicobacter mustelae) or gut infections (e.g., ECE)* (concerning): Listed by Ferret World as common infectious/inflammatory causes of appetite loss in the species.
- *Adrenal disease, kidney/liver disease, or heart disease* (concerning): Systemic illnesses common in pet ferrets that can suppress appetite as a secondary sign; PetMD and Dial-A-Vet list these among differentials for anorexia and lethargy.

**When to actually worry**
Because of the ferret's rapid gut transit and small body reserves, sources agree loss of appetite is time-critical in a way it isn't for most other pets. Ferret World states that a healthy adult ferret can develop dangerous blood-sugar drops within a single day of not eating, and that young, elderly, or already-sick ferrets can crash even faster — framing appetite loss as needing same-day veterinary attention ("hours matter, not days"). PetMD similarly describes anorexia in ferrets as "a very serious condition" that can cause dangerous weight loss if not addressed.

Oxbow Animal Health's official care guide lists "abnormal eating or drinking" alongside these signs as reasons to contact a vet: loose, soft, or absent stool; small, dry, or infrequent stools; blood in urine; sneezing or breathing trouble; sores on the feet; visible difficulty chewing; bald patches in the fur; and hunching in a corner or lethargy.

For an acute hypoglycemic crisis specifically (weakness, wobbling/staggering, a glazed stare, drooling, teeth-grinding, or collapse), EWASH (a ferret health/rescue resource) describes rubbing a small amount of honey, Karo syrup, or a product like Nutri-Cal on the gums as emergency first aid — absorbed even if the ferret can't swallow — while getting to a vet immediately; this is crisis management, not routine feeding advice. Additional red flags warranting emergency care: vomiting/retching, black tarry stools or no stool, abdominal pain/bloating, or refusing water as well as food.

**FunFact candidate**
A ferret's digestive tract has no cecum and is so short and simple that a meal passes all the way through in roughly 3 to 4 hours — one of the fastest transit times of any common pet mammal (Merck Veterinary Manual). That's the underlying reason free-choice/frequent feeding is the norm for the species, and also why skipping meals turns dangerous for a ferret in hours, not the day-or-two grace period owners might expect from a cat or dog.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two real disagreements surfaced in sourcing, plus one softer one:

1. Fat percentage in the diet: VCA Animal Hospitals states a good ferret diet is 10-15% fat, while Chewy's guide states ferrets need "at least 30%" fat. These are very different numbers from two mainstream sources; Merck Veterinary Manual's fat figure could not be confirmed (the source text described fat requirement as "variable" without citing an exact number). Given the spread, present fat content as "check the guaranteed analysis on ferret-specific food" rather than asserting one fixed percentage as settled.

2. Whether any fruit/vegetable is acceptable: Winter Park Veterinary Hospital's own client handout says small amounts of vegetables and fruit are fine "as a TREAT." This directly conflicts with Oxbow Animal Health's official guide and with VCA/Chewy, all of which say to avoid fruits and vegetables entirely because ferrets, as obligate carnivores, can't digest plant matter well and it risks diarrhea/blood-sugar swings. The weight of sources (including the ferret-food manufacturer and two major vet-adjacent consumer sites) leans toward "avoid," but a real veterinary clinic's own guidance says otherwise, so this should be presented as a genuine disagreement rather than settled fact.

3. Kit weaning age: A veterinary hospital handout (Winter Park Vet) ties weaning to tooth eruption at ~2 weeks with kitten chow starting at 4 weeks, while a ferret rescue/orphan-care resource cites weaning "safely done at 6-8 weeks." These may be describing different things (start of solid-food introduction vs. full weaning off the mother), but the exact age range is not fully consistent across sources, so I did not present a single hard number as definitive.

### Hedgehog

**Feeding schedule**
Fed once daily, in the evening, since hedgehogs are nocturnal (VCA, LafeberVet, and a feeding-chart source all agree on this timing). Water is left available 24/7 via bottle or bowl, checked/cleaned daily.

Daily dry food amount is genuinely disputed across sources, not just imprecise: PetMD's own hedgehog care sheet says 3 to 4 teaspoons of hedgehog pellets per day, while PetMD's separate "What Do Hedgehogs Eat" article says 1 to 3 tablespoons (roughly 3 to 9 teaspoons) of commercial diet daily, nearly a 2 to 3x spread on the same publisher's site. LafeberVet (an exotic-vet CE resource, the most clinically-oriented source found) instead frames the base as 2 to 3 teaspoons of high-quality reduced-calorie cat food as the protein component, plus 1 to 2 teaspoons of chopped produce, without giving one fixed "total" number, and stresses adjusting by body condition rather than a flat quantity. A lower-tier feeding-schedule blog (ExoPetGuides) tapers portions by age: roughly 1.5 to 3 tbsp/day for hoglets (4 weeks to 6 months), 1.5 to 2 tbsp for juveniles (6 to 12 months), 1 to 2 tbsp for adults (1 to 3 years), and 1 to 1.5 tbsp for seniors (3+ years); treat these specific numbers as lower-confidence since the site is not a vet or academic source, but the general "portions shrink slightly with age/activity" pattern is consistent with the vet advice to feed to body condition.

Insects are a supplement, not the daily centerpiece: LafeberVet recommends 5 to 6 mealworms or 1 to 2 crickets, 3 to 4 times weekly; VCA and PetMD both say insects (gut-loaded mealworms/crickets) 2 to 3 times a week.

Baby hedgehogs (hoglets) begin sampling solid food around 3 weeks old and are weaned by 4 to 6 weeks (LafeberVet). An established breeder source (Hamor Hollow) notes weaned baby hedgehogs eat only about a tablespoon or less per feeding, and drink roughly 1 to 3 ounces of water per night, useful for gauging whether a young hedgehog is eating enough. That same breeder source flags 2 consecutive nights of not eating as the point to contact the breeder or a vet for a newly-homed hedgehog specifically.

**Diet composition**
Base diet: a high-quality hedgehog-specific kibble/insectivore diet, or high-quality low-fat ("light"/reduced-calorie) cat food used as a base or mixer, described by multiple vet-facing sources (VCA, PetMD) as the main daily food. LafeberVet gives the only concrete macronutrient targets found: moderate-to-high protein (30 to 50% on a dry-matter basis) and fat (10 to 20% dry-matter basis), consistent with the general "insectivore, not full carnivore" framing used everywhere else.

Protein/insect supplementation: gut-loaded mealworms, crickets, earthworms, and dubia roaches (gut-loading = feeding the insects nutritious food for roughly 24 to 48 hours before offering them to the hedgehog, per LafeberVet). Waxworms are explicitly called out (LafeberVet, plus a feeding-chart source) as too fatty to be a staple insect and should only be an occasional treat. Hard-boiled eggs and, per PetMD, occasional pinkie mice are mentioned as optional additional animal protein for some owners, though this is less universal across sources than the insect/kibble base.

Produce: small amounts of chopped, cooked vegetables and fruit, about 1 to 2 teaspoons daily to every other day (LafeberVet), such as peas, corn, cooked carrot, apple, banana, pear. Vegetables should be cooked/softened rather than served raw and hard, to avoid choking, per VCA and PetMD.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat items named across sources: gut-loaded mealworms, crickets, earthworms, and dubia roaches (in moderation, not daily-staple amounts); cooked carrot; apple, pear, banana; hard-boiled egg; freeze-dried chicken liver (mentioned by a breeder source specifically as an enticement treat for shy eaters). Waxworms are edible but explicitly flagged by LafeberVet and a feeding-guide source as too high-fat to be more than a rare treat.

Foods to avoid, named specifically across VCA, PetMD, and LafeberVet: avocado (toxic), raw meat and raw eggs, milk/dairy products (causes digestive upset/diarrhea), nuts and seeds (choking hazard plus excess fat), and hard raw vegetables like uncooked carrot (choking hazard). Lettuce and celery are called out not as toxic but as nutritionally low-value filler that shouldn't substitute for real food. General "unapproved people food" is also flagged as a category to avoid.

**Why they stop eating**
- *New-home / transport stress* (depends): Reduced or zero food/water intake for the first few days after a hedgehog is brought home from a breeder is described as normal by multiple breeder sources (Hamor Hollow, Critter Connection): stress from the move suppresses appetite and can also cause temporary loose or discolored stool. Fecal output, not bowl level, is the recommended way to confirm a shy new hedgehog is actually eating. Breeders flag roughly 2 nights of zero eating as the point to intervene/call for help.
- *Quilling (spine replacement)* (depends): Hedgehogs shed and replace their spines in episodes, most intensely at 4 to 6 weeks old and again around 16 weeks (about 4 months), sometimes with smaller repeat episodes later in life (Hedgehog World). It is described as uncomfortable, causing grumpiness and reduced activity, but sources did not describe appetite loss as a routine part of quilling itself; one behavior source (MyHedgehogCare) specifically lists food refusal beyond 24 hours during this period as a vet-worthy warning sign rather than an expected symptom.
- *Cold stress / hibernation (torpor) attempts* (concerning): African pygmy hedgehogs cannot safely hibernate the way wild European hedgehogs do. When ambient temperature drops (sources vary on the exact threshold, roughly the mid-60s°F and below), they can enter a hypothermic torpor state that suppresses appetite, activity, and immune function and can become fatal within hours without gradual rewarming. Recommended ambient housing temperature ranges cited across sources cluster in the low-to-mid 70s through mid-80s°F.
- *Dental disease / oral pain* (concerning): Loose teeth, periodontal disease, infections, or hard food/objects lodged in the mouth are cited (SpectrumCare) as a common medical cause of reduced eating, often signaled by drooling, bad breath, chewing on one side, or dropping food.
- *Neoplasia (cancer/tumors)* (concerning): Cancer is unusually common in captive African hedgehogs. A peer-reviewed case report (PMC) citing multiple retrospective studies gives tumor incidence rates of 29%, 36%, and 53% across different study populations, roughly 85% malignant, with typical presentation in middle-aged animals (about 3.5 to 3.9 years old). Oral tumors specifically (e.g. squamous cell carcinoma) can make eating physically painful, and reduced appetite plus weight loss are listed as general cancer symptoms.
- *GI upset, parasites, infection, or organ disease* (concerning): General illness causes (gastrointestinal obstruction/upset, parasites, respiratory infection, liver or kidney disease) are listed by SpectrumCare as medical reasons a hedgehog stops eating; these typically come with other symptoms like diarrhea, lethargy, or breathing changes.
- *Water source issues* (depends): Some hedgehogs are picky about bottle vs. bowl, or reject unfamiliar-tasting tap water (chlorine/minerals); a clogged sipper bottle can also cut off water access and secondarily reduce food interest. This is a fixable husbandry issue rather than illness.

**When to actually worry**
Multiple independent-ish sources converge on roughly 24 hours as the outer limit before a vet visit is warranted: SpectrumCare states plainly to see a vet immediately if a hedgehog "has not eaten or drunk anything for 24 hours," and separately advises immediate care if a hedgehog has stopped eating for a day, seems weak, feels cold/overheated, has trouble breathing, drools or paws at the mouth, has diarrhea, or is losing weight. A behavior-focused source (MyHedgehogCare) independently lists "refuses food for more than 24 hours" as a vet-call trigger during quilling specifically.

For a newly-acquired hedgehog still adjusting, established breeders (Hamor Hollow, Critter Connection) treat the threshold a bit more loosely, roughly 2 nights, before recommending contacting the breeder or a vet, since some initial stress-related food refusal is expected.

Watch-for signs that turn "not eating" into an urgent case: lethargy or weakness, wobbling/loss of balance, open-mouth or labored breathing, unresponsiveness or a body that feels cold (possible torpor), drooling, bad breath, blood near the mouth, chewing only on one side or dropping food (possible dental/oral tumor pain), and ongoing weight loss. Hedgehogs are also repeatedly described (SpectrumCare) as an species that "hides illness until they are quite sick," which is the stated reason vets favor early evaluation over a wait-and-see approach once 24 hours has passed.

**FunFact candidate**
When a hedgehog encounters a new taste or smell, including a new food or treat, it can produce frothy saliva and contort itself to spread that foam across its own spines using its tongue, a behavior called self-anointing. Documented triggers specifically named by The Hedgehog Program include new foods like banana, egg, and kibble, but also non-food smells (lotion, soil, cleaning products). Despite decades of observation, no single scientific explanation has been confirmed. Leading theories include camouflaging scent, a defensive function, or simple sensory exploration, but sources are explicit that none of these has been proven.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two real, sourced disagreements worth flagging rather than averaging:

1) Daily kibble/food amount: PetMD's own two hedgehog articles disagree with each other, one says 3 to 4 teaspoons/day, the other says 1 to 3 tablespoons/day (about 3 to 9 teaspoons), a 2 to 3x difference on the same publisher's site. The more clinically-oriented LafeberVet source avoids giving one fixed total and instead frames it as roughly 2 to 3 teaspoons of protein (cat food) plus 1 to 2 teaspoons of produce, adjusted to body condition. Given this spread, any published number should be presented as a starting range to weigh/adjust from, not a precise figure, and ideally attributed per source if stated as a specific number.

2) Cancer/tumor incidence rate: a peer-reviewed case report (PMC) citing multiple retrospective studies gives three different incidence figures from three different study populations: 29%, 36%, and 53%. A secondary pet-health site summarizing the same literature rounds this to "29% to 51.5%" or "29% to 53%" depending on which page of that site is checked. There is no single settled "the rate is X%" figure, it is a range across several distinct studies, and should be presented that way (e.g., "studies have found tumor rates anywhere from roughly 29% to over 50%") rather than picking one number as definitive.

Minor, lower-stakes variance also exists in cited ideal ambient temperature (sources range from about 70 to 85°F depending on the page) and insect feeding frequency (2 to 3x/week per VCA/PetMD vs. 3 to 4x/week per LafeberVet); these are close enough to treat as normal source variation rather than a real dispute.

Source-quality note: VCA, PetMD, LafeberVet (exotic-vet CE site), the PMC academic case report, Hedgehog Welfare Society, The Hedgehog Program, and the two breeder sites (Hamor Hollow, Critter Connection) were treated as higher-confidence. SpectrumCare, ExoPetGuides, Hedgehog World, and MyHedgehogCare appear to be newer pet-content sites without clear veterinary/editorial credentials; their specific numbers (e.g., the ExoPetGuides life-stage portion table) were used only where they aligned with or supplemented the higher-confidence sources, not as standalone authority. Also note: hedgehogclub.com (formerly the International Hedgehog Association's site) currently 301-redirects to an unrelated, apparently squatted domain and should not be cited or linked from the article.

## Birds

### Cockatiel

**Feeding schedule**
There is no single "meals per day" number in the vet-reviewed sources — cockatiels are managed on free-choice access to a base diet rather than scheduled meals. Lafeber's consumer feeding guide explains why: small birds "have a very fast metabolism and most choose to eat all day long," so the base bowl (pellets) should be refreshed in the morning when birds are hungriest and topped up if it runs empty before evening — it should never sit empty for long. VCA Animal Hospitals adds the fresh-food side of that same routine: fruits/vegetables should make up roughly 20-25% of the daily diet by volume, but must be removed from the cage within about 2 hours (VCA) to prevent spoilage/bacterial growth — Lafeber's own guide gives a slightly looser "a few hours" for the same reason. Extras/treats/table food should stay small: Lafeber caps "snacks" at no more than 10% of the weekly diet, and VCA's practical portion rule is that "a teaspoon for a cockatiel is equivalent to a dinner plate-sized portion for a person." Fresh water must be available continuously and dishes cleaned daily (VCA); LafeberVet's basic information sheet cites an average daily water intake around 13.6 mL/bird/day, while noting "considerable individual variation exists," so treat that as a rough reference, not a target. Life-stage notes: LafeberVet's basic information sheet gives weaning age as 42-52 days and sexual maturity as 6-12 months. Very young hatchlings are normally raised by their parents rather than owner hand-fed — Lafeber's own avian expert states day-one cockatiel chicks are "almost impossible to keep alive" if pulled that early for hand-feeding — which is why most pet owners acquire cockatiels only after they're already weaned onto solid food. I was not able to find a dedicated, vet-sourced hand-feeding schedule (feeds-per-day by chick age) in the sources I could access this session; that level of detail is typically breeder-specific and should come from an experienced breeder or avian vet rather than a general care article. Once weaned, sources don't distinguish a separate juvenile feeding routine from the adult one — both stay on the same free-choice, pellet-based diet, with breeding/laying hens needing extra calcium (see diet composition).

**Diet composition**
The foundation should be a formulated pellet diet — VCA Animal Hospitals puts this at 75-80% of daily intake, calling it the diet that "meets all nutritional needs," with fresh fruits and vegetables filling roughly 20-25% and seeds kept to "a very small portion" rather than the base diet. Lafeber's manufacturer/consumer content is somewhat softer on this ratio (see caveats) but agrees the pattern should be pellets + a modest amount of seed + fresh vegetables, citing their own Premium Daily Diet Pellets and seed/fruit "Nutri-Berries" products as examples of formulated options. On seeds specifically: VCA notes commercial seed mixes contain 4-10 seed types but are "high in fat and carbohydrates and provide a limited or imbalanced source of many nutrients," and birds will selectively pick favorites like black-oil sunflower and millet while skipping the more balanced components. LafeberVet's basic information sheet states plainly that "all-seed diets are deficient in protein, vitamins, and minerals including calcium and vitamin A," and Lafeber's consumer site calls all-seed feeding a leading cause of the malnutrition commonly seen in pet cockatiels, warning it's linked to fatty liver disease. Fresh produce should be washed thoroughly, cut into small pieces, with apple seeds removed (VCA). Small amounts of table food are acceptable in moderation — cooked lean meat, fish, egg, or a little cheese (VCA) — but processed foods should be avoided. Grit is NOT required: cockatiels hull seeds before swallowing them, so, unlike some other bird groups, they don't need grit to digest food — both VCA and the Merck Veterinary Manual warn that birds (especially sick ones) may over-consume grit, risking a potentially fatal gastrointestinal obstruction. Calcium deserves special emphasis for hens: Lafeber and VCA both recommend a calcium-fortified base diet or a cuttlebone to help prevent chronic egg-laying complications and egg-binding, because (per LafeberVet's reproductive-emergencies overview) a laying hen physiologically mobilizes calcium from her own long bones to build eggshells — so a calcium-poor, seed-heavy diet feeds directly into that risk. VCA notes that birds already eating 75-80% pellets generally don't need additional vitamin/mineral supplements, and any supplementation should be run past an avian vet first.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat items named across sources: apple (seeds removed), apricot, banana, blueberries, melon, mango, papaya, peach, pear, and strawberries; broccoli, carrots, leafy greens, bell peppers, pumpkin, squash, sweet potato, and zucchini (Lafeber's bird food guide); plus small amounts of cooked lean meat, fish, egg, or a little cheese (VCA). Toxic/unsafe items with the strongest consensus: avocado, flagged independently by VCA, Lafeber, and the Merck Veterinary Manual — the single most consistently-named toxic food across all three source types (consumer, manufacturer, and veterinary reference). Onion is also named as toxic by both VCA and Merck; chocolate by both VCA and Merck; caffeine and alcohol by VCA. Separately, iceberg lettuce and celery are flagged by both VCA and Lafeber, but as nutritionally empty/low-value foods to avoid rather than as toxins — worth keeping that distinction clear (empty-calorie "avoid" vs. genuinely toxic) since it's an easy point of confusion for owners.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Diet-conversion pickiness (seed-to-pellet switch)* (normal): VCA notes cockatiels, especially mature birds, can strongly resist converting from seeds to pellets, and the transition "may take days, weeks, or months"; the recommended fix is offering pellets when the bird is hungriest (morning) and mixing pellets with seed or moist food initially.
- *Heavy molting* (depends): VCA explains that growing a full new set of feathers sharply raises demand for protein/beta-keratin, calcium, and iron, and that "building lots of new feathers...can be very stressful and taxing on a bird's body," sometimes reducing overall activity.
- *Egg binding / dystocia* (concerning): A hen that can't pass an egg normally shows weakness, straining, reluctance to perch, and abdominal swelling; VCA states small birds "can die within a few hours of becoming egg-bound" due to compromised circulation and airway pressure from the trapped egg.
- *Seasonal hormonal/reproductive behavior* (depends): VCA notes cockatiels are triggered into nesting/reproductive behavior "in the fall" (unlike many parrot species that respond to longer daylight), and Lafeber describes hens as "notorious" for laying eggs without a mate present — the associated reproductive stress and calcium drain can suppress normal appetite/energy.
- *General illness/infection* (concerning): VCA's sick-bird signs — listlessness/withdrawal, sleeping fluffed on two feet, nasal discharge or clicking respiration, tail bobbing, and reduced preening — commonly accompany appetite loss in a genuinely sick bird; Merck's Veterinary Manual separately notes that seed-driven vitamin A deficiency (nasal discharge, sneezing, periorbital swelling, conjunctivitis) reflects chronic malnutrition that can compound illness.
- *New environment or handling stress* (normal): Commonly cited in general avian husbandry guidance as a reason newly-acquired or recently-moved cockatiels may eat less for a short adjustment period. I was not able to find a dedicated citation specific to cockatiels for this exact claim among the sources I could access this session (see caveats) — treat this entry as lower-confidence than the others.

**When to actually worry**
The clearest concrete number found: for egg-bound hens, VCA states small birds "can die within a few hours of becoming egg-bound" — this is a same-day emergency, not a wait-and-watch situation. More generally, VCA's illness-signs guidance (from their "Your Bird's Health" page) lists concrete red flags that warrant taking the bird to an avian vet "right away": becoming listless/withdrawn instead of active and playful, sleeping on two feet with feathers fluffed for warmth (rather than the normal one-foot-up resting posture), nasal discharge, bubbles from the beak or clicking respiratory sounds, tail bobbing (a sign of labored breathing), vomiting (distinguished from normal regurgitation by vomit sticking to the face/chest feathers), and neglected preening leaving the bird looking unkempt or ratty. Separately, Merck's Veterinary Manual notes that chronic seed-only feeding produces its own warning signs even without acute food refusal — nasal discharge, sneezing, periorbital swelling, conjunctivitis, and feather picking from vitamin A deficiency, plus obesity (defined there as roughly 20% over ideal body weight) with associated fatty liver, arthritis, and cardiac risk. I want to flag a genuine gap: I could not find a specific, sourced numeric threshold (e.g., a commonly-repeated "24-48 hours without eating" rule) for exactly how long a cockatiel can safely go without food before it's an emergency — my web search access was exhausted partway through this research, limiting me to direct-URL fetches of known vet sites afterward, and none of the pages I successfully retrieved stated that figure for this species. Rather than inventing a number, I'd recommend either sourcing that specific timeframe directly from an avian vet before publishing it, or keeping the guidance qualitative: any noticeable, sustained drop in eating combined with the illness signs above should prompt a same-day or next-day avian vet call, given how small cockatiels are and how fast their metabolism runs (per Lafeber's feeding guide).

**FunFact candidate**
When a hen cockatiel is preparing to lay, her body temporarily converts calcium from her own long bones into a dedicated reserve (a process LafeberVet's veterinary reproductive-emergencies overview calls "ossification of long bones or osteomyelosclerosis") specifically to supply the calcium needed for eggshell formation. It's a direct physiological explanation for why a calcium-poor, seed-heavy diet is so dangerous for hens: she's drawing shell-building material from her own skeleton, and a deficient diet leaves that reserve — and her — badly short.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Pellet-vs-seed ratio: VCA Animal Hospitals gives a clear clinical figure (75-80% pellets, 20-25% fresh produce, seeds a small minority) and frames all-seed diets as a leading cause of malnutrition. Lafeber's own consumer-facing "Bird Food Guide," by contrast, states "there's no clear superiority between seeds and pellets, and one is not necessarily superior to the other" — a notably softer stance, and one worth reading with the awareness that Lafeber sells both pellet AND seed/fruit-based products (Nutri-Berries). I've weighted the VCA/vet-sourced ratio as the more reliable figure for the article rather than silently averaging the two positions. 2) I could not find a sourced numeric "how many hours/days without eating is an emergency" threshold specific to cockatiels — my web search tool hit its session limit partway through this research, after which I could only pull already-known vet/avian URLs directly rather than search for new ones, and none of the pages I reached stated that figure. I left it out rather than inventing a commonly-repeated round number; flagging this explicitly per your note about a prior correction on this site for presenting a disputed figure as settled. 3) The "new environment/relocation stress" entry in notEatingCauses is standard, widely-repeated avian husbandry advice, but I did not find a dedicated citation for it specific to cockatiels in what I could access this session — treat it as lower-confidence than the other listed causes, all of which trace to a specific fetched source. 4) The 13.6 mL/day water-intake figure comes from a single source (LafeberVet's basic information sheet), which itself notes "considerable individual variation exists" — present it as a rough average, not a hard daily target.

## Dogs

### Golden Retriever

**Feeding schedule**
Puppies (under 12 months): feed a large-breed puppy formula 3-4 times daily on a consistent schedule, following the bag's age/expected-adult-weight guidelines (PetMD's Golden Retriever feeding guide). Large-breed puppy food matters specifically for Goldens because it's formulated to slow growth rate and protect developing joints ("too much growth too fast" is called out as a risk). Transition from puppy to adult food at 12-18 months, ideally over about a week (Hill's Pet, PetMD).
Adults (1+ years): transition to a large-breed adult formula and feed twice daily, morning and evening, with portions set by a veterinarian rather than strictly by bag instructions (PetMD). AKC's general feeding guidance echoes this: portion size should be based on body condition (visible waist, palpable ribs) and adjusted for activity level and climate — dogs in cold weather can burn 10-90% more energy than others.
Seniors: Golden Retrievers are typically considered "senior" starting around age 6 (earlier than small breeds), and senior formulas are recommended to match their slower metabolism and support healthy weight (Hill's Pet).
Caveat: none of the sources found gave a single universal "X cups per day" figure — all explicitly defer to a vet/kitchen-scale-based portion determined by the individual dog's weight, activity, and body condition rather than a fixed number, so I'm not presenting one.

**Diet composition**
Commercial dog food meeting AAFCO "complete and balanced" standards, in a large-breed-specific formula (puppy formula under 1 year, then large-breed adult) is the consistent recommendation across AKC, VCA, PetMD, and Hill's. Key nutrients called out specifically for the breed: omega-3 (fish oil) and omega-6 fatty acids for skin, coat, and joint health, and adequate protein — sporting/working-line Goldens doing field trials or high activity need more than sedentary companion dogs (Hill's Pet, PetMD). PetMD notes Goldens don't need extra vitamin/mineral supplementation on a complete diet, but under vet guidance owners sometimes add joint supplements (for hip/elbow dysplasia support), fish oil, or probiotics. Hill's recommends measuring food with a kitchen scale (not eyeballing scoops) and checking body condition monthly, since the breed is prone to weight gain. PetMD also recommends a slow-feeder bowl or food puzzle for Goldens specifically, to slow down eating and reduce digestive upset/vomiting risk.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treat items (AKC, in moderation, capped at roughly 10% of daily calories per Hill's Pet's "10% rule"): cooked eggs, plain cooked turkey and pork, fully cooked fish (salmon, sardines), plain cheese and yogurt (if not lactose intolerant), unsalted peanut butter and plain peanuts, plain bread, unsalted/unbuttered air-popped popcorn, honey, coconut, cashews, quinoa.
Unsafe/toxic foods (AKC + ASPCA): chocolate, coffee, and caffeine (methylxanthines — vomiting, tremors, seizures, worse with darker chocolate); grapes and raisins (linked to kidney damage); onions, garlic, and chives (allium species — red blood cell damage/anemia; AKC notes garlic is roughly five times more toxic than onion by weight); macadamia nuts (weakness, tremors, fever within about 12 hours per ASPCA); xylitol (sweetener causing hypoglycemia and potential liver failure); alcohol and raw yeast bread dough (bloating plus alcohol toxicity from fermentation); raw meat and raw eggs (Salmonella/E. coli risk); raw bones (GI obstruction risk); almonds (choking/airway obstruction if not chewed); cinnamon (mouth irritation, blood sugar drops); ice cream (sugar and lactose); excessive salt.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Illness/fever or viral infection* (concerning): AKC's veterinary source (Dr. Ann Hohenhaus) states dogs lose appetite with fever/illness the same way people do — described as the single most common reason for appetite loss.
- *Medication side effects* (depends): Antibiotics, chemotherapy drugs, and NSAIDs like carprofen (Rimadyl) can cause nausea/stomach upset that suppresses appetite (AKC).
- *Stress or major environmental change* (normal): Moving homes, rehoming, or losing a companion animal/person can trigger anxiety-driven appetite loss, similar to stress-related appetite changes in humans (AKC).
- *Owner absence / separation* (normal): As pack-oriented animals, some dogs refuse food while their owner is away and eat normally once the owner returns (AKC).
- *Food-related issues (formula change, stale/rancid food, preference)* (normal): A recent switch in food brand/formula, spoiled food, or simple pickiness about meal timing can cause temporary refusal (AKC).
- *Oral/dental pain (pseudo-anorexia)* (concerning): VCA distinguishes 'pseudo-anorexia,' where the dog wants to eat but physically can't — caused by stomatitis, gingivitis, loose teeth, TMJ problems, or oral tumors making chewing painful.
- *Systemic or organ disease* (concerning): VCA lists diabetes, kidney disease, cancer, GI nausea, and mineral imbalances (e.g., low potassium) as true-anorexia causes; poor appetite is described as strongly associated with underlying illness.
- *Bloat/GDV (gastric dilatation-volvulus)* (concerning): Not a cause of gradual appetite loss but an acute, life-threatening emergency where the stomach fills with gas and can twist; VCA lists feeding one large meal a day, eating quickly, and exercising right after eating as feeding-related risk factors relevant to how Goldens are typically fed and exercised.

**When to actually worry**
According to AKC's veterinary-sourced guidance, a dog going roughly a couple of days without eating is generally considered okay if there are no other symptoms — but veterinary care should be sought within 8-12 hours if the dog isn't eating and is also vomiting or has diarrhea (or both). VCA's anorexia resource is more conservative in tone but gives no specific hour/day cutoff, stating instead that any marked change in eating habits "warrants investigation" since poor appetite is strongly associated with illness and is one of the most common reasons owners bring dogs to the vet. Red-flag combinations to watch for: not eating plus lethargy, vomiting, diarrhea, weight loss, or coughing. Separately, for Goldens specifically, sudden restlessness, drooling, a visibly swollen/distended abdomen (especially on the left side), retching without producing vomit, or collapse are bloat/GDV emergency signs that require immediate emergency vet care — VCA states delaying treatment by even hours can be fatal.

**FunFact candidate**
In the Dog Aging Project's large-scale survey of over 50,000 dogs (Texas A&M, published 2025), Golden Retrievers and Labrador Retrievers — the two most common sporting breeds in the U.S. — scored highest of any breed group for "food motivation," with sporting-group dogs about 10% more likely than other dogs to be highly food-driven. Separately, University of Cambridge researchers identified a specific POMC gene deletion that hard-wires constant hunger and a roughly 25% lower calorie burn — but that mutation was documented in Labrador Retrievers (~25% carrier rate) and Flat-Coated Retrievers (~66% carrier rate), not in Golden Retrievers. So while Goldens are genuinely, measurably food-obsessed as a breed, the specific genetic mechanism proven in their Labrador cousins hasn't been confirmed in Goldens themselves — worth stating carefully rather than as one unified fact.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Food-motivation genetics: Some secondary/aggregator sources (e.g., StudyFinds) discuss Golden Retrievers in the same breath as the Cambridge POMC gene-deletion study, which can read as if Goldens carry the mutation too. The primary Cambridge source (cam.ac.uk) only names Labrador Retrievers (~25%) and Flat-Coated Retrievers (~66%) as carriers. Golden Retrievers' high food-motivation is independently confirmed by the separate, unrelated Dog Aging Project/Texas A&M survey — that part is solid — but do not attribute the specific gene mutation to Goldens without a study naming them.
2) Bloat/GDV breed risk: VCA's general bloat article names Great Danes, Saint Bernards, Weimaraners, and Doberman Pinschers as the highest-risk breeds (dogs over 100 lbs at ~20% lifetime risk), and does not list Golden Retrievers among that top tier. I could not find a source ranking Goldens among the highest bloat-risk breeds, so the article should mention general feeding precautions (avoid single large fast meals, slow-feeder bowls, no vigorous exercise right after eating) without overstating Golden-specific bloat risk to giant-breed levels.
3) "How long can a dog safely go without eating": only one source with a concrete number was found (AKC: ~2 days if otherwise asymptomatic, 8-12 hours if accompanied by vomiting/diarrhea). VCA's dedicated anorexia page discusses causes extensively but explicitly declines to give a timeframe. Present the AKC figure as sourced guidance rather than universal vet consensus, since it wasn't independently corroborated by a second primary source.
4) AKC's main Golden Retriever breed page (akc.org/dog-breeds/golden-retriever/) contained no feeding/nutrition content at all despite being the obvious canonical source — feeding-specific detail came from PetMD's and Hill's Pet's breed pages instead.

### German Shepherd

**Feeding schedule**
Puppy (per AKC): 6-12 weeks, four meals a day; 3-6 months, cut back to three meals a day (puppy should lose its rounded "puppy belly" look by around 12 weeks); 6-12 months, transition down to two meals a day. Because German Shepherds are a large breed (adult males run roughly 66-88 lb, females roughly 49-71 lb per breed-standard figures), AKC notes large-breed puppies (20+ lb as adults) mature slowly and can take 15-24 months to finish growing, so growth-formula feeding and portion caution should extend well past the "small breed done at 12 months" rule; VCA adds that some large-breed protocols keep puppies on growth food "closer to 18 or 24 months." Larger breeds generally shift to adult food around 12-14 months (AKC).

Adult (after ~1 year, though a GSD may still be filling out until 18-24 months): AKC says "most owners feed their adult dogs...split between two meals." VCA's general dog guidance says once-or-twice daily is acceptable, with many dogs doing best on two to three smaller meals. For this breed specifically, twice-daily feeding also has a safety rationale: AKC's bloat article cites research that dogs fed two meals a day have about half the GDV/bloat risk of dogs fed once a day - relevant because German Shepherds are a deep-chested breed commonly flagged for elevated bloat risk (see caveats).

Amounts: Neither AKC nor VCA gives one universal "cups per day" figure - both explicitly say to use the specific food's own feeding chart (based on weight/life stage), have a vet calculate calories from weight and activity level, and then adjust by body condition rather than what's left in the bowl. Breed-specific feeding calculators (caninebible.com, petcro.com) publish illustrative ranges - roughly 1.5 to 5 cups/day for growing puppies depending on age and weight, and estimated needs around 1,100-1,350 kcal/day at 4 months rising to roughly 1,375-1,650 kcal/day at 8 months - but these sites are breed-enthusiast resources, not veterinary-institutional sources, and I could not independently verify the underlying numbers via direct fetch (both blocked automated access), so treat them as rough estimates only, not settled figures.

Royal Canin's own GSD feeding program divides life stages as puppy (2-15 months), adult (15 months-5 years), and senior (starting around 8 years) - noted as one manufacturer's staging, not a universal standard.

**Diet composition**
Staple diet is a complete-and-balanced commercial dog food. For puppies, VCA specifically recommends "large-breed growth food, which slows their growing rate, but not final size," stating this "has been shown to decrease the incidence or severity of hip dysplasia in adults" - a real concern for this breed, which one North American study (cited via Wikipedia) found to have hip dysplasia at 18.9% and elbow dysplasia at 17.8%. Royal Canin's breed-specific formulation similarly emphasizes "precise mineral content (calcium and phosphorus)" during growth for bone/joint development.

Adult GSDs are described by VCA as typically maintaining a healthy weight naturally and generally not needing a special diet as they mature - management is mostly about restricting calories if the dog starts gaining weight. Royal Canin's adult GSD formula is built around "highly digestible" proteins to support digestive performance, reflecting the breed's known tendency toward digestive sensitivity. Fresh water should be available at all times.

A meaningful subset of German Shepherds need diet management beyond a normal maintenance food: Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI) is, per VCA, "suspected to be an inherited condition" in this breed specifically. Management requires a highly digestible, low-fat diet combined with pancreatic enzyme replacement (VCA names Viokase and Pancreazyme as examples) added at every meal, sometimes with vitamin B12 supplementation - a genuinely breed-linked dietary requirement, not generic advice.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Safe treats (per AKC): plain cooked lean meats such as turkey (skin, fat, and bones removed) and pork; fully cooked fish like salmon (no more than about twice a week) and shrimp (fully cooked, shell removed); plain unsalted peanut butter (checked carefully for NO xylitol); fully cooked eggs; small amounts of lower-fat cheese (cottage cheese, mozzarella) if the dog tolerates dairy; plain unsalted air-popped popcorn; unsalted cashews/peanuts in small quantities; plain bread in small amounts (no raisins, no spices); quinoa; and from AKC's produce guide: apple (cored, seeds removed), banana, blueberries, cantaloupe, cranberries, cucumber, mango (pit removed), orange flesh (peel/seeds removed), pear (pit/seeds removed), pineapple (peeled), pumpkin puree, raspberries (moderation - naturally contain some xylitol), strawberries, watermelon (rind/seeds removed), carrots, celery, green beans, peas, and small amounts of broccoli or Brussels sprouts.

Unsafe/toxic foods (per AKC and ASPCA): chocolate and caffeine (methylxanthines - vomiting, tremors, seizures, worse with darker chocolate); grapes and raisins (linked to acute kidney failure); onions, garlic, and chives (Allium plants damage red blood cells and cause anemia - AKC notes garlic is "five times more toxic" than the rest of that plant family); xylitol (artificial sweetener - causes hypoglycemia and potential liver damage); macadamia nuts (AKC calls them "one of the most poisonous foods for dogs" - weakness, tremors, hyperthermia); avocado (persin toxin plus high fat, pancreatitis risk); alcohol and raw yeast dough (alcohol toxicity, plus the dough itself can expand and cause dangerous stomach bloating - worth flagging for this specific breed given its bloat predisposition); cherries, and the seeds/pits of apples, pears, peaches, and mangoes (trace cyanide); almonds (choking/esophageal blockage risk if not chewed); cinnamon; raw or undercooked meat and eggs (Salmonella/E. coli risk); and excessive salt. Given this breed's documented predisposition to sensitive digestion, pancreatitis-type risk, and EPI, high-fat table scraps and fried foods are a particular concern beyond the general toxicity list.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Owner absence, travel, or a new/unfamiliar environment* (normal): AKC notes dogs are pack animals and "may not eat when their owners are away," and VCA lists stress or environmental change among causes of appetite loss.
- *Individual quirks (only eats at certain times, dislikes a new food batch/formula change, food gone stale)* (normal): AKC lists time-of-day feeding preferences and dislike of stale, expired, or reformulated food as ordinary, non-medical reasons a dog skips a meal.
- *Short-term illness (e.g., a viral bug)* (depends): AKC calls illness the most common reason dogs stop eating, noting a dog "may not eat for a couple of days" while fighting it off before appetite returns on its own.
- *New medication (antibiotics, chemotherapy, NSAIDs like Rimadyl)* (depends): AKC specifically names these drug classes as common causes of a temporarily upset stomach and reduced appetite.
- *Dental or oral pain (gum disease, loose teeth, mouth/throat inflammation, jaw pain, oral masses) - "pseudo-anorexia"* (concerning): VCA distinguishes this as the dog wanting to eat but being physically unable to; it requires a vet/dental exam to resolve, not just patience.
- *Systemic illness - kidney disease, diabetes, GI obstruction or ulcers, nausea from any cause, cancer, poison exposure, fever* (concerning): VCA lists these as causes of "true anorexia" (the dog genuinely doesn't want food) and stresses that changes in eating pattern "may signal something serious" and warrant early vet involvement.
- *Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI) - breed-linked* (concerning): Counter to typical appetite loss, VCA describes GSDs with EPI as showing an INCREASED appetite alongside progressive weight loss over months, plus chronic bulky/greasy stools, because the pancreas isn't producing enough digestive enzymes to absorb nutrients from food eaten normally or even eagerly.
- *Bloat / Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus (GDV) - not refusal to eat, but an acute eating-adjacent emergency* (concerning): A veterinary source (VIN's Veterinary Partner) states German Shepherds and Boxers "appeared to have a greater risk for bloating than did other breeds," and VCA's own emergencies page uses a German Shepherd as its example: restlessness after a large meal and unproductive attempts to vomit signal the start of GDV. AKC's dedicated bloat article notes shock can set in "within an hour or two" if untreated.

**When to actually worry**
Combining AKC and PetMD veterinary guidance: for an adult dog that is otherwise acting normal (still walking, drinking, no other symptoms), AKC says a couple of days without eating isn't automatically alarming (most often it's a self-resolving minor illness), and PetMD frames the safe self-monitoring window similarly as 24 to 48 hours. That window shrinks sharply the moment other symptoms appear - AKC specifically advises contacting the vet within 8-12 hours if the dog isn't eating AND is vomiting or has diarrhea. Neither AKC's nor VCA's articles gave a separate, explicit hour-count for puppies specifically (VCA's anorexia article states plainly that "no specific timeframe is provided," only that changes should be taken seriously and a vet involved early) - as a large-breed puppy still growing, a young GSD skipping meals generally warrants faster follow-up than an adult, though I did not find a sourced numeric threshold specific to puppies. Separately and urgently: a distended/bloated abdomen combined with restlessness, retching, or unproductive attempts to vomit after eating is not a "wait and see" situation - this is the bloat/GDV pattern specifically illustrated with a German Shepherd on VCA's emergencies page, and AKC's bloat article notes it can progress to shock within an hour or two without treatment.

**FunFact candidate**
German Shepherds are one of the breeds most linked to Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI), which VCA describes as "suspected to be an inherited condition" in this breed. What makes it counterintuitive: affected dogs don't lose their appetite - they often eat with increased appetite, sometimes ravenously - yet still progressively lose weight over months and develop soft, bulky, greasy-looking stools, because their pancreas isn't producing enough digestive enzymes to actually absorb the nutrients from all that food. So for this breed, an eating disorder can look like the opposite of "not eating" - a dog that eats plenty and still wastes away is a textbook, breed-linked red flag, not a diet win.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
1) Bloat/GDV breed-risk ranking is genuinely inconsistent across sources. AKC's own dedicated bloat article lists its highest-risk breeds as Great Danes, St. Bernards, Weimaraners, Irish Setters, Gordon Setters, Standard Poodles, and Doberman Pinschers - it does NOT name German Shepherd in that list. But a veterinarian-authored article on VIN's Veterinary Partner states "The German Shepherd dog and the Boxer appeared to have a greater risk for bloating than did other breeds," and VCA's own emergencies page uses a German Shepherd as its illustrative bloat example. All sources agree GSDs, as a large, deep-chested breed, carry elevated bloat risk versus small/barrel-chested breeds; they simply disagree on exactly how GSD ranks against the very highest-risk breeds. Separately, some breed-enthusiast sites (germanshepherdplanet.com, puppylongevity.com) cite a specific "15-20% lifetime risk, second-highest after Great Danes" figure for GSDs - I could not verify this against a primary veterinary or academic source, so I'm not presenting it as an established statistic; treat it as unverified.

2) Specific daily feeding amounts (cups/day, kcal/day by age) found on breed-calculator sites (caninebible.com's ~1.5-5 cups/day for puppies; petcro.com's ~1,121-1,343 kcal/day at 4 months and ~1,374-1,646 kcal/day at 8 months) could not be directly verified by fetch - both sites blocked automated access, so these come only from third-party search-snippet summaries of those pages, not a source I read firsthand. The higher-confidence, consistently repeated guidance from AKC and VCA is to feed according to the specific food's own feeding chart plus a vet-calculated calorie target and body-condition monitoring, explicitly rejecting a single universal number, since caloric density varies enormously between brands/formulas.

3) "How long is OK without eating" is framed slightly differently across sources: AKC's veterinary content says a couple of days is not concerning for an otherwise-normal adult dog with no other symptoms (with an 8-12 hour vet-contact window if vomiting/diarrhea is also present), while PetMD frames the safe monitoring window as 24-48 hours. These aren't really contradictory (a couple of days is roughly 48 hours) but the exact wording differs and neither gives a separate number for puppies specifically.

4) One fetch of Royal Canin's GSD breed page returned an apparently garbled height figure ("60-65 inches" tall, which is anatomically implausible for this breed - almost certainly a unit-conversion/scraping error, likely cm misread as inches). I did not use that number; instead I relied on the well-corroborated breed-standard figures (~24-26 in / 66-88 lb for males, ~22-24 in / 49-71 lb for females).

### French Bulldog

**Feeding schedule**
Sources agree puppies need frequent small meals that taper with age, but disagree slightly on adult frequency. Newborn/nursing pups (0-8 weeks, if hand-rearing): fed every 2-4 hours around the clock, roughly 180ml of puppy milk replacer per kg of body weight per day divided across feedings (Dr. Kim Podlecki, DVM, via Dogster) — a Frenchie should never leave a breeder before 8 weeks old. Growing puppies (8 weeks to about 1 year): both PetMD and Dogster's veterinarian author agree on 3-4 meals a day on a consistent schedule, using a food explicitly labeled "puppy" (not an "all life stages" formula) and the weight-based chart on the bag as a starting point. Adults (roughly 1-7 years): PetMD says twice daily; Dogster's vet author instead recommends 2-3 times daily, reasoning that splitting food into more, smaller meals lowers perceived bloat risk versus one large daily meal — this is a real, unresolved difference in guidance, not a typo. Seniors (roughly 7-8+ years): PetMD and Royal Canin both say to move to a senior-specific formula; Royal Canin adds that senior formulas typically bring in joint-support glucosamine/chondroitin and a dental-friendly kibble texture. No source found in this research gave a specific cup or gram amount that applies breed-wide — every source that addressed portions (PetMD, Dogster) says the right amount depends on the individual food's calorie density and the dog's weight/body condition, and directs owners to the feeding chart on the specific bag or a vet-calculated Resting Energy Requirement rather than a fixed number.

**Diet composition**
All sources agree on a base of complete-and-balanced, AAFCO-approved commercial food matched to life stage (PetMD), with wet or dry food both acceptable as long as it's properly balanced (Dr. Podlecki, DVM, via Dogster), and raw or homemade diets discouraged for puppies specifically because their immune systems are still developing (same source). Royal Canin's breed-specific line (a manufacturer source, read with that in mind) describes puppy kibble shaped for the breed's short muzzle, highly digestible protein, prebiotics for gut flora, and EPA/DHA for skin-barrier support; adult formulas built around a minimum 24% protein plus L-carnitine and digestive-support fiber; and senior formulas adding joint-support glucosamine/chondroitin and a tooth-scrubbing kibble texture. PetMD separately recommends a quality omega-3 supplement for the breed given its predisposition to skin allergies and back problems, and says overweight Frenchies should move to a calorie-restricted formula since extra weight worsens both back-injury risk and airway complications. Because French Bulldogs are brachycephalic and prone to swallowing air while eating (aerophagia), Dogster's vet-reviewed coverage (reviewed by Dr. Ashley Darby, BVSc) recommends slow-feeder/sectioned bowls and smaller, more frequent portions to cut down on air-gulping, plus routinely limiting naturally gas-forming ingredients like broccoli, cabbage, beans, dairy, and soy.

**Safe / unsafe foods**
Per AKC's people-food guide (general to dogs; no French-Bulldog-specific exceptions found), safe treat-only foods include: fully cooked plain eggs; cooked salmon or sardines (fatty acids good for joints/brain); air-popped unsalted popcorn; unsalted, xylitol-free peanut butter; cooked turkey or pork with excess fat and seasoning removed; plain low-fat cheese and plain yogurt in small amounts if the dog isn't lactose intolerant; a few unsalted cashews; and small amounts of plain bread (never bread containing raisins). Unsafe/toxic foods per the same source: chocolate (methylxanthines disrupt metabolic processes, causing vomiting, diarrhea, seizures, and potentially death); macadamia nuts, called "one of the most poisonous foods for dogs" (vomiting, elevated body temperature, inability to walk, lethargy); garlic, stated to be "five times more toxic to dogs" than onions (produces anemia — pale gums, elevated heart rate, weakness, collapse); xylitol (flagged specifically as a peanut-butter additive to check for); almonds (choking/blockage risk, can tear the windpipe if not fully chewed); and cinnamon in quantity (mouth irritation, excessive blood-sugar drops, vomiting/diarrhea, breathing difficulty from inhaled powder). AKC's page also flags raisins as unsafe in passing without detailing symptoms. Specific to this breed, Dogster's vet-reviewed gas article additionally flags broccoli, cabbage, beans, dairy, and soy as foods to limit — not toxic, but likely to compound the breed's already heightened flatulence and GI sensitivity from BOAS.

**Why they stop eating**
- *Teething (puppies)* (normal): Sore gums during teething can make puppies reluctant to eat, especially dry kibble; per Dr. Beth Turner, DVM (Preventive Vet), softened food or cooling teething toys usually resolve it.
- *Heat / high temperatures* (depends): Preventive Vet and easyvetanswers both note dogs commonly eat less in hot weather. For a brachycephalic breed like the French Bulldog this deserves closer watching, since the same anatomy that suppresses appetite in heat also raises heat-stroke risk.
- *Stress or environmental change (new home, moving, routine changes, new pet/family member)* (normal): Cited by VCA Hospitals, Preventive Vet, and easyvetanswers alike as a common, usually short-lived trigger for skipped meals, especially around adoption or a move.
- *Picky eating / boredom with food* (normal): Some dogs simply tire of the same food or hold out for people food. Preventive Vet notes true pickiness is comparatively rare as a cause specifically in puppies.
- *Dental pain (gum disease, abscessed or fractured teeth)* (concerning): VCA Hospitals lists oral/dental pain as a leading 'pseudo-anorexia' cause: the dog wants to eat but physically hurts too much to chew.
- *GI disease (food allergies, inflammatory bowel disease, pancreatitis, parasites, obstruction)* (concerning): Flagged by both VCA Hospitals and Preventive Vet as a common medical driver of appetite loss, including in puppies, where parasites and viral infection are a concern.
- *Underlying systemic illness (kidney disease, cancer, hypothyroidism, organ dysfunction)* (concerning): VCA Hospitals lists these among the serious 'true anorexia' causes, where the dog shows no interest in food at all rather than being physically unable to eat.
- *BOAS-related gastrointestinal signs (breed-specific)* (concerning): Because of the breed's airway anatomy, French Bulldogs with brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome (BOAS) frequently show GI signs like regurgitation or reduced appetite; a study cited in Dogster's vet-reviewed piece found 93% of Frenchies with BOAS also had GI signs, which improved after corrective airway surgery.
- *Competing with other pets at mealtime* (normal): easyvetanswers notes some dogs hold back from eating when they feel they must compete with other animals for food; usually resolves by feeding pets separately.

**When to actually worry**
Two vet-credentialed sources converge on the same puppy figure: Dr. Beth Turner, DVM (Preventive Vet) says puppies "can't go longer than 12 hours without food" because they lack fat reserves, and specifically flags toy/small breeds as being at even greater hypoglycemia risk — directly relevant to a small breed like the French Bulldog. easyvetanswers (vet-review claimed but individual author credentials unclear) independently cites the same 12-hour figure for puppies and a 24-48 hour threshold for adults before an emergency vet visit. By contrast, VCA Hospitals' clinical overview of canine anorexia deliberately avoids a fixed hour count, stating "poor appetite or refusal to eat is strongly associated with illness," that any change in eating pattern "warrants investigation," and advising owners to involve the vet early rather than wait for a specific timeframe. Per Preventive Vet (Dr. Turner), see a vet if a puppy skips one meal and shows no interest in the next, if vomiting or diarrhea appear, if diarrhea lasts over 12 hours, if the puppy seems weak/lethargic or shows hypoglycemia signs, or immediately if there are dehydration signs like pale/whitish gums or sunken skin. For adults, the consistent thread across every source is: appetite loss plus lethargy, vomiting, weight loss, or any other new symptom means don't wait it out — call the vet.

**FunFact candidate**
French Bulldogs' notorious flatulence is tied directly to their feeding anatomy, not just their diet. Because of brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome (BOAS) — their shortened palates, narrow tracheas, and pinched nostrils — Frenchies tend to gulp air (aerophagia) while eating or straining to breathe, and that swallowed air comes back out as burping or gas. A study cited in Dogster's vet-reviewed coverage (reviewed by Dr. Ashley Darby, BVSc) found that 93% of Frenchies with BOAS also had gastrointestinal signs, and those GI symptoms improved after corrective airway surgery — meaning the breed's legendary gas problem is often a downstream symptom of how it's built to breathe, not just what it ate.

**⚠ Disputed / conflicting claims found**
Two disagreements are worth flagging rather than smoothing over. (1) Adult feeding frequency: PetMD says feed adult French Bulldogs twice a day; Dogster's veterinarian author (Dr. Kim Podlecki, DVM) instead recommends 2-3 meals a day, arguing multiple smaller meals lower perceived bloat risk versus one large daily meal. Neither source pairs a frequency with a portion-size number, so "twice vs. three times daily" is a real difference in professional guidance here, not a settled fact. (2) How long is safe without food: easyvetanswers cites 12 hours (puppies) / 24-48 hours (adults) before an emergency vet visit, and Preventive Vet's veterinarian author (Dr. Beth Turner, DVM) independently corroborates the 12-hour puppy figure — but VCA Hospitals' anorexia overview (an institutional hospital source) deliberately declines to give any fixed hour threshold, instead telling owners to contact a vet early whenever eating patterns change rather than waiting for a specific number of hours. The 12h/24-48h figures are presented because a named DVM corroborates the puppy number, but readers should know VCA treats any fixed hour count as an oversimplification. Also: no source found in this research gave a specific cups-per-day or grams-per-day portion figure for French Bulldogs by weight; several lower-tier SEO sites that surfaced in search (promising exact "feeding charts") were not fetched or verified, so no specific portion numbers are included here — treat any site claiming a precise breed-wide cup amount with caution unless it's tied to a named food's calorie density.
