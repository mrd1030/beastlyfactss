## Dental Disease

The most common chronic health issue chinchillas face. Their teeth grow continuously, and without enough coarse hay to grind them down naturally, spikes or impacted roots can develop. Watch for drooling (sometimes called "slobbers"), a wet chin, dropping food while trying to eat, reduced appetite, and weight loss.

This needs an exotic vet's oral exam, and often skull X-rays under sedation to see the full picture. ## Heat Stroke: The Danger Many Owners Underestimate

Chinchillas handle cold reasonably well but are genuinely fragile in heat. A useful rule of thumb: add the temperature in Fahrenheit to the humidity percentage, and treat anything over 150 as dangerous. So 85°F combined with 65% humidity, adding up to 150, is already in risk territory, even though neither number alone sounds extreme. Watch for lethargy, drooling, rapid or panting breathing, bright red mucous membranes, and collapse in severe cases. This is a genuine emergency with real risk of organ failure or death, and it can develop faster than owners expect in a room that simply isn't cool enough. <FunFact>
GI problems in chinchillas are frequently just downstream of a dental problem going unnoticed. The same drooling and dropped food that signal tooth trouble are also worth treating as an early warning for digestive trouble, not two unrelated issues to track separately.
</FunFact>

## GI Problems

Often secondary to dental pain, poor diet, stress, or certain antibiotics. Look for bloating, reduced or absent droppings, and diarrhea.

## Fur Chewing and Barbering

A behavioral issue, sometimes stress-related, where a chinchilla chews or pulls at its own fur or a cage-mate's. This is a different thing entirely from fur slip, the startle-response fur release covered in our [Chinchilla handling guide](/blog/chinchilla-handling-guide/).

## Ringworm

A transmissible skin fungus, worth knowing about since it can spread between animals and, in some cases, to people handling an infected chinchilla.

## Fur Ring (Males)

Shed fur can constrict around the penis, which is an urgent issue requiring prompt attention, not something to wait out.

## When to See an Exotic Vet

See a vet promptly for drooling, changes in appetite or stool, diarrhea, any respiratory signs, or any indication of heat stress. Because chinchillas mask illness so effectively and can decline fast once symptoms become visible, err toward calling sooner rather than waiting to see if things improve on their own.
