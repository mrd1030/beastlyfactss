# Cockatiel Cage Setup: Width, Safety, and the Diet Debate

<AffiliateDisclosure />

A cockatiel's cage is the single biggest factor in its day-to-day wellbeing, and a few specific details, width, bar spacing, and placement, matter more than overall size alone. This covers the setup itself, for costs and ongoing supplies, see our [Cockatiel cost guide](/blog/cockatiel-cost-guide/), or the full [Cockatiel care guide](/guides/cockatiel/) for everything else.

## Cage Size and Shape

Reference tables commonly list a minimum of about 20 by 20 by 30 inches for a single cockatiel, though many keepers aim closer to 24 by 24 by 30 inches for more comfortable movement. Prioritize width over height, cockatiels are horizontal flyers, so a tall, narrow cage wastes exactly the space they'd actually use. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4wuT808" product="Prevue Steel Bird Flight Cage with Wheels (Large)">wide flight-style cage</AffiliateLink> built for this kind of lateral space is worth the extra footprint.

## Bar Spacing, Non-Negotiable

Bar spacing needs to be half an inch or smaller to prevent head entrapment, a genuine and avoidable injury risk with wider spacing.

<FunFact>
The emphasis on cage width over height reflects how cockatiels actually fly in the wild: short, fast, horizontal bursts between perches rather than the vertical climbing typical of some other parrot species. A floor-to-ceiling cage can look impressive in a store display without giving a cockatiel any of the flight space it actually needs.
</FunFact>

Include foraging toys for enrichment, and position <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3Ref6FW" product="Detachable Stainless Steel Feeding Dishes (Okllen)">food and water dishes</AffiliateLink> so droppings can't fall into them.

## Placement and the Kitchen Danger

Keep the cage away from the kitchen entirely. Overheated non-stick or PTFE-coated cookware releases fumes that are rapidly fatal to birds, documented cases describe multiple birds dying within 30 minutes of exposure to fumes from a single accidentally overheated pan, and the danger isn't limited to birds in the same room, the fumes travel. This is one of the few genuinely non-negotiable placement rules in bird keeping, covered from the health side in our [Cockatiel health issues guide](/blog/cockatiel-health-issues-guide/).

Also avoid aerosols, scented candles, drafts, and direct sun on the cage.

## Sleep

Cockatiels need 10 to 12 hours of quiet, dark sleep every night. They're prone to night frights, sudden panicked flapping in the dark, so a cage cover or a small night light can help prevent injuries during these episodes.

## Diet: Pellets First, Seeds as a Treat

The nutritional consensus favors <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4566tQT" product="ZuPreem FruitBlend Pellets for Cockatiels/Lovebirds">pellets</AffiliateLink> making up 60 to 80% of the diet, with fresh vegetables (especially vitamin-A-rich ones) covering another 20 to 30%, and seeds limited to 10% or less, treated as an occasional treat rather than a dietary foundation. A seed-only diet is directly linked to the nutritional diseases covered in the [health issues guide](/blog/cockatiel-health-issues-guide/), so this isn't a minor preference, it's a real health decision.

Provide a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4fbLrVy" product="Penn-Plax E-Cuttlebone">cuttlebone or mineral block</AffiliateLink> for calcium, along with fresh water daily. Ten to twelve hours of UV light exposure, or safe supervised outdoor time, supports healthy vitamin D levels.

## Cleaning Routine

Spot-clean daily and do a deeper clean weekly. This is a small habit that prevents a lot of the bacterial and fungal issues that come from a dirty cage environment.
