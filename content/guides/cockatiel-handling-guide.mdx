# How to Handle and Tame a Cockatiel

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Taming a cockatiel is a trust-building process more than a technique, and it goes faster when you let the bird set the pace rather than trying to rush toward a step-up cue on day one. For everything else about day-to-day care, see our full [Cockatiel care guide](/guides/cockatiel/).

## Build Trust Before You Touch

Sit near the cage and talk softly, offering treats like <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4vEqqcq" product="Kaytee Spray Millet Treat">millet</AffiliateLink> through the bars at first. Once your cockatiel is comfortable with that, progress to offering treats with your hand actually inside the cage. New birds need a genuine adjustment period, give it 7 to 14 days with no handling attempts before starting this process.

## Teaching the Step Up

Offer a flat palm with your fingers pointing down and your thumb tucked in, or use a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4pbY2ge" product="Bird perches (varied diameter)">handheld perch</AffiliateLink>, as the surface to step onto. Avoid offering a single upright finger, most birds find this uncomfortable to grip and are more likely to bite it than step onto it.

Keep sessions short, 10 to 15 minutes is plenty, and let your cockatiel's comfort level dictate how quickly you move forward rather than working to a fixed timeline.

<FunFact>
A cockatiel's crest works almost like a mood dial. Held upright and relaxed, it signals calm curiosity; flattened tight against the head with a rigid body, it signals fear or aggression; swept sharply forward, it can mean startled alertness. Learning to read crest position before a bite happens is one of the more useful skills in taming an unfamiliar bird.
</FunFact>

## Signs of Fear or Stress

- Hissing
- A flattened or agitated crest
- Lunging
- Tail-flicking
- Fluffed feathers combined with rapid breathing
- Scrambling to the far side of the cage
- Neck-stretching

## Signs of Comfort

- Soft chirps
- Preening
- Beak grinding
- A relaxed, upright crest

If you see stress signs, back off and try again later. Never chase a loose bird to try to catch it, a panicked, exhausted bird can collapse or fly into a window hard enough to injure itself, and forcing the interaction sets back the trust you're trying to build.

## Daily Interaction Matters

Cockatiels are social birds and need regular interaction to stay well-adjusted, this is part of why the taming process is worth the patience it takes. A cockatiel that trusts you is also simply easier and safer to handle for routine things like cage cleaning, nail trims, or a [vet visit](/blog/cockatiel-health-issues-guide/).
