# Common Guinea Pig Health Issues (And Why Vitamin C Matters So Much)

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Guinea pigs mask illness well, which means routine care matters more than it might seem to when everything looks fine. A handful of conditions come up often enough that every owner should be able to recognize them. ## Upper Respiratory Infections

Caused by bacteria like *Bordetella bronchiseptica* or *Streptococcus* species. This one is genuinely urgent: guinea pigs are obligate nasal breathers, meaning they can't fall back on mouth-breathing the way many other animals can if their nasal passages are blocked.

**Watch for:** sneezing, nasal or eye discharge, and labored breathing. All of these warrant a same-day vet visit rather than a wait-and-see approach.

## Vitamin C Deficiency (Scurvy)

Guinea pigs, like humans, can't produce their own vitamin C, which makes this a genuinely species-specific concern. Deficiency weakens the immune system, slows wound healing, and disrupts normal bone and tooth development. This is almost entirely preventable through diet, covered in full in our dedicated [Guinea Pig Scurvy guide](/blog/guinea-pig-scurvy-vitamin-c-guide/) and in the diet section of our [tank setup guide](/blog/guinea-pig-tank-setup-guide/). <FunFact>
Humans and guinea pigs aren't the only animals that lost the ability to make their own vitamin C. Certain primates and fruit bats share the same quirk, all through the same broken gene, evolving independently of each other over millions of years rather than inheriting it from one shared vitamin-C-deficient ancestor.
</FunFact>

## Dental Disease

Guinea pig molars can overgrow even when the front incisors look completely normal, which is part of why this condition catches owners off guard.

**Watch for:** drooling, weight loss, and unusually small droppings. Chronic cases sometimes need trimming under anesthesia every 4 to 6 weeks, which is part of why prevention through a proper <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4h9WW2u" product="Timothy hay (Oxbow Western Timothy Hay, 90 oz)">hay-based diet</AffiliateLink> matters so much.

## GI Stasis

Life-threatening and can develop quickly. A guinea pig that stops eating and is passing very little or no stool needs urgent veterinary attention, not a wait-and-see approach. Ongoing vet costs for issues like this are covered in our [Guinea Pig cost guide](/blog/guinea-pig-cost-guide/).

## Skin Mites and Ringworm

Both cause itching, hair loss, and flaky skin. Worth noting: ringworm and some mite infestations are zoonotic, meaning they can spread to people in the household, so these are worth treating promptly rather than letting them run their course.

## A Few Other Conditions to Know

- **Bumblefoot (pododermatitis)**, often linked to wire flooring or unsanitary bedding. Solid-floor housing with clean <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4voH9Ap" product="Small-pet paper bedding">paper bedding</AffiliateLink> goes a long way toward preventing it.
- **Bladder stones (urolithiasis)**, related to excess calcium, oxalate, or phosphate in the diet.
- **Ovarian cysts**, which show up in intact females.

## When to See a Vet

Because guinea pigs hide illness well, an annual wellness exam matters even when your guinea pig seems completely healthy. See a vet promptly, not on a wait-and-see basis, for refusal to eat, drooling, labored breathing, diarrhea, lethargy, or a hunched posture. A cavy-savvy exotic vet is worth locating before an emergency, since general small-animal clinics aren't always equipped for guinea pig-specific issues.
