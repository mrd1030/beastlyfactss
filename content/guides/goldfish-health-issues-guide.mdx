# Common Goldfish Health Issues (And What Actually Prevents Them)

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Goldfish are hardier than their reputation suggests when kept in the right conditions, but a handful of issues come up often enough to be worth knowing before they show up in your own tank. Prevention leans heavily on the setup itself, our [Goldfish tank setup guide](/blog/goldfish-tank-setup-guide/) covers the tank size and filtration side of that, and see our full [Goldfish care guide](/guides/goldfish/) for everything else.

## Ich (White Spot Disease)

Caused by the parasite *Ichthyophthirius multifiliis*. Look for tiny white spots that resemble grains of salt across the body and fins, along with flashing (rubbing against tank surfaces) and clamped fins. This is one of the more treatable common ailments with the right medication.

## Fin Rot

Frayed or disintegrating fin edges, usually bacterial and closely tied to water quality. Correcting the underlying water conditions is as important as any direct treatment, and keeping up with water changes using a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3Rg5gTT" product="Water conditioner">water conditioner</AffiliateLink> is part of that correction.

## Swim Bladder Disorder

Shows up as buoyancy problems, floating at the surface, sinking to the bottom, or swimming at odd angles. Often linked to constipation, diet, or simply the unusual body shape of fancy goldfish varieties. A short fast followed by a single cooked, de-shelled pea is a common first response, and switching to sinking or gel-based foods going forward reduces the risk of it recurring.

<FunFact>
The at-home fix for a lot of swim bladder cases is almost comically simple: a short fast followed by a single cooked, de-shelled pea. It works because swim bladder problems are so often tied to constipation and diet, especially in round-bodied fancy varieties, rather than anything more serious going on internally.
</FunFact>

## Dropsy

A swollen belly with scales that stand out in a pinecone-like pattern. This is usually a visible symptom of organ or kidney failure rather than a standalone disease, and it frequently carries a poor prognosis.

## Fungal Infections

Cottony white patches that typically develop after an injury or a period of stress gave an opening for fungus to take hold. Keeping handling to a minimum helps avoid creating that opening in the first place, covered in our [Goldfish handling guide](/blog/goldfish-handling-guide/).

## Ammonia Poisoning

From an uncycled or poorly maintained tank. This is entirely preventable with proper cycling and a regular water change schedule. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4p1T7yd" product="Water test kit">water test kit</AffiliateLink> lets you catch an ammonia spike before it becomes a visible problem, rather than finding out after the fact.

## When to See a Vet

Aquatic vets exist and are worth consulting for persistent loss of buoyancy, severe fin damage, visible ulcers, ongoing lethargy or appetite refusal, or when an over-the-counter treatment isn't working. That said, most of what's on this list is prevented, not treated, through consistent water quality, quarantining new fish before adding them to an established tank, and appropriate feeding.
