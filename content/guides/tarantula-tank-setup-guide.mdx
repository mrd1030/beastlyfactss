# Tarantula Enclosure Setup: Shape, Substrate, and a Humidity Debate

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Tarantula enclosures are refreshingly simple compared to most exotic pets, no complex lighting, no elaborate temperature gradients. The details that do matter, though, matter quite a bit, especially enclosure shape and substrate depth. This covers the setup itself, for what all of it costs, see our [Tarantula cost guide](/blog/tarantula-cost-guide/), or the full [Tarantula care guide](/guides/tarantula/) for everything else.

## Enclosure Shape and Size

Most commonly kept pet tarantulas are terrestrial, and the enclosure should be **horizontal, wider than it is tall**, specifically to limit fall risk. A reasonable adult minimum is around 20 by 10 by 10 inches, or roughly a 10-gallon tank, sized to about three times the tarantula's leg span. Keep the overall height low, under about 12 inches, since falls are a genuine danger for most tarantulas.

Use a secure lid, but avoid mesh tops. A tarantula's feet can get caught in mesh and cause real injury. Acrylic with drilled ventilation holes is the better choice.

## Substrate

Use at least 3 inches of depth, filling roughly half to two-thirds of the enclosure. Coconut fiber, peat, or organic pesticide-free soil all work well, they hold moisture appropriately and support natural burrowing behavior.

<FunFact>
Many wild terrestrial tarantulas spend much of their time in burrows they dig themselves or take over from other animals. Substrate depth in captivity isn't just about holding moisture, it lets a tarantula express a burrowing instinct it retains even in an enclosure where it will never actually need to escape a predator.
</FunFact>

## Furnishings

Provide at least one hide, <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3SKmfOx" product="Cork bark round hide">cork bark</AffiliateLink>, a half-log, or half of a terracotta pot all work. Add a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/44hAzR9" product="Small/shallow water dish">shallow water dish</AffiliateLink>, changed daily, and keep it shallow enough that there's no drowning risk.

## Temperature

Room temperature, roughly 70 to 80°F, is fine for most homes with no supplemental heat needed. If your space runs cold, use a heat mat mounted on the side of the enclosure, never the bottom, paired with a thermostat. ## Humidity

Sources genuinely disagree here. Some care guides recommend a moderate 40 to 60% for many commonly kept dry-adapted species, while others, often written from a more clinical or exotic-vet perspective, suggest 65 to 75%. In practice, the most consistent approach is a reliable water dish combined with lightly moistened substrate, using a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4vPKMjX" product="Fine mist spray bottle">fine mist spray bottle</AffiliateLink> sparingly, rather than heavy misting, which raises mold risk without a clear benefit.

## Lighting

None required. Most pet tarantulas are nocturnal, and bright light or direct sun should be avoided rather than added.

## Diet

Feed appropriately sized crickets, <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4uFr6hU" product="Dubia Roaches (feeder bag)">dubia roaches</AffiliateLink>, or mealworms, nothing larger than the tarantula's abdomen. Adults typically eat every 1 to 2 weeks, or about 3 to 5 insects spread across a week, slings (young tarantulas) eat more frequently. Remove any uneaten prey within 24 hours, live insects left in the enclosure can actually injure or stress a tarantula, particularly one that's close to a molt, covered in more detail in our [Tarantula health issues guide](/blog/tarantula-health-issues-guide/).

House tarantulas singly. Most species are solitary and cannibalistic, cohabitation isn't a workable setup regardless of enclosure size.
