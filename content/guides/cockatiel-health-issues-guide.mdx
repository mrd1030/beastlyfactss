## Nutritional Disease From Seed-Only Diets

This is the most common preventable illness in pet birds. As one avian practice puts it plainly, birds that eat only seeds, especially sunflower seeds and peanuts, are the most prone to hypovitaminosis A, a vitamin A deficiency that shows up across multiple body systems. Standard seed mixes provide vitamin A at levels well below what a cockatiel actually needs.

Calcium deficiency, sometimes leading to a condition called nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism, is common too, and cockatiels are noted as especially prone to it. <FunFact>
Wild cockatiels live in flocks where predators single out the weakest-looking bird, so hiding illness is a hardwired survival strategy, not a choice a bird makes. A cockatiel can mask sickness well enough that owners often don't notice anything is wrong until the illness has already progressed, which is exactly why routine wellness exams matter so much for this species specifically.
</FunFact>

## Reproductive Disease

Chronic egg-laying, egg binding, soft or shell-less eggs, and oviduct prolapse are all common, even in single female cockatiels with no male present. Egg binding specifically is a life-threatening emergency, not something to wait out at home.

## Feather Destructive Behavior

Often traced back to boredom, loneliness, or stress rather than a purely medical cause. If your cockatiel starts plucking or damaging its own feathers, both environmental changes and a vet visit are worth pursuing, since the underlying cause isn't always obvious from the outside. Consistent, patient handling, covered in our [Cockatiel handling guide](/blog/cockatiel-handling-guide/), goes a long way toward preventing the boredom and stress that often sit behind this.

## Obesity and Fatty Liver Disease

A high-fat, seed-heavy diet combined with limited activity leads here. This ties directly back to the nutrition point above, diet really is the common thread running through most of this list.

## Psittacosis

Caused by *Chlamydia psittaci*. Worth knowing about specifically because it's zoonotic, meaning it can pass to people in the household.

## Foreign Body and Toxin Ingestion

Cockatiels are noted as frequent offenders when it comes to ingesting rope fibers or heavy metal objects, curiosity plus a beak built for exploring makes this a real risk in a home that isn't fully bird-proofed.

## The Kitchen Danger Worth Repeating

Overheated non-stick cookware releases fumes that are extremely dangerous, often fatal, to pet birds within minutes. This isn't a minor caution, it's one of the most serious preventable risks a bird owner can control simply by keeping birds out of the kitchen and being careful with cookware.

## When to Call an Avian Vet

Because cockatiels hide illness so effectively, don't wait on: discharge from the eyes, nose, or mouth, bleeding, changes in droppings, sitting on the cage floor instead of perching, persistently fluffed feathers, tail-bobbing or labored breathing, or any sudden change in appetite or behavior. Annual wellness exams are worth doing even when nothing seems wrong.
