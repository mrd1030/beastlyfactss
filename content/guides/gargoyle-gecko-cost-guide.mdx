# How Much Does a Gargoyle Gecko Really Cost?

Gargoyle geckos sit at an interesting price point: genuinely affordable for a common reticulated animal, but morphs and proven breeding lines can push the number into four figures. For the full care picture beyond budgeting, see our complete [Gargoyle Gecko care guide](/guides/gargoyle-gecko/).

## The Animal Itself: $50 to $1,000

Common reticulated or striped animals start as low as $50, with most typical pet-trade geckos landing in the $100 to $300 range. Color and pattern are the real price drivers, not sex or personality. Reticulated grey, black, and white, the original wild-type look, stays cheapest. Striped, red-striped, and orange-striped animals run higher, and saturated "super red" or selectively bred white and black lines can reach $500 to $1,000 or more, especially from proven breeding lines.

That's the gear alone. Add a common reticulated or striped gecko and a typical first setup runs roughly $280 to $745. Add a premium morph instead and the total climbs past $1,000. Some keepers swap the coconut fiber and loose decor for a bioactive starter kit, which bundles substrate and hardscape together. Either way, most of this gear only needs replacing once every few years, this species doesn't need a dedicated heat source in most homes, which is one reason the setup stays cheaper to run than a desert species, covered in detail in our [Gargoyle Gecko tank setup guide](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-tank-setup-guide/).

<FunFact>

## Vet Costs

A routine exotic exam commonly runs $50 to $150. A common-illness visit with diagnostics runs $150 to $600, and advanced care, imaging or surgery, can reach $800 to $2,500 or more. There's no gargoyle-specific fee data out there, these are general exotic reptile vet pricing benchmarks. Our [Gargoyle Gecko health issues guide](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-health-issues-guide/) covers what typically drives an unplanned visit, mostly humidity mistakes in one direction or the other.

## The Long-Game Cost

15 to 20 years is typical for this species, with some individuals still breeding past 20. That's the real cost-of-ownership number to sit with, this isn't a short-term pet, and the setup you buy now should be one you're comfortable maintaining for decades.
