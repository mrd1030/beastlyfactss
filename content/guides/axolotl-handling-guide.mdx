# Should You Handle an Axolotl? Only When You Truly Have To

Axolotls are not a hands-on pet, and this isn't a preference, it's about their physical vulnerability. Handling should be minimal and reserved for genuine necessity, not casual interaction. For everything else about day-to-day care, see our full [Axolotl care guide](/guides/axolotl/).

## Why Hands-Off Is the Right Default

Axolotls have sensitive, scaleless skin and delicate external gills, the feathery structures on either side of their head. Unlike fish, they don't have a robust protective slime layer to fall back on when touched. Handling risks physical damage to the skin and gills, real stress, and an increased chance of infection at any point of contact, the kind of injury covered in more detail in our [Axolotl health issues guide](/blog/axolotl-health-issues-guide/).

Their skeleton is also largely cartilage rather than solid bone, which is part of why keeping pressure and air exposure to a minimum matters so much. This is a genuinely fragile animal in ways that aren't obvious just from looking at one.

## When Handling Is Unavoidable

Sometimes a move is genuinely necessary: tank maintenance, a water change that requires temporarily relocating the axolotl, or a health check. When that happens:

- Guide the axolotl into a submerged container while it's still underwater, rather than lifting it out by hand or scooping it into a net.
- Avoid nets entirely if you can. The fine gill filaments and toes can snag in mesh, causing real injury.
- Keep the axolotl in water at all times during the process. Air exposure and physical pressure are both genuine risks given how much of their skeleton is cartilage rather than bone.

<FunFact>
Those feathery gills aren't just decoration, they're doing the actual work of breathing. That's exactly why a snagged gill filament in a net is a bigger deal for an axolotl than a scrape would be for most animals, it can interfere with how the animal takes in oxygen, not just how it looks.
</FunFact>

## Bringing a New Axolotl Home

Rather than handling a new arrival to "settle it in," acclimate through drip-matching, slowly introducing your tank's water to the bag or container the axolotl arrived in so temperature and water chemistry shift gradually rather than all at once. This avoids the stress of a sudden environmental change without requiring any direct handling at all. Getting the tank itself right before your axolotl ever arrives makes this whole process easier, see our [Axolotl tank setup guide](/blog/axolotl-tank-setup-guide/) for the full setup.

## The Honest Takeaway

If part of what you want from a pet is physical interaction, an axolotl isn't going to offer that, and pushing for it isn't good for the animal. The relationship here is built through observation and careful tank maintenance, not touch, and that's worth knowing before you commit to keeping one.
