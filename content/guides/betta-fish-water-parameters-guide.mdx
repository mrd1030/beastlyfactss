# Betta Fish Water Parameters: The Numbers That Actually Matter

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Most betta fish problems that get blamed on "bad luck" or a "weak fish" trace back to one thing: water chemistry nobody was actually measuring. A betta in a beautifully decorated tank with the wrong parameters will decline just as fast as one in a bare tank - decor doesn't fix chemistry. For the full housing, diet, and enrichment picture, our [Betta Fish care guide](/guides/betta-fish/) covers the rest of the setup; this article is just about the numbers in the water itself.

Notice that ammonia and nitrite have a target of zero, not "low." Unlike temperature or pH, there's no safe nonzero baseline for either - a cycled, established tank should read exactly 0 ppm for both, consistently.

## Why "Just Change the Water" Isn't Specific Enough

Generic advice to "do regular water changes" skips the part that actually matters: you're managing a nitrogen cycle, not just diluting mess. Beneficial bacteria colonies convert toxic ammonia (from waste and uneaten food) into nitrite, then into far less harmful nitrate. In an uncycled tank - most commonly a brand-new setup - those bacteria colonies don't exist yet, and ammonia has nowhere to go but up.

This is why bettas frequently decline in the first two weeks of a new tank: the tank looks clean, but the biological filtration hasn't established. <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3SSRCGG" product="API Freshwater Master Test Kit">A liquid-reagent test kit</AffiliateLink> (not paper strips, which are notoriously imprecise) is the only reliable way to know whether a tank is actually cycled before adding a fish.

<FunFact>
A cycling tank often shows a predictable pattern: ammonia rises first, then drops as nitrite spikes, then nitrite drops as nitrate rises. This whole process typically takes 4 to 6 weeks without a fish in the tank - which is why "fishless cycling" before ever buying a betta is the safest approach.
</FunFact>

## Does Water Hardness (GH/KH) Actually Matter?

General hardness (GH) and carbonate hardness (KH) get far less attention than the "big five" parameters above, but they're not irrelevant. GH measures dissolved minerals like calcium and magnesium; KH measures the water's buffering capacity - its ability to resist pH swings. Bettas tolerate a fairly wide GH and KH range, but very soft, poorly buffered water (common with reverse-osmosis or heavily filtered tap water) can allow pH to crash suddenly after a water change, which is far more stressful than a stable pH that's slightly outside the "ideal" range.

If a liquid test kit consistently shows very low KH alongside unstable pH readings between water changes, a small, consistent dose of a buffering product (sold at any aquarium supplier) resolves it. This is a secondary fix, though - get ammonia, nitrite, and temperature right first, since those matter far more.

## Testing Schedule That Actually Works

- **New or recently set up tanks:** test ammonia and nitrite every 1-2 days until both consistently read zero for at least a week.
- **Established, cycled tanks:** test weekly, and always test immediately if the betta seems off - clamped fins, lethargy, or loss of appetite often shows up in the water before it shows up as a visible symptom.
- **After any water change over 30%:** a quick recheck confirms the new water didn't introduce chlorine or chloramine (always dose with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4tXcbib" product="API Tap Water Conditioner">dechlorinator formulated for both</AffiliateLink>).

## The One Number Keepers Get Wrong Most Often

Temperature gets treated as a nice-to-have, but it directly affects everything else on this list.
