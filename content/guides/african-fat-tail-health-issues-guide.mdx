## Metabolic Bone Disease (MBD)

Widely described as the most common serious issue in this species, and it can be fatal if it progresses far enough. Caused by calcium or vitamin D3 deficiency, often alongside missing UVB.

**Watch for:** lethargy, difficulty standing or walking, swollen joints, muscle twitches, bones that feel unusually flexible, and spinal curvature.

## Retained Shed (Dysecdysis)

Fat-tailed geckos, like leopard geckos, go through a complete shedding cycle and are genuinely prone to shed getting stuck, particularly on the toes and tail tip, when humidity runs too low or there's no moist hide available. This is the single most common fat-tail-specific problem, and it's almost entirely preventable.

Retained skin that constricts can cut off circulation and cause tissue loss if not addressed. It's manageable at home with a proper <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B008YDHDD2?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Exo Terra Gecko Cave for Reptiles and Amphibians, Reptile Hideout, Medium, PT2865">moist hide</AffiliateLink> lined with <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4bpnvNm" product="Sphagnum moss (for moist hide)">damp sphagnum moss</AffiliateLink> and correct humidity, see a vet if constriction or tissue death has already started.

The fat stored in this species' tail isn't just a curiosity, it's a genuine energy reserve, similar to what a leopard gecko carries. ## Impaction

Terrestrial geckos feeding on loose substrate can accidentally ingest it, leading to a gut blockage.

**Watch for:** bloating, lethargy, appetite loss, and straining.

Reduce risk with appropriate substrate choices and correctly sized prey. Our [African Fat-Tailed Gecko tank setup guide](/blog/african-fat-tail-tank-setup-guide/) covers the substrate debate for this species in more detail, since the risk is more about an already unhealthy gecko than the substrate itself.

## Egg-Binding, Tail Loss, and Eye Issues

Females can experience egg-binding (dystocia), a genuine emergency requiring immediate vet care. Tail loss happens through rough handling or fright, covered in more detail in our [African Fat-Tailed Gecko handling guide](/blog/african-fat-tail-handling-guide/). Eye problems are also relatively commonly reported in this species and are worth a vet visit if you notice cloudiness, swelling, or discharge that isn't tied to a normal shed cycle.

## The Husbandry Connection Worth Remembering

No moist hide, or an enclosure that's simply too dry overall, is the direct cause of the most common fat-tail health complaint: stuck shed on the toes and tail. Keeping the enclosure too cool and too damp at the same time causes respiratory infection. Skipping calcium, D3, and UVB causes MBD. Get the setup right and most of this list never shows up, the same is largely true of the closely related [leopard gecko](/blog/leopard-gecko-health-issues-guide/), where humidity and calcium mistakes drive most of the same conditions.
