# Is It Legal to Own a Ball Python? State-by-State Guide

Ball pythons are one of the most commonly kept pet snakes in the US, and also one of the most needlessly worried-about when it comes to the law. Years of news coverage about invasive Burmese pythons taking over the Everglades has left a lot of owners assuming any "python" might be swept up in the same restrictions. It isn't. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Ball Python care guide](/guides/ball-python/).

### Why the Federal "Python Ban" Doesn't Touch Ball Pythons

The US Fish and Wildlife Service's 2012 rule under the Lacey Act lists four species as injurious wildlife: Burmese python, Northern African python, Southern African python, and yellow anaconda. The rule's own discussion of import statistics specifically named ball python as one of a dozen heavily imported constrictor species that were considered for the list and left out. A 2017 court ruling, *USARK v. Zinke*, later struck down part of how the interstate-transport restriction applied to the species that are actually listed, but that case has no bearing on ball pythons either way, since they were never part of the listing to begin with.

### Hawaii and New York City: The Two Real Exceptions

<FunFact>
Hawaii bans every species of snake statewide, with narrow exceptions only for a couple of male nonvenomous snakes at a government zoo and a handful of sterile detector-dog training animals, so a ball python is illegal there the same as any other snake. New York City takes a different approach, banning the entire family Pythonidae under its health code, which catches ball pythons by taxonomy even though the ordinance never names the species individually. Outside these two, Florida and Georgia's prohibited-species rules both explicitly stop at the large invasive pythons and leave ball pythons off entirely.
</FunFact>

## The Real Takeaway

Ball pythons are broadly legal across the US, and the federal and state rules aimed at invasive constrictors were deliberately written to exclude them. Hawaii and New York City are the two places where that doesn't matter, since both ban them through a different mechanism entirely, a total snake ban and a family-wide ban, respectively. Everywhere else, check local city or county ordinances the way you would for any exotic pet, but don't assume the Everglades headlines apply to your snake.
