# Is It Legal to Own a Chinchilla? State-by-State Guide

Chinchillas have a reputation problem more than a legal one. Search around and you'll find posts claiming California or Texas restrict ownership, but neither claim survives a look at the actual regulatory text. The real legal story here is a treaty most people have never heard of, and a fur-trade history that has nothing to do with keeping one as a pet. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Chinchilla care guide](/guides/chinchilla/).

### The CITES Detail Worth Knowing

Wild chinchillas were hunted to near-extinction for their fur, and the long-tailed chinchilla is now IUCN-listed as Critically Endangered, with an estimated 10,000 or fewer left in the wild. That's why the genus sits on CITES Appendix I, the same tier as species like the giant panda. But the listing carries a specific annotation stating that specimens of the domesticated form aren't subject to the Convention's restrictions. Since every pet chinchilla in the US today comes from decades of captive breeding, this treaty has essentially zero effect on buying, breeding, or owning one domestically.

### A Real Enforcement Story, Just Not About Pet Owners

<FunFact>
In October 2021, a USDA administrative law judge permanently revoked the dealer license of the nation's only USDA-licensed research-chinchilla supplier, Moulton Chinchilla Ranch in Minnesota, and fined the owner $18,000 after inspectors documented over 100 Animal Welfare Act violations between 2013 and 2018, including untreated wounds and filthy cages. It's a genuinely current, well-documented federal enforcement action, but it targeted a commercial breeder-dealer under Animal Welfare Act licensing rules; it has no bearing on private pet ownership.
</FunFact>

## The Fur Law People Confuse With a Pet Ban

California's AB 44, effective January 2023, banned the commercial sale of new fur products statewide, and chinchilla fur is often mentioned alongside mink and fox in coverage of the law. But the statute regulates the sale of fur products and pelts, full stop. It never mentions live animals or pet ownership, so it has no connection to keeping a pet chinchilla, despite the species' historical association with the fur trade fueling some confusion.

## The Real Takeaway

We could not find a single US state, county, or major city that actually bans owning a pet chinchilla. The states most often named by blog posts, California and Texas, both explicitly permit it once you check the real rule text. The only genuine paperwork requirement anywhere is Hawaii's standard import process for bringing a non-native mammal into the state, and even that isn't specific to chinchillas.
