# Is It Legal to Own a Box Turtle? State-by-State Guide

Box turtles get treated very differently from a species like the red-eared slider. There's no invasive-species angle here, since box turtles are native across most of the US. Instead, the law is almost entirely built around one idea: these turtles reproduce so slowly that even modest wild collection can crash a local population for good. That shapes both how you can acquire one and, in a few states, how you're allowed to keep one you already have. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Box Turtle care guide](/guides/box-turtle/).

### The States Where Even Legal Ownership Comes With Strings

Most people assume that once a box turtle is legally yours, the law stops caring. Florida, West Virginia, Indiana, Louisiana, South Carolina, and Kansas all disagree to some degree. Florida's cap of two turtles per person applies no matter how you acquired them, and the state expects you to be able to show a receipt with the purchase date, species, and supplier. West Virginia's 2021 overhaul went furthest: even owners who'd had a pet box turtle for years needed to file for a state authorization letter by a March 2022 deadline just to keep it legally going forward.

### Why the Law Treats Box Turtles This Way

<FunFact>
Box turtles typically don't reach sexual maturity until they're 8 to 10 years old, and lay only three or four eggs a year, with very few hatchlings surviving. Wildlife agencies describe this as a species where "removing even a few adult turtles can destabilize a local population for decades," and long-term field studies have documented population crashes of 70 to 80 percent in some intensively studied areas over several decades. That slow-reproduction biology is the reason behind nearly every wild-collection restriction on this list, not an invasive-species concern like you'd see with a slider.
</FunFact>

## A Real, Recent Enforcement Case

In January 2026, a Mississippi man was sentenced to a year of federal probation and a $3,000 fine under the Lacey Act for possessing and selling three-toed box turtles without the state's required commercial propagation permit, then exporting them through New York to China. It's part of a broader, ongoing pattern; wildlife-trafficking researchers have tracked dozens of major turtle-trafficking cases across more than a dozen states since 2018, box turtles included.

## The Real Takeaway

Don't assume a box turtle you find in the yard is yours to keep; check your state first, since several genuinely restrict or ban this outright. And don't assume a legally purchased, captive-bred box turtle comes with zero conditions either; Florida, West Virginia, and a handful of other states attach real caps or paperwork requirements even to turtles you bought fair and square.
