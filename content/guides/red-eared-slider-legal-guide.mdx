# Is It Legal to Own a Red-Eared Slider? State-by-State Guide

Red-eared sliders are the most common pet turtle in the US, and also one of the most misunderstood when it comes to the law. Most people have heard of "the turtle ban," but that federal rule doesn't actually stop anyone from owning one. What actually restricts red-eared sliders is a patchwork of state invasive-species laws, and one state just rewrote its rules with a deadline that's coming up fast. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Red-Eared Slider care guide](/guides/red-eared-slider/).

<LegalDisclaimer />

## The Federal Rule Everyone Misunderstands

Since 1975, the FDA has banned the *sale* of viable turtle eggs and live turtles with a shell under 4 inches, under 21 C.F.R. § 1240.62. This was a public health measure, not a wildlife law: small turtles were linked to a significant share of salmonellosis cases in children who put them in their mouths. A 2010 industry lawsuit challenging the rule failed, and it remains in force today, unchanged. Critically, the rule regulates commercial sale and distribution, not private ownership. You can legally own a red-eared slider of any size in all 50 states under this particular law.

### Colorado's Overhaul Is Happening Right Now

<FunFact>
This one is genuinely time-sensitive. Colorado Parks and Wildlife approved a full herpetofauna rule overhaul (Chapter W-7) in March 2026, effective May 1, 2026, which formally classifies red-eared sliders as an "Invasive Herptile" for the first time. Anyone may now take unlimited red-eared sliders from the wild, and wild-caught invasive herptiles must normally be euthanized, except sliders, which may be kept alive for the animal's life. If you already owned a red-eared slider before the rule took effect, you must file a CPW Legacy Report by July 30, 2026 to keep it legally. That deadline is days away as of this writing.
</FunFact>

### Connecticut and North Carolina

Connecticut bans importing, buying, selling, or exchanging red-eared sliders outright, and separately prohibits releasing them into state waters, under Conn. Gen. Stat. Ann. § 26-78. North Carolina's rule is broader still, prohibiting import, transport, purchase, sale, and possession, with permits reserved for research and education. A grandfather clause lets owners who already had a slider before August 1, 2018 keep, transport, or export it.

### Vermont's New Law

Vermont's Act 47 (2025) prohibits importing, possessing, or selling a red-eared slider unless it was legally acquired as a pet before July 1, 2025, or is held under a commercial pet-dealer permit. A separate provision in the same act bans intentionally taking any reptile or amphibian from the wild in Vermont starting January 1, 2027.

## Everywhere Else: Generally Legal, But Never Legal to Release

Outside of the states above, red-eared sliders don't require a special permit in most of the country. California, for example, has no slider-specific ban and allows keeping one, though releasing any aquatic animal into state waters is illegal under general law, and wildlife officials note sliders compete with native western pond turtles for food and basking sites. Minnesota treats it as a "regulated invasive species," legal to possess, buy, and sell, but never to release. The pattern holds nationwide: ownership is usually fine, release is not.

## The Real Takeaway

If you already own a red-eared slider, the law almost never asks you to give it up, but it may ask you to do paperwork, especially right now in Colorado. If you're thinking about getting one, check your specific state before you buy, since restrictions here vary more by state than almost any other pet on this site. And wherever you live, never release a pet turtle into a pond or lake; it's illegal nearly everywhere and harmful to native turtle populations.
