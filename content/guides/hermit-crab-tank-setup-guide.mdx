# Hermit Crab Enclosure Setup: Humidity, Deep Substrate, and Extra Shells

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Hermit crab care is genuinely different from most small-pet setups, this is a species that needs both land and water access, and its most important requirement, humidity, is the one first-time owners most often get wrong. This covers the setup itself, for costs, see our [Hermit Crab cost guide](/blog/hermit-crab-cost-guide/), or the full [Hermit Crab care guide](/guides/hermit-crab/) for everything else.

## Tank

Use a glass terrarium, roughly a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4pjiyeT" product="10 gallon tank">10-gallon tank</AffiliateLink> for 2 to 3 small crabs, more for larger crabs or bigger groups. The lid needs to seal in humidity, glass or acrylic works best; if you're using a screen lid, cover part of it with plastic wrap or tape to help retain moisture. Keep at least two crabs together, they're a genuinely social species, covered in more detail in our [Hermit Crab handling guide](/blog/hermit-crab-handling-guide/).

## Temperature

Aim for 75 to 85°F, with the warm side around 80°F. Use a side- or back-mounted heat mat. **Never place a heat mat under the tank,** heat rising up through deep substrate can be dangerous, even fatal, to a crab that's buried or molting underneath.

## Humidity: the Single Most Important Number in This Whole Setup

Maintain 75 to 85% relative humidity. This isn't a nice-to-have range, it's essential, hermit crabs breathe through modified gills that must stay moist to function, and inadequate humidity leads directly to the dehydration and gill suffocation covered in our [Hermit Crab health issues guide](/blog/hermit-crab-health-issues-guide/).

<FunFact>
A hermit crab's gills are an evolutionary in-between, not quite the true gills of its aquatic ancestors, not quite the lungs of a fully land-adapted crab. That halfway design is exactly why it needs consistently moist air to breathe at all, even though it would drown if kept fully submerged.
</FunFact>

Monitor with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">hygrometer</AffiliateLink>, and maintain the range through a combination of moist substrate, a well-sealed lid, and a dedicated moss pit. Avoid over-misting or flooding the enclosure, the goal is consistent humidity, not standing water everywhere.

## Substrate

Mix <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0B52DC9VB?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Fluker's Hermit Crab Sand Substrate">play sand and coconut fiber</AffiliateLink> at roughly a 5:1 ratio, aiming for a "sandcastle consistency" that holds a tunnel shape when packed. Depth matters here: at least 6 inches, or about 3 times the height of your largest crab, since hermit crabs burrow to molt and need genuine room to do it. Moisten with dechlorinated water, using saltwater for the first wetting can help prevent mold. Skip calcium sand, it cements when wet, and avoid gravel or mulch.

## Water: Both Fresh and Marine, Both Required

Provide two separate <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eKyN0W" product="Large soak-able water dish">soakable water dishes</AffiliateLink>, one with dechlorinated fresh water, one with marine saltwater made from an <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B000255NKA?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Instant Ocean Sea Salt (50 Gallons)">aquarium salt mix</AffiliateLink>, never table salt. Both should be deep enough for a crab to fully submerge but include an easy way to climb back out, hermit crabs can and do drown if they can't exit. Use ceramic or otherwise non-metal dishes, hermit crabs are sensitive to metal.

## Shells: the Detail That's Easy to Overlook

Offer <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0CGM3P8SK?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="12PCS Natural Hermit Crab Shells">multiple natural, unpainted shells</AffiliateLink> per crab, ideally 3 to 5 or more, spanning a range of sizes so your crab has room to size up as it grows. Purple Pinchers specifically tend to prefer shells with round, turbo-style openings. Painted shells are toxic and should never go in the enclosure. Our [Hermit Crab cost guide](/blog/hermit-crab-cost-guide/) breaks down what a decent shell rotation actually adds to the budget.

## Diet

Hermit crabs are omnivorous scavengers. Offer a varied diet with calcium sources like cuttlebone, fresh fruit and vegetables, protein like dried shrimp or insects, and leaf litter. Feed in the evening, since they're most active at night, and remove leftovers the next morning.

## Lighting

A standard 12-hour light, 12-hour dark cycle works well. Hermit crabs are nocturnal, and there's no established UVB requirement for this species.

Once this setup is in place, most of what shows up in our [Hermit Crab health issues guide](/blog/hermit-crab-health-issues-guide/) simply won't happen, humidity and substrate are doing the preventive work.
