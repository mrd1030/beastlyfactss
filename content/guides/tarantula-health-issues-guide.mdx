# Common Tarantula Health Issues (Prevention Matters More Than Treatment)

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Veterinary care for tarantulas is genuinely limited, many areas simply don't have a vet who treats invertebrates. That reshapes how tarantula health works in practice: prevention through correct husbandry does most of the heavy lifting, and knowing what's normal versus what's actually wrong matters more than knowing what to do once something's gone wrong. Get the enclosure right first, our [Tarantula tank setup guide](/blog/tarantula-tank-setup-guide/) covers the husbandry side, and see our full [Tarantula care guide](/guides/tarantula/) for everything else.

## Dehydration

The most common issue keepers run into. Look for a shriveled, wrinkled abdomen, lethargy, and what's sometimes called a death curl, the legs curling in tightly underneath the body. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/44hAzR9" product="Small/shallow water dish">shallow water dish</AffiliateLink> and correct humidity, checked with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">thermometer/hygrometer combo</AffiliateLink>, prevent most cases, and mild dehydration often resolves quickly once conditions are corrected.

## Molting Complications

A stuck or incomplete molt can be fatal. The best prevention is maintaining proper humidity and, critically, never disturbing or feeding your tarantula during a molt, our [Invertebrate Molting 101 guide](/blog/invertebrate-molting-guide/) covers what a normal molt looks like across species. Once a molt is complete, wait about a week for the new exoskeleton to fully harden before offering food again.

## Falls and Trauma

A ruptured abdomen or visible hemolymph (the tarantula equivalent of blood) leaking from an injury is a real emergency. Some keepers can stem a minor leak with a small amount of cornstarch applied to the wound, but anything beyond minor bleeding needs specialist attention if you can find it, and often the outcome depends on how quickly you can act. Most falls happen during attempted handling, our [Tarantula handling guide](/blog/tarantula-handling-guide/) covers why this is a species best left in its enclosure.

<FunFact>
Hemolymph doesn't stay confined to vessels the way mammal blood does, it flows freely through the body cavity and bathes the internal organs directly. That's part of why even a moderate abdominal rupture can turn fatal so quickly, there's no closed circulatory system holding things together while the injury is dealt with.
</FunFact>

## Oral Nematodes

Show up as white material around the mouthparts along with decreased appetite. This is rare in captive-bred spiders and can be treated by a vet under anesthesia when one is available, but it's genuinely uncommon.

## Mites and Mold

Both are husbandry issues rather than illnesses in the traditional sense. Improving ventilation and general enclosure sanitation resolves most cases.

## Dyskinetic Syndrome (DKS)

A poorly understood condition causing a loss of motor coordination. Unfortunately it's often fatal, and there isn't a well-established treatment.

## The Thing That Isn't Actually a Health Problem

A tarantula that stops eating for weeks is very often just entering a pre-molt period, this is completely normal behavior, not a sign of illness. Don't panic or assume something is wrong based on appetite alone.
