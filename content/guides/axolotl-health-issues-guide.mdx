# Common Axolotl Health Issues (Most Trace Back to Temperature)

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Axolotls are hardy in the right conditions and surprisingly fragile outside them. A large share of what goes wrong traces back to water that's too warm, which makes temperature control the single most important thing to get right. Get the tank itself dialed in first, our [Axolotl tank setup guide](/blog/axolotl-tank-setup-guide/) covers the husbandry side, and see our full [Axolotl care guide](/guides/axolotl/) for everything else.

## Fungal Infection

Shows up as a white or grey, cotton-like growth on the gills or body. It's typically triggered by warm water, poor water quality, stress, or an existing injury that gave the fungus an opening. Treatment starts with correcting water quality and cooling the tank down with the help of a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4wCcGzE" product="Aquarium chiller, 1/3 HP compressor (79 gal capacity)">chiller</AffiliateLink>, and salt baths can help. Severe cases need a vet-prescribed antifungal.

## Impaction

Caused by ingesting gravel or substrate that's too large to pass. Look for bloating, floating instead of resting on the bottom, appetite loss, and an absence of waste. Mild cases sometimes resolve with fasting and cooler water under a vet's guidance, but severe impaction can require surgical intervention. This is almost entirely preventable by using only fine sand or a bare-bottom tank, more detail on that in our [Axolotl tank setup guide](/blog/axolotl-tank-setup-guide/).

## Ammonia and Nitrite Burns

Red, inflamed patches on the skin or gills, usually from an uncycled or poorly maintained tank. Water changes and a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3Rg5gTT" product="Water conditioner">dechlorinator</AffiliateLink> correct this, but the underlying fix is making sure your tank is properly cycled and regularly checked with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4p1T7yd" product="Water test kit">water test kit</AffiliateLink> before you rely on it.

<FunFact>
An axolotl's gills are a surprisingly good early warning system. They tend to curl or angle forward under heat stress well before more obvious signs like appetite loss ever show up, which makes them worth checking daily, not just when something already looks wrong.
</FunFact>

## Heat Stress

This is the condition that ties back to temperature most directly, and it's worth taking seriously. Watch for curled or forward-facing gill tips, appetite loss, floating, and pale patches on the skin. Extended exposure above roughly 72°F starts to suppress the immune system, and temperatures above about 75°F can be fatal. If you see these signs, dropping the water temperature immediately is the first and most important response, a reliable <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">thermometer</AffiliateLink> catches the problem before it gets to this point, and our [Axolotl cost guide](/blog/axolotl-cost-guide/) breaks down what a proper chiller setup runs.

## When to See a Vet

Find an aquatic or exotic-amphibian vet before you need one, general small-animal clinics often aren't equipped for this species. See a vet promptly for fungal growth that hasn't improved within 48 hours of correcting water quality, suspected impaction, red streaking or open wounds that suggest a bacterial infection, or appetite loss lasting 5 days or more.

Most of what shows up on this list traces back to a setup issue rather than bad luck, which is worth remembering the next time the water looks a little warmer than usual.
