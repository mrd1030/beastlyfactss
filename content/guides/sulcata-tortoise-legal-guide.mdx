# Is It Legal to Own a Sulcata Tortoise? State-by-State Guide

Sulcata tortoises aren't a "which states ban it" story the way a lot of exotic pets are. No state prohibits owning one. The real legal fact worth knowing is a federal import rule from 2000, and the real reason this species keeps showing up in rescue headlines has nothing to do with legality at all. It has to do with just how big these tortoises get. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Sulcata Tortoise care guide](/guides/sulcata-tortoise/).

### The Federal Rule Behind Why Pet Sulcatas Are US-Bred

In March 2000, USDA prohibited importing live sulcata tortoises, along with the leopard tortoise and Bell's hingeback tortoise, after finding they carry exotic ticks that vector heartwater disease, a livestock illness with mortality rates above 60 percent in cattle and sheep. Interstate movement of tortoises already in the country still requires a veterinarian to certify the animal is tick-free within 30 days of transport. The practical result: virtually every pet sulcata sold in the US today is domestically captive-bred, not imported, and that's been true for about 25 years.

### California's Desert Tortoise Mix-Up

Sulcata tortoises are not restricted under California law, but they're frequently confused with the California desert tortoise, a native, federally protected species that is illegal to keep as a pet in the state. If you're shopping for a sulcata in California, this is worth double-checking with a seller, since the two are easy to conflate by name alone.

### The Size Problem, Not a Legal One

<FunFact>
An Arizona sanctuary owner told NPR in May 2026 that her rescue alone takes in roughly 1,250 surrendered sulcatas a year and refers another 1,000 to other rescues, largely because owners weren't prepared for a tortoise that can reach 200 pounds and live many decades. One escaped sulcata, a roughly 35-year-old, 100-plus-pound tortoise named Rex, wandered off through a left-open gate in a Phoenix suburb and became enough of a neighborhood event that residents organized to track down his owner before he was eventually rehomed. It's a genuinely current, well-documented illustration of why this species overwhelms rescues, even though owning one is perfectly legal almost everywhere.
</FunFact>

## The Real Takeaway

If you're considering a sulcata tortoise, legality is the easy part; almost nowhere in the US restricts ownership. The harder question is whether you can provide for an animal that may reach 200 pounds and outlive you. That's the actual reason this species keeps showing up in rescue and surrender stories, not any law standing in the way.
