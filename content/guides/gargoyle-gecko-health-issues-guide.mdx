# Common Gargoyle Gecko Health Issues and What Causes Them

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Gargoyle gecko health problems overwhelmingly come down to two husbandry errors: an enclosure that's too wet, or one that doesn't give the gecko enough hiding spots. Get those two things right and most of this list never shows up. See our [Gargoyle Gecko tank setup guide](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-tank-setup-guide/) for the husbandry side, and our full [Gargoyle Gecko care guide](/guides/gargoyle-gecko/) for everything else.

## Metabolic Bone Disease (MBD)

Caused by calcium or vitamin D3 deficiency, often combined with inadequate UVB. Watch for a kinked or wavy tail, a soft jaw, curved limbs, tremors, appetite loss, and loss of coordination.

Check the two calcium sacs under the throat, depleted white sacs are a visible warning sign worth checking regularly. Gargoyle geckos may be more prone to early MBD than crested geckos, but early cases often resolve within months once diet and <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0D6SP118Q?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Repashy SuperFoods Calcium Plus 4 oz JAR">supplementation</AffiliateLink> are corrected. Advanced cases need a vet for bloodwork and imaging, existing deformities are usually permanent.

<FunFact>
The two pale sacs under a gargoyle gecko's throat aren't decorative, they're visible calcium reserves. A healthy gecko's sacs look plump and white; watching them shrink over time is one of the earliest, most visible warning signs of a calcium problem, well before a kinked tail shows up.
</FunFact>

## Stuck Shed Leading to Toe or Tail-Tip Loss

The most common gargoyle-specific husbandry failure, and it traces directly to low humidity. Retained shed wraps around the narrow toes like a tourniquet, cutting off circulation until the toe dies and eventually drops off.

Prevent it with a proper daily wet-and-dry humidity cycle, <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4vPKMjX" product="Fine mist spray bottle">misting heavily and letting it dry back down</AffiliateLink> rather than keeping the enclosure constantly damp. Early stuck shed often softens with a gentle warm soak; a constricting band or a blackened toe needs a vet.

## Respiratory Infection

The direct result of the opposite mistake, an enclosure kept constantly wet with poor ventilation. Signs include labored or open-mouth breathing, mucus or bubbles around the nose, a clicking or wheezing sound, lethargy, and appetite loss. This needs a vet for antibiotics, and it can be fatal if untreated.

The fix is letting humidity drop back down between mistings rather than keeping things perpetually damp. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">hygrometer</AffiliateLink> makes it easy to confirm the enclosure is actually cycling instead of just guessing.

## Floppy Tail Syndrome (FTS)

A gargoyle-specific condition where the tail hangs over the gecko's back or to one side, usually from repeatedly sleeping upside-down on bare glass. Over time this can deform the tail base and hips, making it difficult for the gecko to defecate or, in females, lay eggs.

The fix is almost entirely about the enclosure: clutter the terrarium with enough <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QQNV3L" product="Climbing branch (Mopani wood)">hides and branches</AffiliateLink> that your gecko doesn't default to sleeping flat against the glass. Severe cases may need a vet, occasionally tail amputation. This is a different problem from the tail-drop response covered in our [Gargoyle Gecko handling guide](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-handling-guide/), that one's a defense mechanism, not a husbandry-caused condition.

## Parasites and Impaction

Internal parasites are diagnosed through a fecal exam, exactly why new geckos should be quarantined and tested before joining an established collection. Impaction, a gut blockage, is linked to ingesting loose substrate, which is why non-particulate substrate matters for this species, covered in more detail in our [Gargoyle Gecko tank setup guide](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-tank-setup-guide/).

## When to See a Vet

Any kinked tail, soft jaw, or tremor (possible MBD); open-mouth breathing or mucus (possible respiratory infection); a tail that stays deformed despite adding hides (possible FTS); or any sign of parasites or impaction.

The same two husbandry mistakes, too wet or too dry, show up across small gecko species.
