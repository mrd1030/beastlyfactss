  - q: "How do I safely thaw frozen prey for my ball python?"
    a: "Seal the frozen rodent in a bag and submerge it in warm — not hot — water until it feels warm all the way through, usually 15 to 20 minutes for a medium rat. Never microwave prey: it heats unevenly, which can leave hot spots that burn a snake's mouth while the inside stays frozen."
  - q: "Is frozen/thawed prey actually better than live prey?"
    a: "Yes. Live rodents can bite and seriously injure a snake, sometimes fatally, especially if the snake isn't hungry enough to strike immediately. Frozen/thawed removes that risk entirely and is the standard recommendation from reptile veterinarians."
  - q: "Is it safe to handle my ball python right after it eats?"
    a: "No — wait at least 48 hours before handling. Handling too soon after a meal is one of the most common causes of regurgitation, which is stressful for the snake and nutritionally costly, and repeated episodes can lead to more serious digestive issues over time."
  - q: "What size prey should I feed an adult ball python?"
    a: "Prey roughly the same width as the widest part of the snake's body — usually a medium rat for adults. Prey that's too large risks regurgitation; prey that's too small provides inadequate nutrition over time."

Moving a ball python to a separate feeding container was once common advice to avoid substrate ingestion, but it's now understood to add unnecessary stress and is linked to higher rates of food refusal in an already food-shy species. As long as loose substrate isn't a choking hazard, feeding in place is the better practice."

# Ball Python Feeding: Everything You Need to Know

Ball pythons are one of the most popular pet snakes in the world, and one of the most notorious food refusers. Understanding their feeding behavior will save you a lot of stress. For the rest of their housing and enrichment needs, see our full [Ball Python care guide](/guides/ball-python/).

## Feeding Schedule

- **Hatchlings (0-6 months):** Every 5-7 days
- **Juveniles (6-18 months):** Every 7-10 days  
- **Adults (18+ months):** Every 10-14 days

## Prey Size

Feed prey that is roughly the same width as the widest point of your snake's body. For most adult ball pythons, that's a medium rat. Too large = regurgitation; too small = nutritional inadequacy.

## Frozen/Thawed is Best

Always feed **frozen/thawed (F/T)** rodents. Live prey can injure your snake. Thaw in warm (not hot) water or a bag submerged in warm water. Never microwave. The prey should feel warm to the touch.

## Why Ball Pythons Refuse Food

1. **Breeding season (Oct-March):** Males especially go off food during this period - completely normal
2. **Shedding:** Most snakes refuse food when in shed (opaque eyes, dull skin) — see our [complete guide to reptile shedding](/blog/reptile-shedding-complete-guide/) for the full process
3. **Incorrect temperatures/humidity:** The #1 husbandry-related cause
4. **Stress:** New enclosure, too much handling, too little hide cover
5. **Seasonal response:** Wild instinct to fast in cooler months

## When to Actually Worry

A healthy adult ball python can go 3-6 months without eating. Worry if: rapid weight loss, lethargy plus refusal, mucus around the mouth, or wheezing accompanies the fast.
