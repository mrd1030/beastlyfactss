# Common Betta Fish Health Issues (And Why Water Quality Comes First)

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Almost every common betta illness traces back to one of two things: water quality or water temperature. That's genuinely good news for owners, because it means the first response to a sick betta is almost always the same starting point, before anything else. See our [Betta Fish water parameters guide](/blog/betta-fish-water-parameters-guide/) for the specific numbers to target, and our full [Betta Fish care guide](/guides/betta-fish/) for everything else.

## Fin Rot

A bacterial infection that shows up as frayed, ragged, or discolored fins. Treat mild cases with clean water and aquarium salt, adding antibiotics if it's more advanced or not improving.

## Ich (White Spot Disease)

Caused by the parasite *Ichthyophthirius multifiliis*. Look for small white grains across the body and fins, along with scratching against tank decor. Treatment involves gradually raising the tank temperature, an adjustable <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eQY1L5" product="Submersible aquarium heater">aquarium heater</AffiliateLink> makes this easy to control precisely, and using a copper or formalin-based medication.

## Velvet

Caused by *Oodinium*, a parasite that gives the fish a fine gold or rust-colored dusty sheen. This one moves fast and can be fatal quickly, so early treatment matters. Darkening the tank and using an antiparasitic or copper treatment are the standard response.

## Columnaris

A bacterial infection caused by *Flavobacterium columnare*, notable for being highly contagious between fish if you keep more than one tank.

## Swim Bladder Disorder

Usually caused by overfeeding, constipation, or infection, and it shows up as buoyancy problems, floating, sinking, or swimming at odd angles. Fasting the fish for 2 to 3 days often resolves mild cases, and offering a single cooked, de-shelled pea afterward can help things move along.

## Dropsy

Raised, pinecone-like scales are the telltale sign. This isn't really a single disease, it's a visible symptom of organ failure, and it's usually a poor prognosis rather than something to actively treat.

<FunFact>
Dropsy's pinecone look isn't the disease itself, it's what happens to a fish's scales when fluid builds up inside from organ failure. By the time that visual sign shows up, the underlying problem is usually already advanced, which is part of why the prognosis is so often poor.
</FunFact>

## Mycobacteriosis (Fish Tuberculosis)

Rare, but worth knowing about because it's zoonotic, transmissible to people through contact with infected water, especially through broken skin. Use gloves if you have any open cuts and are doing tank maintenance on a tank you suspect might be affected.

Before reaching for medication, test and correct water quality and temperature.
