# Betta Fish Tank Setup: Bigger, Warmer, and Cycled

<AffiliateDisclosure />

The bowl-on-a-desk image most people have of betta fish is outdated. Modern betta care centers on a proper heated, filtered tank, and the setup itself isn't complicated once you know what actually matters. This covers the setup itself, for costs, see our [Betta Fish cost guide](/blog/betta-fish-cost-guide/), or the full [Betta Fish care guide](/guides/betta-fish/) for everything else.

## Tank Size

Five gallons is the current standard minimum, and it's a genuine improvement over older advice. Bettas can technically survive in smaller volumes, but bigger tanks are easier to keep stable, temperature and water chemistry swing less in more water, which means fewer sudden stresses on the fish. Use a lid, bettas are surprisingly good jumpers.

## Heater

A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eQY1L5" product="Submersible aquarium heater">15 to 25 watt adjustable heater</AffiliateLink> sized for a small aquarium is typically right for a 5-gallon betta tank. Target a stable temperature of 76 to 82°F, with 78 to 80°F being the sweet spot for long-term health. Avoid rapid temperature swings, consistency matters more than hitting an exact number. Full target ranges, including pH, are covered in our [Betta Fish water parameters guide](/blog/betta-fish-water-parameters-guide/).

## Filter

A gentle, low-flow filter is important, bettas have long, flowing fins that struggle against strong current. <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3T8xyAd" product="Sponge filter">Sponge filters</AffiliateLink> are widely considered the gold standard for this exact reason, they provide filtration without pushing the fish around.

<FunFact>
A betta's flowing fins are exactly what make it vulnerable to the kind of filter most other tropical fish do fine with. A power filter's outflow that a tetra barely notices can exhaust a betta and shred its fins over time, which is the real reason sponge filters became the default recommendation for this species specifically.
</FunFact>

## Water: Cycling and Maintenance

Cycle the tank (establishing the beneficial bacteria colony that processes waste) before adding your betta, rather than adding the fish to unestablished water. Always treat tap water with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3Rg5gTT" product="Water conditioner">dechlorinator or conditioner</AffiliateLink> before it goes in the tank. Test ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate periodically with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4p1T7yd" product="Water test kit">test kit</AffiliateLink>, and aim for a pH close to neutral, in the 6.5 to 7.8 range.

Do a 20 to 30% water change weekly using a gravel vacuum, this removes waste from the substrate without stripping out all the beneficial bacteria that keeps the tank stable, which a full water change would do.

## Environment

Provide hides, silk or live plants (avoid sharp plastic decor that can tear fins), and something like a betta hammock or leaf near the surface. This last point matters more than it might seem, bettas breathe air directly through a labyrinth organ, so keeping clear access to the surface is a genuine physiological need, not just a nice touch.

## Diet

Bettas are carnivores. Feed 2 to 4 small, high-protein betta pellets once or twice a day, roughly matching what would fit in an amount the size of the fish's eye. Fast one day a week to prevent digestive issues. Frozen or freeze-dried bloodworms and brine shrimp make good occasional treats.

**Don't overfeed.** It's the single most common mistake in betta keeping, and it leads directly to bloating, swim bladder problems, and fouled water from uneaten food. Remove any food your betta doesn't eat within a few minutes, more on what those problems look like in our [Betta Fish health issues guide](/blog/betta-fish-health-issues-guide/).

Once this setup is in place, day-to-day interaction with your betta is mostly observation rather than hands-on, see our [Betta Fish handling guide](/blog/betta-fish-handling-guide/) for what that actually looks like, or browse the rest of our [Fish care guide category](/guides/category/fish/) and the [Betta Fish encyclopedia profile](/encyclopedia/animal/betta-fish/) for more.
