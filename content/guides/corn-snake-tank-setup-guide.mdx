# Corn Snake Tank Setup: Floor Space, a Mandatory Thermostat, and a Humidity Debate

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Corn snakes are a genuinely forgiving species to set up correctly, with one non-negotiable exception: whatever heat source you choose, it needs a thermostat. Everything else here has some flexibility. This covers the setup itself, for costs and ongoing supplies, see our [Corn Snake cost guide](/blog/corn-snake-cost-guide/), or the full [Corn Snake care guide](/guides/corn-snake/) for diet and handling.

## Enclosure Size and Orientation

Corn snakes are terrestrial, so floor space matters more than height, though they do appreciate some climbing opportunity. Adult minimum is a 40-gallon breeder (36x18x18 inches), with 4x2x2 feet (48x24x24 inches) as the preferred size for a fully grown adult. Front-opening PVC enclosures are generally preferred over glass, they hold humidity better and tend to be more escape-proof, which matters for this species. House corn snakes singly.

**Security is not optional.** Corn snakes are notorious escape artists, and a loose lid or a gap at the corner of a screen top is often all it takes. Confirm the enclosure locks or latches securely before your snake ever goes in.

**Every heat source must run through a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/44hAxJ1" product="Under-tank heat mat + thermostat kit">thermostat</AffiliateLink>.** This is the one rule on this list with zero exceptions. An unregulated heat mat can climb to around 120°F, hot enough to cause a serious burn. When you set up your thermostat, place the probe where your snake actually spends time, inside the warm hide or on the substrate surface, not taped directly to the heat mat itself, since that gives a misleading reading.

## Humidity: A Genuine Disagreement Worth Knowing About

This is one area where sources don't fully agree. Some specialist husbandry guides recommend a higher baseline around 65 to 75%, arguing that wild corn snake habitats run more humid than conventional reptile-keeping wisdom assumes. Many other sources and vets cite a lower 40 to 60% baseline as sufficient, raised to 60-70% specifically during shedding.

In practice, either range can work well. What matters more is consistency and providing a dedicated humid hide with damp <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4bpnvNm" product="Sphagnum moss (for moist hide)">sphagnum moss</AffiliateLink> so your snake can access higher humidity during a shed regardless of what the rest of the enclosure reads. Measure with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">digital hygrometer</AffiliateLink> placed at substrate level, not up near the lid where readings run drier than what your snake actually experiences.

<FunFact>
This humidity disagreement is a good reminder that reptile husbandry advice keeps evolving as more field data comes in. Wild corn snake habitat surveys have pushed some specialist keepers toward higher humidity numbers than the classic care-sheet range, even though plenty of corn snakes have thrived for decades under the older, drier guidance too.
</FunFact>

## UVB

Not required, corn snakes have been kept successfully for decades without it, but it's increasingly viewed as a beneficial addition for long-term health. If you add it, use a low-output T5 bulb spanning about half the enclosure on the warm side, targeting a UVI around 2.0 to 3.0. Provide a 12-hour light and dark cycle either way, using a basic LED if you skip UVB entirely.

## Substrate

<AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3TkxAVF" product="Aspen shavings substrate">Aspen shavings</AffiliateLink> are the classic choice, they dry quickly and suit a lower-humidity setup well. Coconut fiber, cypress mulch, or a soil-based mix work better if you're running toward the higher end of the humidity range. Whatever you choose, keep it 3 to 4 inches deep to allow natural burrowing behavior. Avoid pine or cedar entirely, both contain oils that are toxic to reptiles, and skip gravel or loose bark chips, which carry impaction risk. Spot-clean daily and do a full substrate change every 3 to 4 months.

## Furnishings

- At least two hides, one on the warm side, one on the cool side
- A sturdy <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eKyN0W" product="Large soak-able water dish">water bowl large enough to soak in</AffiliateLink>, changed at least twice a week
- Branches or cork bark for enrichment and light climbing
- Never a hot rock, they're a well-documented burn risk and simply not worth the danger

Corn snakes eat frozen-thawed rodents sized to roughly the width of the snake at its thickest point, on a schedule of every 7 to 10 days for juveniles, stretching to every 10 to 21 days as they reach adulthood.
