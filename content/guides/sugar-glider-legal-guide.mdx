# Is It Legal to Own a Sugar Glider? State-by-State Guide

Sugar gliders sit in an unusual legal spot: genuinely banned in a handful of states, but also the subject of more bad information online than almost any other small pet on this list. Several states commonly named as "banned" on aggregator sites turn out to allow them without issue once you check the actual regulation. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Sugar Glider care guide](/guides/sugar-glider/).

### California, Alaska, and Hawaii

California's Title 14 restricted-species list (14 CCR § 671) places "all species" of Marsupialia, which includes sugar gliders, under a "(W)" Welfare Animal designation. Permits are issued to zoos, researchers, and exhibitors, not private owners. Alaska works differently: the state only allows species affirmatively named on its "Clean List," and sugar gliders aren't on it, so no import or possession permit can be issued at all. Hawaii's restriction works the same way, by omission from its own approved-species list, with the state citing bark-stripping damage to native trees like koa as the ecological concern.

### Pennsylvania's Ongoing Fight

Pennsylvania doesn't ban sugar gliders outright, but its Game Commission almost never grants the possession permit that would be required. A bill to change this, House Bill 692, was introduced in January 2025 and referred to the House Game and Fisheries Committee, where it has sat without a vote since February 2025. It's the sixth attempt to legalize sugar gliders and hedgehogs in Pennsylvania since 2017.

## Claims That Don't Hold Up

<FunFact>
A few states are frequently listed as banning sugar gliders by pet blogs, but don't hold up against the actual rule text. Georgia explicitly permits ownership with proof the animal came from a USDA-regulated source. Massachusetts added sugar gliders to its no-permit-needed exemption list back in 2014. And Wyoming classifies sugar gliders as a "domesticated animal" by name, which puts them outside the wildlife-permit system entirely. If a site tells you one of these three states bans sugar gliders, it's describing outdated or incorrect information.
</FunFact>

## Everywhere Else: Generally Legal

Outside of California, Alaska, Hawaii, and functionally Pennsylvania, sugar gliders don't require a state wildlife permit in most of the country. As always, city and county exotic-pet ordinances and landlord or HOA rules can still add restrictions on top of state law, so it's worth a quick local check even in a state where gliders are broadly fine.

## The Real Takeaway

If you live in California, Alaska, or Hawaii, sugar glider ownership isn't a gray area, it's a real ban with real enforcement. Pennsylvania sits in a frustrating middle ground where the permit exists on paper but isn't realistically available. Everywhere else, don't take a blog post's word for a ban that may not exist; Georgia, Massachusetts, and Wyoming owners in particular are often told they're breaking a law that doesn't apply to them.
