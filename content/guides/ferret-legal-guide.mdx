# Is It Legal to Own a Ferret? State-by-State Guide

Ferrets are legal to keep as pets in every US state except two, and no state-level permit or license is required in the vast majority of them. But "legal in your state" isn't the whole story - a handful of cities add their own restrictions on top, which is where most of the real confusion comes from. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Ferret care guide](/guides/ferret/).

## Where Ferrets Are Actually Illegal

### California

California classifies domestic ferrets as a restricted wild animal under Title 14 of the California Code of Regulations (§671) and Fish and Game Code §2118, with a "detrimental" designation reflecting concern that escaped or released ferrets could establish feral populations and prey on native birds and small mammals. The ban has been in place since 1933 and covers owning, importing, transporting, or selling a ferret - there's no personal-pet permit available. Violations are a misdemeanor carrying up to six months in jail and/or a fine up to $1,000, plus a separate civil penalty that can run from $500 to $10,000.

### Hawaii

Hawaii prohibits ferret ownership and import outright under Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 150A, which governs animal introductions into the state. The reasoning is different from California's: Hawaii is one of the only rabies-free places on Earth, and ferrets are a known potential rabies carrier, so the state treats them as a public health risk rather than an ecological one. Penalties are unusually severe - up to $200,000 in fines and three years in jail - and Hawaii's Department of Agriculture has publicly documented enforcement actions, including seized ferrets reported in local news.

### New York City

New York State has no ferret ban at all - ferrets are legal statewide with no permit required, though rabies vaccination is required like any other pet. New York City is the exception: ferrets have been restricted under the city's health code since 1959, formally codified into the current ban (§161.01) in 1999. The city's Board of Health revisited it as recently as a vote that fell one short of the six needed to overturn it, so the ban remains in place. The restriction also extends to other small mustelids and related animals, including minks, badgers, wolverines, skunks, and mongooses - if you're moving to or visiting NYC with a ferret from a state where it's legal, the city ban still applies.

## California's Ban May Not Last Much Longer

<FunFact>
This is genuinely in motion, not settled history: in June 2025, the California Fish and Game Commission voted unanimously to accept Petition 2025-003, which seeks to remove domestic ferrets from the state's restricted species list. As of this writing, the Commission still hasn't issued a decision, and the petitioners have since filed a writ action in Sacramento County Superior Court to force a ruling. Nothing has changed yet for California owners, but this is the most active legal challenge to any state's ferret ban right now.
</FunFact>

## Everywhere Else: Generally Legal, But Always Double-Check

Outside of California, Hawaii, and NYC, ferrets don't require a state hunting, exotic-pet, or wildlife permit in the large majority of the country. That said, "legal at the state level" doesn't automatically mean "no restrictions at all" - a few things worth checking before bringing one home:

- **City and county ordinances.** Some individual cities and counties add their own exotic-pet rules even in states where ferrets are otherwise fine statewide - NYC is the best-known example, but it's not necessarily the only one.
- **Landlord and HOA rules.** Plenty of leases and homeowners' association agreements restrict "exotic" or "non-traditional" pets by name, regardless of what state or city law says.
- **Rabies vaccination requirements.** Several states that allow ferrets still legally require rabies vaccination, the same as they would for a cat or dog.

## The Real Takeaway

If you live outside California, Hawaii, or New York City, you almost certainly don't need to worry about ferret-specific state law - just double-check your lease and local city ordinances the way you would for any less-common pet. If you live in one of those three places, this isn't a gray area worth testing: California and Hawaii's penalties are real and enforced, and NYC's ban applies regardless of where you're arriving from.
