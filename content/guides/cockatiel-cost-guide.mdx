# How Much Does a Cockatiel Really Cost?

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Cockatiels are relatively affordable to buy, but the real budgeting story here is how long you'll be caring for one. A 15 to 20 year lifespan means this is a long-term financial commitment even though the entry cost is modest. For the full care picture beyond budgeting, see our complete [Cockatiel care guide](/guides/cockatiel/).

Normal grey is typically the cheapest color mutation, and buying directly from a breeder is common. The cage is frequently the single biggest line item in the whole setup, often costing more than the bird itself, which is worth keeping in mind if you're tempted to cut corners there instead of elsewhere; our [Cockatiel cage setup guide](/blog/cockatiel-tank-setup-guide/) covers what actually matters in choosing one. Most first-time owners land in the $320 to $860 range for a complete setup. If you're weighing a cockatiel against a similarly sized bird, our [Budgie vs Cockatiel](/blog/budgie-vs-cockatiel-guide/) comparison is worth a look before you commit.

<FunFact>
The "normal grey" cockatiel is actually the closest color to the wild type found across the Australian outback, and it's usually the least expensive mutation for a simple genetic reason: it requires no selective breeding to produce. Every other color variation carries a rarity premium built from generations of deliberate breeding work.
</FunFact>

A pellet-based diet with fresh food costs more than a seed-only diet, but it's the healthier choice and worth the difference, covered in more detail in our [Cockatiel health issues guide](/blog/cockatiel-health-issues-guide/).

## The Vet Cost That Catches People Off Guard

An initial or annual avian exam commonly runs $85 to $200 in the US, and that's before any fecal testing or bloodwork, which adds more. Reproductive emergencies, like egg binding, can run $300 to $800 or more.

Avian vets are a genuinely specialized field, harder to find and often pricier than a general small-animal clinic, so it's worth locating one near you before an emergency forces the search. Pet insurance for birds is uncommon, so most owners end up covering vet costs directly, which makes a dedicated emergency fund worth having.

## The Real Budgeting Takeaway

The bird itself is the smallest number in this whole picture. What actually determines your long-term cost is the quality of the cage you buy (worth investing in upfront, since a too-small cage becomes a welfare issue, not just an inconvenience) and how seriously you take avian vet care over a 15 to 20 year relationship.
