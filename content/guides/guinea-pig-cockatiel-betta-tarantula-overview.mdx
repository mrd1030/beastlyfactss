# Four Pets With One Rule Each Worth Knowing First

These four don't have much in common on the surface, a rodent, a bird, a fish, and a spider, but each one comes with a single piece of information that matters more than everything else in its care.

## Guinea Pig: Needs a Friend, and a Nutrient Most Pets Make on Their Own

Guinea pigs are highly social and shouldn't be kept alone, which quietly doubles most of the numbers in a typical care budget. The bigger surprise for a lot of new owners: guinea pigs can't produce their own vitamin C, the way humans can't, which makes diet a genuinely species-specific concern rather than a generic "rodent food" situation.

## Cockatiel: The Kitchen Is a Real Hazard

Cockatiels tame well with patience and make genuinely social, interactive pets. The one rule worth burning into memory: overheated non-stick cookware releases fumes that are rapidly fatal to birds. Keep the cage, and the bird, out of the kitchen entirely.

## Betta Fish: Not a Bowl Fish, and Not a Hands-On Pet

Bettas need a real heated, filtered tank, the bowl image is outdated and genuinely unkind to the fish. They're also not handled by hand at all, ever, a net or cup protects the protective slime coat that keeps a betta healthy.

[Cost](/guides/betta-fish-cost-guide/) · [Handling](/guides/betta-fish-handling-guide/) · [Health Issues](/guides/betta-fish-health-issues-guide/) · [Tank Setup](/guides/betta-fish-tank-setup-guide/)

## Tarantula: The Whole Point Is Look, Don't Touch

Most tarantulas, the Chilean rose hair included, are best kept as strictly observation pets. A fall can be fatal to the spider, and a bite or a brush with its urticating hairs is unpleasant for you, so the safest and most respected approach is admiring one from just outside the glass.
