# How Much Does a Tarantula Really Cost?

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Tarantulas are one of the most affordable exotic pets to keep, both upfront and over time. The tradeoff is that this is genuinely a decades-long commitment for a female, which makes the low sticker price a bit misleading if you only look at year one. For everything else about day-to-day care, see our full [Tarantula care guide](/guides/tarantula/).

## Vet Care and Insurance

There's no pet insurance for tarantulas, and veterinary care is genuinely rare, many areas simply don't have a vet equipped to treat one. This isn't a species where you're budgeting for regular vet visits the way you might with a mammal or bird. A small emergency fund is still worth having, but it's a much smaller consideration here than with most pets, and our [Tarantula health issues guide](/blog/tarantula-health-issues-guide/) covers what those rare emergencies actually look like.

<FunFact>
Female tarantulas keep molting throughout their entire adult lives, unlike most invertebrates that stop once they reach maturity. Males stop molting for good once they mature, which is a big part of why they live so much shorter than females, four to seven years total against 15 to 20 or more.
</FunFact>

## The Real Number to Sit With: Longevity

Female Chilean rose hairs commonly live 15 to 20 years or more, males considerably less, typically 4 to 7 years. If you're getting a female, you're looking at a genuinely long-term commitment on a very small ongoing budget. This is arguably the cheapest long-lived exotic pet you can keep, low setup cost, low monthly cost, and no vet bills in the typical case, but the time commitment is real and worth thinking through before you buy.

Weigh that against what daily life with one actually looks like.
