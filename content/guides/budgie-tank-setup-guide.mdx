# Budgie Cage Setup: Width Over Height, and a Few Rules That Actually Matter

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Budgies are small, but their housing needs get underestimated more than almost any other part of their care. The cage sold as a "budgie starter cage" at most pet stores is usually a bare minimum at best, and a few specific details matter more than overall size alone. This covers the setup itself, for costs and ongoing supplies, see our [Budgie cost guide](/blog/budgie-cost-guide/), or the full [Budgie care guide](/guides/budgie/) for everything else.

## Cage Size and Shape

An absolute minimum for a single budgie is often cited around 18x18x18 inches, but a wider flight-style cage serves them far better, something closer to 30x18x18 inches for a pair, with 32 inches or more in length being a genuine improvement over the minimum.

The key detail: prioritize width over height. Budgies fly side to side in short bursts rather than climbing vertically, so a tall, narrow cage wastes the exact space they'd actually use, while a wide one gives them real flying room. ## Bar Spacing, Non-Negotiable

Bar spacing needs to be half an inch or less. Wider spacing creates a real risk of a budgie squeezing through and escaping, or worse, getting its head caught while trying. Avoid round cages entirely, and check that any cage you're considering doesn't use zinc or lead in its finish, both are toxic if ingested through normal chewing and preening behavior.

## Perches: Variety Matters More Than Material

Provide a mix of perch diameters, textures, and materials. Don't rely on smooth wooden dowels as the only perch option, the uniform surface and diameter contribute to arthritis and pressure sores over time from a foot that never has to adjust its grip. Skip sandpaper perch covers too, they don't actually file nails effectively and can cause irritation to the feet.

## Diet: Pellets, Not Seeds, Should Lead

This is where a lot of budgie care goes wrong. The veterinary consensus is that pellets should make up roughly 60 to 80% of a budgie's diet, with fruits, vegetables, and greens covering another 20 to 25%, and seeds treated as an occasional extra rather than the foundation. A seed-only diet is directly linked to malnutrition, obesity, and fatty liver disease, covered in more detail in our [Budgie health issues guide](/blog/budgie-health-issues-guide/).

Provide a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4fbLrVy" product="Penn-Plax E-Cuttlebone">cuttlebone or mineral block</AffiliateLink> in the cage at all times, it supports calcium intake and gives your budgie something appropriate to condition its beak against.

<FunFact>
A budgie's beak never stops growing, which is exactly why chewing on a cuttlebone or mineral block matters beyond just the calcium. Without something appropriate to condition it against, the beak can overgrow and interfere with normal eating.
</FunFact>

## Light, Temperature, and Environment

Budgies need roughly 10 to 12 hours of darkness and uninterrupted sleep every night, a cage cover is a simple, effective way to support this on a consistent schedule.

Keep ambient temperature comfortable, a practical range is about 65 to 85°F, with 70 to 75°F being ideal, and avoid drafts, direct sun exposure, and sudden temperature swings. Budgies don't have sweat glands and are more heat-sensitive than that range might suggest.

One serious warning worth repeating clearly: never use nonstick or PTFE cookware anywhere near a budgie. Overheated nonstick pans release fumes that are frequently fatal to birds, often described as causing sudden death with little warning. Keep birds out of the kitchen entirely and consider replacing nonstick cookware in a household with pet birds.

## Social Needs

Budgies are highly social flock birds by nature. A single budgie needs substantial daily interaction from you to stay well-adjusted, or better, a companion budgie to socialize with. This isn't a nice-to-have, it's close to as important as diet or cage size for long-term wellbeing.
