# Chinchilla Housing: Dust Baths, Not Water, and a Cool Room

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Chinchilla care has a few rules that genuinely surprise new owners, especially around bathing. Get the cage, the temperature, and the dust bath right, and the rest of this species' care is fairly manageable. This covers the setup itself, for pricing on all of it, see our [Chinchilla cost guide](/blog/chinchilla-cost-guide/), or the full [Chinchilla care guide](/guides/chinchilla/) for everything else.

## Cage

Go tall and multi-level, with solid, non-wire floors and shelves, wire flooring contributes to sore feet over time. A reasonable minimum is around 24 by 24 by 36 inches, and bigger is genuinely better for a species this active. Use metal, not plastic, chinchillas chew constantly, and ingested plastic can cause a fatal blockage. <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B001NJ0DAY?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Critter Nation by MidWest Homes for Pets Double Unit Cage">Multi-level small-animal cages designed for ferrets</AffiliateLink> are popular among chinchilla keepers for exactly this combination of height, solid flooring, and metal construction.

Keep the room cool and dry, ideally in that 50 to 68°F range, and out of direct sun and drafts. Apply the same rule mentioned in our [Chinchilla health issues guide](/blog/chinchilla-health-issues-guide/): add the Fahrenheit temperature to the humidity percentage, and treat a combined total over 150 as dangerous territory. ## Dust Baths: Not Water, Ever

Chinchillas need a dust bath, not a water bath, and this is one of the most important things to get right. Provide <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4vgxhss" product="Chinchilla dust (Oxbow Poof)">chinchilla dust</AffiliateLink> (a fine volcanic ash) in a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4w2STJO" product="Chinchilla dust bath house">dust bath house</AffiliateLink> 2 to 3 times a week, some owners go daily, for about 10 to 30 minutes at a time, then remove the container so it doesn't become a permanent fixture in the cage. Use roughly 1 to 2 inches of dust in a shallow container.

**Chinchillas must not get wet.** Their fur is extremely dense, among the densest of any mammal, and trapped moisture in a coat that thick promotes fungal growth rather than washing anything away. There's no safe way to bathe a chinchilla in water the way you might a dog.

<FunFact>
Many chinchilla owners furnish their cage from the ferret aisle rather than the small-mammal one. Tall, solid-floored, metal ferret cages happen to check every box a chinchilla actually needs, even though they're built and marketed for a completely different animal.
</FunFact>

## Diet

Grass hay (timothy or orchard) should make up 80 to 90% of the diet, available at all times, not rationed. <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4h9WW2u" product="Timothy hay (Oxbow Western Timothy Hay, 90 oz)">Timothy hay</AffiliateLink> is the standard choice. Add plain, hay-based pellets, roughly 16 to 20% protein and 15 to 35% fiber, at about 1 to 2 tablespoons a day. Keep treats minimal, and provide safe, untreated wood chews to support natural dental wear alongside the hay, more on why that matters in our [Chinchilla health issues guide](/blog/chinchilla-health-issues-guide/).

## Bedding

<AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4voH9Ap" product="Small-pet paper bedding">Unscented paper bedding</AffiliateLink> or fleece both work well. Avoid cedar and pine shavings entirely, both carry respiratory and impaction risks.

## Exercise

Chinchillas need daily activity. A solid <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4gmXgL1" product="Solid exercise wheel (chinchilla)">metal wheel</AffiliateLink>, 15 inches or larger, is the right choice. **Never use an exercise ball,** they're a genuine overheating and injury risk for this species specifically, even though they're marketed broadly for small pets. That injury risk matters more than usual given how fragile this species' skeleton is, more in our [Chinchilla handling guide](/blog/chinchilla-handling-guide/).
