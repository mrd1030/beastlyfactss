# How to "Handle" a Goldfish (Net and Cup, Not Hands)

Like most fish, goldfish should not be routinely picked up by hand. This isn't an overcautious rule, their protective slime coat is a genuine, physical barrier against disease, and bare hands remove it on contact. See our full [Goldfish care guide](/guides/goldfish/) for everything else on day-to-day care.

## Why the Slime Coat Matters So Much

That coat is a goldfish's primary defense against parasites, bacteria, and general infection, in addition to protecting its scales. Touching a goldfish, especially with dry hands, strips this layer away. Research on fish handling has found that this kind of contact leads to decreased chemical protection, disrupted regulation of salts and water balance in the body, and a loss of the physical barrier against invading organisms, exactly the functions the slime coat exists to provide.

The coat can take 24 to 48 hours to regenerate. During that window, the fish is meaningfully more vulnerable to infection than usual, which is why even a single instance of unnecessary handling isn't as harmless as it might look. Some of the fungal and bacterial issues covered in our [Goldfish health issues guide](/blog/goldfish-health-issues-guide/) trace back to exactly this kind of opening.

<FunFact>
It's not just infection risk. Losing the slime coat also disrupts a goldfish's ability to regulate salts and water balance in its own body, essentially a mechanical stress response layered on top of the infection risk from touching them, and it's a big part of why "just a quick scoop by hand" is never really quick or harmless.
</FunFact>

## The Right Way to Move a Goldfish

**Guide it into a container of tank water** rather than lifting it out directly, this is the gentlest option and avoids the slime-coat issue almost entirely.

**If you need to use a net**, wet it first before it touches the fish, and choose a soft rubber net over a knotted nylon one, which can catch on fins and scales.

**If bare hands are genuinely unavoidable** in an emergency, wet them first and keep well clear of the gills and eyes, both are especially sensitive.

Whatever method you use, minimize the time the fish spends out of water. Every second counts more than it might seem to.

## What "Handling" Actually Looks Like Day to Day

For a goldfish kept in the properly sized, well-filtered tank covered in our [Goldfish tank setup guide](/blog/goldfish-tank-setup-guide/), direct contact is rarely needed at all. Container transfers for water changes, gentle net use when moving a fish to a new tank, and otherwise just leaving it alone in its own water is the standard.

## The Takeaway

A goldfish isn't a pet you interact with by touch, and that's simply how the species works, not a limitation of your setup. The relationship is built through water quality, feeding, and observation, which is plenty for a fish that can live well over a decade when kept properly.
