# Is It Legal to Own a Hognose Snake? State-by-State Guide

Hognose snakes get flagged by first-time keepers for one reason: they're mildly venomous, in the sense that they have small rear fangs and a weak venom evolved to subdue toads. That's led to a persistent assumption that they must fall under the same permits as cobras or vipers. In practice, every venomous-reptile law we checked draws its line well above hognose. The real legal wrinkles are elsewhere. For everything else about keeping one, see our full [Hognose Snake care guide](/guides/hognose-snake/).

### The "Venomous Snake" Myth

A real medical case report on a keeper bitten by a Western hognose during feeding documented local swelling, bruising, and mild cellulitis, with a full recovery in about five months and no systemic effects. That's consistent with how state law treats the species: Florida's wildlife agency states outright that rear-fanged colubrids like hognose "are not regulated as venomous" in the state, and Alabama, Kentucky, and Louisiana all explicitly name Heterodon as an exception to their venomous-reptile bans. Even South Carolina's pending 2025 bill to expand venomous-reptile regulation was drafted to exclude hognose.

### Georgia's Real Exception

Georgia is the clearest genuine restriction here. Its Department of Natural Resources lists Eastern and Southern hognose under species that "may not be held as a pet," and states the restriction applies regardless of the animal's origin or morphology, meaning captive breeding doesn't create an exception the way it does in most other states.

### Colorado's New Split Rule

<FunFact>
Colorado's 2026 herpetofauna overhaul (Chapter W-7, effective May 1, 2026) legalized possessing, selling, and transporting captive-bred Western and Plains hognose with proof of origin, but pointedly excluded Eastern hognose from that legalization. At the same time, wild collection of any native Colorado hognose is now banned outright. It's a clean, current example of the captive-bred-versus-wild-collected distinction that runs through hognose law generally.
</FunFact>

## What's Still Unverified

California is frequently cited on aggregator sites as requiring a hognose permit, but we couldn't confirm this against the state's actual restricted-species list, and the format of that claim doesn't match how California's list is normally structured. Texas's rules on non-native venomous snakes never mention hognose at all, so it's genuinely unclear whether the state would classify it as venomous under that statute. Treat both as open questions rather than settled law until confirmed directly with the relevant state agency.

## The Real Takeaway

If you're getting a hognose snake, the venomous-reptile angle almost never applies to you, no matter where you live. Georgia is the one state where hognose ownership itself is off the table. Colorado owners of Eastern hognose specifically should be aware their subspecies wasn't part of the 2026 legalization. Everywhere else, this is one of the more broadly legal reptiles on this list.
