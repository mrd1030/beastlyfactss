# Goldfish Tank Setup: Bigger Than You Think, Filtered More Than You'd Guess

<AffiliateDisclosure />

If you take away one thing from goldfish care, make it this: the bowl is wrong, and the tank you actually need is bigger than most people expect for what looks like a small, simple fish. This covers the setup itself, for what it all costs, see our [Goldfish cost guide](/blog/goldfish-cost-guide/), or the full [Goldfish care guide](/guides/goldfish/) for everything else.

## Tank Size

Plan for roughly a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4pjiyeT" product="10 gallon tank">20-gallon-plus tank</AffiliateLink> for a single fancy goldfish, with at least 10 additional gallons for each fish after that. Single-tail varieties, comets and commons, need considerably more room than fancies and are genuinely better suited to a pond than an indoor tank long term, a minimum of about 30 gallons for one, 40-plus gallons each if you're keeping more. Long and wide beats tall, goldfish are horizontal swimmers. For the full argument on exactly why a bowl or tiny tank fails a goldfish long term, see our deep dive on [Goldfish Tank Size and the Bowl Myth](/blog/goldfish-tank-size-bowl-myth/).

## Filtration: Go Bigger Than Feels Necessary

Goldfish are unusually heavy waste producers for their size. Filtration should process your total tank volume at least 4 times an hour, and ideally 5 to 10 times. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eQAk5K" product="Canister filter (Fluval 107, up to 30 gal)">canister filter rated for smaller tanks</AffiliateLink>, or a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4vNGY2v" product="Canister filter (Fluval 407, up to 100 gal)">heavier-duty canister rated for larger setups</AffiliateLink>, or a hang-on-back unit with mechanical, biological, and chemical media all work, and running two filters is common practice among experienced keepers rather than overkill.

<FunFact>
A single goldfish can produce a bio-load comparable to several tropical fish combined, which is exactly why experienced keepers often run two separate filters on a goldfish tank as standard practice, not as backup equipment for when something breaks.
</FunFact>

## Water

Goldfish are a coldwater species, comfortable in the 65 to 75°F range, no heater strictly required. Keep ammonia and nitrite at 0 and nitrate under roughly 20 ppm. Do weekly water changes of 25 to 50%, and always treat new water with a <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3Rg5gTT" product="Water conditioner">dechlorinator</AffiliateLink> before it goes in the tank. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4p1T7yd" product="Water test kit">test kit</AffiliateLink> is the only reliable way to confirm those numbers rather than guess at them.

## Cycling

Cycle the tank before adding any fish. This applies to goldfish as much as any other species, an established beneficial bacteria colony is what actually processes the waste this species produces in such quantity.

## Diet

Goldfish are omnivores, and a balanced diet includes quality pellets as the base, vegetables, and occasional protein. For fancy varieties specifically, sinking pellets or gel-based foods reduce the swim bladder issues that can come with their rounder body shape, covered in more detail in our [Goldfish health issues guide](/blog/goldfish-health-issues-guide/). Avoid overfeeding, it's one of the most common mistakes and a direct contributor to the waste load your filtration has to handle.

## The Bottom Line

None of this is complicated, but it does require accepting that a goldfish needs meaningfully more tank and filtration than its size or price tag suggests. Set it up right from the start and this is a genuinely low-maintenance, long-lived fish, some goldfish live well over a decade in a properly maintained tank. Once the tank is right, day-to-day interaction is mostly hands-off, see our [Goldfish handling guide](/blog/goldfish-handling-guide/) for why net and container beat bare hands.
