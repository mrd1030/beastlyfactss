# Guinea Pig Housing and Diet: Why Vitamin C Is Non-Negotiable

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Guinea pig care is fairly forgiving once the basics are right, but there's one dietary detail that genuinely sets this species apart from almost every other small pet, and getting it wrong has real health consequences. This covers the setup itself, for what it costs to put together, see our [Guinea Pig cost guide](/blog/guinea-pig-cost-guide/), or the full [Guinea Pig care guide](/guides/guinea-pig/) for everything else.

## Enclosure Size

Minimum footprint is about 7.5 square feet (roughly 30 by 36 inches) for one guinea pig, and 10.5 square feet for a pair, more if you keep a larger group. Bigger is always better here, guinea pigs are active and benefit from room to move.

Use a solid floor, not wire, since wire flooring is a common cause of bumblefoot, covered in more detail in our [Guinea Pig health issues guide](/blog/guinea-pig-health-issues-guide/). Sides should be at least 12 inches high. <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3SOqokx" product="C&C cage (guinea pig)">C&C cages</AffiliateLink> (built from storage cubes and coroplast panels) are widely recommended by rescues for their combination of space and affordability. Avoid glass tanks and plastic bins, both have poor ventilation, and skip wire flooring entirely.

## Temperature

Keep the room between 65 and 75°F, out of direct sun and away from drafts. Heatstroke becomes a real risk above 85°F, so this isn't a species that tolerates a hot room well. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/3QHrM81" product="Digital thermometer/hygrometer combo">digital thermometer</AffiliateLink> in the room takes the guesswork out of confirming you're actually in range.

## Bedding

<AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4voH9Ap" product="Small-pet paper bedding">Paper-based bedding</AffiliateLink>, kiln-dried pine, or fleece liners all work well. Avoid cedar, raw pine, and sawdust, all of which irritate the respiratory system over time.

## Social Needs

Keep guinea pigs in pairs or small groups. This isn't a nice-to-have, guinea pigs are highly social and tend to become withdrawn or depressed when housed alone, part of why a realistic budget plans for two from the start, more on that in our [cost guide](/blog/guinea-pig-cost-guide/).

## Diet: Where Vitamin C Becomes the Whole Story

Guinea pigs cannot synthesize their own vitamin C, unlike most other pets, which makes their diet meaningfully different from a rabbit's or a hamster's even though the basic hay-heavy structure looks similar on the surface.

Bell pepper is a particularly good vegetable choice, high in vitamin C and low in sugar.

<FunFact>
Bell pepper isn't just a low-sugar vitamin C option for guinea pigs, it actually carries noticeably more vitamin C by weight than an orange does, exactly why it shows up so often as a top diet pick for this species specifically.
</FunFact>

On the vitamin C requirement itself, guinea pigs need 10 to 50 milligrams a day, with the exact amount depending on their condition (young, old, stressed, healthy, or pregnant animals all need different amounts). The vitamin C in commercial pellets degrades over time, so pellets should be used or replaced within 90 days of their manufacture date. Buying a fresh bag more often than you might expect is part of responsible guinea pig ownership, not overcautious. A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/44CDAMd" product="Vitamin C supplement (Oxbow Natural Science)">vitamin C supplement</AffiliateLink> in oral drop or tablet form is a reliable way to close any gap, and it's worth understanding the deficiency risk itself in our dedicated [Guinea Pig Scurvy guide](/blog/guinea-pig-scurvy-vitamin-c-guide/).

Don't add vitamin C to the water. It changes the taste, which often leads guinea pigs to drink less, working against the exact goal you're trying to achieve.

**What to avoid:** alfalfa hay for adults (too high in calcium, appropriate only for very young guinea pigs), iceberg lettuce (essentially no nutritional value), and high-sugar fruit as anything more than an occasional treat.
