# African Fat-Tailed Gecko Tank Setup: Built for the Ground, Not the Branches

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Unlike most of the geckos commonly kept as pets, fat-tailed geckos are ground-dwellers and burrowers, not climbers. For the full care picture beyond setup, see our complete [African Fat-Tailed Gecko care guide](/guides/african-fat-tail/).

## Enclosure Size

A <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4eSbKl2" product="20 gallon long tank">20-gallon long tank</AffiliateLink> works as a minimum for a single adult, though a 36x18x16 inch (40-gallon breeder) enclosure is a meaningfully better choice. Floor space matters far more than height, this is a terrestrial species that spends its time on the ground and underground, much like the [leopard gecko](/blog/leopard-gecko-tank-setup-guide/) it's so often compared to.

## Temperature and Heat Source

Yes, this species needs supplemental heat, unlike some of the other geckos in this series. Warm side (basking surface) should run 88 to 92°F, cool side 75 to 80°F, with a nighttime drop to around 70 to 75°F. Provide belly heat through an <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0BGS517FT?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="REPTIZOO Dimming Thermostat, PID Temperature Controller with Memory Function, Reptile Thermostat for Reptile Heat lamp">under-tank heat mat covering about a third of the floor, always on a thermostat</AffiliateLink>, with the probe positioned on the actual basking surface. Skip heat rocks and strong overhead basking bulbs entirely, both carry real burn risk. If using deep substrate, a heat mat under a warm hide often works better than one under thick substrate. An <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4flGgUg" product="Infrared temp gun">infrared temp gun</AffiliateLink> makes checking your actual surface temperatures much easier than a stick-on thermometer alone.

## Humidity

Higher than a leopard gecko needs, but this is still a mostly-dry enclosure overall. Ambient humidity around 50 to 70% works well, maintained with a permanent humid hide running 70 to 80%, that's the piece that actually prevents shedding problems, covered in more detail in our [African Fat-Tailed Gecko health issues guide](/blog/african-fat-tail-health-issues-guide/). Some keepers run a seasonal cycle, but a consistent moist hide matters more than chasing an exact seasonal number.

<FunFact>
Fat-tailed geckos are more vocal than most pet geckos, and the chirps and squeaks aren't random, they communicate real things like discomfort during handling. It's one more way this species diverges from the leopard gecko it gets compared to constantly, despite sharing eyelids, a lack of sticky toe pads, and a terrestrial lifestyle.
</FunFact>

## Substrate: The Detail That Makes This Species Different

Naturalistic sandy-soil mixes work well for healthy adults, a common blend is roughly 70% topsoil to 30% play sand, or a coconut fiber and <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0002DIZKC?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Zoo Med ReptiSand, 10 Pounds, Desert White">reptile sand</AffiliateLink> combination. Paper towel is the right choice for quarantine and juveniles. Provide enough depth, at least 4 inches, for genuine burrowing behavior.

A note on the sand debate: pure loose sand is sometimes flagged as an impaction risk, but the more accurate picture is that impaction tends to happen in geckos that are already unhealthy, dehydrated, or kept at incorrect temperatures, not simply from naturalistic substrate on its own. Still, avoid pure sand for very young or unwell animals as a precaution.

## Lighting

Fat-tailed geckos are nocturnal and can survive without UVB if given a high-D3 calcium supplement, but <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B00M9ONY1W?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Zoo Med 5.0 T5-HO 14&quot; Terrarium Hood with ReptiSun T5 HO 5.0 UVB Lamp">low-output UVB</AffiliateLink> in the 2 to 5% range is increasingly recommended. Run a 10 to 12 hour photoperiod, use a dim red or moonlight-style bulb rather than bright white light at night.

## The Non-Obvious Requirement: Build for Digging, Not Climbing

Skip the tall branches and climbing structures that other gecko species need, the [gargoyle gecko](/blog/gargoyle-gecko-tank-setup-guide/) and [mourning gecko](/blog/mourning-gecko-tank-setup-guide/) both want vertical space that would be wasted here. Instead, prioritize three hides, a warm one, a cool one, and a permanently moist one, plus enough substrate depth for real burrowing.
