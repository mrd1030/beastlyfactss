# Common Hermit Crab Health Issues (There's Almost No Vet Care, So Prevention Is Everything)

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Here's the thing to understand upfront about hermit crab health: there's essentially no practical veterinary care available for this species, similar to the situation with pet tarantulas. That reshapes everything about how to approach their wellbeing, husbandry and prevention do nearly all of the work. Get the enclosure right first, our [Hermit Crab tank setup guide](/blog/hermit-crab-tank-setup-guide/) covers the humidity and substrate details that prevent most of what follows, and see our full [Hermit Crab care guide](/guides/hermit-crab/) for everything else.

## Post-Purchase Stress (PPS), the Leading Cause of Death

Every pet hermit crab is wild-caught, not captive-bred, which means every single one goes through a real shock from capture, shipping, and a completely new environment. This adjustment period is where most hermit crab deaths actually happen.

**Watch for:** lethargy, burrowing and staying hidden, refusing food, and rapid loss of multiple limbs, which is a particularly strong indicator of advanced PPS. Once it's progressed significantly, there's often not much that can be done.

**The prevention approach:** quarantine and isolate new crabs, and improve humidity and temperature gradually rather than making sudden changes. A hermit crab generally isn't considered past the PPS risk period until it has successfully molted once in your care, that first successful molt is a real milestone, and one covered from the handling side in our [Hermit Crab handling guide](/blog/hermit-crab-handling-guide/), which explains why a newly arrived crab that buries itself and disappears for days is behaving normally rather than dying.

## Limb Loss

Can happen from stress, fights with cage-mates, mites, or generally wrong conditions. The good news is that lost limbs do regenerate over successive molts, first appearing as small buds before growing back to full size.

<FunFact>
Before a molt even begins, a hermit crab actually reabsorbs part of the calcium from its old exoskeleton to help build the new one underneath. That quiet, internal rebuilding process is a big part of why a molting crab needs total darkness and zero disturbance, it isn't just resting, its body is mid-renovation.
</FunFact>

## Dehydration and Gill Suffocation

Hermit crabs breathe through modified gills that must stay moist to function properly. Signs include an ashy appearance, lethargy, and an inability to fully retract into the shell. ## Bad or Interrupted Molts

Often caused by inadequate humidity, <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0B52DC9VB?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Fluker's Hermit Crab Sand Substrate">substrate</AffiliateLink> that's too shallow or dry, or a crab being disturbed while molting. This is frequently fatal, which is exactly why leaving a buried crab alone matters so much, no exceptions, regardless of how long it's been.

## Mites and Mold

Generally traceable to poor hygiene or inadequate ventilation in the enclosure.

## What "Seeking Help" Actually Looks Like for This Species

Since clinical vet care is rarely an option, the real response to a sick or stressed hermit crab is correcting the husbandry issue behind it, and isolating any crab that's aggressive, injured, or showing PPS signs from the rest of the group while it recovers.
