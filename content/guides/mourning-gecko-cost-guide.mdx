# How Much Does a Mourning Gecko Really Cost?

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Mourning geckos are one of the most affordable reptiles you can keep, and the way they're typically sold, in small groups rather than individually, changes the usual cost math in an interesting way. For the full care picture beyond budgeting, see our complete [Mourning Gecko care guide](/guides/mourning-gecko/).

## The Animal Itself: $15 to $65 Each, Less When Bought in Groups

Individual hatchlings and juveniles commonly run $15 to $50, with some listings reaching $65 to $100 for specific localities like Hawaiian or Philippine Yellow-Bellied types. Mourning geckos are parthenogenetic, every one is female, and they reproduce without a male at all. Breeders frequently sell them in pairs, trios, or larger lots at a lower per-animal price, a group of three might run around $60 total, roughly $20 each. Most keepers start with two and let the colony grow on its own.

You may see "Type A, B, C, D" labels on listings. These appear to refer to locality and appearance rather than genetically distinct morphs, and there's no strong evidence of a meaningful price premium tied to type. Price is driven mostly by age and how many you buy at once, not color.

## Vet Costs

A routine exotic exam commonly runs $35 to $105, with a fecal test adding $12 to $110. An illness visit with diagnostics typically runs $150 to $300 or more. Given the animal itself often costs less than a single vet visit, it's worth deciding in advance what you're prepared to spend if something goes wrong, our [Mourning Gecko health issues guide](/blog/mourning-gecko-health-issues-guide/) covers the problems most likely to send you there.

## Lifespan: A 10 to 15 Year Commitment

<FunFact>
With consistent humidity, calcium supplementation, and a varied diet, mourning geckos typically live 10 to 15 years in captivity. Egg-laying females have higher, ongoing calcium demands, so staying on top of supplementation is what separates a gecko that hits the low end of that range from one that hits the high end.
</FunFact>

It's a smaller long-term commitment than a crested or gargoyle gecko, both of which reliably live well into their teens.
