# How to Handle an African Fat-Tailed Gecko

African fat-tailed geckos are among the calmest, most genuinely handleable pet geckos available, often described as even more docile than their close cousin, the leopard gecko. For everything else about day-to-day care, see our full [African Fat-Tailed Gecko care guide](/guides/african-fat-tail/).

## What to Expect

Adults are tolerant of handling and usually stay calm throughout a session. Juveniles tend to be shyer and take longer to settle. Any gecko, regardless of species, can bite if it's sick, scared, or genuinely provoked, but this is the exception rather than the norm for this species.

## Getting Started

Wait about two weeks after bringing a new gecko home, don't begin handling until it's eating regularly. Support the body fully and let it walk across your hands rather than gripping it. Avoid handling right after feeding or during a shed cycle.

## Tail Drop

Like most geckos, a fat-tail will drop its tail if grabbed roughly or badly frightened. Never grab or restrain the tail. The regrown tail comes back looking different, smoother and more bulbous than the original, but it does regrow.

<FunFact>
A fat-tail's tail isn't just for balance, it's a genuine fat reserve the gecko draws on during lean periods, which is exactly why losing it is such a costly event and never something to risk by grabbing one by the tail.
</FunFact>

## Reading the Vocalizations

Fat-tailed geckos are more vocal than many other pet geckos, and the sounds actually mean something. Chirping or squeaking generally signals discomfort, often related to handling, treat it as a cue to ease off. A slow tail-swish combined with an arched back is a clearer "leave me alone" signal.

## The Leopard Gecko Comparison

These two species are constantly compared, and for good reason, they're close relatives (same subfamily), both have eyelids, both lack sticky toe pads, both are terrestrial. The main practical difference that affects care rather than handling: fat-tails need meaningfully higher humidity than leopard geckos, and are more sensitive to conditions that run too dry.
