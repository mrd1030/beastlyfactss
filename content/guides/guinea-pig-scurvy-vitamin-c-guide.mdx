# Guinea Pig Scurvy: Why Vitamin C Isn't Optional

<VetDisclaimer />
<AffiliateDisclosure />

Guinea pigs are one of a small handful of mammals, alongside humans and a few other species, that cannot produce their own vitamin C. Every other common small pet, rabbits, hamsters, chinchillas, can synthesize what they need internally, which is exactly why generic small-pet advice so often misses this one guinea-pig-specific requirement. For everything else about day-to-day care, see our full [Guinea Pig care guide](/guides/guinea-pig/).

## What Scurvy Actually Is

Vitamin C is essential for producing collagen, the structural protein that holds together skin, blood vessels, joints, and connective tissue throughout the body. Most mammals manufacture their own vitamin C in the liver using an enzyme most guinea pigs simply don't have a working copy of. Without a reliable dietary source, collagen production breaks down over days to weeks, and the effects show up everywhere collagen matters most: joints, gums, blood vessel walls, and wound healing.

<FunFact>
Guinea pigs, humans, and fruit bats are among a small handful of mammal groups that independently lost the ability to make their own vitamin C at some point in evolutionary history, and in each case the same underlying gene is the one that stopped working.
</FunFact>

The early signs are easy to miss or blame on something else entirely, which is part of why scurvy remains one of the more common preventable illnesses in pet guinea pigs. A guinea pig that seems a little slower or less interested in food than usual is worth a closer look at diet before assuming it's nothing.

## Why Fortified Pellets Alone Aren't Enough

Most commercial guinea pig pellets are fortified with vitamin C at the time of milling, but vitamin C is notoriously unstable and degrades quickly when exposed to heat, light, and humidity. A meaningful share of that fortified vitamin C is already gone within weeks of milling, even in an unopened bag, and continues degrading faster once the bag is opened and exposed to air. Relying on the pellet bag's nutrition label as your only vitamin C source is one of the most common ways owners end up with a scurvy case despite feeding a "complete" diet.

## The Best Sources, and How to Use Them

Bell peppers are the standout source: red, yellow, and orange peppers contain substantially more vitamin C than green ones, and a quarter of a medium pepper covers most of a single adult guinea pig's daily requirement by itself. Dark leafy greens, kale, parsley, cilantro, and romaine, are good secondary sources worth rotating in for variety and to avoid over-relying on any single vegetable. Fresh food has to be part of the daily routine, not an occasional treat, since guinea pigs have no internal reserve to fall back on between servings the way species that can synthesize their own vitamin C do.

## Treatment and Recovery

A guinea pig showing signs of scurvy needs a vet visit, not a wait-and-see approach, especially once joint pain or gum bleeding is visible.
