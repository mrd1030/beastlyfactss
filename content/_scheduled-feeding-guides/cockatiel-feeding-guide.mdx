# Cockatiel Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

There's no strict meal schedule for a cockatiel the way there is for a dog, what actually matters is getting the ratio of pellets to seeds to fresh food right, and catching appetite loss quickly in a bird this small. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Cockatiel care guide](/guides/cockatiel/).

## Feeding Approach

Cockatiels are free-choice eaters, not scheduled ones. Their fast metabolism means most choose to eat throughout the day, so refresh the base pellet bowl in the morning when they're hungriest and top it up if it empties before evening, it should never sit empty for long.

Fresh fruits and vegetables should make up roughly 20 to 25% of the daily diet by volume, but pull anything uneaten within a couple hours to prevent spoilage and bacterial growth. Treats and table food should stay small, no more than about 10% of the weekly diet, a teaspoon for a cockatiel is roughly equivalent to a dinner-plate-sized portion for a person. Fresh water needs to be available continuously, with dishes cleaned daily.

Weaning typically happens around 42 to 52 days old, and most pet owners acquire cockatiels only after they're already weaned onto solid food, hand-rearing very young chicks is specialist territory best left to an experienced breeder or avian vet. Once weaned, cockatiels stay on the same pellet-based diet as adults, laying hens are the one group that need extra calcium consideration.

## What Cockatiels Actually Eat

The foundation should be a formulated <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4566tQT" product="ZuPreem FruitBlend Pellets for Cockatiels/Lovebirds, 2 lb">pellet diet</AffiliateLink>, roughly 75 to 80% of daily intake according to veterinary sources, with fresh vegetables and fruit filling another 20 to 25% and seeds kept to a small minority rather than the base diet. This matters more than it might sound like: commercial seed mixes are high in fat and carbohydrates but deficient in protein, vitamins, and minerals including calcium and vitamin A, and birds will pick their favorite seeds (sunflower, millet) while skipping the more balanced components. All-seed diets are a well-documented cause of the malnutrition commonly seen in pet cockatiels, and it's linked to fatty liver disease.

Fresh produce should be washed and cut small, with apple seeds removed. Small amounts of table food are fine in moderation, cooked lean meat, fish, egg, or a little cheese, but skip processed foods. Cockatiels don't need grit, they hull seeds before swallowing, and overconsuming grit is actually a real risk, especially for a sick bird. Laying hens benefit from a calcium-fortified diet or a cuttlebone, since a hen physiologically pulls calcium from her own bones to build eggshells.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe: apple with seeds removed, apricot, banana, blueberries, melon, mango, papaya, peach, pear, strawberries, broccoli, carrots, leafy greens, bell peppers, pumpkin, squash, sweet potato, zucchini, and small amounts of cooked lean meat, fish, egg, or cheese.

Toxic, not just something to limit: avocado is the single most consistently flagged toxic food across veterinary, manufacturer, and consumer sources alike. Onion, chocolate, caffeine, and alcohol are also genuinely dangerous. Iceberg lettuce and celery aren't toxic, but they're nutritionally close to empty calories, worth skipping in favor of more nutrient-dense produce.

## Why Cockatiels Stop Eating

1. **Resistance to switching from seeds to pellets.** This can take days, weeks, or even months in a mature bird. Offering pellets when the bird is hungriest, and mixing them with seed or moist food at first, tends to help.
2. **Heavy molting.** Growing a full new set of feathers raises the demand for protein, calcium, and iron sharply, and can be genuinely taxing on the body.
3. **A new environment or handling stress.** A short adjustment period of reduced eating after a move or a new home is commonly reported, though this one is less rigorously documented for cockatiels specifically than the others on this list.
4. **Seasonal hormonal behavior.** Cockatiels can be triggered into nesting behavior in the fall, and hens are known to lay eggs even without a mate present, both come with real physiological cost.
5. **Egg binding.** A hen that can't pass an egg normally shows weakness, straining, and reluctance to perch. This is a genuine emergency, not something to monitor.
6. **General illness.** Listlessness, sleeping fluffed on two feet, nasal discharge, clicking respiration, and tail bobbing commonly accompany appetite loss in a genuinely sick bird.

## When to Actually Worry

We couldn't find a single, consistently sourced number for exactly how many hours or days a cockatiel can safely go without eating, and would rather say that honestly than invent a round figure. What's well documented instead is a set of concrete warning signs: becoming listless or withdrawn, sleeping on two feet with feathers fluffed (rather than the normal one-foot-up resting posture), nasal discharge, bubbles from the beak or clicking respiratory sounds, tail bobbing, vomiting (distinct from normal regurgitation, vomit sticks to the face and chest feathers), and generally unkempt, ratty-looking plumage from neglected preening.

Given how small cockatiels are and how fast their metabolism runs, treat any noticeable, sustained drop in eating alongside these signs as a same-day or next-day call to an avian vet rather than something to watch for a few days first. For egg-bound hens specifically, this is a same-day emergency, small birds can die within a few hours of becoming egg-bound.
