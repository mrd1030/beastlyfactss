# Rabbit Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

A rabbit's gut depends on constant food throughput the same way a guinea pig's does, which is exactly why a skipped meal deserves same-day attention here, not a wait-and-see approach. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Rabbit care guide](/guides/rabbit/).

## Feeding Schedule by Life Stage

- **Nursing kits (birth to about 7 weeks):** Mother's milk only at first, notably just once or rarely twice a day for 2 to 10 minutes per session, rabbit milk is unusually rich, so kits can go roughly a day between feedings. Weaned by around 8 weeks.
- **Transition (3 to 7 weeks):** Alfalfa hay and alfalfa-based pellets introduced with free-choice access alongside nursing. New greens typically start around 12 weeks in small amounts.
- **Juveniles (7 weeks to 7 months):** Unlimited alfalfa hay and alfalfa-based pellets, the extra calories and calcium support growth. Vegetables added gradually from about 3 months, one new item at a time.
- **Transition to adult (roughly 4 to 6 months):** Alfalfa is phased out in favor of grass hays, and pellet portions are gradually reduced over several weeks as unlimited grass hay takes over as the diet base.
- **Adults (7 months to about 5 years):** Unlimited grass hay as 80 to 85% of the diet. Measured pellets, roughly 1/8 to 1/4 cup of high-fiber, timothy-based pellets per 5 to 6 lbs of body weight daily, sources vary on the exact ratio. Vegetables, roughly 1 to 4 cups per several pounds of body weight depending on the source, at least 3 varieties of leafy greens. Fruit and treats stay capped low, roughly 1 to 2 tablespoons once or twice a week, not daily.
- **Seniors (6+ years):** Unlimited grass hay continues, alfalfa may be reintroduced if the rabbit is underweight, with a higher treat allowance and more individualized, vet-guided feeding.

Fresh water needs to be available at all times, by bottle or bowl, at every stage.

## What Rabbits Actually Eat

Alfalfa hay is richer and reserved for babies, juveniles, pregnant or nursing does, and underweight seniors, not healthy adults, the extra calcium and calories can contribute to obesity and bladder sludge.

Pellets should be a fresh, high-fiber, <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B06XX68LM2?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Oxbow Garden Select Adult Rabbit Food, High-Fiber Rabbit Pellets">plain timothy-based pellet</AffiliateLink>, not colorful mixes with seeds, nuts, or dried fruit, which are linked to obesity and dental disease. Pellets are measured for adults, not free-fed the way hay is.

Vegetables should be fresh leafy greens as the bulk of the fresh portion, romaine, cilantro, parsley, basil, arugula, bok choy, and carrot tops, with at least 3 varieties offered daily and new items introduced one at a time to watch for soft stool. Non-leafy vegetables like bell pepper, cucumber, and carrots go in smaller, more limited amounts, they're higher in sugar and starch than their reputation suggests. Fruit is purely a treat, fed a couple times a week at most.

There's no gut-loading or calcium dusting here, that's a reptile practice. The closest equivalent is gradual diet transitions, sudden changes to hay, pellet brand, or vegetables can disrupt the cecal gut flora and trigger soft stool or GI stasis.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe in small amounts: fresh parsley, cilantro, basil, and mint, small pieces of apple, banana, strawberry, or blueberry, and limited bell pepper, cucumber, or carrot.

Toxic, never feed: avocado (can cause respiratory distress and fatal heart failure), chocolate, onions, garlic, and chives (can cause hemolytic anemia), and rhubarb (high oxalic acid, linked to kidney damage). Iceberg lettuce isn't toxic but has near-empty nutritional value and can cause digestive upset in quantity. Skip processed human foods sometimes mistaken for treats too, bread, crackers, cereal, and especially yogurt drops, which are explicitly flagged as inappropriate despite being sold commercially as rabbit treats.

## Why Rabbits Stop Eating

1. **GI stasis.** A slowdown or stoppage of normal gut motility, one of the most common life-threatening emergencies in pet rabbits. Often the downstream result of low fiber, dehydration, pain, or stress, and self-perpetuating once it starts.
2. **Dental disease.** Rabbit teeth grow continuously, and overgrown teeth or spurs can cut the mouth or make chewing painful, one of the most frequent underlying causes of appetite loss, and one that can develop silently.
3. **Stress.** New housing, a new pet in the household, loud noises, or handling changes are real triggers, even when the cause seems minor to an owner.
4. **An abrupt diet change.** Switching hay, pellet brand, or vegetables too quickly can disrupt cecal bacteria balance and cause reduced eating or soft stool.
5. **Pain from an unrelated illness.** Urinary tract disease, gas, respiratory infection, or reproductive disease can all cause secondary appetite loss.
6. **Fur ingestion during a heavy molt.** Swallowed fur combined with dehydrated gut contents can form a hard-to-pass mass. Regular brushing during molts helps prevent it.
7. **Certain medications.** Some drugs used to treat other conditions can themselves slow gut motility as a side effect.

## When to Actually Worry

Because rabbits are obligate continuous grazers, any noticeable drop in eating or fecal output is treated as urgent, not something to wait out. Sources put the alarm point anywhere from 3 to 12 hours without food or droppings, and agree that fasting beyond about 24 hours risks fatty liver disease. The practical takeaway across sources: don't wait through a full day hoping it resolves, same-day vet contact is the safe default the moment a rabbit skips a meal or stops producing normal droppings.

Warning signs that make it more urgent regardless of the exact hour count: no fecal pellets at all, or pellets that are small, misshapen, or strung together with mucus, a hunched or tense posture, audible tooth grinding, a bloated or tense abdomen, lethargy, drooling, and reduced water intake. Roughly 70% of rabbits recover with prompt veterinary treatment, but delayed cases can require days of hospitalization, early intervention genuinely matters here.
