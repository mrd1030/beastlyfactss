# Guinea Pig Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

A guinea pig's gut depends on near-constant food throughput, which is exactly why "skipping a meal" is a much bigger deal here than it is for most other pets. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Guinea Pig care guide](/guides/guinea-pig/).

## Feeding Schedule

Guinea pigs are near-constant grazers, so continuous access matters more than counting meals. Fresh grass hay, <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/4h9WW2u" product="Oxbow Western Timothy Hay, 90 oz">timothy, orchard, or oat/meadow</AffiliateLink>, should be unlimited and available at every life stage, sources put it at 70 to 80% of the diet, and it should never run out. A measured daily portion of guinea-pig-specific fortified pellets goes on top, typically in the morning. Fresh vegetables are offered daily as a small handful, any time of day, with anything uneaten pulled after 30 minutes to a couple of hours to avoid spoilage. Treats, fruit included, should stay under about 10% of the daily diet.

Babies start nibbling hay, pellets, and vegetables within hours of birth even while still nursing, and are typically fully weaned by 3 to 5 weeks old. Juveniles under about 6 months do best with alfalfa hay added alongside or instead of grass hay, the extra protein and calcium support growth. Adults should stick to grass hay as the staple, alfalfa's richer profile can contribute to obesity if fed regularly. Pregnant, nursing, senior, or ill guinea pigs benefit from alfalfa and need roughly 2 to 3 times a healthy adult's vitamin C.

## What Guinea Pigs Actually Eat

Grass hay is the staple, fed unlimited, it isn't filler, it supplies the long-strand fiber that keeps digestion moving and the abrasive chewing that wears down ever-growing teeth. On top of that, a daily measured portion of commercial pellets made specifically for guinea pigs, not rabbit or other rodent pellets, which contain no added vitamin C since those animals can make their own. Look for stabilized vitamin C on the label, and use pellets within about 90 days of manufacture, that vitamin degrades with light, air, and time.

Fresh vegetables go in daily, rotated among varieties: bell pepper, ripe tomato, romaine lettuce, broccoli, cauliflower, carrot, spinach, kale, parsley, cilantro, and cucumber are all commonly used. Because guinea pigs can't make their own vitamin C, a daily source is mandatory, whether that's fortified pellets, vitamin-C-rich vegetables, or <AffiliateLink href="https://amzn.to/44CDAMd" product="Oxbow Natural Science Vitamin C supplement">a direct supplement</AffiliateLink>. Never dose vitamin C through the water bottle, it oxidizes quickly and a picky drinker may drink less if the water tastes different.

Fruit is an occasional treat, not a staple, small amounts of orange, kiwi, apple, or pear are fine. Fresh water needs to be available at all times.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe: bell peppers, ripe tomato, romaine lettuce, cucumber, carrot, celery, broccoli, cauliflower, dandelion greens, parsley, cilantro, spinach, kale, and small bits of orange, kiwi, apple, or pear.

Genuinely toxic: avocado (linked to heart muscle damage), chocolate, onions, garlic, chives, and leeks (can cause hemolysis, with symptoms sometimes taking 3 to 5 days to appear), rhubarb (high oxalic acid, linked to bladder stones), raw potato and unripe tomato or their plant leaves, mushrooms, and fruit pits or seeds. Skip high-starch or high-fat treats too, nuts, seeds, peanut butter, corn, bread, and pasta aren't toxic, but guinea pigs will pick them over healthy hay and pellets, causing nutrient gaps and obesity. As strict herbivores, meat, eggs, and dairy are off-limits entirely.

## Why Guinea Pigs Stop Eating

1. **Dental disease.** The single most commonly cited cause. Guinea pig teeth grow continuously, and uneven wear creates sharp points or misalignment that make chewing painful, often invisible to an owner without a vet exam.
2. **GI stasis.** Gut motility slows or stops, usually from inadequate fiber, dehydration, pain, or stress. It's both a cause and effect of not eating, and a genuine emergency.
3. **Illness or pain elsewhere.** Respiratory infections, urinary tract issues or bladder stones, arthritis, or injury commonly suppress appetite as a secondary sign.
4. **Environmental stress.** A new home, cage move, new cage mates, or loud noise can cause temporary refusal in a species that's naturally nervous by nature. A bonded companion helps.
5. **Dietary neophobia.** Guinea pigs establish food preferences early in life and don't adapt well to sudden changes, a new hay type or pellet brand can get turned down simply for being unfamiliar. Introduce new foods gradually.
6. **Pregnancy toxemia.** A real risk in overweight or late-term pregnant sows, presenting with appetite loss and potentially fatal without prompt treatment.
7. **Vitamin C deficiency.** Decreased appetite is one of the earliest signs, appearing within 2 to 3 weeks of a deficient diet, alongside foot inflammation, weight loss, and a rough coat.

## When to Actually Worry

For guinea pigs, skipping meals is basically never normal the way it can be for other pets, their digestion depends on constant food throughput, so the safe window is short. Sources put the point to call a vet at 8 to 12 hours without eating or producing droppings, and once GI stasis actually sets in, it can become life-threatening within 24 to 48 hours. With prompt treatment, many guinea pigs start improving within 24 to 72 hours.

Signs that warrant a same-day call rather than waiting: reduced or absent droppings, or droppings turning small or dry, lethargy or reluctance to move, a hunched or tense posture, audible teeth grinding, noticeable weight loss, a bloated or firm abdomen, reduced water intake, and labored breathing. Really, any noticeable drop in eating at all is reason to contact an exotics-experienced vet promptly rather than waiting to see if it resolves on its own.
