# German Shepherd Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

German Shepherds mature slowly for a large breed and carry a couple of genuinely breed-linked feeding considerations worth knowing before you're troubleshooting them blind. For the rest of their breed-specific quirks, see our [German Shepherd guide](/guides/dog-german-shepherd/).

## Feeding Schedule

Puppies eat 4 meals a day from 6 to 12 weeks, dropping to 3 meals by 3 to 6 months (the rounded puppy-belly look should be gone by around 12 weeks), then down to 2 meals by 6 to 12 months. Because this is a large breed, adult males run roughly 66 to 88 lbs and females 49 to 71 lbs, growth extends well past what smaller breeds need, some large-breed feeding protocols keep puppies on growth food until 18 to 24 months, and the shift to adult food typically happens around 12 to 14 months rather than the standard 12-month cutoff.

Adults do well on 2 meals a day, and there's a real safety reason behind the split beyond convenience: research cited by AKC found dogs fed twice daily have roughly half the bloat risk of dogs fed once a day, relevant here since German Shepherds are a deep-chested breed commonly flagged for elevated bloat risk.

There's no single universal cups-per-day figure. Use the specific food's own feeding chart based on weight and life stage, have a vet calculate calorie needs from activity level, and adjust by body condition rather than a fixed number, caloric density varies too much between brands to trust one generic figure.

## What German Shepherds Actually Eat

A complete-and-balanced commercial dog food is the baseline. For puppies, a large-breed growth formula that slows growth rate without capping final size has been shown to reduce the incidence or severity of hip dysplasia in adults, a real concern for this breed, one study found hip dysplasia rates around 19% and elbow dysplasia around 18%. Breed-specific puppy formulas also tend to emphasize precise calcium and phosphorus ratios for bone and joint development during that extended growth period.

Adults typically maintain a healthy weight naturally and don't need a special diet as they mature, management is mostly about restricting calories if weight starts creeping up. This breed also has a known tendency toward digestive sensitivity, a highly digestible protein source helps.

A meaningful subset of German Shepherds need real dietary management beyond normal maintenance food: Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency is suspected to be an inherited condition specifically in this breed. It requires a highly digestible, low-fat diet combined with pancreatic enzyme replacement added at every meal, sometimes alongside vitamin B12 supplementation, a genuinely breed-linked dietary need, not generic advice. Fresh water should be available at all times.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe in moderation: plain cooked turkey or pork, fully cooked fish like salmon (no more than about twice a week), fully cooked eggs, small amounts of lower-fat cheese if tolerated, plain air-popped popcorn, apple (cored, seeds removed), blueberries, carrots, green beans, and pumpkin puree.

Never feed: chocolate and caffeine, grapes and raisins (linked to acute kidney failure), onions, garlic, and chives (garlic is roughly five times more toxic than the rest of that plant family), xylitol, macadamia nuts, avocado, and alcohol or raw yeast dough, the dough itself can expand and cause dangerous bloating in a breed already predisposed to it. Given this breed's documented tendency toward digestive sensitivity and EPI, high-fat table scraps and fried foods deserve extra caution beyond the general toxic-food list.

## Why German Shepherds Stop Eating

1. **Owner absence or a new environment.** Dogs are pack animals and may not eat while their person is away, or during a stressful move or change.
2. **Individual quirks.** Time-of-day feeding preferences, disliking a reformulated food, or a batch that's gone stale are all ordinary, non-medical reasons.
3. **Short-term illness.** The most common reason overall, a dog may not eat for a couple of days while fighting off something minor before appetite returns on its own.
4. **New medication.** Antibiotics, NSAIDs like carprofen, and other drugs can cause a temporarily upset stomach and reduced appetite.
5. **Dental or oral pain.** Sometimes called pseudo-anorexia, the dog wants to eat but physically can't, from gum disease, loose teeth, or oral pain. Needs a vet or dental exam, not just patience.
6. **Systemic illness.** Kidney disease, diabetes, GI obstruction, nausea, or fever can all suppress appetite as a genuine warning sign.
7. **EPI.** Unlike everything else on this list, this breed-linked condition shows up as increased appetite alongside progressive weight loss and bulky, greasy stools, not refusal to eat.
8. **Bloat.** Not appetite loss but an acute, eating-adjacent emergency, restlessness after a large meal and unproductive attempts to vomit are the pattern to watch for.

## When to Actually Worry

For an otherwise normal adult, still walking and drinking with no other symptoms, a couple of days without eating generally isn't alarming, most often it's a minor, self-resolving illness. That window shrinks sharply the moment other symptoms appear, contact a vet within 8 to 12 hours if refusal comes with vomiting or diarrhea. There's no separately sourced hour-count specific to puppies, but as a large breed still growing for well over a year, a young German Shepherd skipping meals generally warrants faster follow-up than an adult would.

Separately and urgently: a bloated or distended abdomen combined with restlessness, retching, or unproductive attempts to vomit after eating is never a wait-and-see situation. This is the classic bloat pattern for a deep-chested breed like this one, and it can progress to shock within an hour or two without treatment.
