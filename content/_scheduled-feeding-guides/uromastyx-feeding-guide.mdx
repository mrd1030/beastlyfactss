# Uromastyx Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

For a Uromastyx, heat isn't just comfort, it's the actual mechanism that breaks down food, which changes how you should think about a Uromastyx that's eating but still losing weight. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Uromastyx care guide](/guides/uromastyx/).

## Feeding Schedule by Age

- **Babies and juveniles:** Fresh vegetables and greens daily, offering as much as they'll clean up in one sitting.
- **Adults:** This is where sources genuinely disagree. Figures range from 4 to 5 times a week, to 5 to 7 days a week, down to just 2 to 4 times a week being described as sufficient, with one source explicitly warning that overfeeding is a real, common mistake, not just a theoretical risk. Treat this as a wide range rather than a fixed number, and lean toward the less-frequent end if your Uromastyx is holding weight easily.

Timing matters regardless of frequency: put food down about 1 to 2 hours after the basking light comes on, so the animal is already warm enough to digest what it eats, and leave it available for daytime grazing. Some keepers pull the dish 2 to 3 hours before lights-out.

## What Uromastyx Actually Eat

Uromastyx are treated as strict to near-strict herbivores in captivity. The bulk of the diet is dark leafy greens and edible flowers, chopped or shredded, not whole leaves, which are a choking and impaction risk especially for young animals: collard greens, dandelion greens and flowers, mustard and turnip greens, endive, escarole, arugula, watercress, and hibiscus leaves and flowers. A few times a week, add other vegetables: cactus pad or prickly pear (notably calcium-rich), butternut and summer squash, bell peppers, shredded carrots, green beans, and peas.

Seeds and legumes, soaked or sprouted lentils, white millet, small amounts of sesame, are a real point of disagreement, some sources say no more than once a week to avoid obesity, others allow small amounts several times weekly. Treat seeds as limited, not a daily component either way. Fruit, papaya, fig, apple, prickly pear, berries, is a rare treat, guidance ranges from once a month to weekly, err toward less rather than more.

Uromastyx get most of their water from food and often don't drink from a bowl.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe, sparingly: papaya, fig, apple, prickly pear, berries, cherries, apricot, sprouted lentils, dandelion flowers, and hibiscus flowers.

Avoid: spinach (high oxalates, specifically linked to bladder stones in this species), avocado (toxic), iceberg lettuce and celery (essentially empty nutrition), citrus fruit and tomatoes (too acidic, can cause diarrhea), corn, potatoes, and bananas (high phosphorus, work against the calcium ratio this species needs), sunflower seeds (too fatty), and frozen greens fed long-term (linked to a thiamine deficiency causing tremors that can mimic metabolic bone disease). High-oxalate items like beets and parsley, and goitrogenic items like broccoli and cabbage, are fine occasionally but shouldn't be daily staples.

## Why Uromastyx Stop Eating

1. **Brumation.** Triggered by cooler temperatures and shorter daylight, appetite drops or stops for roughly 1 to 2 months in most descriptions.
2. **Shedding.** Young, fast-growing Uromastyx can shed every month or two, and appetite commonly dips around it, a normal, temporary reaction.
3. **Breeding-cycle changes.** After mating, females often eat and move less for a period, and males can turn territorial during breeding season, both can look concerning but are considered normal seasonal patterns.
4. **New-environment stress.** Newly acquired Uromastyx commonly refuse food for roughly 1 to 2 weeks while settling in.
5. **General husbandry stress.** Inadequate hides, too much handling, loud noise, or an unsuitable setup can suppress appetite, usually resolving once the stressor is corrected.
6. **Insufficient basking temperature.** Uromastyx digest via hindgut fermentation that only works well at very high body temperatures, roughly 120 to 140°F on the basking surface depending on the source. Too cool, and appetite drops, or the animal eats but poorly digests it, leading to weight loss despite eating.
7. **Illness.** Mouth rot, respiratory infection, internal parasites (more common in wild-caught animals), metabolic bone disease, and organ disease are all genuine medical causes.
8. **Impaction.** Usually from ingested substrate or unchewed fibrous material, showing as reduced defecation or thin, stringy stool alongside poor appetite.
9. **Dietary boredom.** Repeatedly offering the same one or two vegetables can lead to picky refusal, rotating greens usually resolves it.

## When to Actually Worry

Short refusals are usually not an emergency: acclimation fasting of about 1 to 2 weeks in a newly acquired animal, brief shedding dips, and a seasonal brumation slowdown of roughly 1 to 2 months are all commonly described as normal, provided the animal still looks alert, hydrated, and isn't losing significant weight.

The recurring concrete threshold: if appetite loss persists beyond about a week outside of an obvious brumation, shedding, or acclimation context, it's time for a reptile-experienced vet. For a brumating animal, weight is the real distinguishing signal, losing more than roughly 5 to 10% of body weight while not eating is inconsistent with ordinary brumation, weigh a fasting Uromastyx weekly to catch this. See a vet regardless of duration for lethargy, sunken eyes or tented skin, inability to defecate or thin stringy stool, visible swelling, mouth discharge, or labored breathing.
