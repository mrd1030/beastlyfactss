# Pacman Frog Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

Pacman frogs have the opposite feeding problem from most pets, getting them to stop, not to start. Here's how to actually manage that, and how to read a frog that's genuinely gone off food. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Pacman Frog care guide](/guides/pacman-frog/).

## Feeding Schedule by Age

- **Juveniles:** Fed daily by most sources, though one specialty retailer narrows it to small prey items every 1 to 2 days.
- **Adults:** Genuinely unsettled across sources. Several sources say 1 to 2 times a week, an exotics veterinary clinic says 2 to 3 times a week. Treat roughly 1 to 3 times a week as the range rather than chasing one exact number.

No source gives a fixed prey count per feeding. Instead, size prey no wider than the frog's own head, and judge portion by body condition, round but not visibly bloated. Pacman frogs are described across multiple sources as highly food-motivated animals that will keep eating past the point of being full, so body condition is the real control here, not a schedule.

## What Pacman Frogs Actually Eat

The staple rotation is gut-loaded crickets, dubia roaches, and earthworms or nightcrawlers. Mealworms and waxworms work as occasional treats only, they're notably fatty, and silkworms and hornworms are soft-bodied, lower-impaction-risk options. For adults only, thawed, never live, never microwaved, frozen rodents, pinky mice up to adult mice, appear in feeding guidance roughly every other week, used sparingly, one veterinary source specifically warns about obesity and liver complications from overuse. Juveniles should not be given rodents at all.

Gut-load feeder insects on a nutrient-dense diet for roughly 24 to 72 hours before offering them. Dust with calcium and vitamin D3 daily for juveniles and a few times a week for adults, some vet guidance says every other feeding instead, both land in a similar range. A general reptile multivitamin about once a week is also common. A cricket-only diet is a real metabolic bone disease risk, most feeder insects besides earthworms run low in calcium relative to phosphorus, which is exactly why dusting and variety both matter.

Always feed with feeding tongs, never fingers. These frogs have a genuinely strong bite, and letting them associate fingers with food is worth avoiding from day one.

## Foods to Avoid

Live rodents carry real injury and infection risk to the frog. Never microwave thawed rodents, it creates hot spots that can burn the frog's mouth, and never re-feed something previously thawed and refused. Skip wild-caught insects entirely, and fireflies specifically are documented as lethally toxic to insectivorous amphibians, this is corroborated by university entomology research, not just hobbyist warnings. Avoid loose, particulate substrates like sand or gravel, they're not food, but Pacman frogs strike blindly at prey and can swallow substrate along with it, a real impaction risk.

## Why Pacman Frogs Stop Eating

1. **Temperature or humidity out of range.** One of the most common causes. Target roughly 75 to 85°F daytime, 65 to 75°F nighttime, and 50 to 80% humidity.
2. **Stress from a move or handling.** Newly acquired frogs, or ones in a freshly changed enclosure, commonly refuse food for a period while adjusting. Overhandling is specifically named as a stressor.
3. **Shedding.** Pacman frogs periodically shed, and typically eat the shed skin, showing reduced food interest during that cycle.
4. **Aestivation.** A heat or drought response, not true hibernation, where the frog seals itself in a hardened mucus cocoon and survives for months without food or water. Can be triggered in captivity by environmental drift at any time of year. Frogs under about 12 months should never be allowed to aestivate, it can be fatal at that age, keep temperature and light consistent to prevent it in young frogs.
5. **Breeding-cycle changes.** Deliberate breeding practice involves a cool, dry dormancy period of roughly 60 days before warm, wet conditions trigger spawning, breeding pairs eat and drink very little during that window. Not something that happens to an ordinary pet on its own.
6. **Pickiness.** Some frogs are genuinely fussy about an unfamiliar prey type and may refuse it at first.
7. **Illness.** Parasites, bacterial or fungal infection, including red-leg syndrome, present with appetite loss alongside lethargy and weight loss.
8. **Impaction.** Blind lunging at prey can mean accidentally swallowing loose substrate, causing a blockage that itself suppresses appetite and reduces normal defecation.

## When to Actually Worry

Pacman frogs are unusually fasting-tolerant thanks to large fat reserves in their pear-shaped bodies, but the exact safe duration mostly comes from hobbyist sources rather than veterinary ones, treat specific week counts as rough consensus, not clinical fact. Commonly repeated figures: healthy adults can fast 2 to 4 weeks without concern, some tolerating up to about 6 weeks, juveniles are said to tolerate roughly up to a week before it's worth investigating further.

Body condition and behavior matter more than the calendar. A frog that stays active, alert, and hydrated with normal-looking feces and stable weight is generally fine even while skipping meals, weighing weekly and treating roughly 10% weight loss over a couple of weeks as a signal to act is good practice. See an exotics vet for a frog that's lethargic, visibly losing weight, sunken-looking, not producing normal feces, refusing food for a month or more, or showing red or inflamed skin. Young frogs under about a year that stop eating and appear to be going dormant should not be left to sleep it out, that specific pattern can be fatal at that age.
