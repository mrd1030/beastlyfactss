# Corn Snake Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

Corn snakes are generally reliable eaters, which is exactly why a sudden refusal tends to worry new owners more than it needs to. Here's what a real feeding routine looks like, and how to read the difference. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Corn Snake care guide](/guides/corn-snake/).

## Feeding Schedule by Age and Weight

Specialist reptile-care sources give the most granular breakdown, keyed to both age and weight rather than age alone:

- **Hatchlings (0 to 3 months, under about 30g):** Every 5 to 7 days, starting on pinky mice.
- **Young juveniles (3 to 9 months, 30 to 100g):** Every 7 days.
- **Juveniles (9 to 18 months, 100 to 300g):** Every 7 to 10 days.
- **Sub-adults (18 to 36 months, 300 to 600g):** Every 10 to 14 days.
- **Adults (3+ years, 600g+):** Every 14 to 21 days.

A vet-content source gives a notably more frequent version across the board, every other day for babies, weekly for juveniles, every 1 to 2 weeks for adults. That's a real disagreement, especially for hatchlings, roughly a 2 to 3x difference in frequency. The specialist consensus above is more consistent across independent sources and is worth weighting more heavily, but it's not silently settled.

Size prey at roughly 1 to 1.5 times the width of the snake's body at its widest point, or about 10 to 15% of body weight per meal. Two smaller prey items can substitute for one larger one if you're between sizes. Let body condition and monthly weigh-ins guide the schedule more than the calendar, and don't feed on too tight a schedule once the snake is an adult, one source warns it can produce obesity within 12 to 18 months.

## What Corn Snakes Actually Eat

Whole, frozen-thawed rodents are the staple: pinky mice for hatchlings, moving up through fuzzies and hoppers to adult mice or small rats as the snake grows. Frozen-thawed is strongly preferred over live prey, a live rodent can bite, scratch, or claw a snake that isn't ready to strike, and that can cause a serious infection. Buy from a commercial or captive-bred source, not wild-caught, to avoid parasites and disease.

Thaw prey in the refrigerator overnight, then warm it in warm, not boiling, water to roughly 98 to 102°F to mimic living prey. Never microwave it, and never re-freeze prey that's been thawed and refused.

Supplementation generally isn't necessary. A whole-prey diet, bones and organs included, provides complete nutrition, unlike insect-eating reptiles. Where dusting is used at all, it's specifically for hatchlings on pinky mice, a light calcium/multivitamin dusting every 3 to 4 feedings, tapering off on larger prey. Corn snakes don't need UVB the way many lizards do, so vitamin D3 supplementation generally isn't necessary either, and over-supplementing calcium can itself cause mineral imbalance or kidney stress.

## What to Avoid

Corn snakes don't really have a treat culture, appropriately sized whole prey is both the staple and effectively the only food, with occasional variety (day-old chicks, quail eggs) as acceptable rather than a true treat. Skip live prey (injury risk to the snake), wild-caught prey of any kind, meat scraps or cooked food, prey that's been thawed and refused then re-frozen, and insects, corn snakes reportedly have an aversion to them and they aren't a natural food source for this species. Don't feed again within about 24 hours of the last meal either, that's not enough time to digest.

## Why Corn Snakes Stop Eating

1. **Shedding.** Appetite typically drops 5 to 7 days before a shed, recognizable by the eyes clouding over or turning a milky blue, and sometimes for a short window after.
2. **Brumation.** A deliberate breeder practice or a seasonal instinct, done properly by gradually dropping enclosure temperatures over 1 to 2 weeks, followed by 2 to 4 months without food. A mandatory 10 to 14 day fast at normal temperature has to come first, undigested food in a cooling gut can rot and cause a fatal infection.
3. **Breeding season, mainly in males.** Males especially may stop eating for weeks to months after brumation while searching for a mate, showing more restlessness and tongue-flicking instead. Normal as long as the snake stays active and isn't losing excessive condition.
4. **Environmental stress.** Recent rehoming, cage changes, excessive handling, or an enclosure without adequate hides can suppress feeding. Waiting about 48 hours after any disturbance before offering food again is common advice.
5. **A warm side that isn't warm enough.** This is a fixable husbandry problem, not a benign phase, corn snakes need roughly 82 to 85°F on the warm side to digest food safely, and can't digest a meal properly without it.
6. **Prey presentation issues.** Improperly thawed or cold prey, or a snake balking at a switch from live to frozen-thawed, can cause refusal that has nothing to do with health. Scenting the prey against live-prey bedding or a bit of broth is a commonly cited fix.
7. **Illness or parasites.** If refusal comes with other signs, see below, an underlying medical issue is the likely explanation and needs veterinary diagnosis.

## When to Actually Worry

Sources disagree meaningfully here. A healthy adult can physically survive roughly 2 to 3 months without food thanks to a slow reptile metabolism, and reptile-specialist sources describe refusal as not unusual until it passes about 1 to 2 months, outside of a planned brumation or a female's pre-egg-laying fast. A more conservative vet-content source instead recommends a call after missing just a couple of feeding sessions, especially outside of a shed cycle. Both are legitimate perspectives talking past each other, keeper field experience with a species known for long voluntary fasts versus standard vet caution.

Babies have far less reserve than adults, a hatchling going without food for over about a week is already worth taking seriously and may need veterinary attention. Regardless of duration, see a vet if refusal comes with any of: visible weight loss, lethargy or loss of coordination, wheezing or abnormal discharge, open-mouth breathing, vomiting or regurgitation, diarrhea, abdominal swelling, or sores in the mouth or on the skin. Monthly weigh-ins are a better guide than counting days, and give more benefit of the doubt to a snake that's active and holding weight than one that's lethargic or visibly thinning.
