# Angelfish Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

Angelfish are true ambush predators under that calm, ornamental look, and they'll gorge themselves given an open-ended amount of food, which is exactly why portion discipline matters here. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Angelfish care guide](/guides/angelfish/).

## Feeding Schedule by Life Stage

- **Adults:** Once or twice a day, a pinch of food at a time, letting them clear it within about 2 to 5 minutes before removing anything left over.
- **Juveniles:** 2 to 3 times a day to support faster growth.
- **Fry:** Don't eat for the first several days post-hatch, living off the yolk sac. Once free-swimming, start on live baby brine shrimp, several times a day, freshly hatched rather than after the shrimp's own yolk reserves deplete. Fry are gradually weaned onto crushed flake somewhere between weeks 3 and 6, depending on the source, and aren't community-tank ready until around week 9.
- **Breeding adults being conditioned:** Intensified feeding, up to 4 times a day with high-protein foods like tubifex or bloodworms.

## What Angelfish Actually Eat

Angelfish are omnivores with a protein-leaning diet. The staple is a high-quality flake, generally preferred over pellets for this species. Supplement with live or frozen protein at least twice a week: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, mysis shrimp, and tubifex worms, though tubifex and blackworms specifically should come from a reputable cultured or commercial source, never wild-collected, parasite and disease risk is real there. Plant matter in smaller amounts, blanched spinach, zucchini, and shelled peas, adds fiber and doubles as a remedy for bloating or constipation.

There's no gut-loading or dusting here, that's reptile husbandry, not fish. The closest equivalent is timing brine shrimp harvest so fry get freshly hatched nauplii rather than depleted ones.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe and beneficial: live or frozen bloodworms and brine shrimp, mysis shrimp, daphnia (which also works as a mild natural laxative for a bloated fish), and blanched shelled peas, zucchini, or spinach offered weekly for fiber.

Use caution with: tubifex or any worms from unknown outdoor sources, real parasite risk, stick to commercially cultured or frozen. Overfeeding, regardless of food type, is repeatedly named as the single most common mistake with this species, linked to poor water quality, bloating, and swim bladder problems. Small fish under about 2 inches are natural prey for angelfish given their ambush biology, not a food-safety issue, but a real pitfall if you're expecting them to coexist peacefully with tiny tankmates.

## Why Angelfish Stop Eating

1. **New-tank stress.** Newly introduced angelfish commonly refuse food for the first 2 to 4 days in a new tank, pH shock during acclimation is a specifically named contributor. Normal within that early window.
2. **Poor water quality.** Appetite loss combined with hiding is a top indicator of degraded water conditions, ammonia or nitrite buildup, rather than illness on its own.
3. **Territorial or social stress.** Angelfish are territorial, aggressive tankmates or a disturbed tank layout can suppress appetite.
4. **Internal parasites.** A stomach parasite specific to cichlids can cause extreme weight loss and a sunken belly even in a fish that's still eating voraciously. A separate parasite causes weight loss, appetite loss, and white stringy feces, with elevated mortality in fry and juveniles.
5. **Pickiness.** Newly acquired angelfish sometimes reject an unfamiliar food before accepting it once settled in.

## When to Actually Worry

Sourcing genuinely blurs two different numbers here: survival capability versus a responsible recommended maximum. One guide states angelfish can survive up to two weeks without food but recommends one week as the real maximum, other sources put the generally-fine window around 7 to 10 days. Treat any specific day count as rough hobbyist consensus, not an established clinical threshold.

The concrete physical warning sign is a sunken or pinched belly, signaling exhausted fat reserves and the start of muscle catabolism, advanced enough that it may not fully reverse even with refeeding. Because a cichlid-family parasite can cause a fish to keep eating while still developing a sunken belly, appetite alone isn't a reliable check, watch the belly shape too. A day or two of refusal with otherwise normal behavior isn't urgent, especially in a new or rearranged tank. Refusal beyond about 4 to 5 days, or any refusal paired with a sunken belly, clamped fins, lethargy, color loss, or stringy white feces, warrants water testing, quarantine, and a consult with an aquatic vet or experienced aquarist if it doesn't resolve.
