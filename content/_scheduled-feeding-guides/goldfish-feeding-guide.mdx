# Goldfish Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Goldfish don't have a true stomach, which changes how feeding actually works for them and why overfeeding is such an outsized risk. Here's a real feeding routine, and how to read a goldfish that's gone off food. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Goldfish care guide](/guides/goldfish/).

## Feeding Schedule by Life Stage

- **Fry:** Don't feed for the first couple of days after hatching, they're still absorbing their yolk sac. Once free-swimming, offer infusoria or baby brine shrimp nauplii, 3 to 6 times a day for the first month, tapering to about 3 times a day through roughly the first 4 months.
- **Juveniles:** 2 to 3 small feedings a day, reflecting higher caloric needs for growth.
- **Adults:** Sources genuinely diverge. One veterinary charity's own goldfish page recommends once a day, while its general fish page says 2 to 3 times daily, and other sources describe 1 to 2 times daily as standard. There's no single settled number, treat "1 to 2 small meals a day" as the safe range, and consider splitting into two feedings since goldfish have no stomach to hold a large single meal.

Portion size matters more than the exact frequency. Rules cited across sources range from "eaten in 30 to 60 seconds" to "under 2 minutes" to "2 to 5 minutes," the exact number varies, but the underlying principle is consistent: feed less than you think, and remove anything uneaten.

**Pond and outdoor goldfish** need to scale feeding down with water temperature. A normal diet is fine above roughly 50°F. Between about 39 and 50°F, switch to a reduced, more digestible wheat-germ-based formula fed less often. Below that, stop feeding entirely, the fish enters a low-metabolism dormancy and uneaten food just fouls the water.

## What Goldfish Actually Eat

Goldfish are omnivores. A quality <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0006G7OEI?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Hikari Gold Fancy Goldfish Pellets, 11 lb Mini">sinking pellet formulated specifically for goldfish</AffiliateLink> should be the base diet, not tropical fish flake, the nutrient profiles differ. Flakes work, especially for smaller or younger fish, but lose a meaningful share of certain vitamins within about 30 seconds of hitting water, making pellets the more nutritionally stable staple. Sinking pellets are also preferred for fancy, round-bodied varieties, since gulping floating food at the surface makes them prone to swallowing air.

Supplement 2 to 3 times a week with blanched or softened vegetables (shelled peas, spinach, kale, lettuce, zucchini, cucumber), plus high-protein treats in moderation, bloodworms, brine shrimp, and daphnia, and algae wafers. There's no gut-loading or calcium dusting involved the way there is for reptile or invertebrate feeders, nutritional balance here comes from genuine diet variety instead.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe: shelled peas (mashed or whole, often used to relieve constipation), spinach, kale, lettuce, zucchini, cucumber, bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, algae wafers, and the occasional small bit of orange, watermelon, or squash. Peas are a genuinely useful short-term fix for constipation, but shouldn't become a long-term staple.

Never feed: bread (swells and can cause blockage), avocado (contains persin, potentially deadly to fish), chocolate (toxic even in tiny amounts), citrus (can irritate gills and disrupt tank pH), raw meat, cheese, and onions. Tropical fish flake isn't toxic but is nutritionally mismatched to a goldfish's needs, skip it as a primary food.

## Why Goldfish Stop Eating

1. **Water quality problems.** Ammonia, nitrite, or nitrate spikes, especially in a newer tank still cycling, are the single most commonly cited reason a goldfish stops eating.
2. **Overfeeding, constipation, or swim bladder trouble.** Excess or low-fiber food causes gas buildup that presses on the swim bladder, showing up as appetite loss alongside abnormal floating or sinking.
3. **Stress from environment or tank mates.** A new tank, incompatible tank mates, bullying, or an undersized enclosure are repeatedly named triggers, with appetite loss often the first visible sign.
4. **Disease, parasites, or infection.** Ich, internal or external parasites, and fungal infections often show up as reduced appetite before more visible symptoms appear.
5. **Low water temperature.** Below roughly 50°F, outdoor pond goldfish slow into a natural, low-metabolism dormancy and eat little to nothing until spring. Normal for pond fish, not illness.
6. **Breeding season.** As temperatures rise in late spring, goldfish preparing to spawn, especially chasing males, may temporarily go off food. A normal, hormone-driven shift.
7. **Diet monotony.** An unbalanced, overly repetitive diet, or too much treat food crowding out regular pellets, can reduce interest in eating.

## When to Actually Worry

Short gaps of up to about 3 to 4 days are fine without any special arrangement for a healthy adult in an established tank. Beyond about 5 days, effects on health may start to show, and beyond a week to 10 days, arranging a feeder is a good idea. Outright survivability is commonly cited around 1 to 2 weeks thanks to a slow cold-water metabolism, but that's an upper survival bound, not something to aim for.

The real trigger to seek help is 24 to 48 hours of refusal combined with any of: pronounced lethargy, pineconing scales (a classic dropsy warning sign), clamped fins, floating sideways or sinking abnormally, gasping at the surface, visible wounds or white spots, cotton-like fungal growths, or a gaunt, visibly thinning body. That combination, not the fasting alone, is what actually warrants an aquatic vet.
