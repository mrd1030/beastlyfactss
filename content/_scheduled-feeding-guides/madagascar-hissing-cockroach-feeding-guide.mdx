# Madagascar Hissing Cockroach Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

Hissers are nocturnal, which changes how you should actually judge whether feeding is happening at all, and their diet looks a little different from what most keepers expect. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Madagascar Hissing Cockroach care guide](/guides/hissing-cockroach/).

## Feeding Schedule

Sources don't agree on a single frequency here. Pet-industry care sheets recommend keeping a dry protein source, high-quality dry dog food, cat food, or a commercial roach chow, available essentially all the time, topped up with fresh produce 2 to 3 times a week in a quantity the colony finishes within 24 to 48 hours so leftovers don't mold. An academic extension fact sheet instead describes a sparser regimen for a small pet colony: dry food about once a week, fruit added only every couple of weeks. That's a real difference in approach, not just wording, and both are worth knowing rather than picking one.

There's no distinct feeding schedule tied to life stage, newly hatched nymphs are independent immediately and feed themselves from birth. The one documented age-related difference is that breeding females need reliable access to protein-rich food to produce healthy broods.

## What Hissing Cockroaches Actually Eat

High-quality dry dog food is the most consistently recommended staple, several sources rate it as having a better overall nutritional makeup for hissers than cat or bird food, though those work as substitutes. A commercial roach or insect chow is an accepted alternative. Their wild diet is decaying plant matter and fallen, overripe fruit, so captive feeding is built to approximate that rather than something meat-heavy.

Offer fresh produce a few times a week: romaine lettuce and other dark leafy greens, dandelion greens, carrots, squash, sweet potato, apples, bananas, oranges, and grapes. Rinse produce and use pesticide-free when possible, chop into manageable pieces, and remove anything uneaten within a day or two, mold and fruit flies are the main colony-health risk from feeding.

Water needs care too. Most sources favor a water source that can't drown a roach, a cotton ball or sponge saturated with water in a shallow dish, or a commercial water gel, changed regularly, since an open dish poses a real drowning risk to small nymphs.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe: apple slices, banana, orange, romaine lettuce, dandelion greens, carrots, grapes, sweet potato, and squash.

Use caution with: iceberg lettuce, nutritionally much weaker than romaine, any produce treated with pesticides, and letting spoiled or moldy leftovers sit rather than removing them within a day or two. Skip diets that are too protein-heavy, hissers are naturally protein-sparing and don't need a meat- or pellet-heavy feed the way some other insects do.

## Why Hissing Cockroaches Stop Eating

1. **A nocturnal feeding schedule you're just not witnessing.** Hissers are light-averse and most active after dark, feeding is often genuinely happening overnight even when it looks like nothing's being eaten.
2. **Approaching a molt.** Nymphs go through about six molts over roughly five months to reach adulthood, and commonly go quiet and eat less beforehand. They need elevated humidity during this window to shed successfully.
3. **Stress from a new environment.** Newly acquired hissers commonly need an adjustment period before settling into visible normal feeding.
4. **Isolation.** Hissers are normally kept in groups, and a solitary individual can become insecure and hide rather than feed visibly.
5. **An enclosure that's too cool.** Temperatures dropping much below about 70°F cause lethargy and slow activity, including feeding. This resolves once the correct 75 to 85°F range is restored.
6. **Illness.** Genuine sickness signs include lying in one place without hissing when touched, lethargy beyond normal sluggishness, a dull exoskeleton, weight loss, discharge from the mouth or eyes, and abnormal feces.

Unlike many temperate reptiles, hissers are a tropical species, no source found documents a seasonal dormancy or brumation period for them in captivity, so that common exotic-pet explanation doesn't apply here.

## When to Actually Worry

The only concrete duration figure found, that a hisser can survive about a week without food, comes from a single lower-authority source with no independent corroboration, treat it as a rough benchmark rather than a validated one. There isn't strong veterinary-sourced guidance on exactly how long is fine to go without eating for this species.

What should prompt real concern, per keeper health guidance: inactivity or lying in one place, failure to hiss when handled, lethargy beyond normal pre-molt sluggishness, a dull-looking exoskeleton, visible weight loss, discharge from the mouth or eyes, visible sores, and abnormal feces. A visibly sick hisser is reported to commonly survive only about 2 to 3 days once symptoms are obvious, which argues for acting quickly, checking temperature and humidity and consulting an exotics-experienced vet, rather than waiting once multiple symptoms stack up.
