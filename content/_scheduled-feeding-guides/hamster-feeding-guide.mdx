# Hamster Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

A hamster's cheek pouches can hold roughly a fifth of its own body weight, which changes how you should read a day of apparent silence at the food bowl. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Hamster care guide](/guides/hamster/).

## Feeding Schedule

This is a real point of disagreement across sources, worth knowing rather than presenting as settled. Some veterinary sources describe pelleted food as something that should stay continuously available, hamsters are natural hoarders and rarely overeat pellets. Other feeding charts instead describe twice-daily measured feedings with specific daily targets, roughly 2 tablespoons for a standard-size hamster and about 1 tablespoon for dwarf hamsters, though a broader 1/8 to 1/3 cup range shows up elsewhere too. Both approaches are legitimate, pick one and stay consistent.

Fresh extras have firmer numbers: about 1 teaspoon of fresh vegetables a day, plus roughly a teaspoon of fruit around 3 times a week. Keep total fresh food, vegetables and fruit combined, under about 10% of the diet, and treats like seeds, nuts, and popcorn to 1 to 2 times a week, under 5 to 10% of the total.

Weaning timing varies by species, dwarf hamsters typically wean around 3 weeks, Syrians nurse a bit longer, roughly 26 to 28 days, with all species fully weaned and self-sufficient by 4 to 5 weeks.

## What Hamsters Actually Eat

The staple should be a nutritionally complete, species-appropriate hamster pellet or lab block, not a loose seed mix on its own, hamsters will pick out the sugary, fatty bits and leave the fiber-rich pieces, contributing to dental problems and obesity. A strict seed-only diet is specifically flagged as too high in fat and nutritionally poor.

Add small daily amounts of fresh vegetables, broccoli, cucumber, bell peppers, kale, spinach, peas, zucchini, and carrots, plus occasional fruit, apple with seeds removed, banana, melon, and pear. Timothy hay is recommended by several vet sources to encourage the chewing that wears down continuously growing teeth, a supplement here, not the diet base the way it is for rabbits or guinea pigs.

Hamsters are genuinely omnivorous in the wild and eat insects, protein-rich treats like mealworms or boiled unseasoned egg reflect that. Scatter or hide food around the cage rather than relying only on a bowl, it encourages the natural foraging and hoarding behavior that's core to this species. Fresh water needs to be constantly available through a valveless sipper bottle, checked daily for leaks.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe, in moderation: mealworms, boiled unseasoned egg, small amounts of nuts and seeds, plain pasta, unsalted popcorn, fresh vegetables and fruit as listed above, and timothy hay.

Genuinely toxic: grapes and rhubarb, explicitly flagged as poisonous to rodents. Raw onion and raw garlic carry real hemolytic anemia risk, though a couple of sources claim cooked versions in small amounts are lower-risk, treat that cooked exception cautiously rather than as confirmed. Chocolate, bitter almonds (distinct from common sweet almonds, a frequent point of confusion), raw kidney beans, raw potato, eggplant, and avocado are all named as unsafe. Citrus is genuinely disputed, some sources avoid it entirely, a specialist hamster-food resource argues occasional citrus is fine for Syrians specifically, with extra caution recommended for diabetes-prone dwarf breeds regardless.

## Why Hamsters Stop Eating

1. **Dental problems.** Cited as the single most common cause of appetite refusal. Continuously growing incisors that overgrow or get injured make chewing painful, so the hamster avoids food even while hungry.
2. **Wet tail.** A serious bacterial intestinal infection, especially dangerous in young or recently weaned hamsters, that can be fatal within about 48 hours if untreated. Appetite loss is an early sign alongside soiled, wet hindquarters.
3. **Cold-induced torpor.** Below roughly 65°F, hamsters can enter a hibernation-like state with minimal activity and little to no eating, it can look alarmingly like the hamster has died. Gradual warming is the first response, this is distinct from true hibernation, which pet hamsters don't undergo.
4. **Stress.** A new environment, handling, or cage changes can cause a temporary, lower-urgency dip in appetite.
5. **Respiratory infection or other illness.** Usually comes with other symptoms like labored breathing, discharge, or lethargy alongside reduced eating.
6. **Diabetes in predisposed dwarf species.** Campbell's and Chinese dwarf hamsters have documented high diabetes susceptibility, and it's listed as a specific cause of appetite and energy changes in those breeds.
7. **Cheek pouch impaction.** Uncommon, but soft or moist food can clump and lodge in a pouch. Watch for one-sided swelling, face-pawing, drooling, and sudden disinterest in collecting food, this needs a vet.

## When to Actually Worry

Sources give a tiered picture that converges on real urgency: 6 to 12 hours without eating while otherwise active is worth watching, 12 to 24 hours, or any refusal paired with lethargy or diarrhea, warrants a vet call, and 24+ hours without any food intake is described as critical, with 48+ hours considered life-threatening given a hamster's fast metabolism.

The important nuance: because hamsters hoard food in cheek pouches and cache it around the enclosure, you may not directly witness eating for a day or more even though the hamster is fine and quietly working through a stash, some hamsters can go 3 to 4 days relying on a pre-provisioned hoard. That's a different situation from one visibly refusing available fresh food while showing other symptoms, which is the real emergency. See a vet now regardless of exact hours for labored breathing or wheezing, wet or soiled hindquarters, discharge from eyes or nose, a swollen abdomen, seizures, noticeable weight loss, or a hunched, lethargic posture.
