# Red-Eared Slider Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

A red-eared slider's diet genuinely shifts as it grows, and its feeding biology has one quirk worth knowing before you're troubleshooting a turtle that "won't eat." For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Red-Eared Slider care guide](/guides/red-eared-slider/).

## Feeding Schedule

Juveniles eat daily, adults move to every 2 to 3 days rather than daily. Offer as much as the turtle can finish in about 15 to 20 minutes, then remove any uneaten food, sliders will overeat given an open-ended amount, and leftover food fouls the water fast. A juvenile-specific portion cue some sources use: roughly the size of the turtle's own head, or whatever it clears in 10 to 15 minutes.

## What Red-Eared Sliders Actually Eat

Sliders are omnivores whose protein-to-plant ratio shifts with age, young turtles need proportionally more animal protein, adults shift toward being mostly herbivorous. Sources disagree meaningfully on the exact percentage split (see the FAQ above), so treat any specific number as a rough guideline, not a settled figure, the direction is what matters.

**Commercial pellets** made for aquatic turtles are a real staple, larger floating pellets for adults, smaller sinking pellets for juveniles and hatchlings. **Animal protein** includes live feeder fish like goldfish or guppies (use some caution here, feeder fish can carry parasites or bacteria), earthworms, snails, crickets, mealworms, and occasionally small amounts of cooked, never raw, chicken or beef. **Plant matter** should include dark leafy greens, romaine, collard, mustard, and kale, plus dandelion greens, shredded carrots, squash, and green beans, fresh is preferred but frozen vegetables work occasionally.

Feeder insects can be gut-loaded on a nutrient-dense formula before offering them. One thing that isn't optional: sliders have no salivary glands and can only eat while at least partially submerged, always offer food in the water, never on the basking dock.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe: kale, collard, mustard, and dandelion greens, carrots and carrot tops, squash, green beans, berries, melon, apple in moderation, earthworms, crickets, and gut-loaded feeder insects.

Avoid: avocado (contains persin, potentially affects heart and respiratory function), onion, and rhubarb, all flagged as potentially toxic. Iceberg lettuce isn't toxic but is nutritionally near-empty, swap it for a real leafy green. Skip dairy entirely, turtles can't digest lactose, and avoid processed human food like bread or pasta. Raw grocery-store meat and anything wild-caught carry real parasite and contamination risk. Spinach isn't forbidden but contains oxalates that can bind calcium if fed too often, keep it occasional rather than a staple.

## Why Red-Eared Sliders Stop Eating

1. **Brumation.** A natural cold-season slowdown, roughly October through March, during which appetite drops even though the turtle stays otherwise responsive. Indoor sliders kept warm often don't fully brumate, so refusal in a consistently warm tank is more likely something else.
2. **Water or basking temperature too low.** One of the most common, fixable causes. Target roughly 75 to 80°F water with an 85 to 90°F basking spot, water below about 72°F slows metabolism enough that digestion essentially stops.
3. **Stress from a new or changed environment.** A recent tank change, rearrangement, or new handling routine can suppress appetite for a week or two while the turtle adjusts.
4. **Poor water quality.** High ammonia or nitrite from an undersized filter or infrequent water changes causes chronic stress that suppresses appetite.
5. **Shedding or pickiness.** Sliders may eat less while shedding scutes, and can refuse a newly offered food type simply because it's unfamiliar.
6. **Gravidity.** A female carrying eggs commonly loses her appetite, even for favorites, since developing eggs take up significant internal space. Normal on its own, but a week or more past expected laying while weak or straining is egg binding, a genuine emergency.
7. **Illness.** Refusal paired with wheezing, nasal discharge, swollen eyelids, or abnormal floating points to underlying illness rather than a normal lull.

## When to Actually Worry

Outside of brumation, guidance converges around roughly 2 to 3 weeks of total food refusal as the point to call a vet, sooner if warning signs appear alongside it. Appetite loss plus lethargy is a stronger signal of illness than brumation, especially when tank temperatures are stable and warm, there's no seasonal excuse for it then.

See a vet regardless of exact duration if refusal comes with: respiratory symptoms like wheezing, clicking, or open-mouth breathing, swollen or closed eyes, floating unevenly in the water, a soft or discolored shell, or significant lethargy even at proper temperature. For a gravid female, straining without producing eggs, or going a week or more past expected laying while weak, is treated as an emergency, don't wait it out.
