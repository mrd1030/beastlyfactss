# Gargoyle Gecko Feeding: Schedule, Diet, and Why They Stop Eating

<AffiliateDisclosure />

Gargoyle geckos look a lot like their crested gecko cousins at the feeding dish, but they're genuinely more carnivorous, which changes what "a complete diet" actually means for this species. For the rest of their care needs, see our complete [Gargoyle Gecko care guide](/guides/gargoyle-gecko/).

## Feeding Schedule by Age

- **Hatchlings and juveniles (0 to 12 months):** Fresh commercial gecko diet (CGD) daily. One detailed care sheet instead splits this into hatchling (0 to 12 weeks, 2 to 3 times daily) and juvenile/subadult stages (once daily), a meaningfully different breakdown, not just different phrasing.
- **Adults (12+ months):** CGD every 2 to 3 days, or every other day per the more granular life-stage breakdown.
- **Live insects:** Most sources say 1 to 2 times a week for juveniles, once a week for adults, one source says at least 3 times weekly. One specialist source is a real outlier, recommending insects no more than twice a month, specifically citing obesity risk in this species. Treat the weekly figures as the majority view and the twice-monthly figure as a notable, more conservative minority position.

## What Gargoyle Geckos Actually Eat

The staple is a commercial powdered <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B00KO450UC?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Pangea Fruit Mix Watermelon Complete Gecko Diet, 8 oz">crested gecko diet</AffiliateLink>, mixed with water to a ketchup-like consistency, roughly 2 to 3 parts water to 1 part powder. Offer it in a small cup or on a feeding ledge, gargoyles are arboreal climbers. Rotate 3 or more brands or flavors for variety and to avoid pickiness.

Unlike crested geckos, live insects here are described as a required supplement, not optional enrichment, gargoyle geckos are unusually carnivorous for New Caledonian geckos. Staples include gut-loaded dubia roaches, discoid roaches, red runner roaches, crickets, and black soldier fly larvae, with hornworms, waxworms, and butterworms as occasional fattier treats. Gut-load insects 24 to 48 hours before feeding and dust with <AffiliateLink href="https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0D6SP118Q?tag=beastlyfacts-20" product="Repashy SuperFoods Calcium Plus 4 oz">a calcium supplement</AffiliateLink> without D3 for most feedings, with a D3-inclusive product periodically. Occasional pinky mice, roughly once or twice a month, are an enrichment treat some keepers use, with clear warnings that overuse causes obesity.

## Safe Treats and Foods to Avoid

Safe, in moderation: calcium-rich, low-phosphorus fruit purees alongside CGD, plus waxworms, butterworms, hornworms, and sparing pinky mice as protein treats.

Avoid: avocado (contains persin, described across sources as toxic to geckos, though the specific "death within 24 hours" claim repeated on some pet blogs isn't traceable to a real toxicology source, treat the general avoid-avocado guidance as solid, not that specific figure). Citrus fruits are too acidic and can cause diarrhea, starfruit and rhubarb are high in oxalates, rhubarb specifically called potentially lethal. Skip garlic, onion, and eggplant, and dairy entirely, geckos are lactose intolerant. Wild-caught insects carry real pesticide and parasite risk, and processed or sugary human food has the wrong nutrient profile.

## Why Gargoyle Geckos Stop Eating

1. **Shedding.** Many geckos skip a meal or two in the days leading up to a shed, returning to normal once it's complete.
2. **Breeding season.** Males commonly go off food at the start of breeding season, and females may stop eating while gravid or before laying due to physical discomfort, sometimes for a week or more.
3. **Stress.** A new enclosure, rehoming, substrate changes, excessive handling, or being bullied by a tankmate can suppress appetite, geckos may hold out for weeks while adjusting. Juveniles housed together can also nip and eat each other's tails, one reason breeders keep juvenile group sizes small.
4. **An enclosure that's too cool.** Gargoyle geckos need adequately warm temperatures to digest food, a warm side that runs too cool slows digestion and drops appetite as a direct husbandry symptom.
5. **A mild winter slowdown.** Unlike species with true brumation, gargoyle geckos show a lighter seasonal slowdown, reduced appetite, less climbing, more hiding, typically lasting a few weeks with no deliberate cooling required.
6. **Illness.** Reduced eating alongside visible lesions, a swollen belly, rubbery limbs, respiratory issues, or mouth sores points to a real health problem rather than a normal dip.

## When to Actually Worry

A healthy, well-hydrated adult can reportedly go up to 2 to 3 weeks without food. Juveniles are more vulnerable and shouldn't go past about 4 to 5 days given their growth needs, and a sick or already-underweight gecko shouldn't go more than a few days without eating either. Water is more time-critical than food, don't let a gecko go more than 2 to 3 days without water access regardless of appetite status.

See a vet if the gecko hasn't eaten in about two weeks and is also losing weight, or if appetite loss persists beyond roughly a week alongside other red flags: rapid weight loss, most easily seen as thinning at the tail (gargoyle geckos use it as a fat-storage organ), lethargy, visible lesions, mouth sores, eye film or swelling, labored breathing, or rubbery limbs, a metabolic bone disease sign. Regular weighing is the most objective way to catch a real problem early, a small amount of natural fluctuation isn't itself alarming.
