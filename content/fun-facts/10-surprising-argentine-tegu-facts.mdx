# 10 Surprising Argentine Tegu Facts

Tegus get lumped in with "big scary lizard" stereotypes more than almost any other pet reptile, but the actual science behind them is stranger, and far more impressive, than the reputation. For care basics, see our full [Argentine Tegu care guide](/guides/tegu/).

1. They can warm their own bodies like a mammal, for part of the year. During breeding season, tegus raise their internal body temperature 4 to 10 degrees Celsius above the surrounding air, something no reptile had ever been documented doing before this was discovered.

2. They do it even in a pitch-dark burrow. This isn't just basking working extra well, tegus sustain the elevated temperature overnight with no external heat source available, which is what made the discovery so surprising to researchers.

3. Their heart rate more than sextuples for the occasion. Between winter dormancy and breeding season, a tegu's heart rate increases more than sixfold, part of the physiological shift that lets them generate and hold onto that extra body heat.

4. Scientists eventually traced the mechanism to their muscles. Research published in 2024 found the effect comes down to how mitochondria function inside skeletal muscle tissue, generating metabolic heat directly rather than through shivering.

5. They're not dangerous or venomous, despite their size and reputation. Tegus have no venom glands and aren't considered a threat to people, the real risk to an owner is a strong bite and sharp claws, not any toxin.

6. They're genuinely intelligent, by reptile standards. Tegus are known to learn their own names, recognize their regular keeper, and can be litter-trained, behavior usually associated with mammals, not lizards.

7. Adults can reach 4 feet long and 10 or more pounds, among the largest lizards commonly kept as pets, a big part of why they're rated for experienced keepers only.

8. They brumate through winter like many temperate reptiles, slowing down dramatically and eating little to nothing for months, then emerging in spring right around when that unusual body-heating ability kicks back in for the season.

9. Argentina isn't the only place they live anymore. Escaped and released pets have established real, breeding wild populations in parts of Florida and Georgia, where wildlife agencies now treat them as an invasive species of concern.

10. They're omnivores with a genuinely varied diet in the wild, eating fruit, eggs, insects, and small vertebrates, part of why a healthy captive diet needs more variety than a typical insect-only lizard diet.

<FunFact>
Before this was documented in tegus, generating your own body heat overnight with no outside source was considered a defining trait of "true" endotherms, birds and mammals, not reptiles. A 2-kilogram lizard quietly doing it in a dark burrow once a year rewrote a piece of that textbook definition.
</FunFact>
