# 10 Surprising Hedgehog Facts

Hedgehogs are small, spiky, and quiet, right up until you learn what they actually do with their own spit. For care basics, see our full [Hedgehog care guide](/guides/hedgehog/).

1. They "self-anoint" with their own frothy spit. When a hedgehog encounters a new or interesting smell, it often licks and chews the source until it works up a thick foam, then twists around and spreads that foam across its own spines.

2. Nobody's fully sure why they do it. The leading theories include masking their own scent from predators, coating their spines with something distasteful or mildly toxic, and even a form of self-comfort when settling into something new.

3. They're covered in 5,000 to 7,000 spines, and none of them are barbed like a porcupine's. Hedgehog spines are modified hairs, smooth and solid, meant to deter rather than embed in an attacker.

4. Rolling into a ball is a full-body defense system. A specialized muscle running around their body lets a hedgehog pull its spiny skin down over its face, legs, and belly, turning it into a nearly complete armored sphere.

5. They're mostly solitary and see poorly. Hedgehogs rely far more on smell and hearing than eyesight, part of why sudden movement, not sight, is usually what startles them.

6. Wild hedgehogs hibernate, but the African pygmy hedgehog kept as a pet doesn't, and forcing one into a cold-induced dormant state is actually dangerous, not natural hibernation, and needs immediate vet attention.

7. They're surprisingly fast for their size. Despite the waddling image, hedgehogs can move at a genuine trot when motivated, and pet hedgehogs are famous for running for miles on a wheel overnight.

8. Baby hedgehogs are born with spines already, just soft ones under their skin at birth that harden and emerge within the first day or two, sparing the mother during birth.

9. They're lactose intolerant. A saucer of milk left out for a hedgehog is classic folklore, but dairy causes real digestive problems, not a treat.

10. Anointing isn't limited to scary or toxic things. Hedgehogs have been observed self-anointing after encountering a totally new food, a new type of bedding, or even a new toy, suggesting it may be more about processing novelty in general than a strict defense response.

<FunFact>
Self-anointing looks bizarre in person: a hedgehog will chew something new until it's frothing at the mouth, then contort its body into genuinely impressive positions to paint that foam onto spines it can't see. It's one of the most iconic hedgehog behaviors, and still one of the least understood.
</FunFact>
